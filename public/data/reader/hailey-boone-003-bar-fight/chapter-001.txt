Molly Henderson was supposed to be in Paradise Valley for forty-eight hours, and she had packed accordingly. One canvas overnight bag with two flannels she had bought at the Livingston feed store and one black t-shirt with THE BAD PENNY across the chest in a font that looked like a threat. Louis the three-legged pit-mix rode shotgun in the U-Haul cab. Three cardboard boxes of Cal's things from the Livingston apartment were in the back, along with a set of jumper cables she had grabbed off her own workbench at four that morning for no reason she could name, which was fine, she was allowed to pack a pair of jumper cables.

She left the bar at two. Tito was behind the stick with a list of the three things she did not trust him to remember and a paragraph about what to do when the number-four tap started foaming. He read it, said, "Go to Montana, Henderson," and poured himself a shift beer he was not supposed to drink. She let that go. That was what you let go on the day you closed the bar for the week.

The drive up I-90 was the twelve minutes she had driven a hundred times. Cottonwoods on the Yellowstone full yellow, aspens on the hillsides turned a gold that almost had sound to it, and in her rearview a sky that had been making promises for three days. The truck radio played the Livingston station that ran country from before 1994 and local weather every twenty minutes. Seventy-two by noon, the weather said, a high of seventy-two through Sunday. She drove with the window cracked because Louis liked the window cracked, and because her left wrist, which had been broken on a patch of ice behind the bar in 2021, could tell when cold was coming from sixty miles out and this morning it had not said anything.

She took the Paradise Valley turn-off at four-fifteen.

The last time she had come up this road was Cal's seventieth. She and Cal had eaten steaks at the McClaren bunkhouse with a lot of men whose names she did not know, and Cal had leaned in at some point in the evening and said, "Moll, I'd have wanted to have been your father," and she had said, "You kind of were," and then one of the hands had asked if she knew how to run a pool table and she had put her beer down and run the pool table, and she and Cal had not gone back to it. That was July. He had been dead six months and twelve days. Some days she did the math. Today she was not doing it.

The cabin yard was where it had always been, a quarter-acre of crushed gravel and cottonwoods and an old fire-pit ringed in rocks that had been the same rocks for ten years, and behind it Cal's 1997 Ford pickup which she had inherited along with the cabin and which would not start. It sat under one cottonwood with the registration sticker she was going to have to renew and then un-renew. Under the other cottonwood, facing the gravel drive with its tailgate dropped, was a 1989 Ford F-250 she had never seen in her life. Cracked windshield, hood dusty in a particular way a working truck is dusty, and a USMC sticker in the back window faded to pink. She pulled the U-Haul up next to it and cut the engine.

Louis was already asking about the door.

She got out. Louis got out on the driver's side because his back left was the missing leg and that was his side. He went three steps toward the water trough by the fire-pit, sniffed, reconsidered, walked to the nearest cottonwood, and peed against it for a full minute. Watching Louis do anything was one of Molly's available pleasures and she took them where she found them.

Then Garbage came around the cottonwood.

Molly had never actually seen the chicken. Cal had written her about it in two different postcards the year after Wyatt Reeves brought in the new foreman woman Cal was so impressed with. Cal's theory was that the chicken was in love with Ruthie the blue heeler and could not be reasoned with on the subject. Molly had taken this as the kind of thing Cal said at seventy.

The chicken, a real-enough brown hen with a small red comb and one eye on Molly the whole time, walked up to the U-Haul's front left tire and bit it.

Molly stood by the driver's door and watched. There was a low whump of beak on sidewall. The chicken held on a second, like a chicken making sure the tire had understood, then released, took two measured steps backward, came forward, and bit it again.

Molly said, out loud, "I heard about you."

The chicken, having made her point, walked off across the gravel and around the back of the cabin like a woman who had somewhere to be.

Louis watched the chicken go and did not move. Louis had views about chickens that he had developed behind the bar in 2022 and had never substantively revised.

Molly turned to look at the cabin.

The cabin was the cabin. One room built in 1987 for a hand that had not stayed, a bathroom bolted to the side of it, and a tin roof that had been three different colors in her lifetime. The current color was dark green and it was being replaced in one corner by new shingles that were still orange-fresh in the afternoon light. There was a nail gun lying on the ridge. There was a man on the roof.

He had his back to her. He was on his knees running a course of shingles along the lower edge near the stovepipe, and he had not looked down yet. He was tall in the way a man can still be tall when he is kneeling, with shoulders that took up more of the roof than shoulders were supposed to, work jeans gone soft at the knees, flannel shirt with the sleeves rolled to the elbow, and the right forearm catching the sun in a way the left did not. She did not know what she was looking at on the right forearm yet. She was going to know in about ninety seconds. Right now she was a woman who had pulled into her dead uncle's yard and found a stranger on the roof, and the first thing she did was take inventory.

She called up, "Hey."

He looked down without startling. He had been tracking her since the gravel, she understood, from the way he looked at her, which was the look of a man who had been deciding for thirty seconds what to do with his face when she arrived, and had decided to do nothing with it. He swung a leg over the ridge, came down the side of the roof on the balls of his feet, found the top of the ladder without looking for it, and was on the ground in twelve seconds. He wiped his right hand on his thigh and walked to her across the gravel.

Up close he was what the shoulders had been advertising. Six-two, maybe six-three, a face that had been outside for a living, and a burn scar that started at the back of his right wrist and ran up the inside of his forearm to the crease of the elbow, shiny and pink and still raised in places, the kind of scar a man does not grow out of in a year. She saw it once. She was not going to see it again, and that was because he was not going to let it be a thing she kept seeing. She understood that inside the first five seconds.

He said, "You must be Henderson."

His voice was low and even and not asking.

She said, "I'm selling, I need you to move your truck."

He said, "I'm not moving my truck."

She said, "Excuse me."

He said, "I live here."

There was a beat in the yard where the only sound was Louis, who had arrived at her feet and was looking up at the man with the specific open interest of a dog meeting a man who smells like the ground. The man did not reach down. He looked at Louis for one second the way you look at a dog who has a missing leg, once, with the arithmetic done, and then he looked back at her.

She said, "Since when."

He said, "April."

"April what."

"Seventeenth."

She did the math. She had been behind her own bar on April seventeenth. Cal had been alive on April seventeenth. Cal had died on the twenty-third.

She said, "Who are you."

He said, "Sawyer Wrangel."

"Did my uncle tell you he was going to die."

"No."

"Did he tell his lawyer he had a tenant."

"No, apparently."

She let out a breath that was the sound her face made when her mouth was already moving to the next problem.

"Lease?"

"In a drawer in the cabin."

"Show it to me."

He walked to the cabin. He walked like a tall man who had been on uneven ground for a living, a slight lean forward off the hips, weight on the balls of his feet, a man whose body was the first tool he reached for. She followed him. Louis followed her. The screen door had a new spring on it that Cal had not put there.

Inside, the cabin was cold the way a cabin is cold at four-forty in late September with the door open all day. Cal's couch was still Cal's couch, Cal's woodstove still Cal's woodstove, sitting south of the one bed like a black dog that had chosen a spot on the floor ten years ago and had not been consulted about it since. The bed was made, actually made, with a quilt she did not recognize and sheets tucked military. The sleeping loft over the kitchen was still there and the ladder to it was still broken, a clean split in the third rung Cal had said he would fix and had not. The bathroom door was open. From where she stood she could see the toilet, and the toilet seat was cracked clean through, one hairline fissure from hinge to nose.

She said, "Your toilet seat's cracked."

He said, "Yes."

"You're going to fix that."

"I've been meaning to."

"How long."

"Three months."

She looked at him.

He looked at her.

He said, "It's on the list."

He went to the dresser by the woodstove, a pine dresser Cal had built in 1988 with drawers that opened a little wrong, and pulled out a manila envelope from the top drawer and handed it to her. She opened it standing up.

The lease was two pages. Cal's hand, not a lawyer's. April 17, 2026. Furnished cabin on McClaren ground. $400/month, six months, option to extend, tenant maintains the cabin in condition received. Signed in Cal's bad cursive. Signed underneath by one Sawyer Wrangel in a hand that looked like it had been filling out government paperwork for fifteen years. Four hundred a month for a cabin that could rent for twelve hundred in this valley without the view. Cal had written the number in pencil and then gone over it in pen because he had meant it.

She handed the lease back. He took it without a word.

She said, "My lawyer does not know about this."

"Okay."

"I mean he does not know this exists."

"Okay."

"Why didn't Cal tell him."

Sawyer did the smallest shrug, the kind that said I have wondered about this too, and I have an answer, and it's probably not a useful one. He said, "Cal did a lot of things in his own order."

She wanted to hit him for being right about Cal. She did not. She stepped back out onto the porch to call the lawyer, because what she was going to do standing on a porch in September in her dead uncle's yard was call a lawyer.

The porch was the long low porch Cal had built along the south side. Two chairs. A coffee can full of bent nails that Cal had been going to straighten out for eleven years. Her phone had one bar. She got Kennedy's voicemail, because Kennedy was in Great Falls on Tuesdays, and she left a message that was three sentences and did not contain any of the words she wanted to use.

She hung up.

Then, because Kristin Mills was the person she had been going to call tomorrow anyway, and because her thumb was already on the screen, and because the plan she had driven up here with had to have something done to it before she walked back inside the cabin and sat down across from the man who lived there, she called her real-estate agent.

Kristin picked up on the second ring. "Hi sweetheart. You in the valley?"

"I'm here."

"And?"

Through the open cabin window behind her, Molly could hear Sawyer putting a kettle on. The cabin had a kettle. Sawyer knew which burner to put it on. She was going to sit with that for a second.

She said, "I'm going to need forty-eight hours. Cabin'll be ready to show Thursday afternoon."

"Forty-eight hours."

"Yeah."

"You want me to line up photos?"

"Line up photos."

"Any particular angle?"

"The one that doesn't show the toilet."

Kristin laughed. "Okay honey. I'll call you tomorrow."

She hung up. She stood on the porch. She was looking at the cottonwood with her uncle's truck under it and the cottonwood with a stranger's truck under it and the gravel drive where Louis had stopped halfway between the porch and Sawyer's F-250 and was looking at Sawyer's F-250 like he was considering his options.

Through the open window she heard the kettle click off. She heard two mugs come down off a shelf. She heard him pour.

She had said forty-eight hours out loud, in a voice that carried, six feet from an open cabin window that was not covered by anything. She knew she had. She knew he had heard it. She stood there for another ten seconds to give herself the dignity of having chosen to go inside on her own.

She went inside.

He had set the two mugs on the kitchen table. Black coffee. One of the mugs was Cal's WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE, which Cal had gotten at some point and had apparently brought up to the cabin. The other was plain white, chipped at the rim, from the McClaren bunkhouse. He had poured hers into the Uncle mug without asking.

He did not say anything about the phone call.

She sat down. She picked up the mug. The coffee was hot. It was good coffee. It was better coffee than she made at the bar, which was a low bar, but it was also better than Cal's coffee, which was not.

She said, "What are you drinking."

He said, "The same as you."

"Where'd you get it."

"Hutterville Roasters. Half a pound every other Friday. Cal had a standing order I took over."

"You took over Cal's coffee order."

"It was easier than changing it."

She drank. Louis, having made his decision about the F-250, came back inside through the open screen and lay down on the rug by the woodstove. The rug was Cal's. The woodstove had not been lit since at least eight this morning, by the feel of the cabin.

She said, "When's the last time that stove was loaded."

He said, "Six-fifteen this morning."

"It's out."

"Yep."

"You going to load it."

"When the sun drops."

"It's getting cold."

"Not yet."

She wanted to argue with that. Her wrist said he was right. She did not argue with that.

She said, "Where are you sleeping tonight."

He said, "Floor. Sleeping bag under the bed. Been sleeping there since the news."

"What news."

"That you were coming."

"Since when."

"Monday."

Her mother, who tracked Molly's itinerary the way a weather app tracked her location, had told someone at McClaren that Molly was coming, and someone at McClaren had told Sawyer. He had slept on the floor for two nights by himself in a cabin he lived in, to make the math work in advance.

She set her coffee down.

She said, "When were you going to mention the lease."

He said, "When you asked."

"I had to pull in and find you on the roof for you to mention it."

"I wasn't going to holler it at you from the driveway."

She looked at him. He looked back. His eyes were a grey-blue more grey than blue at four-fifty in the afternoon, and he did not break the look. She had been running a bar for six years, she had stared down three hundred drunks and one truly committed insurance adjuster, and she had a working theory that if you held a man's eye for six seconds past the point he wanted you to, he would flinch.

She counted. She got to nine. He did not flinch.

She was the one who looked down at her coffee.

It was a feeling she did not have a word for yet, and she put it somewhere out of the way and drank her coffee.

She said, "Tell me about this roof."

He said, "Needs the south-facing half redone. Cal had the shingles delivered in May. I've been working on it Sundays."

"Sundays."

"Sundays."

"What are you doing the rest of the week."

"Hand work for Wyatt. Fence for three other outfits in the valley when Wyatt doesn't need me."

"And."

"And what."

"And what else."

He took a long drink of his coffee. He set the mug down. He said, "I was a fire jumper for eleven seasons. I quit in August."

"Okay."

She did not ask the follow-up. She had run a bar for six years. She knew what a man looked like when the first sentence had cost him something and the second sentence was going to cost him more and he was not going to be the one to spend it. She let the information sit on the table next to the coffee.

Louis got up off the rug, walked across the cabin floor in his diagonal three-legged walk, and leaned against Sawyer's left calf. He did not ask Sawyer for anything. He put the full weight of his shoulder into it and held it there. Sawyer, without looking down, dropped his hand from the mug and put it on the dog's head and left it there.

Molly watched.

She did not comment.

She did not comment in the specific way a woman does not comment when a thing has happened in a room she will be thinking about at one in the morning lying three feet from a man in a sleeping bag. She did not want this cabin or this chicken or this man or this dog's opinion. She wanted forty-eight hours and eighty thousand dollars and a truck for scrap and her Saturday night bar shift.

She stood up. She took her mug to the sink, rinsed it because Cal had taught her to rinse a mug before it went back, and set it upside down on the dish rack Cal had bolted to the counter in 1996 with four screws that had been wrong for that countertop and had worked anyway.

She said, "I'll get my bag."

He said, "Okay."

She went back out into the yard. Her U-Haul was where she had left it, and the cottonwoods had not moved, and the chicken was somewhere behind the cabin living her own chicken life. The sun had dropped the length of a beer glass in the hour Molly had been on the porch and in the cabin, and the light was the specific gold light this valley did in late September that made everything look like it was going to matter. Her wrist had started to say something about cold by Thursday.

She hauled her overnight bag down out of the U-Haul and stood in the gravel with it in her hand, looking at the cabin. Through the window she could see him at the sink, rinsing his own mug. He had heard her say forty-eight hours on the phone. He was not going to bring it up. That was worse than if he had.

Louis was sitting on the porch step now, facing the cabin door. He was not looking at Molly. He was looking at the door Sawyer was about to come back through, and he was waiting for it to open.

Molly said, to Louis, quietly, "Don't."

Louis did not turn his head.

She walked to the porch. She stepped over him. She went inside.
