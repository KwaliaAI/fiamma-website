Tito's van pulled into the yard at ten-oh-two Saturday, and Garbage came around the cottonwood at ten-oh-three to bite the front left tire, which was exactly what Molly had warned Tito about the night before.

Tito got out. He looked at the chicken. The chicken looked at him. He said, "That's the chicken."

She said, "That's the chicken."

Brenda got out of the passenger side in her EMT off-duty hoodie with the Park County patch, a tool roll in one hand, a thermos in the other. She walked past the chicken without looking at it. Marco was in the back seat with the floor molding across his lap, and he was going to be the harder problem of the day, because Marco had seen the photos Molly had sent Brenda and Marco had opinions.

Marco got out. He said, "Where's the base run, Henderson."

She said, "Inside."

Marco walked past her with the molding on his shoulder and the look of a carpenter who had been composing a plan for eight hours on the drive up. Sawyer was at the porch stacking rounds against the south wall. Marco stopped in front of him, said, "Marco Villegas," and stuck his hand out.

Sawyer shook the hand. "Sawyer."

Marco said, "You the lease."

Sawyer said, "Yeah."

Marco nodded. That was Marco's intake form completed. He went inside.

Brenda was pouring coffee out of her thermos and looking at Sawyer over it. "Brenda. Pulled Tito out of a ditch in '22. Patched him up about six times. Be warned."

Sawyer said, "Sawyer."

Brenda said, "Uh-huh."

Louis came out in his three-legged walk and met Tito on the top step, and Tito, who had been bringing Louis jerky twice a week for two years at the bar, sat down right there and opened a baggie of jerky without asking.

Molly said, "Don't feed him that, Tito."

Tito said, "Too late."

Sawyer said, "Coffee's on," and went inside to get Brenda one.

Molly stood in her own yard with Marco already audibly measuring something with a tape he had pulled without asking where the tape was kept, and she understood the way she had understood it sometimes at her bar on a Friday night at nine-thirty: the day was going to be run by the people who had shown up.


Sawyer and Tito went to the west side for the weather stripping. Brenda took the porch post Molly had been meaning to look at. Marco was inside along the east wall with the molding in sections and the chop saw, talking to the molding. Molly had heard Marco talk to lumber before. She did not interrupt.

At eleven-twenty Tito drove a galvanized roofing nail through the pad of his thumb clean through and came around the cabin and held the hand out to Brenda.

Brenda did not look up from the post. "How bad."

Tito said, "Through and through. Quarter inch."

She looked at it for three seconds, reached into her roll for gauze and shears, and wrapped it in four efficient moves. "You're going to need a tetanus."

Tito said, "I've done worse."

Brenda said, "I know, I was there."

Tito went back to the weather stripping and used the left hand.

Molly watched the whole thing from the kitchen window. She had made four sandwiches nobody had asked her to make, because that was what Cal would have done at eleven on a Saturday with a cabin full of people. Sawyer came in for water and leaned on the counter.

He said, "You're on your fourth sandwich."

She said, "I know how many sandwiches I'm on."

He said, "Okay," and went back out.


Marco was talking to Sawyer on the porch when she brought the beers.

Marco was saying, "I don't mean any disrespect. I know you've been here six months. The subfloor's half an inch off across the run. I'm shimming. I'm doing it right."

Sawyer said, "I believe you."

Marco took a bite of his sandwich. "Henderson, this is a good sandwich."

Molly said, "Marco."

Marco said, "I'm doing the base," and went back inside. The saw ran once and then again, his voice continuing through the open door, addressed now to the shim under the subfloor.

Sawyer ate the rest of his sandwich without comment. Molly handed him a beer. She sat in the porch chair and drank half of hers in one swallow and she looked at her cabin with three of her regulars working on it in the October sun.


Deb pulled in at two.

Her Bronco came up the drive slow because Deb drove every piece of gravel in Park County like a vet who had pulled one too many foals out of a bad ditch. Brenda waved from the porch. Tito said, "Reeves," without looking up.

Deb walked to Molly at the chair. "Henderson."

Molly said, "Reeves."

Deb looked at Sawyer, who was at the woodpile splitting a round he did not need to split because the pile was full from yesterday, and she looked at him for about ten seconds.

Deb said, "Kitchen."

They went inside. Molly poured two mugs.

Deb said, "How long has he been splitting that pile."

Molly said, "He's not splitting a pile. He's splitting one round."

Deb said, "Uh-huh."

She drank. She looked out the kitchen window at Sawyer, who did not appear to know they were in the kitchen and who did know they were in the kitchen because Sawyer knew where every woman in a quarter-mile was at any given minute.

Deb said, "He's not going anywhere."

Molly said, "He has until February."

Deb said, "Sure."

She set the mug down. "I'm staying for dinner. Cash is meeting me here at five with a pie June made, because June said the word cabin on the porch last Thursday and she's been baking since Wednesday."

Molly said, "Okay."

Deb said, "He any good."

Molly said, "At what."

Deb said, "Any of it."

Molly looked at her coffee. She set it down. "All of it."

Deb said, "Okay," and walked back out. The screen door clapped once. Deb went straight to Brenda on the post and inside ninety seconds Brenda was laughing.

Molly washed the mug. The saw was running behind her. Marco was saying, to the molding, "There we go."


The bar story came out at four.

Marco had stood up from the base run for the first time in two hours. Louis was across three laps. Sawyer was on the porch step and was not going to join the conversation.

Marco said, "Tell me the best thing you ever saw at the bar, Henderson."

Tito said, "Oh, do the menu woman."

Molly set her beer down.

She said, "Fall of '23. Tuesday. Three-thirty. Woman walks in, sits at the south stool, says 'margarita.' I make it. She takes a sip. Orders a cheeseburger. Eats it. Orders the fish. Eats it. Chicken special. Eats it. Does this for three hours. Every single item on the food menu. Every one. One margarita the whole time, nursed. Pays the tab, one sixty-four. Leaves a hundred cash on top. Walks out. Never says anything else."

Marco said, "You never saw her again."

Molly said, "Never saw her again."

Brenda said, "I was there. I watched her eat the fish."

Tito said, "My theory is she was dying."

Brenda said, "My theory is Tito's theory is why he's never had a date."

Sawyer, on the step, tipped his beer back. He said, quietly, "Bet."

Molly said, "Bet what."

He said, "That Tito's right."

Tito said, "Thank you, Wrangel."

Brenda said, "Don't encourage him."

Molly said, "I don't think she was dying."

Sawyer said, "No."

He did not explain further. He was looking at his beer label. He had a theory that was not Tito's and was not hers, and he was not going to offer it in a group on her porch.

Marco drained his beer and went back inside to the base run.


Cash came at five with two pies and the news that he had eaten a slice of one in the truck on the way down. He said hi to Deb. He said, "Wrangel," and Sawyer said, "Hollander," and Cash was done with his social obligations for the hour. He sat on the step. Louis came to him. Cash put his hand on Louis's head and did not say anything else.

Marco finished the base run at six-fifteen, hands on hips, sawdust from the chest down, a pencil behind his ear that had been there for six hours. He said, "Yeah."

Molly said, "It looks good, Marco."

Marco said, "It is good."

At seven the porch was paper plates and empty bottles and one shoe Louis had moved from one end to the other for reasons Louis did not share.

Brenda said, "Hank's."

Marco said, "One beer. I'm driving home sober."

Brenda said, "You say that every time."

Marco said, "I say it when I mean it."


Hank's at eight was the second-Saturday crowd, the pool-table crowd, louder than Friday karaoke by about a third. Hank nodded from behind the bar the way Hank nodded, which meant I kept you a table and I will not make you ask. He had. Deb and Cash took the booth across the room because Cash did not do group tables on any reliable basis. Sawyer was at the end of the long table against the wall.

Brenda said, "Who's got the next round."

Molly said, "Me."

She went to the bar. Hank was pouring a pitcher for a four-top and she was three feet from it, and she was already clocking them, because six years behind a bar had her clocking a four-top before she clocked her own purse.

They were out-of-towners. She could tell by the coats. Park County coats had a specific wear to them and these did not. Two on each side, two beers in, and the one nearest her with his back to her had the voice of a man who had been loud since he got there. He was telling a story about a woman in Bozeman. The story was not flattering to the woman in Bozeman.

Hank pushed four beers across. "They been here an hour."

Molly said, "I hear them," and took the beers back.

Marco was already looking at the four-top. That was the problem. Marco did not look at a four-top the way another man would; Marco looked at a four-top the way a man looked at a finish-carpentry joint done poorly. Marco had already clocked the stitching on the jackets and was not going to let it go.

Marco said, "Look at that stitching."

Brenda said, "Don't."

Marco said, "I'm just saying. That's production stitching with the wrong thread tension. Those jackets are going to fail at the arm seam in about six months."

Tito said, "Marco."

Marco said, "I'm not saying it to them. I'm saying it to you."

But Marco's voice was Marco's voice, and the voice of a carpenter on the job site carried, and the man at the four-top turned the whole way around on his chair.

He said, "You got something to say, chief."

Marco said, "I was telling my friend about stitching."

The man said, "About my coat."

Marco said, "About stitching."

The man stood up.

It was eleven steps from the four-top to Molly's table, and the man took six of them before Brenda was out of her seat.

Brenda said, "Hey."

She said it in the voice she used at a scene, trained to insert a beat into a situation that did not have a beat in it, and the voice did the work. The man stopped. He looked at Brenda. He said, "You his lawyer."

Brenda said, "I'm an EMT. Sit back down before I have to work."

Molly was already standing. She had been standing for about four seconds without knowing she had stood up, which was her bar-owner reflex from six years of being the first person out from behind the stick. She was between Marco and the man before she had planned to be. She was going to say sit down please, which was a play she had run three hundred times at the Bad Penny.

She did not run the play.

Sawyer had stood up from his end of the table.

He came up behind her left shoulder and past it without touching her. He was not in a hurry. He was fast the way a man was fast who had walked into rooms on fire and had understood in three breaths what the room was doing. He was in front of the man before Molly could finish the breath she had started for sit down.

Sawyer said, "Hey."

He said it low. He said it the way a man said it to a horse that was about to shy.

The man looked up.

Sawyer was six-two. The man was five-ten. That was part of it but it was not it. It was that Sawyer had put himself at the specific distance that read as I am inside your reach and I am not afraid of your reach, and the man, whose whole body had been loaded for Marco, who would have given Marco a shove and taken the answer it produced, did not know what to do with a fire-jumper at fourteen inches saying hey the way you said it to a horse.

Sawyer said, "You and me are going to sit down in a second. You're going to sit at your table. I'm going to sit at mine. We're going to be okay about this."

The man said, "Your buddy, he..."

Sawyer said, "I know."

The man said, "Said..."

Sawyer said, "I know."

Sawyer did not raise his voice. His hands were at his sides, open, relaxed, visible, which Molly clocked the second after she had clocked everything else, because that was the thing a man did who had been trained to not spook somebody who was spookable. The hands said I am not about to. The man, whose whole body had been a wind-up from chair to Molly's table, could feel the hands being at rest and did not have an answer to it.

Sawyer said, "You okay."

The man said, "I'm..."

Sawyer said, "Yeah. You're good. Let's both go sit down."

He stepped back one step. Just enough to give the man a direction that was not forward. The man's whole body had been running on forward, and the step gave him somewhere else to go, and he took it. Two steps back, then three, then he turned around and walked back to his chair. The three other men at the four-top had not moved, because they had clocked Sawyer in the first second and made the arithmetic that whatever they did was going to be on their own, and none of them had been drunk enough to sign up for that.

The whole thing had been four seconds, Molly's count. It might have been three.

Sawyer came back to the table. He sat down. He picked up his beer.

Marco was white. Tito had his good hand on Marco's shoulder. Brenda sat down and reached across and took Marco's beer and replaced it with her own half-full club soda.

Hank came over from behind the bar and said to Sawyer, "Thank you," which was not a thing Hank said to anybody, and Sawyer said, "It's all right," and inside ten minutes the four-top paid and left and Hank's went back to the noise it had been running on.


Molly did not say anything for a while.

She had broken up four hundred bar fights. She had a working filing system for the ways men broke them up: size, tone, the larger asshole outranking the first. She had never in six years filed what she had just seen.

She had watched a man put himself at fourteen inches and not move his hands and take four seconds to defuse a wind-up that was going to put Marco on a floor, and she had watched him do it not as a performance. He had arrived at the problem and addressed it in the minimum calories it required and returned to his beer.

She had been running on I-will-take-a-punch-to-see-who-flinches since she was twenty-two, and what she had just watched was not a better version of what she did. It was a different category.

She did not say any of this. She was not going to. It was the kind of thing a woman filed in a particular drawer and did not open on a Saturday night at Hank's.

Sawyer caught her looking.

He said, quietly, "You good."

She said, "Yeah."

He said, "You were about to."

She said, "I was."

He said, "I know."

She said, "I would've."

He said, "I know."

He drank his beer. He did not say I got him before you had to. He had done it because he had been closer and calmer, and that was the whole thing.


Marco asked at the cabin.

They were in the yard at ten-forty-five under the cottonwoods unloading the tools back into the van, because Brenda had a shift at six. Molly was on the porch with a trash bag. Cash had gone. Deb had driven back to Bozeman at nine-thirty.

Marco was at the back of the van with the chop saw in his arms. Sawyer was holding the other end.

Marco said, "You were a jumper."

Sawyer said, "Yeah."

Marco said, "Tito told me on the way back."

Sawyer said, "Okay."

Marco said, "How many seasons."

Sawyer said, "Eleven."

Marco said, "When'd you quit."

Sawyer said, "August."

Marco said, "Rough season."

Sawyer said, "Rough season. Took some time."

Marco looked at Sawyer for a beat. Marco was not Tito. Marco had a carpenter's eye for a joint, and what he had in front of him at the back of the van was a seam sealed with the caulk of a four-word sentence, and Marco knew a caulk job when he saw one.

Marco did not press.

Marco slid the saw into its case and closed it. Marco said, "Thanks for the hand, Wrangel," and he meant the fight at Hank's and he meant the day at the cabin and he meant the four-word seal he had just left alone, and Sawyer said, "Yeah," and that was that.

Molly was on the porch ten feet from the van, inside the pool of porch light. The kitchen window above her was open. She had heard the whole conversation through the open side door of the van.

Her face did a thing.

It did the thing a face did when you had been in a kitchen all day watching a man take the long way around a question, and now you had heard him take the short way around the same question in the dark.

Sawyer looked up across the yard. He saw her face. He did not look away, and she did not look away, and then Marco slapped the van door and said, "Okay, rolling," and Brenda hugged Molly on the porch step and held it a beat longer than a hug usually held, and they got in the van, and the van rolled out of the yard.

Sawyer stood under the cottonwood until the tail-lights were gone.

She stood on the porch until he started walking to her. He stopped at the bottom step.

He said, "You heard that."

She said, "Yeah."

He said, "Okay."

She said, "Okay."

He came up the step. He did not touch her. Louis leaned against his calf. He put his hand on the top of the dog's head without looking down.

She said, "You coming in."

He said, "Yeah."

They went inside. The cabin smelled like cedar sawdust and pie and a day that had had people in it. She loaded the stove from the south side. He was at the sink washing his hands.

She said, to his back, "Don't."

He said, "Don't what."

She said, "Don't bring it up."

He said, "I wasn't going to."

She said, "Okay."

He dried his hands on the kitchen towel. He turned around. He said, "Later."

She said, "Yeah."

He said, "Not tonight."

She said, "No."

He nodded once.

She got in the bed on the south side. Sawyer was on the north. She lay on her back with her hand on her stomach under the quilt and she thought about a man who had stepped inside fourteen inches at Hank's and walked out on four seconds, and who had said rough season, took some time in the dark to a carpenter who had seen the caulk.

Those were the same man.

She did not know what to do with them being the same man.

She closed her eyes.

Sawyer was on his back next to her. The stove held the cabin at sixty-two. He could hear her breathing go slow inside three minutes, and he stayed awake a long time after that, because there was a voicemail on his phone he had not listened to since October second of last year, and he had carried it into the yard today, and into a four-second stand at a bar he did not know, and into a van door where a carpenter had left him a seam he had not asked to have left.

He closed his eyes anyway. He did not sleep.
