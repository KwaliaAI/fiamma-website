She came up out of sleep at four in the morning and she was cold.

The kind of cold that had gotten into the quilt and through it, a cold that had been working at her for an hour before her body had decided to come up for air about it. Her breath was visible in the gap between her mouth and the quilt. The cabin window above the sink had fogged at the bottom and was clear at the top, and through the clear part the sky was the black sky Paradise Valley did at four in the morning on the night the first hard frost showed up. The porch thermometer had said forty-two when she went to bed. It was not saying forty-two now.

The woodstove had gone out.

She knew it had gone out before she looked. The cabin had the particular cold of a cabin where the fire had given up around two-thirty, and she had not woken up at two-thirty because she had been pressed against a man who was doing his own thermal work, and the fire had gone and nobody had gotten up.

She was on the south side of the bed.

She lay there for a count of three doing the arithmetic on that. She had gone to sleep on the east side every night she had been in this cabin. The east side was six feet from the woodstove. The south side was about eighteen inches from it, because the bed ran east-west against the log wall and the stove sat south of the foot, and Cal had set the bed and the stove there in 1987 for a reason the reason of which had become very clear to her approximately forty seconds ago.

She had moved in her sleep.

She was always the one who migrated. She had been told so by two boyfriends and by her mother on a trip to Cody in 2019 where they had shared a queen and Molly had woken up on top of her mother's arm. The south side was six inches from a woodstove. Her body knew what her body was doing.

Sawyer was on his back next to her. She could tell from the breathing. He was not asleep and he was not pretending to be. He was the kind of man who did not bother to pretend to be asleep when he was not. His eyes were open to the log ceiling, she was going to guess. She did not turn her head to check.

She got out of bed.

She did it in one move because the air was going to be worse than the quilt and you did not stage the move. She put her feet on the cold floorboards. She crossed the eighteen inches to the stove with the quilt off her shoulders and the cabin air on her skin through the t-shirt she had slept in, which was his t-shirt, which she had been wearing since nine. She crouched at the stove door.

The stove had coals under the ash, not many, but enough. She opened the door. She got the poker off the hook and she moved the ash off the coals, and she got three pieces of kindling off the stack and laid them over the coals the way Sawyer laid kindling, which was the way Cal had laid kindling, which was the way she had been taught to lay kindling when she was twelve. The kindling took on the third breath she blew on it. She laid in two small rounds on top. She closed the door the right amount. She stayed crouched.

The fire caught.

She stayed where she was for another count of ten watching the seam of the door go orange, and then she stood up and she went back to the bed.

She got in on the south side because that was where her pillow was now, and she had moved her pillow in her sleep too, because her body had moved her pillow when it moved her. She pulled the quilt up. She lay back.

He was still on his back. He was still awake. She knew he had watched her do the fire because the dark on his side of the bed was the particular dark of a man who had been watching a thing without commenting on it, and that was a particular dark she had been sleeping three feet from since Monday.

She did not say anything about the side of the bed.

He did not say anything about the side of the bed.

After a minute he turned onto his side facing her and he put his hand on her stomach on top of the t-shirt, palm flat, fingers spread, the way a man put a hand on a woman when he was not asking anything of the hand, and she left it there. His body came in against her shoulder and hip. The cabin was starting to climb back toward sixty. She closed her eyes.

She went back to sleep around the time she decided she was not going to think about the south side of the bed until she had to, and she was going to have to, and she was going to do the thinking in daylight, and she could sleep the last two hours on the side of the bed that was now hers.

She slept.

Her shoulders hitched up and in about a quarter-inch and her hand pulled toward her ribs and the coffee in the UNCLE mug came up to the rim and stayed in the mug. He loaded it wrong.

She watched him load it wrong. He was tired and she was tired and the cabin was warm enough to make a man lazy about it, and he laid two pieces in the wrong direction over the coals, the long axis pointing at the door instead of across it, a configuration that was going to smoke at the seam inside an hour and was going to cut the burn time by a third and was going to wake both of them up at three in the morning to a cabin at fifty.
