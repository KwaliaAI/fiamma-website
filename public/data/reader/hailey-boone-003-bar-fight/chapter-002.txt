The sun had been dropping the whole time she had been inside the cabin, and by the time she came back out with her overnight bag and her jumper cables she did not need, the gold was off the cottonwoods and the shadow of the ridge to the west had reached the fire-pit. The thermometer nailed to the porch post said sixty-eight. Molly looked at the thermometer because looking at the thermometer was one of the things a woman could do instead of looking at the fact that she was not going to be driving back to Livingston tonight.

She was not going to be driving back to Livingston tonight.

Sawyer was on the porch, holding her canvas bag. She had set it down in the gravel to think about the thermometer and he had come out and picked it up without asking. Louis was on the step next to his boot.

She said, "I had that."

He said, "I know."

He handed her the bag. He did not make the moment anything. He stepped past her into the gravel and went to the back of his F-250 and came back with an armful of split cottonwood and took it inside and set it down by the stove. That was what a man did on a porch at six-fifteen when the temperature was going to drop like that, and she stood there in her own yard holding her bag and watching him do it.

She went inside.

The cabin was at that specific temperature a cabin gets in late September when the door has been open all day and the woodstove has been out since six-fifteen this morning. Her wrist had started saying things. She set her bag on the foot of the bed, and then she looked at the bed, and then she looked at Sawyer's sleeping bag rolled and tucked under it where he had apparently been keeping it since Monday, and then she looked at the woodstove.

She said, "I'll take the bed."

He said, "Yep."

"I mean — "

"I know what you mean."

He was laying kindling in the stove in the specific crosshatch a man lays kindling when he has been laying kindling a long time, not watching her, and Louis had come in behind her and lain down on the rug like a dog who had decided where he lived. She stood in the middle of the cabin with her canvas bag at her hip and her wrist saying what her wrist said, and she watched the fire come up. Louis watched the fire come up. Sawyer watched the fire come up, which was a thing she would have had to think about at another time when she was less cold.

She said, "I'm hungry."

He said, "I've got bread, cheese, cold beef, beer. Half's yours."

"I didn't ask you to feed me."

"Okay."

"I'll pay you."

"Okay."

He was already getting a paper-wrapped parcel out of the fridge, which was Cal's fridge, avocado-green and from 1983 and still running, and he was setting a loaf of bread on the counter with a wedge of something yellow and cutting both with a folding knife out of his front pocket. Two plates. Her argument, she understood, was over before she had made it.

She accepted, because she was hungry and because accepting felt like winning a round she had not figured out yet. That was a feeling a bar owner had regularly. You handed a man a beer he had not ordered and watched him drink it and waited to see what kind of man he was.

They ate on the porch.

The thermometer had dropped to sixty-five by the time she sat down on the top step with her plate in her lap. She was in her leather jacket with the collar up. Sawyer was in the flannel from the roof with a white t-shirt under it, and he was not shivering, which she resented because her wrist was doing its performance. He sat on the porch chair because she had taken the top step. Louis lay across his left boot. Molly said, "So Cal rented you this cabin in April."

He said, "Yep."

"From a sign at the feed store."

She said, "I should remember that."

He said, "No you shouldn't. I looked worse than now."

"No."

"Good. The cord was bad."

He had been asked a question and he had answered it. That was the problem with him, already, at six-fifty on a Tuesday in late September.


She was in the bed at ten.

She had brushed her teeth standing up at the cracked-seat bathroom because there was nowhere to sit. She had washed her face in water that ran a little rust for three seconds and then clear. She had put on a long-sleeve t-shirt from the overnight bag and kept the jeans she had worn all day, because she was not undressing any further in a cabin where a man was about to be asleep eight feet from her on the floor.

The cabin had a chest-high band of heat by the stove and cold air along the floorboards. Sawyer was moving around in the dark with the sound of a man who had spent two nights figuring out where the squeaks were. The sleeping bag went on the floor on the north side of the bed, as far from the stove as you could get in a cabin this small. He lay down. The floor gave the sound of a man heavier than the floor had been hoping for.

Louis clicked across the floor on his three legs. He stopped. He lay down. She did not look to see whose side he had chosen.

She thought about Kristin Mills, and the word showing, and the eighty thousand dollars she had been telling herself since Cal's funeral she was going to get out of this cabin. She thought about whether Sawyer was awake, which was a question she was not going to ask.

She woke up because she was cold.

The fire had gone out. She knew it had gone out before she opened her eyes, because the air in the cabin had that specific thin dry cold you got when a stove stopped giving, and her left wrist had a full editorial on it, and Louis had gotten up off wherever he had been sleeping and had come to stand next to the bed with his nose on the quilt and was breathing on her face.

She opened her eyes.

The cabin was dark. The one window she could see out of showed a sky with the kind of stars that meant no clouds and no heat holding. Her phone, face down on the floor next to the bed, said three-oh-six when she picked it up.

Sawyer was already at the stove.

He was on one knee in front of it with the door open, laying a piece of split cottonwood on the coals, and the coals were live enough that she could see his face in the orange, and he looked at her when she came up without a sound and did not say anything. He fed the stove another piece. He closed the door to the right amount. He sat back on his heel for a second to see that the fire had caught.

The fire had caught.
