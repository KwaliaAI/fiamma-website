She came up out of sleep at six-fifteen and did not arrive all at once. The log wall was on her left, which was the wrong wall, and the stove was on her right, which was the wrong direction. The gap in the bed next to her was the specific temperature of a gap a tall man had left ninety seconds ago. She lay there for a count of three doing the arithmetic.

The cabin was cold. The stove was going, and the porch door was open, and she could hear somebody outside splitting kindling in a slow Thursday-morning rhythm of a man not trying to get anywhere with it. Louis was not on the rug. Louis was out there too.

She got out on the east side, stood for half a second on the floorboards in bare feet, then put on yesterday's jeans and today's t-shirt and yesterday's flannel and walked to the kitchen window.

The woodpile was behind the cabin, past the gravel turn-around and under the back cottonwood that was more yellow than it had been on Tuesday. Sawyer was at it in the canvas coat and a grey t-shirt and no hat, splitting a round with the slow deliberate swing of a man who had been splitting rounds since he was twenty. Louis was three feet off his left boot, sitting on his one good back leg with his ears forward, the face of a dog who had decided about a man and did not plan to revisit.

She stood at the sink and watched for longer than was useful.

She put the kettle on, got the Hutterville bag down off the shelf, did the coffee in the WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE mug and the chipped white from the bunkhouse because those were the two mugs now, and carried them out onto the south-facing porch.

He heard the screen. He set the maul head-down in the round and walked over, wiping his right hand down his thigh. Louis got up and came with him but did not look at her. Louis was keeping formation.

He stopped at the porch step. She handed him the UNCLE mug.

He said, "Thanks."

She said, "Morning."

He said, "Morning."

They stood on the porch with the coffee and the cold. The chicken was somewhere behind the cabin talking to herself about whatever a chicken talked to herself about. Neither of them said anything for a full minute. The porch thermometer said forty-eight. Their breath was visible. He was on the step below her and she was on the step above him, and the tops of their heads were at the same height, which was a thing she noticed and put in the box with the other things she had been putting in the box since last night.

After a minute he said, "You want a coat."

She said, "I'm all right."

He looked at her and said, "Okay."

She was all right standing three feet from a man she had slept with eight hours ago, drinking his coffee in a flannel she had not buttoned, and she was going to be all right until the sun came up the rest of the way.

He finished his coffee and set the mug on the porch rail.

He said, "Kristin coming at noon."

She said, "Yeah."

"I'll be on the south fence line after breakfast."

"Okay."

"Be gone till two."

"Okay."

He did not make a point about being gone for the walkthrough. He was telling her the shape of his day. She stood on the porch step above him and drank her coffee.

Louis, on the gravel at the foot of the porch, looked up at Sawyer once and then at Molly once and then back at Sawyer. Louis had a clear sense of where the center of gravity had moved, and Molly was not going to get into a negotiation with him about it at six-twenty in the morning.

Sawyer picked the maul back up and went back to the round.


She went inside and made eggs. She cracked four because she had always cracked four when she had cracked any. Butter in the pan first, butter brown at the edges, eggs over easy, salt before the whites set. She did a slice of bread in the skillet after the eggs came out and carried both plates to the porch.

He was at the splitting stump with a cord-end piece half done. He set the maul down when she came out, took his plate on the porch step, and ate without saying anything about the eggs, which was the best thing he could have said. She sat on the porch chair with her plate on her knees. Louis sat three feet off the step with his nose in the middle range between her plate and Sawyer's, splitting the difference.

They ate.

She said, "Did you sleep."

He said, "Some."

"Some."

"Enough."

She let it go. She would have let it go yesterday too and the day before, but she noticed today that she had let it go, and she did not like the notice very much.

He stood up with his plate, handed it to her, and said, "I'm going to go. Thanks for the eggs."

She said, "Sure."

He picked up the truck keys off the porch rail and whistled once at Louis, the short double-note he had been using since Day Two, and Louis did not go. Louis looked at Molly. Molly, to her own surprise, shrugged at Louis. Louis got up and went with Sawyer to the F-250.

The F-250 started on the first turn of the key, and Louis rode in the bed with his head up on the front rail. Sawyer drove out over the cattle-guard onto the county road, and the dust went up behind the truck and came back down and the yard went quiet.

She was on the porch alone with two plates.


Kristin called at ten.

Molly took it on the porch with her second coffee. The sun was above the cottonwood and the thermometer had come up to fifty-four and the air had the late-September bite of a valley that was going to be hot enough at noon to fool you and cold enough at five to remind you.

"Hey sweetheart," Kristin said. "Just confirming noon."

Molly said, "Noon works."

"Photographer's booked next Wednesday if the walkthrough goes well."

"Okay."

"Harlan'd like under contract before Thanksgiving. He's not in a rush."

She heard, through the kitchen window behind her, the refrigerator door. He was back. She had not heard him pull in because she had been listening to Kristin, and because the truck came quiet up the last hundred feet if you coasted. He had coasted. He was at the counter. She could hear the refrigerator close and then the cabinet where the bread was.

She kept her voice level.

"Kristin."

"Yeah."

"I'm going to tell you late October. Maybe November."

Kristin paused for a second, which for Kristin was long. Then she said, "Late October."

"Or November."

"You want to list late October or November."

"I want to figure out the estate piece first," Molly said, "and the lease piece."

"The lease piece."

"The tenant has a lease through February."

"Sweetheart."

"I know."

"Sweetheart, we can write around a lease."

"I know."

Kristin let it sit. Kristin had sold houses for eighteen years and knew when to let a woman on a porch hear her own sentence in the quiet after she had said it.

Kristin said, "So what am I telling Harlan."

Molly said, "Tell him you're doing the walkthrough today and you'll have a timeline in a week."

"A week."

"A week."

"All right honey," Kristin said. "I'll see you at noon."

"See you at noon."

She hung up. She set the phone face-down on the porch rail. She sat with the porch rail for a full count of ten.

Behind her, through the open kitchen window, she heard the knife come down on the cutting board twice, cleanly. The cabinet closed. Then the screen door did not open, because he had taken the sandwich out the back door, the one onto the woodpile side, and he was going to eat standing at the back of his truck where he could see the south line he was supposed to be going to.

He had heard her. The cabin had one kitchen window and she had been three feet from it, and she had said late October maybe November in a voice a man in the cabin could hear without trying. He had heard her tell Kristin forty-eight hours on Tuesday and he had heard her tell Kristin late October maybe November this morning. She had sat on the same porch both times.

He had taken his sandwich out the back door. He was not going to bring it up.

She picked up her coffee and drank the half inch left. It was cold. She drank it anyway.


Kristin's Subaru pulled up at eleven-fifty-eight. Kristin was always two minutes early. She got out in a navy blazer and jeans and brown boots that had cost her three hundred dollars and had walked the floor of a bar twice a year at the Park County realtors' mixer since 2012. Molly came down the porch step to meet her.

Kristin said, "Hi sweetheart."

Molly said, "Hi."

Kristin did the hug fast and without announcement.

They went inside. Kristin had her pad and her pen. She walked the cabin clockwise from the door through the main room into the bathroom and back around, made three notes, looked up at the broken loft ladder without saying anything about it, put a hand on the stovepipe, and wrote a word on her pad.

In the bathroom she stopped for a second. She said, "Toilet seat's cracked."

Molly said, "It's getting replaced this week."

Kristin said, "Good," and wrote something.

They came back to the main room. Kristin looked at the bed, which was made, because Molly had made it at six-forty with the crispness of a woman who was having a real-estate agent in her cabin by noon. Kristin looked at the quilt. She said, "This is a nice quilt."

Molly said, "It was Cal's."

Kristin said, "I figured."

They went back out to the porch. Kristin leaned on the porch rail and said, "It shows well. For one bedroom at this elevation it's a strong product."

Molly said, "Thanks."

"The woodstove is a plus. The loft is not, but we can fix that with staging when we list."

"Okay."

"The toilet seat is a nothing."

"Okay."

Kristin put the pad into her handbag. She looked at Molly. She said, "So — October? November?"

Molly said, "I'll confirm in a week."

Kristin nodded and did not push.

She said, "You don't owe Harlan anything. You don't owe me anything either. I'm telling you that as your agent, and I'm telling you because I've known your mother since 1993."

Molly said, "I know."

Kristin said, "Call me in a week."

Molly said, "I will."

Kristin got in her Subaru. Kristin left at one-oh-four. Molly sat on the porch chair and watched the dust come up and come back down a second time in one day.

She stayed on the porch for a minute, then got up and went to the cooler Sawyer had been restocking since Tuesday out of a sense of order she had not asked him for. There were four beers in it on fresh ice he had put in that morning. She took two.

The F-250 came back up the drive at two-oh-eight.

He parked it in the same spot it had been in since Tuesday afternoon. Louis got out of the bed and came to her on the porch and sat at her feet like a dog who had remembered where he lived. Sawyer came up the porch step. She handed him a beer. He took it. He opened his with the porch rail like Cal had taught them both. He sat down on the porch step below her chair.

He said, "How'd it go."

She said, "Fine."

He said, "Good."

He drank his beer. She drank hers. The chicken came around the cottonwood at a distance, looked at them, changed her mind about whatever she had been thinking about, and walked back the other way.


They worked on the kitchen floor molding in the afternoon because the molding was next on the list and neither of them wanted to be the one who went inside alone. He did the cuts on the porch with the miter saw he had borrowed from the McClaren shop in July and had not returned because nobody had missed it. She held the lengths and marked them with the carpenter's pencil she had put in her back pocket and forgotten about. He ran a bead of caulk along the seam after each piece went in, and she wiped the bead down with her thumb. Her thumb had caulk on it for the rest of the afternoon.

He told her one thing about his sister in Spokane. She told him one thing about Lacey. Neither of them said anything about the phone call or the walkthrough. They were moving molding.

Louis slept on the rug and twitched his legs through a dream that involved his back leg being present.

At four-thirty the north-wall molding was done. They leaned in the doorway and looked at it. It looked like molding.

She said, "That's good."

He said, "Yeah."

She was looking at the molding with a man who had heard her lie to her agent through an open window at ten that morning and had not said a word about it, and she went back out to the porch.


Tito called at five-fifteen.

She took it on the porch chair with her second beer. Sawyer was at the splitting stump again, because Sawyer was going to split wood until the sun went down and she had given up trying to figure out whether he liked doing it or whether it was just what he knew how to do at the end of a day.

"Hey boss," Tito said.

She said, "Hey Tito."

"Nothing's on fire."

"That's my favorite sentence."

Tito laughed, gravel and no hurry.

"Walk-in seal's going," he said. "Nothing crazy. Keeping an eye."

"Okay."

"Brenda fixed the fourth-stool sticky spot with dishwasher rinse and a towel."

"Tell her I love her."

"I'll tell her."

There was a beat. Tito said, "When you back, Molly."

She said, "Not sure."

Tito did not say anything for two seconds. Then Tito said, "Okay, boss."

She said, "Thanks, Tito."

Tito said, "Talk tomorrow," and was gone.

She sat with the phone in her lap. Sawyer at the splitting stump brought the maul down on a round and set one half up for the next swing. Louis watched him from the porch step.

Her phone buzzed again ten seconds later.

It was her mother.

She looked at the screen, at the cottonwood, at Sawyer at the splitting stump. She put the phone face-down on the porch rail and watched it vibrate itself to voicemail from the wrong side. She did not pick up, and she did not listen to the voicemail when the phone finished vibrating or when the little red dot came up. She carried the phone inside, set it on Cal's pine nightstand, and came back out to the porch.

Sawyer, at the splitting stump, did not look up. He had heard the phone buzz and had heard her not pick it up and had heard her come back out and sit on the porch chair, and he brought the maul down on another round and did not comment.


At seven he came in, washed up at the sink, and started on dinner. She sat at the table with a beer left from the cooler, and he opened a fresh one for himself on the edge of the counter. He got a can of chili from the cabinet and an onion from the basket, peeled the onion with the knife Cal had owned since 1989, and diced it with the speed and carelessness of a man who had diced a lot of onions in his life without ever learning to do it properly. He put the diced onion in a pan with butter, the chili in another pan, and grated cheddar onto a plate.

She watched him do all this. She could watch a man work in a small kitchen for longer than she cared to admit.

He said, "I had a year alone after Cal died."

She looked up.

He said, "Got good at this."

He was stirring the onion. He did not turn around.

She said, "I can see that."

He said, "Yep."

The onion went into the chili. He stirred it. He put the cheddar on top of each bowl, covered them with a dish towel for a minute to let the cheese melt, and carried both bowls out to the porch. She followed with the beers.

They sat on the porch with the bowls on their laps and the beers on the porch rail. The sun had dropped behind the ridge and the temperature with it, and he had lit the stove before he started the chili, so the cabin was coming up behind them while the porch was dropping toward forty-two. Her breath was visible again. She ate the chili. It was good, and not only because he had made it and she had slept with him. The onion and the butter and the cheese were doing their job. She ate all of it.

She set the bowl on the porch boards.

She said, "That was good."

He said, "Noted."


After dinner he said, "What are you working on."

She said, "Nothing."

He said, "Okay."

He drank his beer.

She sat in the porch chair and looked at the cottonwood and the gravel drive and the porch boards, and she knew she did not want him to know she had lied to Kristin that morning. She also knew he already knew. He had been in the kitchen with a knife on a cutting board when she had said late October or November, and he had taken his sandwich out the back door, and he had not said a word about it all day, and he was not going to say a word about it tonight. The lie was hers to carry until she chose to put it down.

She had a feeling in her chest that was not exactly bad and not exactly good. She did not have a word for the feeling, and she was not going to go looking for one.

She drank her beer.

He finished his, stood up, and took the bowls inside. She heard him rinse both at the sink and set them upside down on the dish rack Cal had bolted to the counter in 1996, which was the rack she had rinsed her own mug into on Tuesday at four-fifty.

He came back out with two fresh beers, handed her one, and sat down on the porch step below her chair.


They went inside when the thermometer said forty-two. The stove had the cabin up to sixty and climbing. Louis went to the rug. Sawyer added two pieces to the stove, closed the door the right amount, and stood for a second watching the fire catch. It caught. He came to the couch.

She was already on it, her feet up under her and the wool blanket off the bed across her lap. He sat down next to her and she put her feet across his thigh, and he put his hand on her shin and left it there.

The cabin smelled like cottonwood smoke and onion and beer.

After a while she slid down the couch and put her head on his shoulder and turned her face in against his collar. He did not move his hand off her shin. He did not say anything. She closed her eyes.

His chin came down to the top of her head. Louis, on the rug, did not open his eye.

The stove did its quiet work.
