The third week of October did what the third week of October does in Paradise Valley. The aspens had gone past gold to rust, the cottonwoods had dropped the top half of their leaves in one wind on a Monday, and the stock-tank by the fire-pit had a rim of ice on it in the morning that did not melt until past eleven. The sky was the specific high flat blue it got at this altitude in a dry fall, the one Molly had been looking at for six weeks now and had stopped remarking on, because a thing you looked at every morning stopped being a thing you remarked on and started being a thing that was just the morning.

She had been driving back and forth. She had been doing the thing a woman did when she was driving back and forth and not deciding, which was stop counting the drives.

Sawyer was at the fire-pit at six-thirty on a Thursday evening, feeding it the last of the cottonwood splits Marco had cut too short for the woodstove and too long for the kindling bin. They had a working theory between them that the too-short-too-long pile was its own category, and the fire-pit was the category's purpose. Louis was on Sawyer's work coat on the ground. Molly had a mug in one hand and nothing in the other, and she was on the top porch step because the top porch step was where she sat.

Sawyer had made chili. It had been on since four. They had eaten at five-forty-five out of two bowls that were both clean because she had done the dishes between two phone calls and a contractor email and a text to Kristin Mills that said I'll call you Monday and was a lie. He had carried the bowls inside and had come back out with his own mug and his paperback and had sat in the porch chair. He had read for a while. He had stopped reading a while ago and was watching the fire with the book closed on his thumb.

She said, "You're not reading."

He said, "No."

She did not ask the next thing. She had learned something in six weeks about not asking the next thing. The not-asking was not a strategy. It was a choice she had kept making that had started to feel like a shape.

He said, "There's a voicemail on my phone I haven't listened to."

She looked at him.

She did not say anything.

He said, "It's from a woman named Maggie. Maggie Parrish. Left it October 2, 2025."

The fire put out a small pop and a piece of the too-short-too-long gave up a lick of flame and went. Louis shifted on the coat. Molly did not move her hand. She was holding the mug at the top on three fingers because the bottom was still warm and she had been planning to drink the last of it and had stopped planning to.

He said, "Ben Parrish was a sawyer on my crew. Nine seasons. Good at it. He was the one in front of me in the line on a bad day in August. He did not come out of the burn-over. Two others were hurt. He was the one who didn't."

That was the most he had said about it in six weeks. That was the most he had said about it in a year. She held still.

He said, "Maggie called me three weeks after the funeral. I saw the missed call. I let it go to voicemail. I have not listened to it since."

She said, "You gonna listen to it?"

He said, "No."

She said, "When."

He said, "I don't know."

She said, "Okay."

He drank from the mug. The mug had been empty for a while and she knew it was empty because he had finished it before the chili. He drank from it anyway because a man holding an empty mug and a man holding nothing were different men in that moment and he was picking.

She said, "I'm not going to tell you you should."

He said, "I know."

"I'm not going to tell you when."

"I know."

"I'm not going to bring it up again."

He looked at her then. The fire was low off his face and she could see half of it in the dusk, and the half she could see was steady.

He said, "I know, Molly."

The dog lifted his head, looked at the fire, decided the fire was not asking him a question, put his head back down on the coat.

She stood up. She walked across the porch boards in her socks because she had her boots by the door, and she walked across the three feet of yard between the porch and the fire-pit, and she sat down on the log round next to the porch chair. She did not touch him. She put her mug on the ground by her foot. She looked at the fire.

They watched it.

The cottonwood in the fire was the last of the too-short-too-long. When it was gone the fire was going to be coals and then it was going to be dark in the yard and they were going to go inside. She was not going to say anything about it.

She said, "You want another beer."

He said, "Yeah."

She went inside for the beer. When she came back she had two, and she opened one on the porch rail and handed it to him and opened the other one for herself and sat back down on the log round, and the fire was coals, and she drank her beer in the cold, and he drank his.


He went to bed at ten. He did not say he was going to bed, he just got up off the porch and went inside and brushed his teeth and got under the quilt on the west side against the log wall, and she heard him go, and she stayed on the porch for another twenty minutes because she had said she was going to sit with him and she had sat with him, and now she was going to sit with a thing.

The thing was this. He had told her about a voicemail he had not listened to. He had told her three nights after Brenda had said on the phone, "You are doing exactly what you said you weren't going to do," which Molly had turned over for seventy-two hours without arriving anywhere. He had told her because he had been carrying it and she had been there. When she asked if he was going to listen to it he had said no, when she asked when he had said he didn't know, and when she said okay he had said okay.

She drank the last of her beer. She went inside. She brushed her teeth. She got in the bed on the east side and the quilt was already a held heat from his body on it, and Louis was at the foot on the south side where he slept now, and she put her hand on Sawyer's shoulder and he put his hand on her hand and she closed her eyes.

She did not sleep for a while.

He did.


Friday she drove to Livingston at six a.m. for a bar shift. Lacey was off. She worked it. Sawyer texted at three: fence line south of bridger, home by seven. She did not text back, because the text did not require one, and because he had used the word home and she did not know what she would write that did not lean on the word. She locked up at midnight and drove back Saturday morning.

Saturday they did the things they did on a Saturday. He worked. She went to town for feed and came back with feed and two cans of primer. He split wood. She called Kristin Mills and did not say yes to the showing and did not say no to the showing and got off the phone with her real-estate agent sounding tired for the first time in their working relationship. They had the leftover chili because the chili was still in the pot. She did not ask about the voicemail. He did not bring it up.

Sunday he was at the fence line on Cash's ground. She was on the phone with Brenda, who wanted to know if Molly was coming down for Friday because a regular named Janie had been in three times in a week. Molly said she would be down Friday. Brenda said, "You good." Molly said, "Yeah." Brenda said, "Okay."

He came back at seven. He was tired the way a man was tired after nine hours of fence, which was a tired that sat on the shoulders and did not go to the face. He ate. He went to the porch. He read. He read for real this time.

She did the dishes. He went to bed at nine-forty.


Monday night he was not in the cabin when she got back from a contractor meeting in Livingston that ran long.

She pulled into the yard at nine-fifteen. The porch light was on. The cabin light was on. The woodstove was going, she could see the orange seam through the gap in the door from the porch, and the truck was not there. The 1989 F-250 with the cracked windshield and the USMC sticker that was not his was not under the cottonwood where it was always under the cottonwood, and Louis was on the porch step alone with his head on his paws, looking down the gravel drive.

She stood in the yard.

She said, "Hey bud."

Louis thumped his tail once.

"Where'd he go."

Louis thumped his tail again. Louis did not know.

She went inside. She took her jacket off. She put the kettle on because putting the kettle on was what a woman did in a cabin at nine-fifteen when her phone had no new texts on it and the truck was not in the yard. She stood at the counter and waited for the kettle, and the kettle clicked, and she poured water over a tea bag she did not want, and she stood at the counter some more.

She looked at her phone. There was nothing on it.

She did not text him or call, and she did not do the thing her wrist was telling her to do, which was to get in her own truck and drive up the valley road until she saw him.

She sat down at the table. She drank the tea. Louis came in and went to the rug by the stove and lay down with his head pointed at the door.

At nine-forty-two she heard the F-250 down the county road. She did not stand up, she kept her hands on the mug on the table. The truck came up the gravel and cut its engine. The door opened and closed. Louis lifted his head but did not move.

The porch boards gave under him, then the screen door, and he came in.

He was in the canvas coat. The coat had yard dust on the sleeves and a pine needle on the shoulder. He stopped in the doorway. He looked at her at the table.

He said, "I listened to it."

She set the mug down.

She said, "Okay."

He did not take the coat off. He came across the cabin floor in his boots and he sat down on the chair across from her at the kitchen table, and he put his hands flat on the table the way a man puts his hands flat on a table to steady the table and not himself.

He said, "I drove up to the trailhead. I sat in the truck. I opened it. I listened to the whole thing. I listened to it twice."

She said, "Okay."

He said, "I wanted to tell you."

She said, "I'm listening."

He was quiet for a second.

He said, "She's not crying in it. She had been crying for three weeks and she's out the other side of it. She has the voice of a woman who's been out the other side. She called because she wanted me to know some things. She said Ben told her once that I was the calmest man on that crew. She said she knew what it took to get the rest of them to the shelter. She said Ben would have told me to stop being an idiot about calling her back."

He drew a breath. He kept his hands on the table.

He said, "And then she said." He stopped. He said, "She said Ben had told her once about a country station he used to pick up in the back-country out past Gallatin, on one of the long runs, that played Waylon Jennings on Thursdays at six. She's been trying to find the station for a year. She said she knows he told her the frequency. She can't find it in her notes. She asked me if I remembered."

The cabin was doing the small quiet thing a cabin did with the stove going and two people at a table and nothing else moving.

He said, "And she said, at the end, she said she doesn't blame me. She said she wanted to say that out loud. She said Ben wouldn't blame me. She said she knows I know that and she wanted to say it anyway. And then she said, call me back when you can."

He stopped.

He said, "That's the voicemail."

Molly had not moved her hands from the mug. The tea in the mug had gone cold. She could feel it had gone cold against her palm.

She said, "What was the frequency."

He looked at her.

He said, "103.7."

She did not stand up. She slid her phone off the table toward her, and she opened the radio app she had on her phone from the drive up from Livingston two months ago that she never used and had never deleted, and she typed in 103.7 with one thumb and she hit the play and she set the phone face up on the kitchen table between them.

Static for a second. Then a signal. Then a voice saying that was Patsy Cline for you on a Monday night at nine forty-seven out of Billings, coming up next we've got some classic, and then the classic came in on a chord that was not a Waylon chord, it was a Conway Twitty chord, and then the DJ said hang on, we're switching to our Waylon hour a little late tonight, we had a request, and the Conway Twitty cut off and a Waylon song came on.

The Taker. 1970. Two-and-a-half minutes in with the guitar Waylon had been making sound like a long lonesome argument his whole career.

Sawyer did not say anything.

The song played. Molly had her hands on the mug still. She was looking at him. He was looking at the phone on the table like the phone on the table was an explanation for why he had been in a truck at a trailhead at nine on a Monday night in late October alone with a voicemail from a dead friend's wife he had not listened to in a year.

He laughed.

It was a small laugh at the back of his throat, the laugh of a man who had not expected a laugh to be there. He put his hand flat over his mouth and he laughed again behind his hand and his shoulders went once, and she watched his shoulders go.

She said, "Is this it."

He said, from behind his hand, "It might be."

She said, "This is a Waylon Jennings song."

He said, "It is."

"On 103.7."

"Yeah."

"Out of Billings."

"Yeah."

She started laughing. She did not mean to. She laughed the small helpless laugh a woman laughed when a thing was ridiculous enough that not laughing was worse, and her laugh crossed the table and hit his and he took his hand off his mouth and he was grinning at her with water in his eyes and he was still laughing.

He said, "This is absurd."

She said, "It is wildly absurd."

"We don't even know if this is the station."

"We don't."

"It could be any station in Montana."

"It could be."

"But it's playing a Waylon song."

"Right now."

"At nine forty-seven on a Monday."

"Yeah."

They sat with that. Waylon went through the first chorus. The fire in the stove did what it did. Louis, from the rug, had lifted his head and was watching the two adults at his table with the specific exhausted tolerance of a dog who had seen it all and had opinions he was not going to share.

Sawyer wiped his eyes with the heel of his hand.

He said, "I want to text her."

Molly said, "Okay."

He took his phone out of the coat pocket. He did not take the coat off. He wrote a text with both thumbs, slow, the way a man who had not texted many people in the last year wrote a text when the text mattered. She could not see the screen. She did not try to.

He read it out loud when he was done.

He said, "I listened. I'm sorry it took a year. I think the station is 103.7 out of Billings. A Waylon song is on it right now. If you can pick it up where you are, pick it up. I'll call you this week. Sawyer."

She said, "Send it."

He sent it.

He set the phone face down on the table.

The song on her phone, playing 103.7 out of Billings, went into the last verse. Waylon sang the thing Waylon sang. The DJ came back on. That was The Taker, Waylon Jennings on his nineteen-seventy B-side, and we're going to take a quick break here on 103.7, stay with us, and she reached over and stopped the app, because they had got what they came for.

She said, "Come here."

He came around the table. He did not take the coat off. He sat down on the chair she had already moved back so he could sit next to her, and she leaned her head against his shoulder, and she put her arm across his chest inside the coat, and he put his arm around her, and they sat there at nine-fifty-four on a Monday night with a Waylon Jennings song they had just turned off still in the room in the way a song just turned off is still in the room.

He said, "Thank you."

She said, "Okay."

He said, "Molly."

She said, "Yeah."

He said, "I'm going to call her Thursday."

She said, "Okay."

They stayed there.

The stove put out another small pop. Louis put his head back down on the rug. The cold tea was still in the mug on the table, and the cabin was the cabin at ten o'clock on a Monday in late October with two adults at a kitchen table and a phone face down between them. Outside, down the county road, a truck went by that was not anybody they knew. The truck was somebody's Monday.

She felt him breathe. She felt him breathe out.

After a while he stood up, took the coat off, and hung it on the chair by the stove. He did not say bed. She did not say bed. They took the mugs to the sink, which was what a person did in a cabin before going to bed, and they left them in the sink because the breakfast dishes were already in it and one more mug was not going to hurt anybody. He turned out the kitchen light. She had already turned out the porch light. He checked the stove. The stove was good till three.

She got in the bed on the east side. He got in on the west side. Louis came up off the rug and clicked across the floor on his three legs and lay down at the foot of the bed on the south side.

She put her hand on his chest where his heart was.

She could feel his heart.

His heart was doing what a heart did at ten-ten on a Monday night in late October in a cabin in Paradise Valley after a man who had said he was fine for a year had stopped saying it.

She closed her eyes.

He said, into the dark, "Molly."

She said, "Yeah."

He said, "103.7."

She laughed, once, into his chest, and she kept her hand where it was, and she went to sleep.
