The reopening had been on a sign in the Bad Penny's window since the first Friday of October, and the sign said REOPENING HALLOWEEN NIGHT, COSTUMES ENCOURAGED, NOT REQUIRED, which had been Lacey's edit. Molly's draft had said COSTUMES WELCOME OR WHATEVER, and Lacey had said "no," and taken the marker.

It was Saturday, October thirty-first, and Molly was behind her bar at four in the afternoon cutting limes into wheels that had been pre-cut by the kid who did the prep on Friday but had not been cut to the thickness Molly cut a lime to, which was a sixteenth of an inch and not the quarter-inch the kid had interpreted from the laminated card Molly kept in the walk-in for this purpose. She recut the wheels. She did not make a speech about it.

The bar was the bar again. She had been closed to the public for three weekends and had worked her regular weeknights with the tables pulled to one side and the floor tarped, and now, at four p.m., the tarps were folded in the back hall and the new floorboards along the wainscot that Marco had laid in October had had eight weeks to settle and the pool table had been levelled by Hank on Wednesday, and the jukebox had a new cord she had soldered on Tuesday that was, by her own estimate, going to hold until February. The upstairs apartment was not done. The upstairs apartment was going to be done in January. She had stopped lying about the timeline on the upstairs apartment two weeks ago, and it had felt, when she stopped, like taking off a wet coat.

Louis was at the end of the bar on his folded blanket in the corner behind stool twelve, in the spot where Louis had been since 2022 on the nights Molly did not have the energy to argue with him about being at home. Tonight he was in a pumpkin. The pumpkin was a felt vest Brenda had made him out of a craft-store kit, and Louis had tolerated it for eleven minutes and then decided it was part of him, the way he decided most things, and was asleep in it.

Lacey came out of the back with a case of PBR on her shoulder and said, "Hey boss."

Molly said, "Hey."

Lacey set the case down. She looked at the limes. She looked at Molly. She said, "You recut them."

Molly said, "They were wrong."

Lacey said, "Sure." She picked up the case and walked it to the cooler. On her way past stool twelve she dropped a hand on Louis's head. Louis did not open his eyes. His tail did the thing it did for Lacey, which was one half-thump against the blanket.

Outside the front window the light on 2nd Street was the long gold Halloween light a Livingston afternoon does, cottonwoods two doors down yellow down to the last leaf, and across the street at the diner a woman in a witch's hat was lighting the jack-o-lanterns on the sill for the fourth year running, which meant it was a quarter to five and the diner was about to close and the street was about to start having children on it.

Molly put the limes in a lowball by the well and wiped the board.

Her phone was face-down on the back bar. It had been face-down since ten this morning. Kristin Mills had called on Wednesday and left a voicemail and had called on Thursday and left a longer voicemail and had texted on Friday. Molly had not opened the texts. She had read the banner on the lock screen and she had let it scroll past. She did not know what the text said after the first seven words, which were Harlan is looking for a decision by, and she had decided Wednesday, in the walk-in while carrying a keg, that Harlan Bickman's investment timeline was not her problem. She had decided this and she had not typed it and she had not said it and she had not called anyone. She had just put the phone face-down.

Sawyer had been up at the cabin all day. He had driven down at three and was out back in her gravel lot under the cottonwood with the truck bed down, breaking apart a flat of pumpkins Tito had gotten on sale at the feed co-op because Tito was a man who bought pumpkins on sale and brought them to a bar on Halloween without being asked. Marco was on a ladder in the doorway nailing black crepe around the top of the frame. Brenda was at the jukebox changing the order of the programmed set.

Molly watched them through the window.

She said, to no one, quietly, "Okay."

The Bad Penny on a Halloween Saturday at four forty-five in the afternoon looked the way the Bad Penny looked before a night it had been waiting for: the sticky spot on the number four stool where Eddie always set his club soda down, the jukebox with the new cord and the old guts, the pool table that was level only on Wednesdays and was, tonight, a Saturday, going to be slightly pitched to the east in a way nobody would mention, the row of twelve stools Molly had recovered in the summer of 2023 for the cost of twelve sheets of dark green vinyl and a rented staple gun, the eight tables along the wall that she had refinished herself in July of 2024 because the previous finish had started lifting where the drinks went, and the low pressed-tin ceiling she had bought on Craigslist from a restaurant in Bozeman that had gone out of business in 2022 and had installed with a friend named Denise over a three-day weekend with a lot of cursing and two pizzas. This was what she had been building since 2020. The cabin had not interrupted this. The cabin was twelve minutes away and she could be standing in it in fifteen if she left right now, which she was not going to do, because tonight was a Saturday and tonight she was reopening a bar.


At five-thirty the door chimed and Wyatt walked in.

He was in a flannel and a clean canvas work coat and the hat he wore off the ranch, which was a grey felt hat that looked like the one he wore on the ranch and was not. June was behind him, in a sweater the colour of a cottonwood leaf that month, and she had a foil-covered dish in both hands. Deb came in behind June. Cash was behind Deb. They had driven up from Paradise Valley together in Cash's truck, a two-hour run for an evening, because June had heard a month ago that the reopening was the Saturday of Halloween, and because Cal's niece was reopening her bar on Halloween, and because Paradise Valley, as a functioning unit, turned up for a thing like that. June had called Molly the week before to say they were coming. Molly had said, "You don't have to." June had said, "I know." That was the call.

June walked the dish to the service station. She set it down. She said, "Three things, you can say no to any of them."

Molly said, "Tell me."

June said, "It is a cornbread. Wyatt made it. You can put it out or not put it out. Also Deb brought you a candle. Also Cash has a bottle of something he would like to leave behind the bar in case you need it, which is the question you are going to be asked once tonight."

Molly lifted the foil. The cornbread was in a nine-by-thirteen. It was still warm. Wyatt, who was looking at the jukebox the way Wyatt looked at equipment he did not intend to touch, said, "Buttermilk. Regular."

Molly said, "Thank you."

Wyatt said, "Mm."

Deb came around the bar and hugged her without asking, one-armed, because Deb did not set a thing down to hug. She said, into Molly's shoulder, "Opening a bar on Halloween, you are out of your mind," and Molly said, "I had it coming," and Deb said, "You did," and let her go.

Cash set a paper bag on the bar. Inside was a bottle of mezcal Molly had heard of and never stocked because it was sixty dollars retail. He said, "For the shelf."

Molly said, "Cash, I can't —"

He said, "For the shelf."

She said, "Okay."

They stood at the bar for forty minutes. They had one drink each: Wyatt a club soda with lime, June a glass of the red she had brought from the house because June and Molly had talked on the phone about what June drank, Deb a whiskey neat, Cash a beer. They did not take up a table, because they had decided in advance they were not going to, and they stood at the end of the bar near the service station and they talked to Molly about whatever came up, and one of the things that came up, quietly, was that Wyatt said, "The cabin's looking good," and Molly said, "Yeah," and that was the whole of the cabin conversation that was going to happen. June looked at the jukebox once. She said, "You rewired this." Molly said, "Tuesday." June said, "Of course you did."

At six-fifteen the first of the real crowd came in: two of her Thursday regulars in costume, a man dressed as a bartender and his wife dressed as an angry customer, a joke that had clearly been funny to them for a week in the making. After them came Tito from the back, having parked pumpkins on every windowsill and both ends of the bar. Brenda came up from the jukebox with a thumb up. Marco came down off the ladder and said the crepe was hung. The bar began, around them, to do what a bar did on a Saturday night.

Wyatt set his club soda down empty. He looked at June. June looked at Deb. Deb looked at Cash. They left at six-forty, because they had said they would, and because a two-hour drive back was a real thing, and because they had come to mark the reopening and the reopening was marked.

At the door June turned. She said, "We'll see you Thanksgiving."

Molly said, "Yeah."

June said, "Both of you."

Molly said, "Both of us."

They walked out.

Sawyer was in the side doorway with a pumpkin under his arm. He had heard the end of that exchange and he did not say anything about it. He carried the pumpkin to the end of the bar and set it on the stool that nobody used because the seat tilted, and the pumpkin sat level on the tilt in a way that suggested a man had picked this pumpkin with this stool in mind. He looked at Molly. He did not smile. He did the thing his mouth did not do and his eyes did. He turned and went back outside to finish the lot.


By seven-thirty the bar was full, and by eight-fifteen it was the way the Bad Penny was on a night Molly had built it to be, which was loud without being hostile, crowded without being dangerous, and full of people whose names she knew. She poured. She heard two arguments about the World Series and intervened in neither. She took the cornbread out of the kitchen at a quarter to nine and cut it into squares and set it on the end of the bar with a sign on a cocktail napkin that said W. REEVES, PARADISE VALLEY, and it was gone in twenty minutes. Somebody paid her in Halloween candy for a round. Somebody else paid her in actual dollars but handed them over inside a hollowed-out plastic pumpkin. Marco drew three of her regulars into a very serious discussion about a particular brand of wood stain. Brenda played the jukebox. Tito held down the far end of the bar in a costume he insisted was Retired Trucker and that was visually indistinguishable from Tito.

A woman came in at nine in a wedding dress she had actually gotten married in, cream satin with a bodice she could not sit down in, her friend behind her in a tuxedo painted to look like a skeleton. The woman had been married in this dress in 2014 to a man she was no longer married to, and she announced this to the bar at large on her way to stool four, and the bar at large responded with an ovation that took twenty seconds to die down. Molly poured her a gin and tonic without being asked. The friend in the skeleton tuxedo said, "Do you do this every year," and Molly said, "Only since 2014," and the friend laughed so hard she needed a water.

At nine-twenty the fire captain's wife came in. She was not in costume. She was in a parka over scrubs. She came up to the bar and said, "He's bringing the kid through at ten, I am telling you now so you have your thing ready," and Molly said, "I have it ready," and the captain's wife squeezed her wrist once, said "You reopened on the right Saturday," and left to get back to the hospital.

At ten a trick-or-treater came in. This had been happening at the Bad Penny since 2021. It was the fire captain's son, who was six and a half now and had been four the first time his father had walked him through the front door of a bar on Halloween for reasons Molly understood without asking. The kid was dressed as a welder. He had a plastic helmet. He held out a pumpkin bucket. Molly reached under the bar to the drawer where she kept the good candy on this one night of the year, two bags of the full-size Snickers she had bought Wednesday on her way home and hidden from Lacey, and she put three of them in the bucket. The kid said thank you. His father tipped twenty dollars for a beer he had not ordered yet. Molly poured him a beer he had not ordered yet. The kid went back out onto the sidewalk. The father drank his beer standing and did not sit down.

She watched the kid go.

She looked at Sawyer.

He was at the far end of the bar in the stool that was his, which had been his stool since the second week of October and which the regulars had, without being told, stopped sitting on. He was in a flannel and jeans and boots. He was in no costume. He had a beer in front of him that was half-full. He had a paperback open on the bar next to it, the L'Amour he had been working through, she recognised the cover, Hondo, because Sawyer read L'Amour in a rotation she had come to know the shape of. He was reading. He was not in a hurry. His beer had been there since nine-forty.

She had known what he drank for three weeks.


The crowd crested at ten-thirty and held there. Molly worked the stick with Lacey. She did the thing she did on a Saturday, which was to forget she was doing it while doing it, and to look up at eleven and realise her hair had come out of the knot and her shirt was stuck to the small of her back and her left wrist was aching, which it would, at twenty-eight degrees and a barometric drop, at eleven o'clock at night on a Halloween. She redid the knot with her left hand and a rubber band she took off her right wrist. She kept pouring.

At eleven-fifteen a woman from the Livingston choir, a regular on Wednesdays and not Saturdays, came to the bar with her friend and both of them in nun costumes that they had clearly agreed on in advance and that were, from three feet away, convincing enough to make the twenty-year-old on the next stool move over. The choir woman said, "You reopened on Halloween."

Molly said, "I did."

The choir woman said, "Tell me why."

Molly said, "Because I could."

The choir woman lifted her glass to that. Her friend did too. Molly poured them both another on the house and leaned on the bar for a second and let her right hand rest on the lip of the well.

She looked down at her phone. She did not pick it up. It was still face-down. Kristin had not called today. Harlan Bickman's timeline, which had been the circling fact of September and half of October, had, somewhere in the last ten days, stopped being a circle and started being a line that ran in one direction away from her. Kristin would figure it out. Kristin was a professional. Molly was not going to send the text tonight. She was not going to send the text tomorrow. She was, in the way Molly did things, going to just keep not sending it, until Kristin sent one that said I gather you're staying and Molly would write back yeah, and that would be the whole of it.

She had said this out loud, once, at four in the morning three nights ago in the cabin, to the dark, and Sawyer had said, "Okay," into the dark, because he had not been asleep either. They had not talked about it since. They were not going to.

At eleven-twenty-two Tito, who kept time the way other people kept money, lifted his empty and set it on the bar in Molly's sightline, which was Tito's way of saying he would like another without saying it. Molly drew him a PBR. She slid it across.

She looked down the bar.

Sawyer was still on his stool. His beer was down to the last inch. He was on the last page of the chapter, because she could see by the angle of the paperback that he was near the right-hand margin of a right-hand page, and he would, when he finished it, set a matchbook in the page and close the book. She knew this because she had watched him do it five times in two weeks.

At eleven-thirty she got a clean pint glass from the rack.

She walked the length of the bar. She walked past Lacey, who was pouring a martini for the nun, and past Brenda, who had come up to the service station with an empty and a twenty, and past Marco, who was explaining something with his hands to a woman from the gallery across the street, and past the two stools with the pumpkins on them, and past the stool that tilted, and she came to the end.

She stopped in front of him.

He did not look up. He was on the last paragraph of the chapter. She stood there and let him finish it. She put the clean glass down on the bar. She picked up the half-empty one. She walked the two steps to the tap and drew him a new one, clean head, three-quarter inch of foam, the way he took it at the cabin when she poured and the way he took it here. She walked the two steps back. She set it down in front of him.

He slid the matchbook into the page.

He closed the book.

He set the book on the bar, cover down.

He picked up the beer.

She stayed there half a beat. Her left wrist was aching. The bar behind her was loud. Lacey was handling the well. Louis was asleep in his pumpkin. Outside on 2nd Street a kid in a ghost costume went past the window with his father, and the wind moved the leaves in the gutter, and the jukebox went from Waylon to Dolly without asking anybody.

Sawyer drank once.

He watched her walk back the other way.
