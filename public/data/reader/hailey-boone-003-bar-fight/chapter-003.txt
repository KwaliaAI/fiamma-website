Molly was up at six-forty on Wednesday on four hours of sleep, which was fine. She had slept on less.

Kristin Mills was driving up tomorrow. Kristin had texted at six-fifteen, which was a Kristin move, because a woman who had said walkthrough Saturday on a porch with the window open the day before had not yet told the man inside the cabin about the walkthrough, and the earlier Kristin put the day on the calendar the more time Molly had to do the thing Molly had been putting off. Bickman wants a pre-look before Saturday. I'm driving up Thursday afternoon. 2pm. Couple quick photos.

Molly walked the cabin like she walked the bar after close, corner by corner with her coffee in her hand. The bed she was going to have to strip and remake. The sleeping bag under it was going to have to go somewhere that was not under it. The rug was Cal's and it was fine. The couch was Cal's and it was fine.

The problem was the shelves.

Sawyer had shelves. He had put them up on the east wall with the only two studs the cabin had going that direction, two long pine boards on simple brackets, and on them were his books. A row of Louis L'Amour. A field guide to trees. A field guide to birds. An almanac from 2022. A tin with what she was going to guess was tobacco. A mug that said MOUNTAIN STATES CREW on it.

A woman walking into the cabin for a walkthrough was going to see the shelves and understand, in about one second, that someone was living in this cabin who was not packing to leave.

Molly said, quietly, "Shit."

Louis, on the rug, did not offer an opinion.

She was going to have to ask him to move the books. Which meant she was going to have to ask him today.

She refilled her coffee and went out. His truck was where it had been. His coat was gone and Louis was not in the cabin. Both had gone with him.

She walked around the back of the cabin.

He was a quarter-mile out by the McClaren fence line that ran west of the bunkhouse along the county road, and she saw him before he saw her because he had his back to her. Post-hole digger, fresh post, a handful of staples between his teeth, a roll of barbed wire at his boot. His canvas work coat was on a rock behind him. He was in the flannel from yesterday with the sleeves rolled.

Louis, who had somehow known which way to go without asking Molly, was already there, sitting six feet behind Sawyer's heel with the patience of a dog who had found his person. Louis, three-legged, Bad Penny dog, did not do quarter miles.

Louis had done a quarter mile.

She said, "Hey."

Sawyer looked up. He did not stop setting the post. He said, "Morning."

"What are you doing."

"Fence."

"I can see that. Whose fence."

"Wyatt's."

"You just out here fixing Wyatt's fence at seven in the morning."

"Cal was."

She stopped four feet off his right shoulder. He had the post in and was tamping it with the back end of the digger with the small even strokes of a man who knew how much tamp the ground wanted.

He said, "Cal and Wyatt had a trade. Cal did the west line, Wyatt covered a half-cord of firewood a month and the cabin rent off the ledger when the rent ran late. After Cal died I took it over. I do the west line on Wednesdays, Wyatt does what Wyatt does, nobody writes it down."

"Since April."

"Since May."

She had not known about the trade. Her mother had not known, or had not thought to mention. Cal's lawyer had not known, because Cal had not told him. It was the kind of thing Cal did, which was the kind of thing a man did who had been in a valley since 1987 and had decided which agreements wanted paper and which ones did not.

She said, "Does Wyatt know I'm selling."

"Wyatt knows what Wyatt knows."

"That's not an answer."

"Wyatt knows you pulled in yesterday with a U-Haul. Wyatt can add."

She thought about that. She did not want to think about that. She took a step back from thinking about that.

She said, "I need to talk to you about something."

"Okay."

"At lunch."

"Okay."

She turned around and went back to the cabin. Louis did not come with her. Louis stayed where Louis was, sitting six feet behind Sawyer's heel, watching the fence post.

Molly walked the quarter-mile back alone.


By ten the cabin was as clean as it was going to get on one pass. She had stripped the bed and remade it. She had wiped the kitchen counter and put Sawyer's folding knife in the drawer where Cal's knife had been. She had swept. She had not touched the shelves.

She had worked out, in her head, the script for lunch. She was selling. He had sixty days from signing, which would get him through to December. She would cover his first-and-last on a rental somewhere else in the valley if he needed, which was a thing she was prepared to put in writing. The barn was going to need clearing, because Kristin was going to want to show the barn, and the barn was currently full of about two cords of split wood, a workbench that was not Cal's, and a rolling toolbox she had not opened. He was going to need to be out of the cabin for every walkthrough. Kristin was driving up tomorrow. He had until two.

The script was clean. It was the script she would have run at the bar on a contractor who had bid her twice what the job was worth.

She made sandwiches at eleven-forty from his bread and the rest of yesterday's cold beef, on his cutting board with his knife. He was still on the fence line. He had moved up a hundred yards since seven and he had not come back for coffee, for breakfast, for water. Eleven hours out there without stopping.

The thermometer on the porch said sixty-two in the sun. She put both plates on a cutting board, picked up her coffee, and walked out to the fence with the whole thing carried in front of her like a tray.

Louis, who had apparently been on fence duty for five hours with no water, saw her coming and thumped his tail twice and did not get up.

She said to Louis, as she passed him, "I'll bring you water in a minute."

Louis did not answer.

Sawyer straightened up from a post when she got close. He looked at the sandwiches. He looked at her.

He said, "That for me."

"One of them is."

"Thank you."

He took the closer plate and sat down on the flat rock his coat had been on earlier. She sat on another rock two feet from his, because she was not going to stand up while she did this, and because the rock held the sun the way ground did at noon in Paradise Valley when it had been on the rock for an hour. She set her coffee down in the grass.

She said, "I need you out."

He took a bite of the sandwich. He chewed. He swallowed.

He said, "I heard you on the porch yesterday."

"I know you did."

"Okay."

She went through the script. Sixty days from signing, signing Saturday, out by the first week of December. First-and-last up to two thousand, on paper if he wanted. The barn cleared. The walkthrough tomorrow at two, and he needed to be gone for it, and gone for the three or four after, which was how Kristin ran a cabin.

She said all of it.

He did not interrupt her. He did not stop eating. He took another bite while she was laying out the barn and a drink of his water, and he watched her across the two feet of Paradise Valley grass and listened through the whole thing.

She said, "Any questions."

He said, "I have a lease until February."

"Sixty days is generous."

"I didn't ask for generous. I asked for February."

She opened her mouth. She closed it.

She had expected him to meet her. She had expected him to push on one of the numbers, on the barn, on the walkthrough, on the first-and-last, which she had priced low because she had left herself the negotiating room. She had not expected him to sit on a rock and eat a sandwich and tell her he had a lease.

She tried again. "Sawyer."

"Molly."

"You know I'm going to sell this cabin."

"I do."

"And you know a sixty-day notice period is more than a six-month lease normally runs."

"I know what a sixty-day notice is."

"Then."

"I signed through February. Cal signed through February. I paid through February on the first of this month. Four hundred dollars is with McClaren's bookkeeper. He deposited it. I have the receipt in the truck."

She took a breath. She let it out.

She already had the answer. The lease was the lease, Cal's lawyer had told her on Tuesday afternoon the lease was good through February, and the only way around February was for Sawyer to agree to get out sooner. That was the whole engineering of the conversation she had sat down to have. He had not been supposed to be immovable. He had been supposed to negotiate.

He was eating the sandwich.

She said, "What do you want."

"February."

"That's not."

"You asked."

She looked at him for five seconds. He looked back. He chewed. He was at lunch on a Wednesday with a fence half-set behind him and a sandwich in his hand, and a woman on a rock two feet from him was telling him he had to move out in sixty days, and he was going to finish his sandwich.

She said, "Fine. I'll see you in February."

He said, "You won't. You'll be gone by Saturday."

She opened her mouth.

She closed it.

He took another bite. He was looking at Louis, who had gotten up finally and was walking a slow arc toward the water bowl she had promised and not yet delivered, and Sawyer's hand went to his canteen at his hip and he unscrewed the cap and poured a palmful into his own hand and held it out for the dog, and Louis drank.

Molly stood up. She took her sandwich. She had not touched it. She took her coffee. She went back to the cabin.


She slammed the screen door.

She was not in the habit of slamming doors. At the bar the door was hers and she did not slam it. In the cabin the door was also hers, or going to be, or was currently a door of contested jurisdiction, and she slammed it because she was angry enough to slam something and the door was closest. It bounced off the jamb and opened halfway back.

She said, to no one, "God damn it."

She set the sandwich on the counter and the coffee next to it, and she stood at the counter for a minute with both hands flat on it and breathed.

She had overplayed it, she knew even in the minute. She had told a man she barely knew that she was taking away a thing he had paid for, and she had told him in the voice she used on a contractor who had overcharged her, because that was the voice she had available. She had not asked him what he needed. She had announced.

He had said February. He had said I didn't ask for generous. He had said You'll be gone by Saturday.

The third one she was going to have to sit with later.

Right now she was going to call Kristin.

She picked up her phone and went to the fire-pit because she was not going to have this conversation with the window open and the man in earshot for a second day in a row. She dialed.

Kristin picked up on the first ring because Kristin had been waiting.

Molly said, "Kristin."

"Hi sweetheart."

"Is there any way around a six-month residential lease with a signed landlord."

Kristin was quiet for a second. Kristin had figured this out forty-five minutes before Molly had, which was a Kristin move Molly was not going to acknowledge out loud.

Kristin said, "Not really, honey."

"Shit."

"Is he willing to renegotiate."

"He says February."

"You could offer him a buyout."

Molly put a hand on her hip and looked at the cottonwoods. "A buyout."

"A cash number. He walks, you close on time, everybody moves on. It's clean. Bickman would absorb it; I'd build it into the offer. You'd be looking at a couple thousand. Three. Depends on the man."

"Depends on the man."

"Yes."

Molly kept that. She did not say I'll think about it. She did not say do it. She said, "I'll call you later," and Kristin said, "Okay sweetheart," and Kristin hung up.

Molly stood at the fire-pit for another minute.

She did not know yet if she was going to offer Sawyer a buyout. She knew she was not offering it today. She was going to sit in the cabin and finish her sandwich and figure out how she felt about a man who had listened all the way through a set of ground rules and had not flinched once.

She went back inside. She ate the sandwich at the counter standing up. Louis came in at some point and lay down on the rug without comment.

She did not go back out to the fence line.


He came in at six.

The day had climbed to seventy-four in the afternoon, because that was a thing Paradise Valley did in late September, a sixty-two-degree morning into a seventy-four-degree five o'clock, and it had cooled back into the low sixties by the time the sun went. He came up the gravel on foot because his truck was in the yard. He was dusty and sweat-through, and he had the splitting maul in one hand from the woodpile on his way in. The shirt under the flannel was soaked down the back.

He stopped on the front porch and took the flannel off.

He had been doing this every night, she understood, because he had done it yesterday too and she had let herself not watch. He draped the flannel over the porch rail. Then he took the t-shirt off, which was the part she had not seen yesterday, because yesterday he had still had the flannel on when he came in. Tonight he hung the t-shirt next to the flannel on the rail, because the t-shirt was wet, because his back was wet, because he had been working for eleven hours and had come in hot.

She saw his back and his shoulders and the curve of his lats and the width of him from the top of the waist of his jeans to the shoulder blade, the line of his spine and the way the sheen on his skin caught the last of the light. Three seconds through the kitchen window, because that was how long it took him to take off the shirt and come inside.

She said nothing.

He said, "Shower," which was not a question, and she said, "Go," which came out the way she had meant it, which was a permission-grant and not a question, and he went.

The shower started. Louis was on the rug. The woodstove was going on its afternoon coals and wanted a piece of wood, and she put one in without thinking about it.

Dinner was going to be cold beef, again, the last of it. She sliced it thin, added the heel of the bread and the rest of the cheese to both plates, and carried them out onto the porch. The air was soft at sixty degrees and the light along the ridge was a low peach.

She set the plates on the porch chairs.

He came out of the shower in a clean t-shirt and jeans and bare feet, and he did not say anything about the plates. He sat in the porch chair. He picked up the plate. He ate.

She sat in the other chair. The dog came out with him and lay down between them on the porch boards like Louis was neutral territory, which he had not been at the bar and was apparently being now.

They ate. She finished hers in eight minutes. He finished his in six. Neither of them spoke for the length of it. The light went from peach to grey. A truck went by on the county road two fields over. Somewhere above the cabin a nighthawk made the sound a nighthawk made, once, and did not make it again.

She was watching the jamb of the cabin's back door.

He got up to take the plates in. He came to the kitchen doorway on his way back out and had to duck his head under the jamb, because the jamb was the jamb of a 1987 cabin and was five-foot-ten, which was why Cal had ducked through his own doorway for thirty-four years. Sawyer, at six-two, had to duck. He ducked without thinking about it, without flinching, because he had been ducking through that jamb since April.

He did not flinch at the doorway.

She sat with that.

She had been looking at him for twenty-four hours as the guy in my cabin, and she was, at six-forty on a Wednesday with a plate of cold beef eaten and the sun going down, starting to look at him as something else. The guy in her cabin ducked through doorways without flinching. The guy in her cabin flinched at her, once, for about a second, when she had said sixty days. He had ducked a doorway four inches too low for him without breaking the glass of water in his hand. He had heard generous and flinched.

She was not going to do anything with it tonight.

He came back out with a paperback in his hand, sat in the chair, opened the book somewhere in the middle, and started reading.

She went inside.

She sat at the kitchen table with her phone and scrolled Lacey's thread. Lacey had sent four texts over the day, three about the bar and one about a plumber who was in love with a woman two counties over and was making it everyone's problem. Molly tapped one back, love you jerk, and set the phone face-down on the table.

The cabin was quiet.

She looked at the phone face-down on the table. She looked at the screen door.

She stood up. She opened the fridge. There was one Rainier in the door and a second one on the bottom shelf she had not seen the first time she looked.

She pushed the screen door open with her hip.

He looked up from the book.

She said, "Beer?"

He said, "Yeah."

She got both Rainiers out. She had said Beer? and not I'm getting you a beer or you want a beer. She had said it in one word, which was a question, which was a thing she asked, which was a thing she had not done, as far as she could remember, in the previous twenty-nine hours.

She registered it. She did not comment.

She opened both beers on the edge of the counter with the lip she had opened ten thousand beers with at the bar, and went back out.

She handed him the Rainier. He said, "Thank you," which was what he had said every other time he had been handed a beer in twenty-nine hours, four times by her count, and she was counting. She sat in the other porch chair with her own.

The sun had gone.

The sky along the ridge was the color of a peach that had sat on the counter for twenty minutes, which was a color Molly had opinions about because the bar sold a peach margarita in summer and she had mixed enough of them to know.

She did not say anything. He did not say anything. She drank from her beer. He drank from his. Louis was on the porch boards with his head on his paws, between them, one ear up and one ear down and his eyes closed.

The county road was quiet. The cottonwood above the fire-pit had not moved.

She drank her beer. She did not go back inside.

He turned a page.
