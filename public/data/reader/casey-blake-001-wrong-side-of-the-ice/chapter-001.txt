It's one-thirty on a Tuesday in the first week of November and the rink hallway is the temperature of a vegetable crisper, and I am sitting at the training-room desk staring at a manila folder I have already read three times like it's going to tell me something new.

It isn't.

The folder has a name on it. Cahill, Gabriel, jersey number to be assigned. Inside the folder, on the upper right corner of the intake sheet, I have written WATCH THIS ONE in block capitals with a little box around it because past-me on Monday morning was apparently very confident, and present-me at one-thirty Tuesday is in fact watching it, so. Past-me wins.

Okay so. Background. I'm Nora Voss, twenty-three, one of two athletic trainers attached to Hartwell University men's hockey, and the rink is sixty-two degrees because the heat hasn't kicked on yet and won't until the fluorescent strip above the training table starts humming like a wasp, which is the rink's idea of a thermostat. My Stanley is on the bench beside my red bag, lid clicked shut because I learned the hard way, and the water in it is approaching the temperature of the rink itself, which is to say: cold enough that drinking it feels like a small personal punishment.

The walk from the dorm was six minutes of wind on the back of my neck. My ears are still attached. I checked.

I open Gabe Cahill's file for the third time. Grade II lateral ankle sprain, week eight, transfer from a program I am politely choosing not to think about by name because their head trainer is Lisa Marchetti and Lisa is one of the four people in this profession whose notes I trust on a first read. He's six foot three. Two-ten. Right wing. Coaching staff wants him on a plane to Albany in eleven days for the conference semifinal. Lisa's discharge sheet is clean and tidy and reads like a woman who knows what she's doing. The file is a normal file. The file is fine.

The file is fine and I have written WATCH THIS ONE on it in capital letters with a box around it.

I have not been wrong about a transfer yet.

Anyway.

I close the folder. I pull his three-by-five out of the active-assessment divider where I filed it Sunday night and set it next to the folder on the desk. Under his name and number I have written one line: *Gr. II lat. ankle sprain, wk 8, return-to-play protocol, assess.* Under that line, nothing. The card is waiting for me to have a thought about him as a person and not a file, which I cannot have until he walks through the door.

It is one-forty-one when the door opens.

Real talk: he is supposed to be here at one-forty-five. The door opening at one-forty-one is therefore information about him before he has spoken a word, and the information is: this is a guy who walked the route from the dorm yesterday and timed it and built in a buffer.

I look up.

He has to duck a little under the door frame which he does without noticing he's doing it, which is a thing tall people do, and there is a skate bag on his shoulder and a thermos in his hand and he is wearing a Hartwell hoodie that still has the warehouse fold-creases in it because he has been a student at this school for approximately seventy-two hours.

He says, "Hi. Gabe Cahill. I think I'm on your schedule."

I say, "One forty-five."

He says, "I'm early."

I say, "You are."

He closes the door behind him without being asked. I clock it. Most transfers in the first thirty seconds of a first appointment leave the door cracked because they're treating my training room like a doctor's office instead of a workspace, which is fine, I correct it, we move on. He has closed it like a guy who has been in fifty training rooms and knows the rules. Fine. Filed.

He sets his skate bag down by the wall and his thermos on the bench next to my Stanley, and the two cups are next to each other for one objectively dumb second, hot beverage and cold beverage, his cup and my cup, and my brain says *cute* and I tell my brain to shut up, professionally.

I am being so normal about this.

He says, "Sorry. I wasn't sure how long the walk from the dorm was going to be. I walked it yesterday and timed it."

I say, "It's nine minutes."

He says, "You've timed it."

I say, "Players have told me."

He laughs. It is a quick laugh, not a big one, and his front tooth is slightly off-center when he does it, a small tilt to the left that a puck has arranged for him at some point in the last decade. I see the tooth. I see the laugh. I see, in a quiet professional back compartment of my own head, that the laugh was timed well. Most transfers in the first five minutes do not laugh at all. They perform cooperation. He has skipped the performance and gone straight to ease, which is itself a kind of skill, and I am going to hold the read until I know which one it is.

I am also going to need to not look at his mouth.

This man is going to ruin my whole life.

Anyway.

I say, "Have a seat on the table. Left ankle or right?"

He says, "Right."

I say, "Shoes and socks off. Roll your pant leg up to the knee."

He does those things in the order I said them in, the way a person does them when he has done them a hundred times. He sits on the padded table under the fluorescent strip with his right foot out and his left knee bent loosely. The vinyl on the table is cracked in three places and patched with black tape in two. The strip is doing its low pre-buzz hum because the heat is thinking about coming on. The light from the rink comes through the small high window above the sink in that watery November way and I had stopped noticing it three years ago and I am noticing it again because there is a stranger in the room and his cup is touching my cup.

Cool. Cool cool cool.

I pull the stool over and sit at the end of the table with his foot at my eye level. I say, "I'm going to do a structural check first. Then functional. Then we'll talk about where you are. Tell me if anything hurts, and where. Scale of one to ten, ten is I need you to stop."

He says, "Okay."

I take his foot in both hands.

His foot is, structurally, a foot. A hockey foot. Calf above it that has done what calves of two-ten-pound men who skate three hours a day for fifteen years do. The ankle is thick through the lateral side which I expected from the file, residual swelling at week eight is standard for a Grade II, and the inversion grade feels mild under my thumb which I also expected. I move through the ligament tests, anterior drawer and then talar tilt and then squeeze, and he has good language for the pain scale and uses it without being prompted, which not every athlete does.

He says "three" on the talar tilt. On the deep pressure point over the anterior talofibular ligament he says "six out of ten," and he says it flat, without the protective downshift of a man trying to bank pain for later.

I write *6/10 ATFL deep pressure* on the card and I do not tell him I can hear the difference between a real six and a managed six. The real six is when the number costs them something to say. He just paid.

I say, "Okay. That's consistent with your file."

He says, "Yeah."

I say, "Have you done your morning mobility today."

He says, "Yes ma'am."

I look up.

He says, "Sorry. I'm from North Carolina. It's automatic. I'll stop."

I say, "Don't call me ma'am."

He says, "I won't."

I say, "Nora is fine."

He says, "Nora."

He says it the way you say a word you're putting on a shelf for later. He does not make a thing of it. He does not draw it out. He just says it once, on its own, and then waits for me to give him the next instruction, which would be charming if I were paying attention to it, which I am not, professionally.

(I am paying attention to it. I am a liar.)

I look back at his ankle.

I say, "I'm going to have you do a single-leg balance, thirty seconds, eyes closed, on the right. Then the left for comparison. Off the table."

He says, "Okay."

He gets off the table. He stands up the way he came in, without announcement, a man not in a hurry about his own height. He centers himself in the square of tape on the floor I laid down three weeks ago for exactly this test and he does not ask what the tape is for, which means he has done this drill at least fifty times. He closes his eyes. He balances on the right foot for thirty seconds with a small correction at the twelve-second mark and another at twenty-three. He opens his eyes when I say time, and does the left foot the same way with one correction at the eighteen.

I write the numbers down.

I say, "Lateral hop, five reps. Hop onto the right, land, stick for a one-count, hop onto the left, land, stick."

He does it. The takeoff on the right is maybe two percent slower than the left, a quarter-beat of hesitation I would have missed if I weren't looking for it and I was looking for it because the file said week eight and Grade II and the coaching staff wants him on a plane in eleven days. He sticks every landing.

I write that down too.

I say, "Sit back down."

He sits back down.

I say, "Who were you with for your last return-to-play."

He says, "Lisa Marchetti. She's head athletic trainer at," and he names the program. "She wrote most of what you're looking at."

I say, "I read her notes."

He says, "She's good."

I say, "I know she is."

There's a pause. He's looking at the fluorescent strip, which has started buzzing in the last thirty seconds because the heat has officially kicked on, and he says, "Is that going to go."

I say, "Go where."

He says, "Out. Like, blow. It sounds like it wants to."

I say, "It's been doing that for six years."

He says, "Okay."

I say, "It has not blown yet."

He says, "Reassuring."

I say, "I didn't say it was reassuring."

He laughs again, small, same tilt at the front tooth, and stops before I can decide what I think about it, which is rude of him. The deciding-what-I-think is mine to do. I was going to do it.

I say, "Lisa's notes say you ran a figure-eight on grass Monday of last week."

He says, "I did."

I say, "How did that feel."

He says, "Good. I took it slow. Six out of ten the first pass. Four by the last pass."

I say, "She has it as a four flat."

He says, "The first pass was a six. I didn't say anything because I wanted to do the rest."

And. Okay.

I write *patient-reported 6/10 pass-one figure-eight, not charted by LM* on his card. I write it a little harder than I write the other things, because cooperative-on-entry is a known tactic and I have seen three forwards do it in three years on this job. Volunteer one small discrepancy in the first appointment, build trust capital, withdraw it in week three when the real thing surfaces and you want to be in the lineup.

Or, you know. He's just being honest.

I am going to hold the read.

I say, "Okay. You're at week eight. You're inside the standard window for lateral Grade II return-to-play. I'm not clearing you today. I'm not not-clearing you. I'm putting you on an assessment series."

He says, "How many."

I say, "Four. Every other day. Semifinal is eleven days out. That gets us to the Monday before."

He says, "Okay."

I say, "I'll want a rink session on day three and day five, which means I'll be at practice watching you skate. I'll want honest reporting on the pain scale every time, including anything you don't flag voluntarily because it was only a six for an hour and then it was fine. If you tell me it's fine when it's not fine, I can't help you and we lose a day finding out."

He says, "Okay."

I say, "Lisa said you're good on this."

He says, "I try to be."

I say, "Try harder."

He says, "Okay."

I write on his card: *Assessment series, 4, EOD. Day 3 and 5 on-ice observation. PIP RTP window tracking.* I close the card box. I put the file flat on the desk and put my hand on it and do not move my hand.

The door opens.

Kowalski comes in without knocking the way the starting lineup has been coming in without knocking since my first week in this training room. He is six foot even and defenseman-thick and his face has been arranged by repeated and specific pucks and he has a lanyard around his neck with a laminated card clipped to it. He takes one look at Gabe on the table, one look at me at the desk, and says, "I'm early."

I say, "It's Tuesday. You don't have an appointment Tuesday."

Kowalski says, "I don't have a medical appointment Tuesday. I have an administrative one."

I say, "What does that mean."

Kowalski says, "I need to walk you through the taping order before Friday."

I say, "You've walked me through the taping order three times."

Kowalski says, "Once in September, once in October, once in the first week of November. This is the pre-game three walk-through. It is distinct."

Gabe says, from the table, "What's the taping order."

Kowalski turns to him like a man who has been waiting two minutes for this exact question and unclips the lanyard. He walks over to the table and hands Gabe the laminated card. Gabe takes it and looks at it.

Gabe says, "This is laminated."

Kowalski says, "Coach Brennan had it laminated in 2023."

Gabe says, "Coach Brennan."

Kowalski says, "Former head coach. He retired. The laminate remained."

Gabe says, "Okay."

I say, "Kowalski."

Kowalski says, "Yes."

I say, "Why are you telling him."

Kowalski says, "He's on the card."

Gabe says, "I'm on the card."

Kowalski says, "You're fifth. After me, then Lindqvist, then Osei-Bonsu, then the left wing pair, which is Keller and Danek in that order because Danek taped before Keller in the 2019 Albany game and we lost in a shootout, and then you. Cleared and activated, obviously. Right now you're an asterisk."

Gabe says, "I have an asterisk."

Kowalski says, "You have an asterisk and a pencil notation. I did not laminate you yet."

I say, "Kowalski."

Kowalski says, "Yes."

I say, "That is not how anatomy works."

Kowalski says, "It's not about anatomy."

I say, "What is it about."

Kowalski says, "Winning."

I say, "We're 4-3."

Kowalski says, "We'd be 2-5."

I do not have a response to that. Well. I do. The response is that two-and-five is an invented number from a counterfactual inside Kowalski's head, and counterfactuals are not statistics. The response, fully delivered, would keep Kowalski in the room another six minutes. I want him out.

I say, "I have your taping order. It's in the drawer."

Kowalski says, "Which drawer."

I say, "Top drawer of the desk."

Kowalski says, "That's acceptable."

I say, "Is there something else."

Kowalski says, "Not at present." He turns toward the door. He stops at the door. He turns back and says, "Cahill."

Gabe says, "Yeah."

Kowalski says, "Welcome to Hartwell."

Gabe says, "Thanks."

Kowalski leaves.

The door closes. The fluorescent strip keeps buzzing. Gabe hands the laminated card back to me without comment, and I take it and set it face-down on the desk.

Gabe says, "Is he serious."

I say, "About the laminate or the taping order."

Gabe says, "Either."

I say, "Both."

Gabe says, "Is the taping order a real thing."

I say, "It is a real thing in the sense that Kowalski is serious about it. It is not a real thing in the sense that there is any statistical support for it."

Gabe says, "Have you told him."

I say, "I have."

Gabe says, "How did that go."

I say, "We are 4-3."

He grins. The grin is bigger than the laugh was and uses more of the mouth and the off-center tooth is visible for longer and I am, professionally, on the wrong side of this table.

He says, "I have so many questions."

I say, "Save them for Thursday."

He says, "Is Thursday when you tell me how the team works."

I say, "Thursday is when I look at your ankle again."

He says, "Right."

He says it in a voice that is the verbal equivalent of taking a small step back without moving his feet, which is a thing I notice because I am noticing things about him I should not be noticing.

Anyway.

I say, "Can you retie your skate laces."

He says, "The left one, yeah. The right one I do loose on the top two eyelets for another week."

I say, "Loose like how."

He says, "One finger under the bow."

I say, "Who told you one finger."

He says, "Lisa."

I say, "Okay. Keep doing one finger. Come back Thursday. Same time. I'm putting you in for one forty-five."

He says, "Thank you."

He gets down off the table. He sits on the bench to put his socks and shoes back on and I watch him do it for two seconds because I want to see how he moves weight onto the right foot without thinking about it, and then I stop watching him because two seconds is the limit of professional observation and I have reached it.

I turn to the desk. I write on his card, in the line under my earlier note: *Structural WNL for wk 8 post Gr. II. Functional: single-leg balance clean bilaterally, lateral hop slight L/R asymmetry (~2%) but all landings held. Subjective: 6/10 ATFL, self-reported 6 on figure-eight pass-one (not charted by LM). Cooperative.*

I stop. I look at the word *cooperative*. I cross it out. I write *presenting as cooperative* in its place.

I keep going: *Re-assess Thursday. On-ice observation Thursday afternoon practice. PIP RTP tracking toward 11/22 semifinal cleared or not-cleared decision window.*

Gabe stands up. He shoulders his skate bag, picks up his thermos, and turns toward the desk before he turns toward the door. He says, "Nora."

I say, "Yeah."

He says, "Thanks for squeezing me in."

I say, "It wasn't a squeeze. You were on the schedule."

He says, "Still."

I look at him for one more second. He is standing in the middle of the training room in the fluorescent light with his thermos and his bag, a stranger three days into Hartwell, and he is waiting for me to dismiss him because that's the protocol in a training room and he has been in enough training rooms to know it.

I say, "Thursday. One forty-five. Don't be early this time."

He says, "I will be nine minutes early."

I say, "Gabe."

He says, "I walked it. It takes nine minutes. I can't fake it."

I say, "Thursday."

He says, "Thursday."

He leaves.

The door closes behind him and I sit at the desk for a minute with my hand flat on the file. I can hear his footsteps go down the rubber floor of the hallway and then I can't hear them. I open the card box and put his three-by-five back in behind the divider for active-assessment, between Kowalski and Lindqvist, alphabetical by last name. I close the box.

The fluorescent strip is still buzzing. The heat has been on ten minutes, give or take. I have known the strip does that since my third week on the job. The first time it did it I thought it was going to blow, and then it kept not blowing, and now it is just the sound of afternoon in the training room.

My phone is on the bench under my Stanley. I pick it up.

*Maya: bonfire thing @8 you in*

I write back *can't, late sessions* and she writes back *that's what u said mon. we will discuss* and then a second later *also priya says ur lying about late sessions.* I lock the phone and shove it in my back pocket because Maya is annoyingly correct about 60 percent of the time and I am not in the mood to find out which 60 percent this is.

I stand up.

I reach over and pull the cord on the fluorescent strip. The strip pops and goes off. The training room goes dark except for the rink light coming through the small high window above the sink, which is the watery grey-blue of a November afternoon on a bad side of campus. I pick up the file. I stand there with it for a second.

The note in the upper right corner of the intake sheet says WATCH THIS ONE in capital letters with a box around it.

I do not know what to do with him yet.

I have until Thursday to figure it out. Thursday is on-ice observation. The ice tells me things the training table doesn't.

That is the problem.

That is also the plan.

I pick up my Stanley. I shut the drawer. I go.
