Day six.

I am standing in the rink hallway between the ice door and the training room and Coach Martinez has caught me with his clipboard in one hand and a coffee he is not drinking in the other, and Coach Martinez does not schedule.

The hallway is forty feet of visiting-team lockers and it funnels sound the way a culvert funnels runoff. Eight people are in earshot. I count them without meaning to. Two equipment kids restocking water bottles. An assistant coach on his phone. Kowalski and Lindqvist coming off the ice in half-pads with their sticks balanced across their shoulders like yokes. A facilities guy with a wet mop. The PR intern who is supposed to be getting a quote about Thursday and is instead listening to Coach Martinez the way you listen to a man who does not lower his voice.

Real talk: this is not a place to make a clearance decision. I know this. I am making one anyway.

Coach Martinez says, "Where are we on Cahill."

The duct tape on my left strap is peeling at one corner. I have been meaning to fix it for three weeks. I shift the strap. I make a decision.

I say, "He's tracking well."

Coach Martinez nods once. He writes something on the clipboard without looking at it. He says, "So Thursday's on the table."

I say, "He's tracking well." I say it again because I can hear the shape of my first answer and I do not like the shape. Saying it again does not make it a different shape. "I'll have something for you by Wednesday."

He says, "Tracking well is what I need."

He is already walking. He says, "Nora. Good work."

He goes past me toward the film room, clipboard under his arm, coffee still untouched, and Kowalski, who has stopped in the hallway for reasons I suspect are less about his skate laces than about what I just said, looks at me over the top of his stick.

I do not meet his eye.

*Tracking well* means *continuing to improve at the expected rate.* *Cleared* means *cleared.* I have been an athletic trainer for three years. I know the difference between those two sentences the way I know the difference between a lateral sprain and a medial one, which is to say exactly.

The assistant coach gets off his phone and says, "Martinez said Cahill's tracking well?" to no one in particular and puts his phone back in his pocket. The PR intern is typing into a note app. Kowalski walks off toward the locker room with Lindqvist. He does not look back. That is almost worse than looking back.

(I am being so normal about this. I am a liar.)

I go into the training room and close the door and stand in the middle of the floor with my bag still on my shoulder.

The fluorescent strip is humming. The heat has been on ten minutes and the strip is doing its thing, a low buzz under the building noise that I have learned to stop hearing most days. Today I hear it, the way you hear your own breathing at three a.m. once you have noticed it.

I put the bag on the training table.

I get my laptop out of the bottom drawer and open it.

Gabe's file is still on the desktop where I left it last night because I was working through the afternoon to confirm the lateral was closing in on full range and I wanted the file visible when I came in. I had made notes. They were honest notes. The last line says *borderline, three more days of functional work before clearance consideration.* I typed that on Sunday. I meant it.

I read the line twice.

I just told the coach he is tracking well.

The honest answer, the answer that matches the file open on the screen, is *borderline, I need three more days.* The answer I gave is the one a coach wants to hear in a hallway with eight people listening, which is technically true, because Gabe is tracking well, and which is also going to be interpreted as *greenlight,* because hockey coaches in hallways interpret everything as greenlight unless you spell *no* with a bullhorn.

I did not say no.

I gave him the sentence with *well* in it and I watched him write something down.

Anyway.

I close the laptop. I open it again. The notes are still there. The hallway is still behind me. The heat is still making the fluorescent strip hum. Kowalski is going to walk through that door in approximately twelve minutes because it is Tuesday and Tuesday is his optional tape-check, and I need to have a face on by the time he gets here.

My phone buzzes on the desk.

A Hartwell Athletics email. Subject line: *Thursday Travel Roster v3.*

The attachment loads in the three seconds it takes me to understand what Coach Martinez did with that nod in the hallway. Twenty-two names on the travel list. Cahill is the tenth, right-winger slot. Sent nine minutes ago. To twenty-seven people in the athletic department.

Including the team.

Including Gabe.

I sit down on the rolling stool.

The fluorescent strip hums.

---

Kowalski is on time because Kowalski is always on time. He comes in at twelve past the hour with the laminated card in his hand and the mild ceremonial expression of a man who has brought official documentation to a small bureaucratic meeting.

He says, "So about Thursday's taping order."

I say, "Kowalski, I..."

He says, "I know what you're going to say. You're going to say you'll do it in whatever order works for the rotation. I'm here to offer a service." He sets the card on the training table. It is laminated. I can see the edges where the plastic sealed against itself at the press. The corners are square and clean. "This is the order. Same order as last season. I added a footnote about the 2022 incident where Coach Brennan had Trainer Davis tape us left-to-right in practice and we lost by four on Friday. Footnote's on the back."

I pick up the card.

There is indeed a footnote on the back. It reads: *2022-11-04: LH/RH tape protocol inverted in Tuesday-afternoon pre-scrimmage prep. Lost 1-5 vs. Rochester. Causal uncertain; correlative record.* It is typed in what looks like 10-point Times New Roman.

I say, "You laminated this."

He says, "I laminated it in 2023."

I say, "You laminated it in 2023 and you carry it with you."

He says, "I carry a copy."

I say, "How many copies do you have."

He considers the question with the seriousness it deserves.

He says, "At home, two. One in my gym bag. This one is the working copy."

I say, "The working copy."

He says, "Sometimes I make field annotations."

I say, "Kowalski."

He says, "Coach Brennan had the first one laminated. That was his idea. I'm not going to take credit for a laminated card I didn't laminate, that would be plagiarism."

I say, "That is somehow worse."

He says, "I'm just the messenger." He is a defenseman. He is built like a defenseman, which is to say he is built like a brick shithouse with a data habit. He is looking at me with the calm specific patience of a man who organized the entire Thursday logistical narrative in his head last night and came down to the training room to deliver it. He says, "I brought the backup card too. In case the main one goes missing between now and Thursday morning."

He sets a second laminated card on the table next to the first.

The second card is identical to the first except for a small black dot in the upper-right corner.

I say, "Why is there a dot on this one."

He says, "So I know which one's the backup."

I say, "They are identical otherwise."

He says, "The dot is the differentiating feature."

I say, "Kowalski. Get out of my training room."

He is already walking to the door. At the door he turns around and says, "Are you going to keep both cards or are you giving the backup back."

I say, "I am keeping both cards."

He says, "Excellent."

He leaves.

I sit at the training table and look at the two laminated cards side by side for fifteen seconds. Then I slide them into the top drawer of my desk, which is the drawer with the sharpies and the athletic tape and the one stapler that works. I do not throw them away. I do not put them in recycling or in the bottom drawer with the requisition forms. I am going to remember I have not thrown them away. I am going to remember I have put them where my hand can find them easily.

I am doing a lot of things today I am not examining.

The door opens.

Gabe says, "Hey," already halfway into the room.

---

He is already on the table by the time I turn around. He has a habit of that. The table is where he goes, the way a dog goes to a specific corner of a couch, a place-based confidence I noticed on Day 2 and filed and kept filed. He is in a team shirt and the grey team sweats and he has the left pant leg rolled up to the knee and the ankle is taped from yesterday because he was at the optional skate.

He says, "Martinez said I'm cleared."

My hand is on the rolling stool where I left it. I register my hand. I register the stool. I register the way the fluorescent strip dipped half a note when the building's heating cycled back on.

I say, "I have not signed anything."

He looks at me.

I watch him recalibrate. A quarter-second where his face changes by a very small amount, in a way that is not readable at twenty feet and is extremely readable at four.

He says, "He said. Okay. He said you said I was tracking well."

I say, "I said you were tracking well."

He says, "And he said that..."

I say, "I know what he said. I said you were tracking well. That is not a clearance."

A beat.

He says, "Are those different?"

I say, "Yes."

He is quiet for two seconds and then he says, "Okay." He says it the way you say okay when your brain is doing a small piece of math it had not been expecting to do. He says, "Okay. So what is, what is the actual..."

I say, "You are borderline. You are progressing well. I want three more days of functional testing. I wrote that down on Sunday. I wrote it in your file."

He says, "You wrote it in my file."

I say, "Yes."

He says, "Can I see the file."

I say, "No."

He looks at me with the expression of someone who was about to be charming about something and reconsidered. He puts his hands on the edge of the table on either side of his thighs.

He says, "Okay. So Thursday."

I say, "Thursday is not a decision I have made yet."

He says, "The travel list has me on it."

I say, "I saw."

He says, "You saw."

I say, "The email came in while I was checking." I do not finish saying what I was checking. The fluorescent strip picks the wrong moment to flicker and then settles. "I'm going to send a note to Martinez. I'm going to clarify the language."

He says, "Clarify." His voice has the flat edge of someone testing the word.

I say, "Yes."

He says, "That's going to be a whole conversation."

I say, "It is."

He says, "You don't want to have a whole conversation with Martinez."

I say, "I do not want to have a whole conversation with Martinez in a hallway with eight people listening, which is what the morning's conversation was. I am going to send the note, and if he wants to talk about it he'll come and find me, and we'll talk about it in here with the door closed."

He nods once. He looks at me for a second without saying anything and it is a look I have seen twice before, late on Day 2 and once on Day 4 on the observation deck when I did not think he was looking my way, and now this third time.

It is steady. It has no particular charm in it.

I do not know what to do with it.

He says, "I don't want to play if I shouldn't play."

I say, "I know."

He says, "I want you to say it. Say that you know that."

I say, "I know you don't want to play if you shouldn't play."

He says, "Good."

He sits back a little on the table. The paper crinkles under him the way the crinkly paper always crinkles when an athlete in the 210-pound range sits back on a training table.

He says, "I'm sorry about the hallway."

I say, "You didn't do the hallway. I did."

He says, "I'm sorry about the email chain."

I say, "You didn't do the email either."

He says, "I'm apologising for the way it feels."

I say, "Don't apologise for the way it feels."

He says, "Okay."

There is a half-second of nothing.

Then he says, "For the record. I'd rather get the truth in a closed room than a clearance in a hallway."

I say, "You don't get to grade my method."

He says, "I'm not grading. I'm noting."

I say, "Noting."

He says, "Like in a file."

I look at him.

He says, "Sorry. Too soon."

I say, "Way too soon."

He says, "I'll workshop it for Thursday."

Okay so. The grin is small and the tooth is doing its thing and I am, professionally, on the wrong side of this table again.

I pick up the clipboard. I click my pen. I do not look at him for a second and I am aware I am not looking at him and it is not because I do not want to.

I say, "Range of motion. Show me dorsiflexion."

He pulls his toes up toward his shin, slow and even, and I watch the angle without thinking about his face.

He is doing it cleanly. He gives me five degrees more than yesterday. I write the number down. I do not say good. I do not say nice. I write the number down.

I have him do plantarflexion, inversion, eversion. I have him hop. He hops. He sticks the landings. I write the numbers down.

I say, "Off the table. Single-leg balance, eyes closed, thirty seconds, right then left."

He gets off the table. He is a foot taller than me and he is one foot taller than me in a small training room and the room shrinks every time he stands up in it.

He balances on the right foot for thirty seconds with a small correction at the nineteen mark and another at the twenty-six. The left is clean. I write the numbers down.

I say, "Sit."

He sits.

I say, "I'm going to see you Wednesday and I'm going to see you Thursday morning. Wednesday is a full functional. Thursday morning is a recheck."

He says, "Okay."

I say, "I am not signing anything before Thursday morning."

He says, "Okay."

I say, "If Wednesday goes the way I expect, you are looking at Thursday-morning clearance. That is contingent on Wednesday. Do you understand the word contingent."

He says, "I do."

I say, "Repeat it back."

He says, "Thursday-morning clearance contingent on Wednesday going the way you expect."

I say, "Good."

He says, "Nora."

I say, "Yeah."

He says, "Day 2 you asked if I'd done my morning mobility and I said yes. I'd done the warmup. I hadn't done the mobility. The mobility took fourteen minutes and I had nine."

I write that down.

I write it on his card directly under the dorsiflexion number, in the same hand, in the same blue pen.

I do not say anything for a second.

I say, "Thank you for telling me."

He says, "It's why I came in."

I say, "I assumed you came in because the email said you were on a plane."

He says, "Both things are true."

I look up at him.

He says, "I redirect. It's a thing I do. I am trying to not do it with you. It is going to take a couple weeks."

I write *patient self-reports history of underreporting* on the card. I write it without looking at the card.

I say, "Okay."

He says, "Okay."

---

He leaves at one fifty-eight.

I write the note to Martinez.

I write it in clean professional prose, four sentences long. Subject: *RE: Cahill clearance timeline.* I write *clarify the morning's exchange,* I write *tracking well is a progress descriptor, not a clearance,* I write *targeting end-of-week decision pending Wednesday and Thursday morning functional tests,* I write *will inform roster planning accordingly.* I read the email twice. I do not hedge. There is no *perhaps,* no *maybe,* no *might.* I do not use those words in medical communication and I am not going to start now.

I send it.

I get up from the desk. The training room is quiet. The fluorescent strip has settled.

I walk the length of the room because I need to walk somewhere, and I walk to the cabinet where the ankle tape is kept and I count the rolls that are left, because counting something is better than standing with my phone in my hand waiting for a response.

There are eleven rolls.

I write the number on the requisition pad. I initial it. I hang the pad back on its hook.

My phone buzzes.

Martinez's reply is two words: *Copy. Wednesday.*

I put the phone down on the desk face-up. I look at it for a minute. Then I turn the phone face-down, because Martinez's reply is fine, and Martinez is not the problem in the room.

The problem in the room is that I gave the easy answer in the hallway and I watched it become an email chain and I walked it back, and it is fine now, it is functionally fine, and the cost of that fine is a full half-hour of my morning I am not getting back.

Anyway.

My phone buzzes again.

Not Martinez.

*Maya: priya says u walked past her in the dining hall and didn't see her*
*Maya: she is offended*
*Maya: she says hi*

I write back *tell priya i'm in my head, will buy her a coffee, also i'm fine.*

Maya writes back *u said "i'm fine" which means u are not fine*

I write back *correct.*

She writes back *bonfire @8, you in or am i sending priya*

I lock the phone.

I open the desk drawer. I look at the two laminated cards. I close the drawer. I open the drawer again and take out one card, the backup, the one with the small black dot, and I put it in the top pocket of my red bag.

I do not ask myself why.

I turn off the strip light and lock the training room door behind me.

Out in the rink hallway the building smells like old ice and floor cleaner and somebody down by the Zamboni bay is running a radio on low, some country station I do not recognize. The windchill in the hallway is always about five degrees lower than the rink itself. I pull my jacket tighter at the collar.

At the end of the hallway, by the equipment room, Kowalski is standing with a water bottle and a stick. He looks at me without saying anything and nods once.

I nod back.

Two equipment-room kids come out of the side door behind him pushing a laundry cart stacked with the practice jerseys from the afternoon session, and Kowalski says something to them I cannot hear, and the taller of the two kids laughs.

That is the end of Tuesday as far as the rink is concerned.

The clock above the ice door says 5:47.

I have been in the building since seven.

I go out through the rink doors into the parking lot, where my breath comes out white and the light is already going because it is November and November does this, and my boots crunch on the thin sheet of ice the afternoon has left across the blacktop.

The laminated card is in my bag.

I can feel it against my hip through the canvas.

I walk to my car.
