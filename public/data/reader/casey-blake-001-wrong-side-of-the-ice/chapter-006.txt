It is seven-forty on a Monday and the rink parking lot is a sheet of glare ice with gravel thrown over it in two unserious handfuls, and I am putting my heel down the way I have been putting my heel down for three winters, which is the small grim authority of a person who has never fallen on this patch and intends to keep the record clean.

The record cleans itself. Again.

I unlock the side door at seven-forty-two. The steel clanks shut behind me.

Okay so. The reason I'm here at seven-forty-two and not, say, eight-fifteen like a normal person, is the same reason I moved Gabe Cahill's appointment from four p.m. to ten a.m. on Friday at eleven-seventeen at night. I rebalanced the schedule. That is the sentence I am using.

Anyway.

The training-room light comes on with its half-second delay. The strip over the table buzzes once, settles. I put the red bag on the bench, the coffee on the far counter, and open the laptop.

*Gabe Cahill, 10:00 a.m., secondary tape check, left ankle.*

Ten a.m. is, by my own internal accounting, the third busiest hour of my week. Ten a.m. is Kowalski. Ten a.m. is Lindqvist. Ten a.m. is two freshmen forwards. Ten a.m. is the goalie coach, who likes to come in and use the table as a standing desk. Ten a.m. is a room with witnesses in it. That is the point.

I lay the morning out. Three widths of tape. Pre-wrap in the tan color that hides on skin. Two rolls of the sticky spray. Scissors. The card box from the desk drawer, set on the corner of the table where I can reach it without standing up. The lid has a coffee ring on it from 2024 and I have not replaced the lid.

I take out Kowalski's card. Kowalski is always first.

At seven-fifty-eight the door bangs open and Maya comes in holding two travel mugs and a bagel in her teeth.

I almost drop the card.

Maya is supposed to be in Intro to Nutrition at eight on Mondays. Which means she has blown it off. Which means she has blown it off for a specific reason. Which means I am about to be roasted in my own training room before nine a.m.

Cool. Cool cool cool.

Maya sets one mug down on the counter next to mine. She takes the bagel out of her teeth. She says, "I brought the good coffee."

I say, "I have coffee."

She says, "You have the break-room coffee. I brought the coffee shop coffee."

I say, "Thank you."

She leans against the counter and gives me the eye contact she gives me when she is about to say a thing.

She says, "You moved his appointment to ten a.m."

I say, "Rebalancing the schedule."

She says, "Right."

I say, "Don't."

She says, "I didn't say anything."

I say, "You were about to."

She says, "I was about to offer to cover your Thursday clinic slot."

I pick up the tape scissors and pretend to test them. The scissors are fine. The scissors will be fine on the morning of my own funeral. I say nothing. She chews her bagel the way she chews when she is willing to outlast the bagel.

She says, "I'll take that as a yes."

I say, "I didn't say yes."

She says, "You didn't say no."

I say, "Maya."

She swallows. She puts the bagel on the counter and picks up the coffee and looks at me over the rim with the same steady unhurried attention her mother apparently uses on landlords. She says, "Nora. I will cover the Thursday clinic. I will not ask you anything else. I am not even going to look at you significantly. I am going to drink my coffee and I am going to leave."

I say, "Okay."

She says, "Kowalski's in the hall, by the way. He has a card."

I say, "Another card."

She says, "A different card. He said he'd wait."

She finishes her coffee standing up, which takes approximately fourteen seconds because Maya drinks coffee the way a fireman drinks water, and then she looks me in the eye with affection so specific it is almost rude and says, "I love you, you are being very stupid, I will see you for dinner," and goes out past Kowalski. Kowalski holds the door for her without breaking stride.

Anyway.

Kowalski comes in.

He is in a Hawks practice jersey, sweatpants, and the low rubber slide-sandals every hockey player in the program wears from the locker room to the training room as if the concrete hallway between the two is a sacred space that prohibits real shoes. He is also holding a three-by-five card, which is not his usual medium. His usual medium is laminated, eight-point font, bullet points.

I say, "Morning."

He says, "Morning. Table."

He gets on the table. Ankle retape, pre-practice, standard. I start the stirrup strip around the bottom of the foot and up the medial and lateral sides into the anchor, and he says, "I have to tell you something."

I say, "Mm."

He says, "About the ten o'clock."

I do not stop taping. I say, "What about it."

He says, "Cahill asked me at Sunday skate what his appointment time was."

I say, "Why did Cahill ask you."

He says, "Because he didn't want to ask you."

I run the tape over the lateral malleolus and back down. I say, "Is that an editorial addition or is that what he said."

He says, "That's what he said."

I say, "And why are you telling me this."

He says, "Because I told him it was ten. He said, *like, with other guys there?* I said, *Kowalski at ten, first after Lindqvist.* He said, *huh.* I said, *huh what.* He said, *nothing.*"

I say, "Kowalski."

He says, "I know."

I say, "You don't know."

He says, "I don't, but whatever I don't know, I'm not going to make it worse." He holds his foot still for me. "He's a good guy. I like him. I'm not saying anything, the team isn't saying anything, nobody's saying anything. I just thought you'd want to know he noticed."

I finish the tape in silence. I smooth the last strip down over the anchor. I tap the side of his ankle once to say *done*.

He says, "Thanks."

I say, "What's the card for."

He holds it up. It says, in his small careful handwriting, *Game three. Laminated card still valid. No updates required. K.*

I say, "Acceptable?"

He says, "Acceptable."

He gets off the table. He goes out. I stand at the table for a second with my hand flat on the vinyl and the strip humming overhead in the exact register it has hummed in for three years, and I think, *he noticed*, and then I think, *of course he noticed*, and then I think, *you are a twenty-three-year-old woman with a master plan that consists of scheduling and you are being observed by a twenty-year-old defenseman who laminates his life*, and I pick up Lindqvist's file and do not let myself sit down.

He talks the whole time about a defensive-zone drill Coach Martinez has been running that he thinks is stupid and possibly dangerous and definitely going to cost them a player if they keep running it, and I make noises in the right places and feel the twenty-minute slot settle over the small pit in my stomach like a lid.

The two freshmen come in at eight-forty.

The goalie coach comes in at nine and stands with his elbow on the table and tells me about a golden retriever he is thinking of getting. At nine-forty-eight the room empties out except for me and the strip and Gabe Cahill's file on the desk.

At nine-fifty-one the door opens.

It is not ten a.m.

He is in the doorway in a grey Hartwell Hawks hoodie with the sleeves pushed up to his forearms and his gym bag in one hand and his phone in the other, and he stands there for a second looking at me with the specific patience of a man who is not going to sit down until I acknowledge that he is early.

I am being so normal about this.

I say, "You're early."

He says, "I know."

I say, "The appointment is at ten."

He says, "I know. I came at nine-fifty because I thought the room would be empty at nine-fifty and I wanted to talk to you before it wasn't."

I say, "Gabe."

He says, "Nora."

The strip hums. The Zamboni starts up two doors down. He has been here ten days. He has figured out the building. Of course he has.

I say, "I'm rebalancing the schedule."

I say, "A lot of schedules were adjusted."

He says, "Nora."

I say nothing. Underneath the easy charm is a person who has figured out exactly what I am doing and is asking me about it at nine-fifty-one in a voice that is not trying to win anything.

Real talk: this is worse than him not noticing.

He comes in. He closes the door halfway, not all the way, which I notice. He sets the gym bag by the wall, gets on the table, and the table does its small familiar creak under his weight.

He says, "Nora. Look at me for a second."

He says, "I'm not going to make this difficult. I'm not here to make you talk about it. I'm here for the ankle. I just wanted you to know that I know. And that I'm not."

I say, "Okay."

I say, "Yes."

He puts his foot up. I start the tape.

It is a standard retape. Stirrup strip, two figure-eights at the heel, horseshoe across the malleolus, anchor strip around the top, check the tension with two fingers under the anchor, trim. I have done this tape eleven times on this ankle in ten days. My hands know the ankle the way my hands know the card box and the coffee machine and the door of my dorm room, which is to say my hands are doing the thing while my second-floor head is somewhere else, and that is the problem, because ankle-taping is second-floor work and I am skimming.

I run the lateral strip from the peroneal along the outside and back down. Standard. I set the anchor. Standard. I check the tension at the top with two fingers.

He says, "How long."

I say, "How long what."

I keep my hands on the anchor strip. I say, "I was going to say I had paperwork at nine-fifty."

He does not say anything else for a minute. The Zamboni outside does its slow U-turn. I hear Kowalski laugh at something in the hallway, big and sudden, the particular way Kowalski laughs when someone has said something that is technically funny and also technically stupid.

Gabe says, "You going to the game tomorrow night."

At three-twenty I have the training room to myself. I have an hour before Coach Martinez wants the daily check-in. I pull Gabe Cahill's file and sit with it on the table and start from the top.

Grade II lateral ankle sprain. Eight weeks old. Documented from his Division I program. Clean imaging from week two. Week-by-week functional testing from his previous trainer, a man named Petrov, who writes neatly and uses the full range of medical vocabulary and does not pad his notes. I have read Petrov's notes three times. They are fine.

My own notes on top. Day 1 assessment. Day 3 functional tests. Day 5 practice observation. Day 7 exam. Day 8. Day 9.

Day 9 is this morning.

I read Day 7, which I wrote last Friday at nine-thirty p.m. at this table under this strip light, and I read Day 8, which is Saturday, and I read Day 9, which is this morning, and something catches my eye on Day 7 that I did not notice at the time.

On Day 7 I wrote, *lateral ligament palpation, no point tenderness at the anterior talofibular ligament, some tension along the calcaneofibular.* That is all. I drew the little anatomy diagram I draw for my own notes, with my own shorthand, and I marked the calcaneofibular with a small check.

I look at the diagram.

I look at it again.

The check is in the wrong place.

The check is on the anterior talofibular, not the calcaneofibular. I palpated the calcaneofibular. I remember the specific angle of my thumb. But I made the note about the anterior one and marked it on the diagram by muscle memory, because the anterior talofibular is the one that almost always has some tension at week eight and I have drawn that check on thousands of diagrams. I was in a hurry. It was nine-thirty on a Friday. I was drawing with my second-floor head gone.

Oh.

Oh no.

I go back to the table. I put my hand flat on the vinyl and look at the empty room.

I did not re-palpate the calcaneofibular this morning, because this morning I rushed the reassessment because I wanted him out of the room, and I did a standard retape based on Friday's notes, which are wrong on the diagram in the one specific place that matters, and I set an anchor strip at a tension calibrated for a lateral ligament picture that might be one tissue off from the actual lateral ligament picture.

It is possibly nothing. It is possibly a secondary finding. There is one way to know.

I open the file. I write, at the top of a fresh page, in my clean block handwriting: *Day 10. Return for reassessment, lateral ligament palpation, calcaneofibular focus. 4:00 p.m.*

I have just scheduled a tomorrow-afternoon one-on-one session with Gabe Cahill in my training room, because I rushed the reassessment today, and now I have to have him back in the room.

This man is going to ruin my whole life.

I look at the clock. Three-forty-one. I think about emailing Petrov to ask what the Week Six palpation looked like, open the laptop, draft the email, read it, and close the laptop. I am not going to email Petrov at three-forty-one on a Monday to ask a question I have already scheduled the answer to. I am going to do the palpation, in this room, at four p.m. tomorrow, with my hands and my focus, the way I have done every palpation in three years.

I say out loud, to nobody, "Okay."

The ice is smooth. Somebody, I think Patel, has left the rink-side light on over the south end, the way they do when they like a fresh sheet and want to look at it for a second before they go home.

I walk to my car.

I do not mind the cold.
