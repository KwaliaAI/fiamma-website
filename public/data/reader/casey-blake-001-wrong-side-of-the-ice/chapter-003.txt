The observation deck at Hartwell's rink is a wooden mezzanine six rows deep, built in the seventies for scouts who have mostly stopped coming, and every afternoon at three-thirty I climb the twelve metal steps up to it and sit with a clipboard and a thermos and watch the men I am going to have to touch later.

It is part of the job.

Okay so. Assessments on the training table are one picture. Useful, limited. The only honest picture is a player moving under his own power on ice, which means I am up here with my red first-aid bag on the bench next to me and my hand around a thermos of Lipton that has gone lukewarm on the walk over from the training room. The bag has a duct-tape patch on the left strap from a repair I did in October and have not replaced, because the repair works and I am not a person who replaces things that work.

It is four degrees colder on the deck than on the rink floor. I do not understand the physics of that. I have stopped trying.

Anyway.

Gabe is doing the third-line drill with Lindqvist and the left wing, whose name is Reyes, and Coach Martinez is running them through a tight cycle down-low along the boards on the right side. The drill is ninety seconds long. Gabe has done it twice and he is in the middle of his third set and I am watching his right shoulder.

The right shoulder is doing too much work.

I write that down. Not in the file. On one of my three-by-five cards, which live in a beaten-up tin box on my lap when I am up here and on the training table when I am down there. Index cards are old-school. I know they are. Every other trainer in the athletic department moved to the iPad app two years ago, and I opened the app once and looked at it and went back to the cards. The cards are how I think. Small clean rectangles, one observation each, laid out on the table like a hand of solitaire when I want to see the picture. I cannot do that on an iPad without being a person I do not want to be.

Card one, three-thirty-four: *right shoulder compensating, left leg crossover delayed approx 0.3s on push-off.*

I watch him push off again. It is not my imagination.

Gabe skates like someone who has learned to protect something. The right shoulder is doing the left leg's job and he probably does not know it yet. I know it. That is my job: to know before he does.

Behind me on the bench a radio crackles. One of the student managers brought a little portable up to the deck for the Hawks game rebroadcast and then left it running, which is a pattern, and I lean over and turn it off without looking. The rink goes back to its three ambient sounds, which are blade on ice, puck on boards, and the low hum of the compressor under the seats.

"He's flying."

Coach Martinez has come up the twelve steps without my noticing. He does that. Rubber soles. He is a quiet person when he is watching something work, and I have heard him come up behind me exactly once in three years.

I say, "He's at seventy."

"At seventy?"

"Seventy-two, call it."

Martinez makes the small mouth-noise he makes when he is filing an answer away without agreeing to it. He is in his forties, a former goaltender who blew out a hip in the AHL and now wears exclusively Hartwell quarter-zips, and he is the best coach I have worked with because he does not pretend he knows what my job is.

He says, "How is the ankle."

I say, "Tracking."

He says, "Thursday?"

I say, "Martinez."

He says, "I'm asking."

I say, "I know you're asking. It's Thursday. Ask me Thursday."

He nods. He watches Gabe come around the net and release the puck to Lindqvist on a seam pass that Reyes does not read in time, and he says, in a voice pitched low enough that saying it aloud to me is more than he wants to, "He's the best forward I've had up here in six years."

I write on card two: *Martinez already counting on him.*

Martinez sees me write it. He says, "I know what you're doing."

I say, "What am I doing."

He says, "Taking a note because I said something out loud."

I say, "Yes."

He says, "That's useful of you."

I say, "Thank you."

He goes back down the twelve steps.

I sit with the two cards on my knees. Twenty of four. Practice has another twenty minutes. The picture my job is building: Gabe at seventy percent and his seventy better than most of the lineup at full, Martinez already decided, the planner ten days out from a conference semifinal, and the conversation I am about to have with any of these men going to be about what I can prove, because nobody on the coaching staff is interested in the soft part.

The soft part is the right shoulder.

I watch him for another eight minutes and write nothing.

I am watching his hands on the stick. The slight grip adjustment mid-crossover when he thinks no one is looking. The way he comes down on the right foot and then instantly redistributes to the left like a man paying off a small loan he does not want to carry. I am also watching the way he laughs when Reyes whiffs on a pass and Lindqvist gives Reyes the glove-tap of professional forgiveness. I am watching him be a player and a person on the ice at the same time, and I am aware, in the part of my brain that keeps time, that I have been watching him be a person for about four of those eight minutes and my clipboard hand has not moved.

Four minutes is approximately three minutes and fifty-two seconds longer than the operational ceiling on observing a player as a person from the rink-side bench. I made the ceiling up. I made it up just now, to feel like a professional with thresholds.

I write, on card three: *four minutes not working.*

Then I tear card three in half and put it in my jacket pocket, because that is not going in the tin box.

At four-oh-two Martinez blows the whistle and the team does its last three down-and-backs. I pack up the cards and the thermos and take the twelve steps back down to the rink floor. The cold on the floor hits me in the face in a different register. Gabe is at the far blue line pulling his helmet off, his hair wet and flat against his skull, and Lindqvist says something to him that makes him laugh, and I hear the laugh from sixty feet away.

I do not look over.

I look at the Zamboni bay. I look at the clock above the Zamboni bay. I look at the door to the hallway.

I am being so normal about this.

I am almost at the door when he calls.

"Nora."

I turn. Professionally. Like a person whose name is Nora and who has been spoken to by name in a workplace.

He has skated the long way around to come off near my door, which is information, and his helmet is in one hand and a water bottle is in the other and he is two feet behind the boards on the players' side, leaning on the half-wall with his forearms.

He says, "Did I pass."

I say, "I'm not telling you that."

He says, "Right."

I say, "I am not a scout."

He says, "You write on cards. You looked like a scout."

I say, "I am a trainer."

He says, "Who looks like a scout."

I say, "Who looks like a trainer with a tin box."

He says, "Okay."

He is grinning while he says okay, the front-tooth tilt visible for two seconds, and I clock the grin and clock that I clocked it and clock the clocking of the clocking. Three layers deep. This is fine.

I say, "How's the ankle."

He says, "Four."

I say, "Four what."

He says, "Four out of ten on the second set. Three by the third."

I say, "That tracks."

He says, "Does it."

I say, "I'm not telling you that either."

He laughs. Small one. The good one.

I turn around and walk to the door without saying goodbye, because the protocol on the rink floor is you do not goodbye an athlete, you just leave, and I leave.

At the door out of the rink and into the hall that leads to the training room, the temperature goes up fifteen degrees and my glasses fog, and I take them off and wipe them on the hem of my fleece and keep walking. The hallway smells like industrial floor cleaner and, faintly, like somebody's leftover sub from the players' lounge. I pass Kowalski coming the other way in a hoodie and shower shoes, holding a protein shake, and he nods at me and says *Voss* like it is both a greeting and a reminder that he is watching me, which it is, and I nod back and say *Kowalski* the same way.

---

At four-twenty my phone buzzes on the training table. Maya.

*coffee at 5? the place with the bad muffins.*

I look at the phone. I look at the training table. Gabe's file is still open on the corner of the table next to the tape bin because I taped him that morning and did not put the file back in the drawer, and the secondary strapping job on the lateral ligament has bothered me for the last ninety minutes in a way I cannot name.

The tape job is fine. I did it. It is fine.

I pick up the phone.

*can't, something came up. tomorrow?*

The reply comes in eleven seconds.

*k. tomorrow.*

I put the phone down on the table, face down. I tell myself, clearly and in the voice of a person who knows what she is doing, that I am staying to re-check the tape job because the observation-deck read on the right shoulder changed my picture of the lateral ligament load distribution, and checking my own tape is due diligence, and due diligence is professional.

It is true that my picture changed.

It is also true that Gabe has already left the building.

I could check the tape job in the morning when he comes in for his four-o'clock, which is twenty-two hours from now, and the difference between checking it tonight and checking it tomorrow is not medically meaningful. There is no intervention I can do in the empty training room that I cannot do in a properly staffed training room at ten a.m.

I am staying anyway.

Real talk: this is what we call a flag.

My phone buzzes. Maya again.

*kowalski just walked past me in the quad with a laminated card.*

I look at the card from Tuesday in the top drawer of my desk. The backup card is on top of it because Kowalski delivered the backup last week and I have not yet found a filing logic that feels right. I close the drawer.

*he has several.*

*i have questions.*

*later.*

*k.*

I put the phone face-down on the file. I sit on the stool next to the training table. The training room is twelve feet by eighteen with a fluorescent strip that is not currently buzzing because the heat has not kicked on yet, and above the strip the paint on the ceiling is the original 1974 institutional off-white, and at four-thirty-one on a Friday afternoon in mid-November the light coming through the small high window over the rink-side wall is already going blue.

I pull Gabe's file toward me.

The second-day tape notes are on top. *Lateral ankle, Grade II residual. Support strapping plus figure-eight reinforcement per previous program's protocol. Tolerated well. Pain scale 4/10 morning, 3/10 post-tape.* I wrote *subjectively improving* under it on Day 2, at the end of a sentence I am now going to have to re-read, because the observation-deck picture does not match *subjectively improving* and I want to see whether I was honest with myself on Tuesday.

I read the line twice. Then I put the file down and walk to the taping table and lift the gauze I laid across the kit to keep the tape rolls clean, and I look at the tape I used that morning on Gabe's ankle. Open roll on the left, pre-wrap on the right. I think: the tension was a hair tight on the anterior strap. Possibly. I cannot tell from memory.

I have his ankle in my head. I held it that morning for sixteen minutes. I could describe the specific give of the ligament on the anterolateral aspect the way another person could describe her own front door.

That is also not the question.

The question is whether I am going to put the file in the drawer and go home.

I do not put the file in the drawer.

I walk down the hall to the empty locker room and sign out a training table from the equipment manager's logbook even though I do not need it for anything, because signing a logbook is the sound professional diligence makes, and I walk back to the training room and sit down with the file.

The fluorescent strip kicks on with the heat at four-forty-seven. The room fills with the low B-flat hum I have been hearing for three years, and I ignore it the way a person ignores the sound of her own breathing.

At five-fifteen I close the file.

The tape job is fine. I knew that. I knew that at four-twenty and at four-forty and at five, and I have spent fifty-five minutes confirming it anyway, and I have missed nothing, which is the part I am going to have to sit with later.

I reopen the file. I read my Day 1 note, which says WATCH THIS ONE in all capitals. I do not update it. I do not cross it out. I wrote that note before I met him, and crossing out a prediction after three days of corroborating one side of it is a different act from predicting correctly, and I am going to keep those acts separate in writing if nowhere else.

I close the file again. I put it in the drawer this time.

I pick up my thermos and the red bag and the tin box of cards and I turn off the strip light. The room goes to blue rink-light through the high window and the cold exhale of the compressor through the thin wall, and I lock the door and go out through the side exit into the parking lot, where the first hard freeze of the year has put a thin clean skin of ice across the puddles and my breath is visible the moment it leaves my mouth.

My car is in the second row. 2011 Subaru. Hartwell athletics sticker on the back window. An empty Gatorade bottle has been rolling on the passenger-side floor for the last four days because picking it up requires bending in a direction I do not do voluntarily between November and March. I sit in it with the key in the ignition for a minute before I turn it on. The heater takes two minutes to do anything useful. I sit in the cold with my gloves still on.

I think about the torn-in-half card in my jacket pocket.

I do not think for long. Thinking for long in a cold car in a rink parking lot on a Friday night is a self-indulgence I do not approve of in other people.

I drive back to the dorm through the quad, which is lit the usual yellow of the mid-century sodium lamps the university has refused to replace on aesthetic grounds. Two freshmen cross in front of me in Hawks hoodies, arguing about a midterm I took three years ago.

---

Maya is in the hallway when I come up the stairs.

The third-floor hallway smells like microwave popcorn and old radiator paint, and Maya is leaning in the doorway of our room in sweatpants and my borrowed wool socks with her hair in a clip doing less work than the clip's designer intended, and she has two mugs in her hands.

Maya says, "Tea."

I say, "Thanks."

She says, "Why are you home at seven."

I say, "I'm home at seven."

She says, "You said something came up."

I say, "It did."

She says, "And now it's uncome up."

I say, "It's resolved."

She says, "What was it."

I take the mug. I hold it in both hands and breathe over it, and I say, "A tape job I wanted to double-check."

She says, "A tape job."

I say, "A specific tape job."

She says, "Whose."

I say, "Lindqvist."

Maya looks at me over the rim of her mug. Maya is a senior nursing major. She is better at reading other people than other people want her to be. She has been my roommate for two years, which is one year longer than I have been entirely truthful with her.

Maya says, "Also a pre-appointment read on Cavanaugh's quad, you said. Earlier today. By text."

I say, "I said what?"

She says, "You said you were going to pull Cavanaugh's quad imaging before the end of the day. At two."

I say, "That too."

She says, "So Lindqvist and Cavanaugh's imaging."

I say, "And the insurance paperwork."

She says, "The insurance paperwork."

I say, "They changed the forms."

She says, "They changed the forms at six p.m. on a Friday."

I say, "They changed them yesterday."

She says, "You just said."

I say, "I stayed to do the tape job and the imaging and the paperwork."

Maya holds up three fingers. She is not angry. Maya does not do angry. Maya does the face a person does when she is working out a logic problem she is going to get right.

She says, "You just said it was four different things."

I say, "It was several things."

She says, "All involving one player."

I say, "Several players."

She says, "The transfer?"

I say, "A player."

She says, "The transfer."

I say, "Maya."

She says, "Name another one."

I drink my tea.

The tea is Earl Grey with too much milk because Maya makes it the way Maya's mother makes it, which is the wrong way and also the right way, and it tastes like my sophomore year and my junior year and like this hallway on a hundred-and-six other nights, and I can feel the small specific weight of the mug in my hand and the larger weight of a small mistake behind my sternum.

I say, "Kowalski."

Maya says, "Kowalski's ankles are fine. I heard you tell Coach Martinez this morning."

I say, "I said they were within normal. That is not fine."

She says, "What's the difference."

I say, "Within normal is an assessment. Fine is a verdict."

She says, "Nora."

I say, "What."

She says, "You told Martinez Kowalski's ankles were fine."

I stand with the tea. I stand there long enough that a girl down the hall comes out of her room in a towel and does not register either of us as she goes into the bathroom, and the door closes, and the radiator clanks once, and the fluorescent strip at the end of the hall does the small nervous flicker it does between nine and ten every night.

I say, "Goodnight, Maya."

She says, "Sleep well."

I go into our room. Maya does not follow for thirty seconds, and when she comes in she does not say anything, which is one of the useful things about Maya: she knows the difference between a conversation that is over and one on pause, and she lets a person pick which one happened.

We watch Chopped for an hour without discussing anything. A contestant makes a scallop sauce the wrong color and Maya says *oh no* out loud, and the episode ends with a pastry chef winning on the strength of a dessert involving marshmallow fluff, and I brush my teeth, and Maya brushes her teeth, and the white-noise machine comes on.

---

I am in bed at eleven-forty. Phone on the nightstand. Maya's old stuffed beaver on the pillow beside my head, which has lived on my side of the room since sophomore year because Maya tired of it and I did not. I am thinking about the observation-deck cards in my tin box, and the torn-in-half card in my jacket pocket, and Gabe's right shoulder, and the lateral ligament that did not need re-checking.

I am also thinking, because I cannot help it, about the way he leaned on the half-wall with his forearms.

I am being so normal about this.

Maya is asleep with her white-noise machine on. The machine has four settings and Maya runs it on *rain*, which is a lie because nothing in rain sounds like that, and I have been falling asleep to synthetic rain for two years.

My phone lights up on the nightstand. I look. I should not. I look.

*gabe c.: thanks for the tape job. ankle behaved.*

I read it twice. It is eleven-forty-one. He has waited until eleven-forty-one on a Friday night to send a one-line work-appropriate thank-you, which is information about him before it is information about the message, and the information is: he is sending it now because he was deciding whether to.

I do not write back.

I turn the phone face-down.

I turn over.

The rink is a mile and a half across campus. When the wind is right, which is twice a week in November on a generous week, I can hear the Zamboni from my window on the far side of the quad, running late at night for the seven a.m. practice. It is not running at eleven-forty-two.

I go to sleep.

I do not go to sleep.

I lie there for forty more minutes and then I go to sleep.
