It is six-forty-two on a Monday morning in the second week of November and I am sitting at the training-room desk looking at a PDF with eight fields and a signature box, and the cursor is in field five blinking at me like it has opinions.

It doesn't.

The PDF is the clearance form. Field one through four: Cahill, Gabriel, player ID, today's date. I have typed all of that. My fingers know the player ID without consulting me, which is its own piece of information about my recent week that I am choosing not to look at directly. Field five: *Assessment summary.* Field six: *Functional capacity (%).* Field seven: *Restrictions, if any.* Field eight: *Trainer signature.*

The training room is fifty-one degrees because the overnight thermostat ran the rink down and the heat will not kick on for another twenty minutes, and the fluorescent strip above the desk is doing its low buzz, the one that means the heat has not arrived yet. I have learned the difference between the two buzzes. I did not sign up for that knowledge. It came in the side window.

Okay so.

I write in the summary box: *Subject presents at 87% functional capacity, lateral ligament medially stable, RTP protocol completed yesterday.* I read it. It is the sentence I would write for any athlete sitting at 87% with this file. I have cleared at 85. Twice. Tanner Griffin last March, Lindqvist in October. I have the notes. The number is fine. The note is fine. The form is fine.

I close the laptop.

I sit with my hand on the lid.

Real talk: the form is not fine. The form is the form and the ankle is the ankle and my read on the ankle is what is not fine, and I cannot run a drill on my own read, which is the thing I have been not-thinking for forty-three minutes.

I open the laptop.

The cursor is still in the summary box. The summary box still has my one sentence in it. I look at the signature line. I look at the signature line for approximately twelve seconds.

I close the laptop.

I have done this three times. The laptop is going to lose patience with me before I lose patience with myself, which is an outcome I did not previously think was possible.

I stand up. I put my jacket on. I pick up the red bag without thinking about it because the red bag goes where I go in this building, and I am leaving the building, and the red bag is therefore irrelevant. The strap is on my shoulder before I have negotiated with myself about it. I leave the laptop on the table with the lid closed and the form open underneath the lid, as if closing the lid is a position I have taken on the issue.

It is not a position. It is a stall.

I am being so normal about this.

---

The coffee shop on the south side of campus opens at seven and at seven-oh-four I am there with a black coffee in a to-go cup that I did not mean to order to go. I meant to sit down with it and think. I said *to go* to the barista on autopilot because that's what I say to a barista on a weekday before a nine o'clock session. I sit down anyway. The cup is in my hand. Steam comes off the lid. Fine.

There is a small plastic pineapple on top of the espresso machine. It was not there last Thursday. There is a price sticker on the bottom of the pineapple that has been partially peeled off, which I can see from here. The coffee has a burnt bottom note. This is a bad coffee shop and I come here because it is closer to the rink than the good one.

I drink half the coffee.

I have cleared at 85. The 85% number sits inside my standard range like a sleeping cat. I am not being asked by the data to do anything I have not done. I am being asked by myself, and myself is being weird about it, and I would normally know how to manage myself being weird about something but the something is a guy and the guy is on Coach Martinez's bus to Albany in five days and I cannot tell whether I am being conservative because the data wants me to be conservative or because I am scared of being inside my own head about him.

*"Still not filed?"*

I look up. Maya is at the register with a to-go cup and a backpack on one shoulder and the grey sweatshirt she wore to bed because Maya owns four items of clothing and the grey sweatshirt is in the on-position this week. I did not hear her come in. She slides into the seat across from me without asking, which is a Maya move, and she has her elbows on the table already, which is also a Maya move.

"How do you know about the form."

"Everyone affiliated with the hockey program knows about the form. Kowalski."

"Of course."

"He said it was a matter of record."

"Maya. It is literally a medical record."

"He said *matter of record* like you'd say *matter of public record.* He was carrying a second laminated card. I don't know what was on it. I didn't ask. I think we have all agreed not to ask about Kowalski's second cards."

I put my head down on the table. The laminate is cool against my forehead. There is a sticky spot to the left of my ear from somebody's syrup. I do not move my head.

"How did Kowalski know it hasn't been filed."

"Kowalski's whole personality is knowing. I don't think you're going to find out how. I think he just knew."

"Gabe."

"What about him."

"Does Gabe know it hasn't been filed."

There's a pause. I lift my head off the table. Maya is turning her cup a quarter-turn on the laminate and not drinking it, and her face is doing the thing it does when she is choosing which true answer to give.

She says, "Not from me. I'd guess not yet. But Kowalski is a variable I can't predict."

"Kowalski is not going to tell Gabe."

"Kowalski is going to tell whoever Kowalski thinks has a right to know. He has not disclosed his ranking system. Coach Martinez probably outranks Gabe on it, which buys you a few hours, because Martinez is at morning skate until nine-thirty."

I pick up the coffee and drink the rest of it in one pull. It is lukewarm now and the burnt bottom note has gotten worse. Maya watches me drink it like a woman watching a video of someone else's mistake.

She says, "Do you want me to tell you what to do."

"No."

"Good. I don't know either."

She gets up. She puts her hand on my shoulder for approximately one second on the way out, which is not a Maya move, and I notice the one second of it, and I notice that I notice. She leaves. I stare at the pineapple for a full minute. I take the cup to the trash.

The pineapple is going to live rent-free in my head all day. I can feel it making itself comfortable.

---

The rink at seven-fifty is empty because morning skate doesn't start until eight-thirty, and the Zamboni had its run at six and is parked at the far end of the house rink with the canvas over it. I go to the storage bin and get the cones. I get the stopwatch out of my desk drawer. I go down to ice level.

I do not need to run him through the cones. I have run him through the cones twice this week. Monday and Wednesday. He clocked 87% against his baseline both times. The data does not have new things to say. I know this. I am going to run the cones anyway.

I am going to run the cones because doing the cones is a thing I understand, and the thing I am supposed to do in the training room is not, and I need to stay inside my competence for one more hour while my hands remember what they know.

I set the cones in the diamond pattern on the near-side blue line. I walk the pattern once with the stopwatch to check spacing. I am in the grey Hartwell hoodie over a thermal shirt, a wool hat pulled down over my ears, the same jeans I put on yesterday, and the rink is fifty-five and the breath-fog off my mouth is picking up the blue-white of the overhead lights and carrying them back, and I am about to do a thing.

Somebody coughs from the Zamboni end.

I turn. Patel is by the boards in a red-and-black plaid jacket zipped to his chin, travel mug in his gloved hand. Patel has been at Hartwell for nineteen years. He has said four sentences to me in three years. He is watching me.

I say, "Hi, Patel."

He says, "You need the Zamboni off the ice."

"No. I'm just running the cones over here."

"You're running a test on an empty ice sheet at seven-fifty-five on a Monday."

"I'm doing a dry rehearsal."

He drinks from the mug. He considers this.

He says, "A dry rehearsal of running cones."

"Yes."

"You don't need cones if there's no skater."

"I'm checking the spacing."

"The spacing is the same as yesterday."

I open my mouth. I close it. Patel is correct, and Patel is not a man it is useful to argue with about what is the same as yesterday. He nods at me the way a man nods when he has said a thing and does not need me to agree. He walks back to the Zamboni.

I run the cones myself. In boots. Boots on rubber matting, not skates on ice, because I am not a skater and the drill is a hands-busy drill and not a benchmark. I run the pattern three times. My times: 14.7, 14.9, 14.7. Gabe on skates ran it in 11.2 post-injury and 10.4 baseline. My run is not a benchmark. My run is a thing I do to keep my hands busy while my head works on something my hands can't reach.

After the third run I bend forward with my hands on my knees at the far cone, and I am breathing harder than the effort earned, and I stand there in the overhead light with my breath showing.

I say, out loud, to the cones, "I can't sign it because I can't tell if I'm being careful or if I'm being a coward about it."

The cones do not respond. I had not expected them to.

Patel's voice comes from the Zamboni end, unhurried, the way a man talks when he has been at the other end of the rink for eighteen minutes and has been listening.

"87?"

I straighten up. I say, "How did you."

"I've watched a lot of players run cones."

I do not say anything for a second.

Patel pulls the canvas off the Zamboni with two smooth movements, climbs up, starts the engine. The engine makes its warming-up rumble, which is the rink's loudest and most companionable sound. He does not look at me again. He puts it in gear and drives off to whatever he is doing on the far side, which appears to be routine maintenance on the brush assembly. He does not need a response from me. He has said his thing.

I stand on the blue line with my stopwatch in my hand and look at the cones.

The fluorescent strip over the Zamboni shed is buzzing in the warm-up register because the heat on that end has kicked on. The one over my training room, across the hallway, is probably buzzing too. I cannot hear it from here.

I pick up the cones.

---

Back in the training room at eight-twenty-five. Cones in the bin. Stopwatch in the drawer. Laptop on the table where I left it, lid closed.

I do not open it.

I pull Kowalski's laminated card out of the drawer. It is under a stack of insurance forms and next to a roll of pre-wrap. I look at the order: Kowalski, Lindqvist, Osei-Bonsu, left wing pair, Gabe-when-cleared. The *Gabe* line is in a slightly thinner marker than the rest of the lineup, and the edge of the laminate has the wear pattern of a card that has been updated at least twice since he gave it to me. Kowalski's card has been evolving under my desk. I did not notice. I put it back in the drawer.

I can hear the far thud of sticks through the wall the training room shares with the bench. The morning skate is still going. Gabe is out there at this minute, skating drills, not knowing the form is not filed. Stride work, edge work, cone drill, shooting. He goes in that order. I know the order without watching him because I have watched him do it enough times to know the order, which is itself a piece of information I will think about later.

The cone drill is the one that matters to me. That's where the lateral push is. The lateral push is where the ankle lives. He did the cone drill yesterday. It was clean.

I open the laptop.

The form is still there. The summary box still has my one sentence in it. I move the cursor to the signature line. I stop. I look at the ceiling for approximately twelve seconds. I close the laptop.

I tell myself I am waiting for a data point from today's skate.

I am not waiting for a data point from today's skate. I have already run the data point myself at seven-fifty-five on an empty rink in boots with a plastic pineapple in my head and a stopwatch in my hand.

I say, into the empty training room, "I'm going to lose my nerve about this."

The fluorescent strip buzzes up half a register. The heat has kicked on. I wait for the buzz to settle into the warm-air register and it does. I sit on the training table with my boots on the floor and my hands on my knees.

---

At ten I run the morning treatment session because the morning treatment session is scheduled and the morning treatment session is not the thing that is hard. Three players. Two of whom are not on my worry list. One of whom has a hip flexor I am monitoring.

The hip-flexor player is Reyes, second-line center, twenty years old, an impossibly cheerful disposition for somebody whose hip has been angry for eight weeks. I tape him. I run him through the mobility sequence.

I say, "The hip is tracking well."

He says, "Yeah?"

"Yeah."

I notice that I can use the phrase cleanly when it is actually true. I notice that I have noticed.

On his way out he says, "Cahill cleared yet?"

I say, "I'm still reviewing."

He says, "Okay." Then, "He skates good."

"He does."

He says, "Kowalski's got a card about him."

"I know about the card."

Reyes grins. The grin is the reason people like him. He says, "It's a good card."

He leaves.

I sit on the training table and look at the laptop for a long thirty seconds. I push myself up. I get lunch.

Lunch is a granola bar out of the drawer. I eat it standing.

---

At two in the afternoon Maya comes to the training room.

Maya has never come to the training room on purpose in three years. She is standing in the hallway with her hand on the doorframe in the grey sweatshirt and the same jeans, holding a paper bag from the good coffee shop on the north side.

She says, "I brought you a real coffee."

I take the cup. I sip it. It is not a burnt-bottom coffee. It is a decent coffee. I say, "Why are you here."

"I'm not here to tell you what to do."

"Okay."

"I'm here because you've been in that room for about six hours and I'm going to be in the library until eleven, and I wanted you to know that if you need somebody to sit with you at ten-thirty tonight while you close the laptop for the seventh time, I'll be at the library."

I look at her.

She says, "That's it. I'm going."

She goes.

I stand there with the coffee in my hand. It is still hot. I sit down on the training table. I drink it slowly. The fluorescent strip has settled into its middle buzz, which is the register it uses during the day.

I open the laptop.

The form is still open. I read my sentence in the summary box. It is still correct. I read the rest of the form. It has nothing new in it. I stop at the signature line.

My finger is on the trackpad. I do not move it.

I close the laptop.

I have now closed the laptop six times. I am going to close it seven, eight, nine, however many it takes, and the fact that I know I am going to close it again is the part that is going to keep me up tonight.

---

At eleven o'clock I am still in the training room and the laptop is still on the table and the form is still open underneath the closed lid. I have opened and closed it three more times since three this afternoon. The fluorescent strip buzzes once, sharply, as the heat kicks on for the overnight cycle. I am already standing. My jacket is zipped. The red bag is on my shoulder. The laptop is under my arm because I am not leaving it open on the training table overnight with the form on it where Kowalski could conceivably walk in and read it, which is not a realistic possibility and which has nonetheless lodged itself in my head as the specific scenario I want to prevent.

I pull the cord on the strip. The light pops and goes off. The training room goes dark except for the hallway light through the open door.

I stand in the dark for four seconds.

I say, to the dark, "Tomorrow."

I leave. I do not know if I mean I am going to sign it tomorrow or I am going to keep not signing it tomorrow.

I go to meet Maya at the library.
