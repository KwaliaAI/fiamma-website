Mercer, New Hampshire is four and a half hours north by team bus and it is the kind of cold that does not care what coat you brought. I get to the visitors' room at two with the kit, the radio, and the red bag on my left shoulder because the right one is doing the clipboard. The hallway behind the Hawks' bench is the hallway-behind-the-bench of a barn that has been hosting hockey since 1984.

The visitors' room is sixty-two degrees, two colder than our home rink and four colder than a locker room should be. I put the red bag on the bench. I run my two-minute inventory. Pre-wrap, Elastikon, scissors, the spare roll of tape wrapped in black electrical so I can find it by feel, two ankle braces, the triangle bandage I carry because a trainer I worked under one summer told me the day you don't carry one is the day someone dislocates a shoulder getting off the bus.

My card box is at the bottom of the bag. I do not take it out. I have known what's on Gabe Cahill's card for eighteen days.

Anyway.

Kowalski comes in at two-twenty with his gear in the same order he always puts his gear in. Skates first, helmet second, pads last. He believes unpacking the skates last is bad luck and he has a notebook page supporting the claim. He sees me. He nods.

He says, "Order today."

I say, "Correct order, Kowalski."

He says, "You said correct."

I say, "I said correct."

He says, "I'm going to need to hear that a different way."

I say, "The order you have laminated on the card."

He nods. He puts the skates down. He sits. He looks at the skates for a long second and then at me, and I know the look, and I do not want the look, and I get the look anyway.

He says, "The thing about the six-one."

I say, "Don't."

He says, "I'm just asking."

I say, "You're not just asking."

He says, "Fine. I am asking whether you have internalised the lesson of the six-one."

I say, "The lesson of the six-one was that you and your laminated card were statistically indefensible and got lucky once."

He says, "That's one reading."

I say, "Give me your left ankle."

He gives me his left ankle. I tape it in the order he wrote on the card in his wallet, the order he has been running since freshman year. The tape goes down clean. I cut it with my teeth one time because the scissors are across the room. Kowalski watches me do it without comment because after seven months he has accepted that I cut tape with my teeth sometimes and the team has not lost for this reason.

Lindqvist comes in. Osei-Bonsu comes in. The left wing pair comes in together, arguing about whether a pre-game meal should or should not include a banana, and Lindqvist weighs in that it should, and the argument lasts through all four tape jobs. I do the tape. I check the tension. I move on.

Gabe comes last.

He comes in last because I told him on the phone last night that I wanted him last. I did not say why. The why is that I do not want to tape his ankle in a room with eleven other men and have any of them see my hand do anything my hand might do, and the why is also that the last tape of any game is the one I do most carefully.

I am being so normal about this.

He sits on the bench. He puts his left foot up on my knee the way he has put it on my knee for eighteen days. The ankle is at 87% and I have signed the form and the form is in Martinez's email and a PDF is in a folder on my laptop labelled CLEARANCES which I have opened three times this week and am not going to think about right now.

He says, "Hi."

I say, "Hi."

I do the tape. I do it the way I have done it eighteen times. The lateral strapping goes down over the anterolateral aspect where the secondary finding was and I lay it with the pressure I have calibrated to the millimetre because this ankle is a thing I know.

I finish. I pat the top of the tape job once, flat of my palm. I say, "You're good."

He says, "I know."

I look up.

He says, "Thank you."

I say, "Get out of here, Cahill."

He does not get out of there immediately. He stands up. He tests the ankle once. He pushes off the front foot with his full weight on the taped side. I watch him do it and I do not flinch because I did my job and the ankle is medially stable. He pushes off again, harder, and nods, and goes.

I sit on the bench for one second after he leaves and look at my hands, which are stupid.

I am, professionally, on the wrong side of this room.

---

Warmup starts at six-forty. I stand at the bench with the radio in my ear and watch the Hawks come out in the black road jerseys. Mercer comes out in home white with the orange shoulder stripe that has looked absurd to me in every season for three years.

Gabe skates. Counter-clockwise first, then clockwise, then the tight figure-eight in his own zone that he has been doing since juniors. The right crossover is half a beat late on the outside-edge push, the same half-beat it was Tuesday. The rest is Gabe.

Kowalski comes past the bench on his second loop and does not look at me, but mouths *correct order* at the ice, which is the closest Kowalski comes to gloating. I do not dignify it.

The puck drops at seven-oh-four.

Mercer scores at seven-eleven.

The arena goes up. Mercer's barn holds more noise per cubic foot than any rink in the conference, which is a thing Martinez warned the freshmen about in pre-game and which the freshmen have now learned. The goal is a wrap-around on a clean back-check miss by our second defensive pair and it is 1-0 before I have finished writing the time on the bench sheet.

I look down the bench. Gabe is on the third line tonight. Martinez moved him there for the game because the third line runs against Mercer's top forwards and Martinez wants honest two-way hockey from him, not a dangler's shift. Gabe is sitting forward on the bench with his elbows on his knees, watching the ice. He is not frustrated. He is working.

The third line goes out at seven-fifteen.

Gabe does not score. He does something better. He makes the right play on the wall at the Mercer blue line, takes the hit cleanly, and chips the puck out to Osei-Bonsu coming down the wing. Osei-Bonsu carries into the Mercer zone. Drops it to Lindqvist. Lindqvist roofs it short side past Mercer's goalie. 1-1. The Hartwell bench loses its mind. Gabe skates back to the bench with his head down, the way a player skates back when he has done his job and is not going to make a thing of it.

I feel my chest do something I am not, professionally, going to examine at a hockey game in New Hampshire.

I write *1-1, 7:15, third-line wall play, Cahill assist (unrecorded)* on a fresh card with my left hand to keep my right hand from doing anything embarrassing.

Second period Mercer goes up again at thirteen-forty. Gabe comes off after a shift and says something to the defenseman next to him. The defenseman nods. The next shift, the defenseman makes the play Gabe told him to make. I write nothing down because my job is bodies on the bench, not hockey, but I watch it anyway.

Between periods I sit in the visitors' room with the kit at my feet and I do not speak. Martinez is doing his thing at the whiteboard. Kowalski is eating a banana with the slow deliberation of a man who has a pre-game system. Gabe comes in third-to-last. Sits across from me. Takes off his helmet. Drinks water from the bottle Kowalski passes him without being asked. He meets my eyes for half a second. He does not smile. I do not smile back. The half-second holds.

I am going to need a wellness check.

Third period, five minutes in, Gabe assists on the goal that ties it at 2-2. Faceoff win in the Mercer zone that he pulls back to the point, where Kowalski, of all people, rips a slap shot through traffic that hits a stick and goes in. Kowalski's first goal of the season. The Hartwell bench comes over the boards. Kowalski skates back with his fist in the air. Gabe catches him at the blue line with a glove to the chest and says something I cannot hear and whatever it is, Kowalski laughs.

The winning goal comes at fourteen-oh-nine of the third.

It is Gabe's goal. I will watch the highlight twice that night and three times the next morning, but in the arena itself I watch it once, in real time, and the thing I notice is that he takes the puck along the half-wall at 87% capacity and makes the Mercer defenseman look 60% because he reads the defenseman's hips and goes around him on the side the defenseman is not protecting, and then he is alone in the slot with the puck on his stick and a goalie who has come out too far, and he lifts it clean over the goalie's left shoulder into the top of the net like a man posting a letter. The arena goes silent for one second. The Hartwell bench comes over the boards. Kowalski is the first one on him. I stand at the bench with the clipboard pressed against my ribs and I do not cheer because that is not my job, but my job and my response have converged and I register that precisely, without noise.

Forty-one seconds later Gabe comes to the bench. The third line is done. He sits down. He looks across at me the way he looked across at me in the intermission. I do not nod. He does not nod. The look holds. He puts his helmet back on. He goes back over the boards because the game is not over, and he is a professional.

The horn goes at zero-zero.

3-2 Hawks.

The visitors' hallway after a semifinal win is a specific kind of loud. I know the noise. Tonight it is whooping from inside the locker room and the bass line of a playlist that came on in the first thirty seconds of the door closing, and over the top is Coach Martinez's voice through the wall at a volume that suggests he is giving a speech he had not planned to give.

I stand with the red bag on my shoulder and the kit at my feet and I let the noise happen without me. My job post-game is cold-tub setup in twenty minutes and a walk-through of the bench for minor complaints a player will not report mid-game but will admit after a win. I have done this after every game for three years. I am doing it now.

Pilar from Mercer comes the other way with her own bag and raises an eyebrow.

Pilar says, "Good game."

I say, "Yours?"

Pilar says, "Three concussions this week. I'm going home and eating a frozen pizza."

I say, "Solidarity."

Pilar goes.

Kowalski comes out of the locker room first, because Kowalski comes out of the locker room first after every win. He has a one-page document about it. He walks directly to me.

He says, "I'd like to register an observation."

I say, "Go ahead."

He says, "The taping order today was correct."

I say, "Yes."

He says, "And we won."

I say, "Yes."

I say, "This is not the six-one."

He says, "No. It is not the six-one. But it is also not a loss, which is the category I am tracking."

He says, "I am going to update the card."

I say, "Update the card how."

He says, "The card will reflect that the correct-order taping has been re-ratified by empirical outcome."

I say, "That's a sentence."

He says, "I have been working on it since the third period."

He leaves. He is going to laminate the update by the time the bus pulls into Hartwell. I am sure of this in the way I am sure of three things on this team.

Lindqvist comes out. Osei-Bonsu comes out. The left wing pair comes out together, still arguing about the banana, which is apparently now a post-game argument as well as a pre-game argument. Martinez comes out and puts a hand on my shoulder once, briefly, and says, "Good work, Voss," and goes down the hall to the bus. He has been my coach for three years. The hand on the shoulder is the most he has ever done. I understand it. It registers.

The hallway empties.

I stand alone with the bag and the kit and the smell of arena concrete. The bus is outside. It is ten-forty at night in Mercer, the drive back is four and a half hours, I will be in my own bed at three in the morning with ice still in my sinuses, and that is what a semifinal Friday is, and I know the shape of it.

The locker-room door opens one more time.

Gabe comes out.

He is in his post-game clothes. Hartwell grey sweatpants. Black Hawks hoodie. A pair of slides with socks because he has given up, since November, on trying to wear actual shoes after a game. His hair is wet. He has his gear bag over his shoulder. I can see from the way he walks that the ankle is fine.

He looks down the hallway.

He sees me.

Okay. Here is the thing.

He has looked for me first. He has not stopped at the trainer's room. He has not stopped to talk to Martinez who is five feet away talking to the assistant coach. He has not gone toward the bus. He has come out of the locker room and looked down the hallway and found me.

I clock it the way I clock everything, which is precisely and without making a single sound about it.

I am going to need to sit down for a year.

He walks over.

He stops about three feet from me. He puts the gear bag on the floor. He does not say anything for a second.

Then he says, "Nora."

I say, "Yeah."

He says, "I was going to say."

I say, "I know."

He says, "You don't know what I was going to say."

I say, "Try me."

He says, "I was going to say you did good work."

I say, "Oh."

He says, "I was going to say the other thing too."

I say, "I know."

He is smiling. It is a small version of the smile I saw on him from the first appointment, the one with the off-center tooth tilted to the left a quarter-millimetre. I am not smiling because I do not smile easily and he has learned that. He knows what my not-smiling means. I have taught him, accidentally, across eighteen days. He nods, small.

I say, "Say it."

He looks at me for one second.

He says, "I am, hypothetically, going to tell you a number of things in a parking lot in New Hampshire that I have been not-saying for eighteen days, and the number is large, and the things are unwise, and we have approximately ninety seconds before Kowalski comes to find out what is taking us so long. So I am going to triage."

I say, "Triage."

He says, "Top of the list."

I say, "Go."

He says, "I came back from juniors to play hockey at Hartwell and I am telling you, honestly, that I have not thought about hockey for eighteen days the way I thought about hockey for the previous eight years, and I have scored four goals in that stretch, which is the best of my career, and the reason is not the ankle and it is not Martinez and it is not the line."

I say, "Gabe."

He says, "Number two on the list. I have walked the route from the dorm to the rink one hundred and four times since November. I have timed it on three of those walks. The variance is forty seconds. I know this because I have been writing it down."

I say, "Why."

He says, "Because if I am exactly nine minutes and exactly the right amount of cleared and exactly polite enough, eventually you are going to look at me the way you are looking at me right now."

I do not say anything.

He says, "Number three."

I say, "Gabe."

He says, "Number three is that I have been, professionally, a model patient for eighteen days and I would like, on the record, to stop being professional now."

Kowalski is going to ask what is taking us so long inside the next twelve seconds, because Kowalski has a sense of these things. The thing I have been not-saying for eighteen days is large, and large is not the Voss register, so I take the large thing and I make it small enough to fit in my mouth.

I say, "Cahill."

He says, "Yeah."

I say, "Get on the bus."

He looks at me.

I say, "Get on the bus and sit in your usual seat in the back and eat whatever pizza they hand you and answer Kowalski when he speaks to you and do not look at me one time on the four-and-a-half-hour ride home."

He says, "Okay."

I say, "Tomorrow morning is optional skate at eleven. You're going to be there at ten-fifty. I'm going to be there at ten-fifty-one. We are not going to skate. We are going to sit in the bleachers in section three, which is the section where the heat doesn't reach, and we are going to be there alone because nobody comes to optional skate at eleven on a Saturday after a semifinal win, and you are going to tell me numbers one through three again with the time you need to say them in."

He is looking at me like I am the entire scoreboard.

He says, "Okay."

I say, "And Gabe."

He says, "Yeah."

I say, "I have, hypothetically, walked the route from the training room to the rink with you eighteen times in eighteen days, which is more times than I have walked that route with any other patient in three years on this job, and the reason is not the ankle."

He says, "I know."

I say, "I am telling you anyway."

He says, "I know."

He says, "Thank you."

I say, "Can I carry the kit."

He says, "I'm carrying the kit."

I say, "You're carrying the kit."

He says, "Hand it over, Voss."

I hand it over.

He says, "And you are going to let me hold the door, which I know you do not like, because the door is the kind of door that needs a shoulder and I have one."

I say, "Move, Cahill."

He moves.

He pushes the double door with his shoulder and holds it for me with the kit in the other hand. I go through with the red bag on my shoulder, and the cold of a Mercer parking lot at ten-forty-five in March hits my face and I walk to the bus.

Kowalski is at the bottom of the steps. He looks at the kit in Gabe's hand and the bag on my shoulder and the distance between us, which is the same distance I have been keeping since I met him and also not the same distance, and which Kowalski has apparently noticed. He does not say anything about it. He taps the side of the bus twice to tell the driver everybody is on.

I climb the steps.

I take my seat in the third row, where I always take my seat. Gabe goes past me down the aisle to the back. I do not watch him go. I put the red bag on the floor between my feet. I take out my phone. I open the file labelled CLEARANCES and look at the PDF of Gabe Cahill's signed form for the fourth time this week, and then I close it because I did not open it to read it, I opened it to confirm it is there.

The bus pulls out at ten-fifty-two.

Around mile forty, Kowalski comes up the aisle with a slice of pizza on a paper plate and hands it to me without comment. He goes back to his seat. The slice has pepperoni and a green pepper and a black olive. I do not like black olives. I eat the slice.

At mile seventy I get a text.

*Ankle feels fine.*

I look at the message for a second. I type back.

*I know.*

Three dots. Then: *Section three. Ten-fifty.*

I type: *Ten-fifty.*

A pause. Then: *Good night, Voss.*

I type: *Good night, Cahill.*

I put the phone face-down on my lap. The radio on the bus is playing something a freshman has put on that I do not know. The engine noise is the engine noise of a team bus on a return trip after a win, which is a specific sound, and I know the shape of it, and for the first time I can remember I do not mind being inside it.

The bus hits Hartwell at three-eleven.

I walk back to the dorm with the red bag on my shoulder and the kit under my arm, across the quad in the March cold. Maya is asleep. The Stanley is on my desk where I left it Friday morning, lid clicked shut.

I set an alarm for ten-thirty.

I sleep.
