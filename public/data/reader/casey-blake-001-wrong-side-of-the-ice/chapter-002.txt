It's seven-fifteen on Thursday morning and I am in the training room twenty minutes before Gabe Cahill's appointment because I wanted to be sitting at the desk with his file open when he walks in, and not standing up with a tape roll in my hand pretending to do a task.

Real talk. I read the file on the walk in. From my phone. While walking. Which is a thing I have told incoming freshmen not to do because there is a curb at the end of the quad path that has eaten three ankles since I took this job, and I did it anyway, so.

Past-me on Monday wrote WATCH THIS ONE on the corner of the intake. Present-me at seven-fifteen Thursday has read the file four times in three days. Four. I am asking myself, in the small clear back-compartment of my own brain, why I am rereading the file of a player I have already assessed.

I have an answer. I do not love the answer.

Anyway.

I close the laptop. I put it on the shelf. I lay out the kit in the order I use for everyone, because the order is the order and I am not changing it for him: goniometer, balance pad, timing gate, three cones, red bag.

The fluorescent strip is doing the thing. The thing where it buzzes because the heat kicked on at seven-oh-four, which I know because I watched the thermostat go over like a small uncool god. The strip buzzed through most of my shift yesterday and at one point I stood with my hands flat on the table and listened to it for the length of one breath because it was making my jaw tight. It is making my jaw tight now. I get up. I tap the plastic housing with the back of my knuckle. The buzz resettles at a slightly different pitch. That is the pitch I get today.

I am being so normal about this.

Gabe Cahill walks in at seven-twenty-eight with a coffee in his hand and the look of a man who did not know whether to bring a coffee to a training-room appointment and had decided yes on the walk over.

He lifts the cup. "Is this okay."

"In here."

"Yeah."

"No."

He sets it on the bench by the door without arguing. "Sorry."

"Sit on the table."

He sits. The table is too short for him by about four inches. His feet are on the floor and his knees are higher than his hips, and he adjusts once because that is the kind of man he is, and then sits still.

I am writing the date in the top-right corner of the session page. I do not look up.

I say, "Functional testing today. Single-leg balance, lateral shuffle, hop and stick. We did ROM and palpation Tuesday. How did the ankle feel after you left."

He says, "Fine."

I do not look up.

Okay so. *Fine* is a word I have heard maybe four thousand times in this room and I have a whole internal taxonomy for it. *Fine* like *I am being polite.* *Fine* like *I genuinely have no notes.* *Fine* like *I have decided already what information you're getting and the meeting starts now.* This was the third one. The shape of a word that had been decided on somewhere between the training room and the parking lot and brought in intact, the way a man brings in a cup of coffee he has been holding since the walk.

I write *subjectively improving* on the page and tell myself I will confirm by the end of the session. I leave the pen on the paper because the sentence is not yet true. I will come back to it.

I say, "Anything during the optional skate yesterday."

He says, "No."

"At what point in the session."

"Which point of what."

"Anything. Stiffness. Catching. Zing. The kind of thing you wouldn't bring up."

He laughs. The laugh is a quick one. The off-center front tooth tilts left when he does it, which I had clocked on Tuesday and have been trying to put on a high shelf in my own head, professionally. I am still doing the date in the corner of the page. I am also watching for it. So I catch the half-second where he is calibrating which laugh to use.

He says, "Nora. I'd bring it up."

He says my name the way he started saying it on Tuesday, one appointment in, which is both too quickly and exactly on schedule for a man who wants rooms to like him.

I say, "That's not what I asked."

"I know."

"What did I ask."

"Whether I'd bring it up."

"I asked whether anything happened. *I'd bring it up* is a forecast, not an answer."

He blinks. Something lands behind his eyes that he does not show me. When he answers it is the first thing he has said in two appointments that I believe completely.

"Felt good for the drill work. Went a little tight on the stair up to the apartment."

"Define tight."

"Not sharp."

"Numerical."

"Four."

"Four up the stairs."

"Yeah."

I write it. *4/10 post-skate, stairs, not sharp.* I do not write the whole interaction. I write what he told me, because that is the job. In the left margin of the page where I log things I am not going to bring up and also am not going to forget, I write *redirect present.*

He says, "What brand of tape you using."

I say, "Don't redirect me."

He says, "Sorry. Was that…"

"It was."

"Okay."

A beat. I put the pen down. I look at him across the two feet of table between us and he looks back without the reflex to look away, which is the thing I clocked Tuesday and am going to have to think about separately from his ankle. On my own time. In a place that is not this room.

Cool. Cool cool cool.

I say, "Up on the balance pad. Left foot first. Eyes open, thirty seconds. Then right. Then eyes closed, both sides, same timing."

He stands up. He is a tall man standing up in a small room, which is a fact of training-room work I have dealt with for three years, and which I am, for reasons, noticing differently today than I was noticing it on Monday. I make a decision not to log that. The decision is itself a log.

(I am paying attention to it. I am a liar.)

Anyway.

The balance is clean on the left. He wobbles twice on the right, once at seventeen and once at twenty-six, and on the second wobble his arms come out and he corrects, which is fine and is also data.

I say, "Eyes closed now. Right foot."

He closes his eyes. He holds for eleven seconds. The right knee starts to tremor at fourteen and he steps off.

"Fourteen."

"I know."

I write it. I run him through the shuffle. I run him through the hop and stick on both sides, three reps per side. Left is crisp. Right lands slightly heavy on rep one, cleaner on rep two, cleanest on rep three, which is a pattern I see in ankles that have taught the body to compensate and have not finished untaught. I had it in my own right ankle from a bad landing off a ropes course at sixteen. I do not tell him that.

I say, "Sit back down."

He sits back down. The fluorescent buzzes over us. He does not comment on it the way most players do in the first week, which is its own data point I am putting in the same drawer as the not-looking-away thing.

I take the left shoe off. Then the right. I put my hands on his right ankle and run through the palpation points I logged Tuesday. Lateral ligament stable to anterior drawer. No joint-line tenderness. Mild residual thickening along the anterior talofibular, which was there Tuesday and is here now, and which I am watching. His ankle is cold from the outside and warm from the inside, which is the ankle of a man who has walked in from November with the circulation of an athlete.

I tape him. I use Cramer because that is what I use on him, and I do it in the order I am going to use on him for the rest of the series, because consistency across sessions is one of the four or five things I actually control in an eleven-day window. He sits still. When I run the tension check at the end I say, "Too tight."

"No."

I believe that one too.

I stand up. I put the tape roll back on the shelf.

"We'll recheck Saturday morning."

"Saturday."

"Three-day."

"Not two."

"No."

He nods. He does not argue. He puts his shoe back on and laces it and does not tie it until he is standing, which is a small thing I notice and do not know why I am noticing.

At the door he pauses.

"What brand was that, though."

"Cramer."

"Huh."

"Go."

He goes.

I stand in the training room with my hand on the table and listen to the buzz for three breaths, and then I open the file and write, under the session note, *subjectively improving, three-day recheck.* I look at the sentence. I underline *subjectively*, because that is the word I chose on purpose and I want it on record as chosen.

I close the file. The buzz keeps going. I do not tap the housing this time.

The door opens, which I expected because the door has been opening without preamble since my first week on this job, and Kowalski's head comes in. Kowalski's head-in-the-door is a function of Kowalski's brain and not of a calendar.

He says, "Thirty seconds of your life."

I say, "I have a ten o'clock."

"Thirty seconds."

"Okay."

"Have we confirmed the Saturday order yet."

"Kowalski, it's Thursday."

"I am aware of the day."

"The order is whatever order you told me last Tuesday."

"That's not a confirmation."

"It is the confirmation you are getting today."

He looks at me for a second in the way he looks at me when he is composing a formal observation. Then he says, "Okay," and shuts the door. I listen to his steps go back down the hallway. He is wearing the turf shoes again, which he is not supposed to be wearing in the building. I write *Kowalski, turf shoes (again)* on a three-by-five card and put it in the card box without comment, because the box is where things go when I am not going to do anything about them yet.

Anyway.

At one-fifteen I take my laptop and my clipboard and the red bag, because the red bag goes where I go, and I walk across to the student union café because the insurance forms need to be done in a place that is not the training room and is not my dorm. The Stanley comes too, obviously. The Stanley is forty-ounce. The Stanley sloshes against my hip every other step, ice clattering like a small tip jar inside a coffin, and I am six minutes from a chair I do not yet know is going to be a problem.

The café has four round tables and six stool seats along the window counter, and it is full, because it is one-fifteen on a Tuesday in November in a town where there is exactly one place to do paperwork with a bagel. The stools are taken. Three of the tables are taken.

I get my coffee and a bagel. I put myself at the one empty round table, which has two chairs, and set up with the laptop facing the window so the screen will not glare.

Gabe Cahill walks in at one-twenty-two.

He sees me. I watch him see me. I watch him evaluate the counter stools, which are occupied by three students on laptops and a woman bottle-feeding a baby in a way that suggests she has been there a while, and then evaluate the rest of the tables, which are occupied. He gets a coffee. He walks across.

"Is this seat taken."

I say, "There's a counter."

"There's also four stools and all four are doing schoolwork on laptops."

"So are you."

"I don't have a laptop."

He takes out his phone.

This man is going to ruin my whole life. Anyway.

I look at him, then at the empty chair, then back at him.

I say, "I'm doing insurance paperwork."

"I won't talk."

He sits. He sits across the table the way a man sits at a table he has been at before, which he has not, because he arrived at Hartwell eleven days ago. He does nothing with his face or with his hands, and he drinks his coffee, and he scrolls his phone.

I do the paperwork.

The insurance portal loads and unloads three times because the student union Wi-Fi is the worst Wi-Fi on campus, and I run my form updates through it anyway because this is where the bagel is. I fill out the weekly-hours log. I run the injury-incidence monthly, which was due yesterday and which I did not submit because Kowalski came in about the laminated card on Tuesday and I lost twenty minutes of my actual life I am never getting back. I close the first browser tab. I open the second. I process a supply reorder.

I am being so normal. I am the most normal person in this café. The babies are eyeing me, like, *that's a normal one.*

Forty-five minutes later I have gotten through the backlog and the bagel, and I look up. He has been sitting there for forty-five minutes without asking for anything. That is also data.

He is reading something on his phone with the focus of someone reading real text, not scrolling. He has a habit, I notice, of running his thumb down the side of the phone rather than up the middle, which is the habit of someone who cracked a screen once and lived with it and now holds phones slightly differently. I notice the habit. I do not know why I am noticing the habit.

(I know why.)

(I am still a liar.)

He feels me look up, and looks up himself, and says nothing. He raises his eyebrows by about two millimetres, the movement of a man asking *yes* in the minimum possible register.

I say, "Nothing."

He goes back to his phone.

A beat goes by. He says, without looking up, "There was a table that opened up at the window, by the way. About fifteen minutes ago."

"I saw it."

"Okay."

He does not move. I do not tell him to. The table at the window is still empty. I watch him not move for another four minutes, and then a woman with a stroller comes in and takes the window table, and the moment to say something about the table has passed.

Real talk. The moment to say something about the table passed sixteen minutes ago. The moment to say something about the table passed when I saw the table open up at the window and did not pick up my laptop. The moment to say something about the table passed inside me before it passed outside me, and I am going to have to think about that, in a place that is not this café, on my own time. On a different day. Possibly never.

I close the laptop.

I say, "I'm going back."

"Okay."

I stand up. He stands up, because he is the kind of man who stands up when a woman at his table stands up, and I almost tell him to sit back down because the standing-up is another piece of information and I have already noticed too much in one Thursday.

He says, "Saturday."

"Saturday."

"Three-day."

"Yes."

He sits back down. He does not pick his phone up. He is going to sit there for a while longer, I am pretty sure, and I am not going to turn around in the doorway to check whether he is.

I do not turn around.

Outside the union the wind comes down through the quad out of the north, and I pull my jacket collar up and cross the green toward the rink with the red bag against my hip and the laptop under the other arm and the Stanley swinging on its strap. My breath is visible for the first time this year. I had forgotten how fast November goes from cold to cold. The grass is crunchy. The ice on the puddle at the crosswalk has a pattern like a spiderweb, and I step over it, because at twenty-three in upstate New York you have learned to step over standing water in November or you have learned to limp for six weeks.

I check my laces at the crosswalk.

Right one is a square knot.

What.

I look down at my right shoe and the lace is in a square knot, like a child tied it in a hurry, like a person tied it in the dark, like a person had the lace in her hand at six-forty-five in the morning while she was thinking about a file she had read four times and looped it once and then forgot to loop it again and tightened the wrong end. I do not remember tying it. I do not remember when I tied it. I had a whole assessment with Gabe Cahill on this morning's shoelace and he did not say anything about it, which means either he did not see it, which is unlike him, or he saw it and decided not to say it, which is much more like him, which is.

Cool. Cool cool cool.

I stop in the middle of the green. I kneel on one knee. I redo it in a bow, without taking my gloves off, because taking gloves off in wind like this is a small mistake. I stand up. I keep walking.

In the training room it is fifty-five and ice-smell and the strip light is not buzzing because the heat has cycled off. I sit at the desk. I open the laptop. I pull Gabe Cahill's file.

I read the top-line notes from Tuesday. *Grade II lateral, week 8, cooperative, specific. Pain scale reported 6/10 on session initial. Subjective report consistent with active recovery.* I wrote WATCH THIS ONE in the left margin on Monday morning, before I had met him, in capital letters with a little box around it, because I have written WATCH THIS ONE in capital letters with a little box around it on the margin of every transfer file since I took this job, and it has been right often enough that I have not stopped doing it.

I look at the note. I put my cursor next to it. I think about changing it.

I do not change it. I close the laptop.

The strip light kicks back on when the thermostat cycles, and I hear the hum come up and plateau at the pitch it found this morning. Kowalski has a two-thirty.

I get up.
