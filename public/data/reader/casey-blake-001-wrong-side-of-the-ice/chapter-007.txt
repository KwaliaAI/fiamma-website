I open the training room at six-forty on game-day morning because I have not slept and I want the strip light to be my problem and not the rink's.

The strip comes on with the usual two-second hesitation. Hi, strip. I drop my red bag on the bench by the door and flip Gabe's file to the top page and reread the note I wrote at six p.m. last night. *Return Ch7 for reassessment, lateral ligament secondary.* My handwriting. My timing. My reason. I know the reason. There is nothing to do about the reason except the reassessment, which is the actual point of being in a training room and not, for example, lying on my dorm-issue twin bed at six-forty staring at the popcorn ceiling like a person.

Okay so. Reassessment.

I pull the three-by-five box from the desk and find Gabe's card. Under the last entry I write *reassessment scheduled 7:30 AM day of game 3, lateral anterolateral aspect,* and I put the card back. The strip is buzzing in that low register that means the heat is working. The heat is working. This man is going to ruin my whole life.

I look at the clock. Forty-one minutes.

I drink the water in my Stanley which is the temperature of last night, which is to say cold enough to feel like a small personal punishment, which is the only kind of punishment available in this training room at six-forty in the morning. I take what I have.

Gabe comes in at seven twenty-nine.

He has a coffee and the kind of pre-game quiet the whole lineup carries on home-game days. He sets the coffee on the counter and sits on the table without being told.

He says, "You wanted me early."

I say, "I did."

He says, "Are we breaking up or is this about an ankle."

I do not laugh. I do not. It is a small discipline because it is mildly funny and also because if I laugh at seven twenty-nine on a game-day morning in a training room with the man I am professionally not-noticing, the project is over. Discipline holds.

I say, "Ankle. Sit back."

He sits back. I roll the sleeve of his track pants up and put my hands on the lateral side of his right ankle. The skin is the temperature of someone who has already been on a bike for twenty minutes, which means he did his pre-morning activation like I asked.

I say, "Any different since yesterday."

He says, "Define different."

"Can you answer that without a follow-up question."

"Can I answer that with a follow-up question."

"No."

"Then no."

I let him have the smile, because I have to work my thumbs into the groove below the lateral malleolus and I do not need him tensing against my hand while I do it. The smile is a logistical concession. That is what I am calling it. The smile is logistics.

The thing I am looking for is a pea-sized area of residual swelling on the anterolateral aspect, roughly one fingertip down from the edge of the fibular tip. I press two degrees of pressure into it and watch his face.

The face does a thing.

A small tightening under the jaw I have been trained for three years to clock. He is not as good at hiding it as he is at the other parts of the hiding, which is a separate problem and an interesting one and not for now.

I say, "Point of six."

He says, "Four."

I say, "Point of six."

He is quiet for a breath. He says, "Five."

I write *5/10 anterolateral palpation* in the file and keep my hand where it is. I press again, a quarter-inch up.

He says, "Three."

"Up from the last point."

"Yeah."

"And when you pushed off to get here this morning. Which direction was the discomfort."

"Lateral."

"Not posterior."

"Lateral."

I sit back on my stool. The anterolateral finding is there under my hand the way I have suspected it is there under my hand for the last sixteen hours, and now it has a number, and the number is the problem.

I say, "Gabe."

He says, "Yeah."

"Your risk picture just got narrower."

He does not say anything for a second. He looks at my hand on his ankle, which is still on his ankle because I have not moved it, and then he looks at my face. The easy-going thing he has been carrying for six weeks steps back about two feet. What is left is a twenty-two-year-old from a D-I program who just heard the word *narrower* from the person who signs the form.

He says, "How bad."

"Not bad. Narrower than yesterday's picture."

"Can I play Thursday."

"I don't know yet."

"Okay."

That is it.

He does not redirect. He does not ask about the tape brand. He does not tell a story about a teammate who played through worse. He says *okay,* and he says it the way you say it when you mean *I heard you.* I have been waiting for *okay* since the first appointment and I had imagined it as a defeat of some kind, and it turns out *okay* is just a word, and I do not know what to do with it now that it is sitting between us on the table.

I take my hand off his ankle.

I say, "I'm going to retape for tonight. The taping can address the finding. The clearance for Thursday is a separate conversation, and I will have it with you when I have the data. I'm going to watch you tonight."

"Okay."

"I need you to tell me if it gets above a six in the first period."

He is looking at me. He says, "Okay."

"Verbally. Not nod-at-me okay."

"I'll tell you. Verbally. Not nodding."

I start the tape.

My hands do the tape the way my hands have done tape for three years. Underwrap. First anchor strip below the malleolus. Figure-eight pulling from medial to lateral. Stirrup. Heel-lock. I do not look up while I do it. I am aware he is looking at my hands. I am aware that I am aware. I am aware that I am aware that I am aware. We are at three levels of aware and counting, which is also not for now.

When I am done I say, "Off. Walk to the door. Come back."

He walks to the door and comes back. His gait is clean. The tape is doing what tape does, which is telling a Grade II lateral sprain on its way back to full ligament integrity *you are not doing the work alone today.*

He sits back on the table. He says, "Nora."

"Mm."

"Did you find this yesterday."

I stop writing.

"Did I find this yesterday."

"The secondary finding. Did you find it yesterday."

There is no point.

I say, "I should have."

"Because I said fine in week two."

"Because I rushed the assessment on Tuesday."

"Why did you rush it."

"Gabe."

"I'm asking."

I look at him for a full second. I say, "You know why."

He looks back at me for a full second. He says, "Yeah. I do."

Cool. Cool cool cool. Excellent. Just two professionals doing their jobs in a training room at seven thirty-seven in the morning, both of whom just said the quiet part to each other out loud, in even tones, like a couple of adults. We are adults. We are doing adult things. The strip light is buzzing.

I say, "We're both going to do our jobs tonight. You're going to play the game you can play at eighty-five percent. I'm going to be on the bench. We will have the conversation about Thursday tomorrow morning and not before."

"Okay."

"And you're going to say verbal numbers when it hurts."

"Verbal numbers."

He gets off the table. He picks up the coffee off the counter. At the door he stops and says, "Thanks for catching it."

He is gone before I can figure out what to say back.

I sit with the file in my lap. The strip buzzes. I have the specific and useless thought that in three years of clearing athletes I have never been thanked for finding something, because finding something is the part of the job that makes the athletes hate you. He was not supposed to thank me. He thanked me anyway, and I do not know what to do with that either.

I close the file and go find Kowalski for his pre-game wrap.

---

The locker room at six p.m. is doing what locker rooms do before a home game, which is everyone pretending they are not pretending to be calm.

I am not in the locker room. I am in the training room. The training room is adjacent to the locker room and the door is open and I can hear the entire pre-game ritual of twenty hockey players bickering about Spotify queue rights, which is its own small civilization with its own laws.

I tape Osei-Bonsu first.

I tape him first on purpose. I have thought about it all afternoon, between the reassessment and now, and I have decided that a person who has been a certified athletic trainer for three years and who knows the statistical indefensibility of a superstition about tape order is obligated, at some level, to disprove the superstition under conditions where nothing material rides on the disproof. The Hawks are playing a mid-table conference team they are objectively going to beat regardless of the order in which their ankles are wrapped. The priors are clear. The test is ethical.

I am being so normal about this.

I tape the left-wing pair next, together on adjacent tables, because I am saving time. Then Lindqvist. Then the rest of the lineup, working through the roster in the order their appointments were scheduled. Kowalski goes tenth.

Kowalski sits on the table. He does not say anything. I wrap his left ankle with the same clean figure-eight I have wrapped it with for three years, and the silence above the table is a specific silence, the silence of a man keeping track of what has been done and in what order and for what reason.

When I am finished he says, "Nora."

"Kowalski."

"I was tenth."

"You were."

"Do you want to tell me what I was tenth in relation to."

"It's the roster order for the day."

He stands up. He puts his weight on both feet, tests the tape with a shift, nods once.

He says, "We will see what happens."

I say, "Kowalski."

He says, "That is all I am saying."

He goes out to the tunnel. He walks the way a man walks when he is choosing, out of politeness, not to say any of the other things he could say.

I stand with the tape roll in my hand and the strip light buzzing over my head and I think, for a second, about whether I just did something stupid. Then I decide I did something rigorous. Rigor and stupidity feel, at six forty-three p.m. on a Saturday in November, approximately the same.

I go to the rinkside bench with the red bag and the clipboard.

---

The Hawks win six to one.

Lindqvist scores in the first. Osei-Bonsu scores twice, both in the second, one of them on a breakaway that the visiting goalie has the wrong read on from the moment the pass leaves the boards. Gabe has a goal and two assists, and he does not go above a six in anything I can see from the bench, and he gives me the verbal six once, on a change in the middle of the second, by skating to the bench and saying, "Six and holding," and skating back. He has another shift after that. The six goes to a five by the third.

The lone goal against comes from a Hartwell turnover in the slot that our goalie has no chance on. It is unrelated to any ankle on the roster. I write that down because I am going to need it later, possibly in court.

At the final buzzer I am aware of the score before I am aware of anything else. I carry the score through the handshake line and down the tunnel with the red bag over my shoulder, already doing the math, and the math is that I taped in the wrong order and the Hawks won by five, and that a five-goal margin is either a coincidence of the specific sort that statistics permits or it is the universe conspiring to cost me approximately twelve hours of my week.

I get to the taping room at nine-twelve and turn on the strip light and sit down at the desk.

At nine-fourteen the door opens.

---

Kowalski comes in first.

He is wearing his game-worn base layer and his showerless post-game expression, which is the expression of a man who has not yet forgiven the world for something the world did on purpose.

He says, "Nora."

"Kowalski."

He places a laminated card on the desk. It is the card. It is the card laminated in 2023 by Coach Brennan. It has the order on it and the alternate order on it and the footnote about the 2022 incident.

He says, "I'd like to lodge a formal protest."

"With who."

"You."

"I'm the person you're protesting."

"That's correct."

Lindqvist comes in behind him. He is trying not to smile. He is not succeeding. He says, "I'd also like to lodge one."

Osei-Bonsu says, from the door, "Three."

The left-wing pair are behind Osei-Bonsu. Both of them raise a hand without saying anything, which is the lineup's standard silent-protest gesture, and which means two more.

I say, "How many of you are out there."

Kowalski says, "Twelve."

"Twelve."

"The starting lineup plus the four who were tapped for the bench. All twelve."

"The four on the bench weren't even taped."

"They were affected by the outcome."

Lindqvist, who is a man of specific pleasures, says quietly, "He has a list."

I say, "Kowalski, do you have a list."

Kowalski opens the front of his base layer, removes from an inside pocket a second index card, which is mine, and lays it next to the laminated card.

He says, "These are the twelve affected parties and their specific grievances."

Lindqvist covers his mouth.

I say, "You wrote grievances."

"One per player. Two sentences each."

"In the time between the final buzzer and now."

"The grievances were drafted before the game."

I put my face in my hands for three seconds. The strip light buzzes. The buzzing is the only sound in the building. The buzzing is on my side. The buzzing has been on my side since week three.

I say, into my hands, "You pre-drafted grievances."

Kowalski says, "I anticipated the outcome."

"The outcome was a win by five."

"The outcome was a deviation from the established protocol and the grievance stands regardless of result."

"Kowalski."

"That is my position."

I look up. The entire visible roster from the doorway to the equipment bench is looking at me with twelve different flavors of ceremonial wound. Osei-Bonsu is standing with one hand on his chest like a man at a national anthem. Lindqvist has given up and is laughing openly. The left-wing pair has their arms crossed.

Gabe is not in the room.

I know where he is. He is last because he is saving it. I do not need to see him to know.

I say, "The order was the scheduled roster order for the day. I was running late on three appointments. The order you value is not operationally feasible every game night. We won six to one."

Kowalski says, "Six to one is not the relevant datum."

"Six to one is the score."

"The datum is the deviation. The score is a downstream outcome."

"Kowalski, we beat them by five."

"We could have beaten them by seven."

Lindqvist makes a sound that is not a laugh but is adjacent to one.

I say, "There is no version of reality in which you are beating that team by seven."

Osei-Bonsu, from the doorway, says, "We could have been up four after one."

"Osei."

"I'm just noting."

Kowalski says, "Nora."

"Kowalski."

"Take the card."

I take the card.

There are twelve entries. Each one has a name and two sentences under it. My own three-by-five handwriting is recognizable on the paper. He has filled out my cards in his own hand, in the same style, because of course he has.

I say, "You used my cards."

"They're the correct medium."

"Kowalski."

"I'll see you for the formal apology tour tomorrow."

"The formal."

"Apology tour. In order. Starting with me." He pauses in the doorway. "Defenseman, laminated log, pre-drafted grievance. I hold the most data. I receive the apology first."

"Kowalski."

"Eight a.m."

He goes. Lindqvist follows, patting the doorframe as he leaves. Osei-Bonsu gives me a bow that is probably not strictly necessary. The left-wing pair leaves together, still not speaking. The four bench players trail out behind them, each one achieving a small look of mild grief as they pass.

I am, professionally, the defendant in a tort filed by twelve athletes and one laminating machine.

Anyway.

---

Gabe comes in last.

He has showered. His hair is wet at the edges. He sits on the table, which is my space, which he has been sitting on for six weeks now, and he looks at me with the specific pleasure of a man watching a thing he predicted come true.

He says, "Six to one."

"It was a coincidence."

"Twelve people would like a word."

"The score is not the methodology."

"Tell them that."

"I already did."

"Tell them again in the morning."

"Gabe."

"I am the twelfth person, and I'm going to want mine in complete sentences."

"You're not."

"I am. Kowalski said I get mine last because I hold the least data."

"That's not a promotion."

"It is, in his system."

"You're enjoying this."

He says, "I am enjoying this a lot. You owe twelve apologies. You owe me the most complicated one, because I am last, and because the rest of them are being organized by a man with a laminated card. Your card is going to be long."

I look at him for a long moment. I say, "Gabe."

He says, "Yeah."

"How's the ankle."

He says, "Four. Holding."

"Good."

"Nora."

"Gabe."

"Eight a.m. Kowalski. Then the rest of them in order. Then me."

"I know the order."

"I'm reinforcing the order."

I pick up my three-by-five card box and put Kowalski's list inside it. I put Gabe's card on top and write *Game 3, 6 to 1. Four holding post-game. Reassessment 11/16 for Thursday clearance.* I put the card back.

Gabe gets off the table. He goes to the door. He stops.

He says, "I'm glad you caught it this morning."

He leaves.

I sit in the taping room with the strip light buzzing and twelve apologies on the desk and the laminated card next to the list and the score on the board down the hall. I have not slept the night before. I am not going to sleep tonight either.

I turn off the strip.

The room goes dark except for the rink light through the small high window.
