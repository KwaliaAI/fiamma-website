Ninety-one degrees inside the apartment, no AC, no elevator, and I am making a salad, because cooking anything with a flame tonight would be a war crime.

The building's air conditioning died at two this afternoon with a long mechanical sigh, which, mood. The elevator has been out so long I now think of the stairs as the elevator, so the AC was the last appliance in this building with a work ethic, and its passing leaves me alone in a kitchen one degree short of legally a sauna, slicing cucumber like it owes me money. My Stanley sweats on the counter beside the cutting board, full of what was ice at six o'clock and is now spa water for someone I hate. I drink it anyway. Hydration does not care about your feelings.

Mateo is on the other side of the counter, on the phone with his producer, in Spanish, about the Round of 32 assignment that got firmed up this afternoon. If they advance, Univisión puts him in the booth for the knockouts, so his job and my job are now two trains on the same track, and everyone in this kitchen has seen the schedule. When he talks to his producer his voice drops half a register and speeds up and gets very certain, and he paces exactly three steps each way like the kitchen has a touchline, and it is ninety-one degrees, so the tank top, so the shoulders, sweat at his temples, and every time he hits the turn on that pivot his deltoids do something I have decided not to describe, for my own protection.

I put the knife down. For safety reasons. Mine, his, the cucumber's.

He opens the fridge one-handed without breaking stride, takes out a Coca-Cola Light, and holds the cold bottle against the side of his neck for a second before he opens it, still talking, "sí, sí, dile que sí," and the condensation runs down into the collar of the tank, and I pick the knife back up because the cucumber is not going to dice itself and I need my hands doing something that is not thinking about his neck.

He says something fast that ends in "el sábado" and laughs at whatever comes back, and the laugh crosses the counter and goes up both my arms, and I pick my phone up off the cutting board, because I am a woman with a support system.

> **Tess:** it is 91 degrees in this apartment and the man is on the phone in spanish looking like that
> **Maeve:** looking like what
> **Tess:** exactly

I put the phone face-down. Maeve is useless. Maeve is in Boston, where it is seventy-two degrees and the air conditioning works and her roommate is a cat.

Here is the thing about the last three days, which is that they have technically contained zero events. He put his hand on my wrist in the press box on Saturday, four seconds, maybe five, and neither of us has said one syllable about it since, and we have shared this apartment the whole time like two people in a very polite play about roommates. We watched somebody else's group on the couch with a professional amount of cushion between us. He wrestled the machine into two actual espressos, machine willing, which it was, both times, smug about it. He runs at seven every morning and comes back up the five flights at eight barely breathing, and I have watched the return leg from the rooftop twice with my iced oat-milk coffee like a person conducting a study, and we do not discuss that either. And my press pass is on the counter by the fruit bowl right now, lanyard coiled, dead in his sightline, the espresso machine dark next to the stove, and the wrist thing sitting in the middle of all of it, unfiled. Anyway. I make salads now.

He hangs up. "Bueno, sí. Ahí nos vemos." His phone goes face-down on the counter, mirror of mine, and he rolls his neck and exhales like the call weighed something, and I should leave it alone. I have an entire career built on knowing that a man will be honest if you just stay quiet.

I do not stay quiet.

"Is your cousin on the bench for the Round of 16 if they advance?"

I say it light. I say it in the exact voice I use for is there more lime, and I keep my eyes on the salad while I do it, which is worse, and I know it's worse while it's happening, and I do it anyway.

The kitchen changes temperature without changing temperature.

"Brennan."

"I'm just asking as a person in this kitchen."

"No you're not."

Here is what ten days of sharing a counter with someone buys you: he knows the difference between my voices now. He can hear a question putting on civilian clothes. There was a version of tonight where that would have felt like being known, and instead it feels like being scouted, and the difference between those two things is the entire fight we are about to have.

He's right, and we both know he's right, and I double down regardless, because dropping things has never once been in my skill set. "It's a roster question. ESPN dot com publishes four hundred of those a day."

"You are not ESPN dot com right now. You are a person in my kitchen at eight thirty, asking about my family in your casual voice."

"My casual voice is just my voice."

"Your casual voice is a press conference." He comes around the end of the counter, not at me, at the argument, and his hands have started going, the broadcaster hands, the ones that talk when he talks, carving the air into segments like he's walking a viewer through a back line. "Three days you don't ask about Iván. The minute the knockout assignment is real, you ask. In this kitchen. While I am on the phone."

"You were off the phone."

"Eleven seconds."

"You counted?"

"You waited. Same thing."

"Because it's real now!" I hear my own volume and bring it down a notch, and it costs me. "That's when questions happen, Mateo, that's what a question is. If they advance, your network puts you in a booth and my desk puts me on the deep story, and the deep story has your cousin's name in it no matter who writes it. I'd rather it have mine on top."

"There it is."

"There what is."

He points, not at me, at the counter, at the coiled lanyard by the fruit bowl. "The press pass is the press pass. It does not come off because the kitchen is hot."

He doesn't yell it, either. He lays it down like a card he has been holding for ten days, and it lands flat and quiet and right on the bone, fuck, because I have been telling myself a story where this apartment is a magic circle with the tournament outside it, and he has clearly been awake for every page of that story, taking notes.

So I fight fair, which is my whole problem as a person. Lying would be kinder on everyone.

"I didn't write it Saturday."

"You will."

"I might."

The might does it. The might is honest, and honest is worse than yes; you can actually watch it be worse, it moves through his face like weather. He turns away one step, turns back, pushes a hand through his hair, and when he speaks again the accent has thickened the way it does when he stops budgeting for English.

"I might," he says, tasting it. "And I live with that. I drink coffee across this counter from I might."

"You want a reporter who lies to you better, there are four hundred of us at this tournament."

"I want one who is not in my kitchen."

Okay. That one goes in somewhere around the sternum and stays, because it's his kitchen now, is it, ten days and one espresso machine and the man has annexed the kitchen, and the worst part, the genuinely insulting part, is that some traitor committee in my chest has been calling it his kitchen since Saturday too.

"Producer's fault," I say. "Take it up with Bristol."

It's a good line and it saves nothing. He makes a sound that has never once been a laugh, and I go up on the big gesture, the whole-arm Brennan special, the one that means I am being reasonable and the world is not.

My hand catches the espresso machine's handle.

The machine kicks on.

It does the sound. The sick whale. The full, sustained, forty-fathom moan of a humpback with a sinus infection, at eight forty p.m., directly into the middle of our fight, and it does not stop. It builds, actually, finds a second register, and commits, steam light blinking, like it has been sitting on the counter for ten days waiting for its moment and the moment is now.

We both stop. We both look at it.

The machine wails on between us, doing its haunted-sea-mammal thing into a silence we have both completely abandoned, and Mateo stares at it the way you stare at a teammate who has just passed you the ball in your own box, and the laugh gets out of him before he can stop it, short and dry and half-bitter, the kind that costs him something. He puts one hand flat on the counter and drops his head, and that's the whole laugh, three beats, gone.

Mine takes three beats longer to arrive and then will not leave. I laugh until I'm holding the counter at my own end, the machine howling away between us like the world's worst couples counselor, and I have to wipe my eyes with the back of my wrist.

"Real talk: the machine has feelings. It is in this fight."

He reaches over and shuts it off mid-moan. The silence afterward is enormous. He looks at me across the dead machine, and the anger is still in the room but it has lost its seat, and he drags a hand down his face.

"Rooftop."

"Yeah."

He takes the mezcal down from the cabinet above the fridge, the Casamigos joven he swears by, hooks two glasses in one hand, and I grab my Stanley out of spite, and we climb four flights through stairwell air that has been marinating since June.

The rooftop hits like a swimming pool. Eighty-five, maybe, and moving. I could weep.

The cicadas are in full legislative session. The city runs out in lights in every direction, and away to the east the Estadio Akron is lit up like a docked spaceship for a match that has nothing to do with either of us, which feels personally rude, a whole goddamn stadium glowing at me on my night off.

He pours. He hands me a glass with a stern look attached, and we stand at the railing, because sitting is a later decision, and for a minute nobody says anything and the cicadas hold the floor. The first sip goes down my throat like a lit match, and I keep my face neutral about it, and he watches me keep my face neutral about it, and neither of us comments, which is the closest thing to a ceasefire either of us has offered all night.

"My cousin," he says, mostly to the city. "What do you actually know."

"Twenty-two. Third on the depth chart. Pachuca on the club line and a federation bio about eleven words long. That's what's public, and that's my entire file, which you would know if you'd asked me instead of building a case."

"He is one bad sentence from Pachuca. One. And the sentences are yours."

He says it without heat, which is how I know it's the true center of the thing. Everything in the kitchen was the outer ring. This is the bullseye, up here over the city with a glass in his hand.

"Then it matters who writes them. You want it to be someone who watched him hold a clean sheet, or someone who Googled him on deadline?"

He looks at me for a long second, and I watch the point land, and I watch him decline to hand it to me, which is fair. I wouldn't hand it to me either.

"On the team sheet he is Iván Reyes," he says finally. "At my aunt's table he is Iván Salas Reyes, and the table is longer than the team sheet. You understand? My aunt reads every column twice. Once for him, once for the knives."

"Your aunt."

"Mariana. His mother. My mother's sister." He turns the glass in his hand, and I notice the commentary cadence is gone out of his voice, the segment-by-segment shape of it, and what's left is slower and lower and just his. "She prints them, Tess. The columns. There is a drawer in her house with your colleagues in it, in plastic sleeves."

"That's," I start, and quit, because there is no end to that sentence that helps anybody.

"And the family chat does not sleep during a tournament. Forty-one people. My tía is, ¿cómo se dice?, bien metiche."

"Metiche?"

"Nosy. In everything. With love, but in everything. If you publish a sentence about Iván, she has it before your editor does."

"Metiche," I try. It comes out flat and Bostonian.

"Me-ti-che." He winces like I stepped on his foot. "Closer. Don't frame it."

So that's his side, and his side holds all the way down: a mother with plastic sleeves, a kid one column from the door, a chat with forty-one readers who all share his table or his last name. He drinks and watches the Akron, and his guard is down further than I have ever seen it, down past the broadcast voice, all the way to whatever it costs a man who retired at twenty-six to ask anybody for anything. Including this. Including don't.

Okay so. My half. I could let the cicadas have it, and he gave me his, so I don't.

"Dani Park asks me once a day for the cousin piece. Once a day, Salas. I am running out of ways to say not yet."

"Say it slower."

"I'm serious," I say, but a laugh gets out anyway, one ugly little chuff of it, and he watches it escape with visible satisfaction, and I make myself keep going, because this next part isn't funny. "I left a sure thing at the desk to come do this tournament. Dani went to the wall in three separate meetings to get me this credential, and there are people in Bristol waiting for me to drop it so they can catch it. The press pass is on the counter because that is what I came here as. At some point, maybe next week, maybe the final, I am going to file something with Iván's name in it, and I'm telling you that here, out loud, sober adjacent, instead of letting you find out from the site."

Saying it costs more than I budgeted for. My voice stays level the whole way through, which is a professional skill, and underneath the level voice my pulse is doing something undignified, because it is one thing to know a hard fact about yourself and another to hand it to the man across the railing and watch him hold it.

He's quiet. Down on the avenue a bus exhales at its stop, and somebody's radio crosses the street and dissolves.

"That's it," I say. "That's my whole side."

"It's a real side." He says it like it's being taxed. "I hate that it's real."

"Yours too."

"I know mine is real. Mine has my aunt in it."

And there it sits: nowhere. Resolved exactly nothing. Both of us right, both of us going back down those stairs eventually to the same too-small apartment for the rest of a World Cup. And the deranged part is that my chest is lighter than it has been since Saturday, because apparently I would rather hold a true stalemate than a fake peace, which explains every relationship I have ever been in, but we do not have time to unpack that tonight.

I take a sip of the mezcal. A small one. A penitent one.

"If you sip it like that again I am taking the glass back. This is Casamigos, not medicine."

"In my country we earn our mezcal through suffering."

"In mine we just drink it."

"Teach me to say that. The drinking-it way."

He holds up one finger, professor of exactly this. "First the drinking."

So I drink it. It tastes like a campfire, but a campfire that has decided to respect me, and it goes down with a long agave burn that unbolts something at the hinge of my jaw, and he watches the entire operation and nods once, a coach whose project is coming along.

We end up on the chaise, the double-wide one whose cushions have survived three summers of other people's vacations. Him at one end. Me at the other. Four feet of cushion in between that neither of us put there on purpose, which is exactly why neither of us is going to move it.

I rig the speaker, which is to say I stand my phone upright in my empty glass, screen out, and Phoebe Bridgers comes up out of it, small and silvery.

"This is the sad one," he says.

"It's Phoebe. Show some respect."

"I have respect. We survive a fight, the machine sings to us, and now the saddest woman in America." He tips his glass at the phone. "It's a choice."

"Nobody won that fight, by the way."

"The machine won the fight."

He's not wrong. I put my head back against the cushion. My shoulders, which have spent ten days announcing their sunburn every time fabric exists, have finally gone quiet in the night air, cooling, just skin again, and from my end of the chaise the line of his shoulders against the city lights is frankly a lot, an unreasonable amount, for a Tuesday, for a stalemate.

His glass rests on his knee and his head is back too, throat long in the city light, and I am being very normal about it. (I am a liar. He smells like limes.)

"Your sister," he says, eyes closed. "She knows about me?"

"She knows the building has an espresso machine and a Univisión guy in it. In that order."

"Good order," he says, and his mouth does the thing where it isn't a smile and also entirely is.

(I have been holding my empty glass for forty minutes now, for the record, because putting a glass down is a thing a person does right before she leaves or right before she does something, and since I am doing neither, the glass stays. The glass is load-bearing.)

It's nearly one when the cicadas lose quorum and the playlist runs out and the night ends the way rooftop nights end, without anyone calling it. He stands, collects the bottle, holds out his hand for my glass, and I surrender it, and we go down.

Four flights in the dark, his footsteps ahead of mine, the stairwell still holding the day's heat. Somewhere on the third flight I do the math I have been avoiding all the way down: I know about Mariana and the plastic sleeves now, and he knows about Dani and the once-a-day, and we have never been closer in facts. The stairwell has also never felt longer, which should be against some law of physics, and isn't.

The kitchen at one a.m. is quiet. The machine sits dark next to the stove, smug, decorated war hero of the evening. My abandoned salad is still on the cutting board, and the press pass is exactly where it spent the fight, coiled by the fruit bowl, and nobody moves it, and nobody is going to. He slides the mezcal back into the cabinet above the fridge, washes both glasses by hand, and sets them upside down on the towel like the night is a thing that can be tidied. I fill my Stanley at the tap because tomorrow-me deserves one nice thing, and the water runs lukewarm out of the cold side, because this building gave up on all of us sometime in May.

He stops at the bedroom door with his hand on the frame, and the goodnight is right there, two syllables, the cheapest word in the building, and he doesn't spend it. Neither do I.

His door clicks shut, actually shut, for the first time in ten days, and the couch and I have nothing to say to each other.
