At two in the afternoon the truck said ninety-eight and the truck, by Deb's long experience, was telling the truth for once, because the truth had become impossible to shade. The road east of the McClaren gate had a heat shimmer over it where the asphalt about forty yards out looked like a shallow lake you could walk into. She drove through the lake. The lake was still asphalt.

Marjorie was in the passenger seat with her grey muzzle out the window and lifted into what was in practice more dust than wind. Deb had a paperback she was not going to read on the bench seat, two cans of Coors in a cooler on the floor, and a towel balled up in the footwell. The swim hole was ten minutes east according to Wyatt, and she had been in the truck for six of them.

She had not sat in a river since 2019.

She was almost certain about the year and she was going to let it stay almost. Her skin was hot to the touch and the back of her neck under her hair had been wet since eleven in the morning, and the McClaren kitchen was eighty-six degrees and counting, and she had told Wyatt she was going to go lie in some water. Wyatt had said, "Take Marjorie." She had said, "That was the plan." He had said, "Take two beers." She had said, "That was also the plan," and he had looked at her over his coffee mug and said, "You're catching on."

She took the turnoff where the cottonwoods made a darker patch against the afternoon, and the dirt track ended in a flat pad where two trucks could park if they knew each other. She was the only truck.

The heat hit her across the shoulders, and she stood with her hand on the door and listened. A kingfisher was going by somewhere. A cicada had been at it since noon and was planning to stay at it till September. A horsefly was doing business a few yards off on what she hoped was a cow flop and not her arm.

Marjorie dropped off the running board without asking for help and stood in the dust looking at the path, tail moving once. Some days Marjorie did her years honestly. Some days she remembered which dog she had been.

She grabbed the cooler and the towel and left the paperback.


The swim hole was where Wyatt had said it was.

A bend in the Yellowstone where a cottonwood deadfall had stopped the current and made a pool. The water was green the way bottle glass was green. Sand on the bottom down to your ankles at the edge and down to your thighs two steps out, and then the sand gave up and the green did the rest. Three flat rocks at the near side for sitting. A cliff on the far side of a sort somebody had jumped off of once, a while back, before there were social media platforms to regret it on.

The air had cooled ten degrees in the last six feet because the water was doing its work on the air above it, and she said, "Oh, thank Christ," out loud to the river and the dog. Neither of them commented.

She unlaced her boots, pulled her tank over her head, and shucked the cutoffs at the bank. She walked into the water in her navy one-piece the way a woman walks into cold water on a hundred-degree day, which is all at once, because thinking about it is the thing that ruins it.

The top six inches had been sitting in the sun since the river rose at ten, and the top six inches let her think she had come into bathwater. She waded out to her knees before her feet figured out what the rest of the pool was doing, which was keeping its own counsel at a temperature that belonged in April. She stood in the warm-on-top and the cold-underneath with the line exactly at her kneecaps, and she laughed once into the trees.

"Huh," she said.

Marjorie had waded to the belly and stopped. The belly was the limit of Marjorie's interest. Her belly was now at the line where warm ended and cold began, and her expression said it was a fine arrangement, and she was going to stand there for a while.

Deb went in the rest of the way, because the gradient was the whole trick. She got to her waist and let the cold have her thighs. She let her shoulders in. The line shifted up her ribs. She was in to her neck, and then she sat on the sand and let the warm on her shoulders and the cold on her hip, and she breathed once with the whole of her lungs for the first time since Bozeman.

She swam six strokes and rolled on her back. The cottonwoods made their specific racket against themselves, the dry silver-side-underside sound cottonwoods made in any wind at all, and she floated and told herself that this was what sitting was.

The list started about a minute in.

It was a small list. A polite list. Janie would have called by now if anything was wrong with the Henderson-Rowe thing. Pete had her Friday pencilled. Dr. Fosse in Helena had not pinged back about the Monday referral, and she should poke her on Sunday because Pat did not like being poked on Saturdays, which Pat had told her in 2022 at a conference in Missoula over two glasses of Gewürztraminer.

She said out loud to the sky, "No."

The list stopped.

The list started again in thirty seconds.

She said, "No," again, louder, because the cottonwoods were the only witnesses and they had heard worse.

The list stopped for longer.

She floated. The horsefly moved on. A dragonfly the size of a finger joint hung over her chest for a second and went. She was, for approximately four and a half minutes, sitting.

Then she heard a truck.


She heard the engine from a long way off on the county road, then closer, then stopping. A door closed in the soft way a door closes when the person closing it is not announcing the closing. Feet on the track. She did not stand up. She turned her head toward the bank without lifting it off the water, and through the cottonwoods there was a shape with a six-pack in one hand and a towel over one shoulder, and a hat on his head that she recognised by being wrong.

Cash Hollander came down the path and stopped at the bank.

He said, "Hey."

She said, "Hey."

He said, "Wyatt mentioned you might be out here."

She said, "Wyatt mentioned a lot of things today."

He said, "I was coming out regardless. Been coming here since I was eight."

She said, "Okay."

He looked at Marjorie in her belly-deep and said, "She all right?"

She said, "She's at her depth."

Marjorie did not acknowledge being spoken about.

Cash walked to the flat rock nearest the water, sat down, and pulled two cans off the plastic rings of the six-pack between his boots. He held one out. She walked out of the river to her knees, took it, and walked back in to her hip. She sat on the sand with her shoulders under water and her hand above it, and opened the can. The first swallow was cold the way the under-six-inches was cold. The can was already sweating. The sun through the cottonwoods made a broken pattern on the water around her.

She looked at him on the rock.

He had on jean cutoffs and a grey t-shirt that was going darker around the collar where he had been sweating. The boots he had kicked off. His father's trucker hat was on his head, faded, a little too small for him in a way that made it sit higher than it should have, MONTANA across the front in letters that had been white a long time ago and were now the colour of an old bone. He was wearing it without comment.

She did not say anything about the hat. Marjorie still had his good cap on her bed at the bunkhouse, and this was what a man did in the second day of cap theft, which was wear what he had.

He said, "Warm enough in there?"

She said, "On top. You should try it."

He drank his beer and watched the water. He did not come in yet.

She looked at him longer than she meant to. It was about three seconds and she caught it at two, which was an improvement over some recent measurements. She turned her head back to the cottonwoods. Then her brain did what her brain did.

Widowed about two years, she knew this from Wyatt. Teenage son, also Wyatt. Nine hundred head and running it solo-ish with the boy. He had drunk one beer at dinner on Monday, not two, because she had watched at arrival, because she watched what people drank, which was a piece of equipment she was not proud of. His truck was cleaner than Wyatt's, which was not hard, and cleaner than her own, which was. He had a good laugh and was not using it much. He probably had not been seen by a dentist, and ten years of putting cats into carriers had given her a dentist-radar that fired on its own, and a man who drove the ranch road at six a.m. had teeth that needed looking at, and she would bet a case of beer on it, and that was the fourth thing she was not here to —

She stopped.

She said, out loud, to herself and to the river and accidentally to him, "Never mind."

He said, "Never mind what."

She said, "Drink your beer."

He waited a beat. He drank his beer.

He did it in a way that was not a demand for explanation. He did it like a man who had just filed something without needing to read it twice. She watched him do it with the corner of her attention and she went back to looking at the cottonwoods, and she took a long pull of her own.

The kingfisher went by. She saw it this time, a streak of blue over the far bank that made the trip to somewhere and came back in thirty seconds going the other direction. The cicada was still at it. The water did what water did, which was hold her.

She took a breath with the whole of her lungs for the second time since Bozeman.


He came in about fifteen minutes later. He had worked his way through the first beer and cracked the second, and then he took the second up to his knees and into the water to his waist, and he said, "Jesus," on the under-six-inches beat the same way she had, and she laughed, and he grinned.

They sat in the water. Waist-deep for her sitting, waist-deep for him standing. Beers in the water's hand. The conversation took off from a running start at slow speed.

He asked about Bozeman.

He did it right. He did not ask how long she had been there or what had brought her back, because he knew both already from Wyatt, and he did not ask whether she liked it, which was a question only people from elsewhere asked. He asked about the clinic. She told him about Janie. She told him the five-year plan she had had for twelve years, which was to buy Janie out on the usual terms and run the practice on the understanding she had already been running it on for nine of those years. She had the money. Janie had the signature ready.

She said, "The plan works. That isn't the problem."

He said, "What's the problem."

She said, "I'm not sure I still want it."

She had not said that out loud to a person before. She had said it in her own head on the drive down at about mile eleven, and to the bathroom mirror in Bozeman on Sunday morning at five a.m., and she had not said it to Janie, and she had not said it to Wyatt, and now she had said it to a man she had met yesterday.

He did not jump on it.

He said, "Huh."

He said, "What does that feel like."

She said, "Like I've been packing for a trip I already took."

He said, "Okay."

He did not try to solve anything. He did not ask what she was going to do. He sat in the water with his beer and let the sentence sit between them for a count of four, and then he said, "Ask me a ranch question. You've been itching to," and she was so grateful he had moved them along that she almost thanked him out loud, which would have been too much, so she asked him a ranch question instead.

She asked about the broodmares.

He had four. An old bay named Soda, a chestnut named Lou that he had bought at a sale in 2016 for more than he should have because Lou had the eye of a mare who knew a thing. He told her about a fence project he had been putting off since February because the wire he needed came from Billings and the place in Billings had been under new management, which meant nothing was where it used to be. He mentioned Luke the way a man talks about a thirteen-year-old, in passing and in facts. Luke had been supposed to help on the fence project the Saturday before she got here. Luke had played video games for four hours and eaten a sandwich. He reported this with the tone of a man reporting the weather.

She said, "You weren't mad?"

He said, "He's thirteen."

She said, "You do know how to be mad, though."

He said, "Yeah. I'm holding it in reserve."

She laughed.

She drank the rest of her beer, and he handed her a second one out of the six-pack on the bank because he was close enough and she was not. His hand and her hand did not touch exchanging the can, but they came within the width of a finger, and both of them noticed, and neither of them said a thing.

She took the second beer into the water and kept it above the line.

He told her a story about Cal.

He said, "Cal helped me rebuild the cabin porch in 2019. August. We had a fifth of whiskey between us and it was one of those afternoons where the fifth seemed like a reasonable way to pace the job. He was cutting fence to the south at the same time, going back and forth. I had the new boards lined up. He came back from a cut and he had measured the fence on a piece of scrap paper, and the scrap paper was also the piece he had written the porch measurement on. He cut the fence line on the porch measurement. About three-quarters of an inch short over forty feet. He drove the whole way up from the fence, got out, looked at the fence he had just cut, looked at his hand, looked at me, and said, quote, oh for fuck's sake, unquote, and got a second fifth out of his truck."

She laughed. She laughed for long enough that she snorted some beer and coughed once into the back of her hand.

He said, "Wyatt does not know that story."

She said, "Why doesn't Wyatt know that story."

He said, "Because Cal fixed the fence on his own time and never told him, and I'm not going to be the one who tells him, and neither are you."

She said, "Fine."

He said, "Swear."

She said, "I swear. On the beer."

He said, "That's a real oath in this county."

He ducked under. He came up with the water running off his shoulders in a way she watched longer than she had meant to. His hair was dark from the wet. A drop went down the side of his jaw, taking the path his jaw made. His shoulders had that specific lean-muscle look a man got from doing work, and she was forty-five, and she had watched men come up out of water before, and her body had opinions about this one that she did not have to translate to know the shape of.

She was looking. She did not stop looking fast enough. He lifted his eyes to hers, and she caught the instant he saw her and the instant she knew he saw her, and neither of them looked away for a count. Then she drank her beer and he wiped the water off his jaw with the back of his hand, and they went back to the conversation they had been having like nothing had shifted. It was a lie they agreed on. It was a kind of manners.

Marjorie, on the sandbar where she had retreated from the belly-deep, had fallen asleep under the cottonwood with her chin on a root.

They talked another twenty minutes.


At five-thirty the sun had moved and the cottonwoods had moved with it, and the shade had shifted off Marjorie, and Marjorie had grudgingly shifted too. Deb looked at her phone on the rock and said, "I should head."

He said, "Yeah."

He did not ask her to stay longer. He walked up the bank and picked up his towel and wrapped it around his neck, and the trucker hat that had sat on the rock went back on his head. He did not say a word about the hat, and she did not say a word about the hat.

She dried off on her towel on the bank. She pulled the tank over the wet suit, stepped into the cutoffs, and laced the boots.

She said, "That was a hole."

He said, "Yeah. It's a good one."

He said, "You coming back tomorrow."

She said, "Could be."

He said, "Day after."

She said, "Could be that too."

He said, "Cash Hollander plans his week by elimination."

She said, "How is that going for you."

He said, "I'll let you know Friday."

They went up the path to the pad. His green GMC next to her rental. He threw his towel in the back of his and shut the gate, and he said, "I'd offer you a beer at my porch, but you've had two in the river and you have to drive."

She said, "Tomorrow."

He said, "Tomorrow what."

She said, "Tomorrow a beer at your porch."

He said, "Okay."

He did not press. He did not make a thing of her having said tomorrow instead of tonight. He got in his truck. He waited for her to get Marjorie up on the bench seat, which took a lift. Then he pulled out first and turned right toward his place and she pulled out second and turned left toward Wyatt's, and the dust from both trucks hung in the still afternoon air for a long time after both of them were gone.


She parked by the bunkhouse at five-forty-five with her hair still wet.

She sent Wyatt a text from the porch, four words long, river and bed, don't wait, and Wyatt texted back two minutes later, got it, sleep good, and she was grateful she had a brother who did not make a sentence out of two words.

She ate a piece of cheese and a piece of bread standing at the bunkhouse counter. She drank a glass of water. She took a shower because the river had put sand in places the river was not supposed to, and she got out of it and pulled on a t-shirt she had owned for eleven years.

She went out to the bunkhouse porch with a beer.

It was eight o'clock. The Absaroka range was doing the gold and getting ready to do the pink, and the pink was going to take about twenty-three minutes and then come the bruise colour and then dark. A cicada she did not know the sound of was on. There was an owl somewhere to the west.

She put her bare feet on the porch rail.

Marjorie lay down on the boards.

Across the county road two hundred yards off, a porch light came on at Cash Hollander's place and stayed on. There was a kitchen light too. She did not see a shape in the window this evening. She did not need to. She knew he was in there.

She thought about tomorrow.

She thought about it for a while.
