At five in the afternoon on the fifth of July the Hollenback place was already a full party, and Deb could hear it from the county road a quarter-mile off because the string lights on the big elm had not gone on yet but the accordion had.

She was in the back seat of Wyatt's truck in a cotton dress the colour of a Coors can and a pair of rubber flip-flops she had bought at Albertson's for three dollars. June was in the passenger seat. Wyatt was driving. Marjorie was in the back with her chin on Deb's thigh because she had not been allowed to make the political decision to stay home, and had the expression of a dog who was registering a protest.

Cash was going in his own truck. He had been at the Hollenback smoker since seven that morning.

Wyatt said, toward the rearview, "You clean up."

Deb said, "Compared to what."

He said, "Yesterday."

She said, "I was moving hay yesterday."

June said, "The dress is cute."

Deb said, "The dress is three years old."

June said, "That's a lie. You brought it because it's cute."

Deb did not argue the point.


The Hollenback place sat a half-mile off the county road down a drive lined with cottonwoods somebody had planted on purpose fifty years ago. The drive opened into a yard with a barn to the left and a ranch house to the right and a new pole building out back, and the whole stretch had been strung with lights that were not yet on. The wiring, Wyatt mentioned as they parked, had been done by Pete Raymond's nephew and was not a project to examine too closely.

The smoker had been going since dawn. Deb could smell it from the truck door. A brisket smell with seniority over any other smell in the county, and under it charcoal, and under that the smell of a field mowed yesterday for parking.

A washtub of ice sat at the barn door, already halfway done.

Merrill Hollenback, seventy-two and in a blue cotton dress with a gingham apron, came at her with two open arms and a grin, and Deb let herself be hugged.

Merrill said, "You're Wyatt's sister."

Deb said, "I am."

Merrill said, "Go eat. The brisket's been on the Earth since God made it. Corn in the tub in the kitchen. Beer in the washtub. Pete Raymond will find you. He finds everybody."

Deb said, "Noted."


June took her by the elbow and introduced her around at a pace designed to protect her. Three Hollenbacks of a generation fifteen years ahead. Two daughters-in-law closer to Deb's age, one visibly pregnant. A son called Kenny who had played ball with Wyatt in high school and said, "Your brother was the meanest short-stop in four counties," and walked off. A grandson of six with a stick. A cousin from Butte. Cal was on the porch with a beer, and he lifted the beer at Deb and said one syllable that did for a sentence. The string of faces went by and she let it go, because these were not her people and being at a party where it was all right not to know everybody was a thing she had forgotten the shape of.

She got a plate. The brisket was the brisket the Hollenbacks had been advertising since April, and she ate a bite over the plate because it was that kind of brisket, and when she opened her eyes Pete Raymond was at her elbow.

Pete was sixty-five, short, weathered, in a ball cap that said RAYMOND FEED. He had a beer in his left hand and his right hand palm-out in the sign of the honest man who had approached a stranger at a party.

He said, "You Wyatt's sister."

She said, "I am."

He said, "I owe you a weather observation."

She said, "That's what I heard."

He said, "I have been thinking about this heat since March."

She said, "Go."

He took a breath. He said, "This is the heat that teaches you what's load-bearing."

He drank his beer.

He walked off.

She laughed once into her plate at the pleasure of a man who had walked up, delivered one line of fortune-cookie, and walked off like a man who had been paid for it. June came back with a beer and said, "Did Pete find you," and Deb said, "Load-bearing," and June said, "That's a good one. Wyatt got the truth one," and walked Deb over to the corn.


The band was two men with an accordion and a fiddle and a third man with a washboard who had a day job at the Livingston feed co-op. They were terrible and they were perfect, and the first two songs had the children dancing in a rough circle by the elm tree and the older couples on the barn floor moving slow and not at all on the beat. Deb watched with her beer. She had eaten the brisket. She had met enough people to feel like she was behaving and not so many that she had stopped being on vacation.

Cash was at the smoker. He had come over in the first ten minutes and said "Hey" and she had said "Hey" and he had gone back. He had been civil. He had passed behind her once on the way to the washtub and his hand had not gone anywhere near her, and she had noticed it not-going.

She was waiting. She was aware she was waiting.

The band finished a song.

They started another one that was slower.

Cash came across the grass with his hands in his pockets. He stopped two feet off. He had his father's trucker hat on, the too-small one, dark blonde hair wet at the temples from the hose he had clearly put his head under twenty minutes ago, and a beer in one hand and an expression that was a little careful.

He said, "You dance?"

She said, "I used to."

He said, "This band is terrible."

She said, "I know."

He said, "You want to?"

She said, "Yeah."

He put his beer and the trucker hat on the nearest fence post. She put hers on the post next to his. Marjorie, from under a folding chair where she had been sleeping for an hour, opened one eye, assessed, and did not move yet. Cash walked Deb onto the barn floor with two fingers at the small of her back, not more, and she felt the two fingers through the cotton of the dress and did not comment.

They danced.

Cash did with her what a man did at a barn dance, a two-step he had been doing since he was fourteen, with the casual competence of a man who had danced at weddings and funerals and anniversaries for thirty years. Deb had to think. Her body went half a beat behind her head for the first sixteen bars, and then her head stopped managing and her body took the next sixteen bars on its own, and by the end of the song she had her feet in the right place and the grin on her face was not one she had chosen.

The band did not pause. They went into the slow one.

Cash adjusted his hand on her back and brought her in a little, and her free hand went to his shoulder and she danced the slow one with him in the middle of a Hollenback barn with a string of unlit lights over their heads and fifty people around them minding their own business as a courtesy.

She did not know the song. She was fairly sure the band did not know the song either, but the fiddle was making a case for it being a song and the accordion was holding the bottom end. Cash was humming it at the ear closest to hers the way a person hums a tune he cannot remember the words to, and she could not help him because she had never heard it. She let him do the humming for both of them.

Halfway through the slow one her flip-flop came off her left foot. Her step was wrong and she looked down, and the flip-flop was three feet away, and her bare foot was on barn boards that had been walked over by fifty people with boots from the yard. She laughed into his collarbone.

She said, "I lost a flip-flop."

He said, "I see that."

She said, "I'm barefoot."

He said, "Welcome to the barn."

Cash, without missing a beat, turned them in a half-circle, brought her around over the flip-flop, bent down, picked it up by the strap, and handed it to her without stopping dancing.

She said, "Thank you."

He said, "Any time."

She worked her toes into the thong without looking down and they kept dancing. She had her bare foot in the flip-flop with the dust of fifty other feet on top of it, and she was dancing in a barn in Park County at seven forty-five in the evening with the first man who had made her laugh twice in ten years, and she was, as a matter of practical accounting, happy.

She had not called it happy in a while. She did not call it happy now. She danced.

The song ended. He let go of her hand. He did not let go of her waist for another second, which she felt, and then he did, and they walked off the floor to the fence post.

The hat was gone. Cash looked at the fence post. He looked across the yard to the folding chair. Marjorie was on her belly under it with the too-small trucker hat between her front paws and her chin on the brim, and she had the eye-closed posture of a dog who had not done anything.

Cash said, "She came."

Deb said, "She came."

He said, "That's the second hat."

She said, "It is."

He did not move to retrieve it.


[Cash.]

He walked across the Hollenback yard to his truck a little after ten at a speed that was not a speed he had been at in a while. Deb was at the truck ahead of him with Marjorie in her arms because Marjorie was done. String lights on behind them. The band on its second wind. Merrill Hollenback in the kitchen doorway with a plate he had not stopped for.

He was aware, at the level of a man who had not thought like this in two years, that he had walked through a Hollenback party for four hours without carrying anything. Not the grief, not the ranch, not Luke, not the question of what this is now. He had eaten brisket and poured beer and made the cousin from Butte laugh with a story about a bull his grandfather had sold in 1974, and he had not, at any point, been a man carrying something. The word for that was not going to come to him. He was not going to try to find it.

He took Marjorie from Deb at the truck and lifted her into the back without being asked. Deb got in. He got in the other side. The dome light came on and went off, and he started the engine.


He drove her the back way because the front way went through the gate and the back way went along the river road past the turn-off for the swim hole. He had not asked her where they were going. She had not asked either. The air coming in her open window was off the river, and he kept his hand on the wheel because a hand on the wheel was a thing a man could keep track of.

At the turn-off for the cottonwood bend he slowed without signalling.

He said, "Here?"

She said, "Yes."

He turned.


[Deb.]

The track was dark. The cottonwoods were a tunnel of deeper dark over his headlights, and at the pad there was a pickup camper that had been there since April and looked, by the layer of dust, like it was going to be there until October. Cash parked at the far end under the cottonwood and killed the engine. The silence came in in pieces, the cicada first, then the creek, then a frog.

He said, "Eighty-one."

She said, "The temperature."

He said, "The air."

She said, "I'm taking that as an invitation."

She got out. The grass around the pad was cool against her calves and wet with dew a half-hour earlier than dew had a right to be. He came around the hood with a flashlight he flicked on for one step and then off again because once your eyes had the sky you didn't need one. A three-quarter moon was over the Absaroka range behind them. The valley was the colour of metal. Marjorie was asleep in the truck.

He took her hand for the path down to the bank, a hand for a person in the dark going over a root, and let go at the bank.

The swim hole in the dark was the swim hole without its colour, the green gone to black, the deadfall white where the moon was on it, the flat rocks at the near edge pale against the dark sand. A bat went over their heads and was gone.

She said, "I'm in."

He said, "Yeah."

She pulled the dress over her head. It came off in one motion because it was a dress built for coming off in one motion, and she had not worn it to a barn dance without thinking about this. Her bra went. Her underwear went. She walked past him to the water, went in up to her knees, and kept walking because she was not pretending she had come out here to do anything else.

She heard him behind her getting out of his clothes without hurry. He came into the water at his own speed, and she heard the small breath he took at the under-six-inches beat and she grinned at the dark because he had been warned and had got it anyway.

She went under and came up with her hair flat and the water running off her shoulders, and he was two feet from her, up to his ribs, looking at her.

He said, "Hey."

She said, "Hey."

He kissed her.

He came the two feet and put one hand on her jaw and the other flat between her shoulder blades and brought her into him, and the water was warm between their fronts and cold along her thighs where the line was, and she came against him and her breasts were against his ribs and his hand at her jaw did not move for the length of the kiss.

She said, "I was going to swim."

He said, "Swim."

She pushed off him and went on her back and floated. He stayed up to his chest, watching her. She did a lazy backstroke out to the middle of the pool and came back, and when she was close enough he reached under the water, caught her hip, and brought her upright.

She said, "Cash."

He said, "Yeah."

She said, "Quiet, baby."

He said, "I know."

His hand was between her hip and the small of her back. A truck went by on the county road a hundred yards off with a bass in it that went and went and went. He kissed her again. The water on her shoulders was warm, the water at her hip cold. She got her hands up into his hair and he made a sound into her mouth and she pushed against him and felt him against her and said something short into the kiss that was not a word.

He backed them two steps to where the bottom shallowed, turned her so the flat rock was behind her, and lifted her with a hand under her thigh.

He said, "Here."

She said, "Here."

She got her legs up around his waist, and his hands went under her thighs, and he had her weight. The cold was still under them where the current worked. The top six inches was the warm that had been in the sun all day, and she could feel the exact line of it on her back.

He said, "Okay?"

She said, "Yeah."

He pushed in.

She said, "Oh." Her forehead came down against his and stayed. She felt the sand and grit under his feet shift when he adjusted his stance, and the grit under her toes where her foot had dragged on his calf, and she did not care.

She said, "Quiet, baby. There's a camper."

He said, "Trying."

She said, "God."

She was whispering. She had not whispered during sex in thirteen years and she was whispering now because of the camper thirty yards up the bank and the way valleys carried sound, and the whispering was its own thing, a second layer on the sex, and she had not known she had missed it.

He moved. He was slow at first because of the water and her weight and because the rock bottom did not give him the bracing a floor did, and she felt him find it — his feet working the sand — and when he had it he went the right amount of faster. A stroke of moonlight was on the far bank. She watched it for a breath and closed her eyes.

She said, "Cash."

He said, "Yeah."

She said, "Up."

He brought her up a little. The shift changed the angle, and she said, "There," into his mouth, and he said, "I know," into hers. Every time he moved the water moved, and a small wave went out to the bank and came back. Her knee hit the rock on an out-stroke and she did not say anything because she was not going to interrupt what was happening. She was going to find the bruise tomorrow.

She said, "I'm close."

He said, "Yeah?"

She said, "Yeah."

He said, "Come on."

She came with her mouth at his ear and her teeth at his earlobe to keep the sound down, and she did not do a good job of it but she did not do a bad one either, and he held her through it with his forehead against her temple. He was still inside her and still moving, slower, letting her ride it, and when her breath came back she said, "Go," and he went. Three strokes, four. His hand on her thigh tightened. She said, "Yes," into his ear, and he came with a short breath he mostly kept to himself. He lowered her the inch he had raised her and held her against him with her legs still around his waist.

She could feel his heartbeat against her breastbone.

The creek did its small sound.

He said, "Okay."

She said, "Okay."


They floated for five minutes after. He was on his back with his arms out and she was next to him with her hand on his ribs because she had wanted it there, and the water moved them a slow inch at a time toward the deadfall and back. The moon had moved an inch since they had come in.

She had noticed the thing on Tuesday and not mentioned it, and had noticed it again tonight on the fence post when he put his beer down, and she had been going to say nothing. She said something.

She said, "Your ring finger has a tan line."

He opened his eyes.

He did not move for a second. Then he said, "Yeah."

She said, "You stopped wearing it."

He said, "August."

She said, "Last August."

He said, "She stopped wearing hers a year before I stopped wearing mine. I never asked her why and I figured she knew."

She said, "Okay."

She put her hand flat on his chest and left it there. The water moved them another half-inch toward the deadfall. He reached up and put his hand over hers, and he kept his eyes on the sky.

He said, "It's warm on top."

She said, "And cold underneath."

He said, "Yeah."

They did not say anything else for two minutes. Then the cold got to her legs and she stood up in the shallows with her hand still on his chest and said, "Out," and he said, "Out," and they went out.


They drove back at a quarter to midnight on the back road at thirty-five miles an hour. Her hair was wet against the back of her neck. Her dress was on inside out because she had been in a hurry in the dark, and she noticed it a mile down the road and did not fix it. The cicada had given up for the night. An owl went across the road in front of them once and was gone. Marjorie slept in the back.

He dropped her at the bunkhouse at twelve-oh-two.

He killed the engine, and neither of them got out for a second. He said, "You dance fine."

She said, "I told you I used to."

He said, "Past tense is wrong."

She did not argue. She leaned over and kissed him once, slow, and got out. He lifted Marjorie down to her. He waited in the drive until she was inside, and the headlights stayed on her through the bunkhouse door, and then he backed down and was gone.


Marjorie went directly to the rug and put her chin down on the baseball cap she had stolen on Tuesday, which she had brought to the bed at some point over the last four days and kept there. The trucker hat was still at the Hollenback place. That had been the trade.

Deb stripped down to her underwear and lay on top of the sheet. The bunkhouse was eighty. The box fan was moving air that was not doing much. Her hair dried on the pillow slowly and crookedly, and she did not care. She could smell the river on her shoulder and him on her skin.

She smiled at the dark ceiling.

Marjorie, from the rug, lifted her head, looked at Deb for the count of two, and put her chin back on the cap.
