Cash came in from the north fence at five after five with a shirt that was going to have to be thrown out. It had been blue in the small corner of the collar that still said so. It was charcoal everywhere else, and salt-edged in a pattern that was going to be permanent.

He got the shirt off in the mudroom and dropped it on the concrete and walked to the bathroom.

The shower was hotter than the air, because the heat wave had the water pressure on a schedule the well could not argue with. He let the shower run over his head and he thought about a hayloft.

He thought about her hand on his jaw. He thought about the hay coming off his bale. He thought about her laughing at the piece of hay down her back, and he thought about the last thing she had said before she fell asleep on his chest, which had been okay. He had said okay back.

The shower ran the shampoo into his eyes because it always did. He swore at the tile. He finished.

He was going to June's for dinner at six. Wyatt had called him at nine in the morning and said, "Steak is off. It's cold." Cash had said, "I'll be there." Wyatt had said, "Bring nothing, June's got it," and they had hung up.

He put on a pale blue snap shirt he had not put on since Easter, and he did not run the decision past himself. He rolled the sleeves to his elbows. He got a clean pair of jeans. He stood in front of the mirror for a second and adjusted the collar and then stopped himself adjusting it.

He had known her four days.

He was going to dinner because he wanted to see her again. That had been true since Tuesday and more true since Thursday, and what he was not going to do was overthink it.

The last time he had felt a want this specific he had been forty-three.

He caught himself thinking that. He walked downstairs.

Luke was on the couch with his feet on the coffee table and a book on his chest. Cash said, "I'm going to Wyatt's for dinner." Luke said, "Okay." Cash said, "There's leftovers." Luke said, "I know." Cash said, "Back by ten." Luke lifted one hand off the book and let it drop again. Cash walked out.

He got in the truck and turned right onto the county road, which was the short way to the McClaren gate. The drive took under ninety seconds. He did not think about the blue shirt.


The McClaren kitchen at six was ninety-five degrees and cooling, and the cooling was doing the work of a rumour.

The AC had been dead since Tuesday and the fix-it guy was, per last report, still in Idaho. June had set the table in the kitchen anyway, because the kitchen was where the table lived, and a summer dinner eaten standing on the porch was, by her lights, not a dinner. She had opened every window. She had the box fan in the doorway between the kitchen and the mudroom pushing air from somewhere to somewhere. The box fan was honest about what it was doing.

The table had four places. Tomato salad in a white bowl with a lip of olive oil on it. Cold fried chicken on a platter with a paper towel under it that had been there since yesterday. Potato salad in a green ceramic thing June had brought from Seattle. A jug of iced tea, sweating. Two bottles of Budweiser already opened for the men. A bottle of wine for Deb and June, already a quarter down. The wine was something white June had picked up in Gardiner that had been in the freezer for forty-five minutes before dinner and was cold enough, for one more minute, to justify the word.

Wyatt had his denim jacket on.

He had put it on at five-fifty, because that was what Wyatt did when the house was hot. He believed, on some argument his father had taught him and never finished, that a jacket kept the body cooler, and nobody in the family had talked him out of it yet. He was at the head of the table with the sleeves pushed to the elbow and the collar standing out at the back of his neck.

Cash came through the back door at two past six. He did the knock-and-come-in he had been doing at this door for nine years. June said, "There he is," from the counter. Wyatt said from the head of the table, "Sit down, my wife cooked ambitiously." June said, "My wife did not cook, my wife put cold food on plates," and Wyatt said, "That's an ambition in this kitchen," and June laughed.

Cash set the six-pack he had brought on the counter by the sink. He looked at Deb.

She was in the chair across from where he was going to sit, in a grey tank top and her hair pulled up off her neck, and she had taken her boots off and he could see her bare feet under the edge of the table. She looked at him. She said, "Hi." He said, "Hi." He sat down. His knee did not brush hers. There was about three inches between.

He kept the inches.

June said, "Start, don't wait," and the four of them started.

It was cold chicken and potato salad and tomato, and it was, to a degree Cash had not expected, good. The chicken had come out of June's oven on Thursday and sat in the fridge until it had settled into the thing cold fried chicken becomes on day two, which is the best chicken. The tomato had been grown by a man in Gardiner whose whole personality was tomatoes, and it had been sliced thick with olive oil and a flaky salt Cash had never bought for his own kitchen. The potato salad had mustard in it. The iced tea was what iced tea should be when the ice had mostly lost the argument with the heat.

They talked about the AC for about two minutes. Wyatt said the unit was a 2003 Lennox and the 2003 Lennox was going to outlive the ranch. June said, "It has not outlived this week." Wyatt said, "It's resting." Cash said, "The new guy in Emigrant, Pratt. He's fine. I used him in February." Wyatt said, "I'm going to wait for my guy." June said, "We know," and Cash ate a piece of chicken to cover the laugh.

Wyatt said to Cash, without a change of register, "How you been, man."

Cash had been about to lift his beer. He lifted it. He took a sip. He said, "I'm good, man. Been good."

Across the table June's eyes went to Wyatt's, and Wyatt's to June's, and the look between them lasted less than a second and said the thing the look was for.

Cash saw it.

He did not address it. He put the beer back on the table. He reached for the iced tea jug and refilled the empty beer glass on the far side of his plate, which was not the glass the beer was in, which nobody pointed out. He said, "Hollenbacks on Friday, you going?" and Wyatt blinked once, which was Wyatt's whole response to a subject change, and Wyatt said, "Yeah, we're going," and they were off. The accordion was going to be at the Hollenbacks, and brisket, and a washtub of ice with bottles in it that was going to last ninety minutes before the kids figured out where it was, and Wyatt said, "The kids always figure out where it is," and June said, "The kids are the point," and Cash said, "The kids are a consideration."

Deb, across the table, had not moved.

She had her fork in her hand at shoulder height and it had been there for about a count of three, and she put the fork down on the edge of her plate without loading it, and she picked up her wine glass and she drank from it, and she put the wine glass back down, and she did all of this looking at Cash. She did not look at June, and she did not look at Wyatt. She looked at Cash.

He felt her look. He did not turn his head toward her. His ear on that side knew exactly where her face was. His hand on the beer glass knew. He kept talking to Wyatt about the Hollenback barn and the accordion.

He heard her set her fork down again. He had not heard her pick it up.


Wyatt said, "The kid played baseball this year?"

Cash said, "Yeah. Luke. Third year."

Wyatt said, "Still terrible?"

Cash said, "Three years in, not terrible anymore. He hit his first double in June. Tuesday night game at the Livingston Elks field."

June said, "Against who."

Cash said, "Belgrade."

Wyatt said, "We hate Belgrade."

Cash said, "Wyatt, you have never once in your life said anything about Belgrade baseball."

Wyatt said, "I'm saying it now. On behalf of the Hollander family."

Cash said, "Appreciated."

June said to Deb, "You ever seen him play?"

Deb said, "Not yet."

She said it after a small beat. She said it like a person answering a question in a room she was not, at that moment, entirely in. June nodded and let it go. Wyatt drank his Budweiser.

Cash went on about the double. It had gone to right-centre and skipped on outfield grass the Elks had not cut since Memorial Day, and the right fielder, a kid Luke had known since second grade, had fumbled the pickup because the right fielder was eleven and the grass had ankle-grabbed him, and Luke had stood on second base and looked at the dugout and grinned in a way Cash had not seen his kid grin in a long time.

Wyatt said, "Who do you root for."

Cash said, "The Mariners."

Wyatt said, "That's a self-inflicted injury."

Cash said, "Yeah."

Wyatt said, "Nineteen-seventy-seven to present."

June said to Deb, "They have had this argument since the week I met Wyatt. Word for word."

Deb said, "Mariners."

Cash said, "Thank you."

Deb said, "My dentist in Bozeman is a Mariners fan and I have watched him cry in an office with a patient in the chair about Edgar Martinez. Twice."

Cash said, "Edgar Martinez is a valid reason to cry."

Deb said, "I took his word for it."

June laughed.

Cash looked at Deb then, because the laugh had done it, because she had said Edgar Martinez with a fluency that meant she had heard the name said in her own house for some number of years, and she was looking back at him, and the two-second look she gave him had a thing in it that was not the dinner.

He did not turn his head. He registered it, and he went back to the beer.


The dinner ran another forty minutes. June put a pie out at seven-thirty that she had not made, because the stove had been out of the question and she had bought the pie at the Albertson's in Livingston on Wednesday. It was a peach pie with a lattice top. It was fine.

Wyatt got up at ten past eight to take a call from a man in Emigrant about hay, and he stood in the mudroom with the phone against his shoulder and talked to the hay man in short words, and he came back in and finished his Budweiser and did not say what the hay man had said. June started clearing plates. Cash got up to help and June said, "Sit," and he sat. She carried four plates to the sink in two trips and ran the water on them but did not wash, because she was not going to wash dishes in a ninety-three-degree kitchen.

Deb stood up at eight-twenty and said, "I'm going to head to the bunkhouse," and June said, "Walk her," to Cash, without looking at Cash, and Wyatt, from the table, said, "Walk her," in exactly the same tone.

Cash said, "I was going to."

He had not said he was. He was going to. He picked his hat off the back of the chair and put it on and followed Deb to the back door, and Deb, at the door, turned and said, "June, thank you," and June said, "Tomorrow," and Deb said, "Tomorrow," and the door swung shut.


They walked down the path from the main house to the bunkhouse at eight-twenty-five. The path was forty yards of grass with the dirt track worn in the middle, and the air out of the house was cooler by four degrees because the house was holding on to the day's sun like it had paid for it. The Absaroka range was already past the gold and into the pink-going-grey. A nighthawk went by over the south pasture. Marjorie came down the path behind them at her own pace with her nails on the dry grass.

They did not speak on the walk.

He had his hands in his pockets. She had her arms loose at her sides. The not-speaking had a shape to it that was not angry and was not awkward, and both of them were letting it have the shape it had. Marjorie was twenty feet behind them doing her slow version of keeping up, and the only sound on the path was her nails and the cicadas and Deb's bare feet on the worn grass.

He said, at the step, "Are you okay."

She said, "Yeah."

He said, "Okay."

That was the whole exchange. He did not ask what he meant, and she did not tell him what she meant, and the words sat between them with the full weight of what had happened at the dinner table, and he walked her the last two feet to the bunkhouse porch, and they stopped at the step.

He looked at her. She looked at him. He put his hand on the side of her jaw the way he had put it there on Thursday at the hayloft, and her eyes closed, and he kissed her once. It was a slow kiss, different from the hayloft kisses in a way that had the hayloft kisses inside it. His other hand came up and his thumb ran along the line of her cheekbone. She put her hand over his wrist.

He stepped back.

He said, "See you tomorrow."

She said, "Yeah."

He went back up the path to his truck in the yard. He did not look back. Marjorie stayed on the bunkhouse porch. She was up on the boards with her chin on Deb's bare foot before Deb had her hand on the door.


Deb sat on the edge of the bunkhouse bed at nine-ten with her phone in her hand.

The air coming off the sill was ninety-one on its own and ninety-three with the fan's help, and the curtain moved in small ruffles when the air was moving without conviction. Marjorie was on the braided rug at her feet. The cap was between Marjorie's paws where it had been on Thursday night.

She opened Messages.

Pat Fosse was third in the list. The conversation was open from yesterday at six-oh-four a.m., when Deb had sent the three lines that had done what she had done.

She typed.

Hey Pat. About that referral I sent yesterday, cancel it. He didn't ask for a referral. I'll explain on Monday.

She read it. She read it again. Her thumb was on the send arrow.

She deleted it.

She retyped it in a different order.

Hey Pat, the referral isn't going to go anywhere. Sorry for the whiplash. I'll call you Monday.

She read it. She deleted it.

She stared at the empty text box.

She was not going to send either version at nine-eleven on a Friday night. Pat did not check texts on a Friday night. She was going to have the conversation with herself in daylight or she was going to have it never. The text box was the wrong place.

She put the phone face-down on the blanket.

She lay down on top of the quilt in the same grey tank she had worn at dinner. The fan was pushing the day's heat at her shins in a pattern that was regular enough to make her notice the pattern. The ceiling was pine boards. She counted three of the knots. She counted four.

She rolled onto her side.

The quilt under her elbow was hot. Her forearm was sticking to her ribs. The hair at the back of her neck was wet again, and she pulled it up through the elastic and let it fall against the pillow, and the pillow was hot.

She thought: I did a thing I cannot take back.

She thought that sentence one time. She did not think it a second time. She was not going to make a chorus of it.

She rolled onto her side again and looked at the fan. She looked at Marjorie, who had not moved, and at the cap between Marjorie's paws, and at the pine boards of the wall, and at her phone face-down on the blanket, and she did not pick the phone up.

Her jaw was tight. She unclenched it. It was tight again inside a minute.

She did not sleep.


The Absaroka range went from grey to black at about ten-fifteen. She heard the cicadas, and once the barn owl down in the loading chute. She heard Wyatt and June come out on the porch at some point, and then go back in.

At eleven she sat up and drank a glass of water at the sink. She went back to bed.

At midnight she opened the door to the porch because the porch was two degrees cooler, and she stood in the doorway and felt the air come in across her face, and Marjorie came and stood next to her. They stood like that for a minute. Then Deb closed the door, because the open door let the fan push the cooler out.

She went back to bed.

At two in the morning she looked at her phone. The text to Pat was still a draft. It was still two drafts. She had saved neither. She closed the app.

She put her forearm across her eyes.

She was awake at three. She was awake at four. The Absaroka range out the window went through the specific colours it went through at four-thirty in June, dark blue going to lighter blue going to grey. She watched the window. She did not sleep.

At five-fifty-eight the porch light came on at Cash's place. She could see it through the bunkhouse window at an angle past the cottonwood. The porch light went off, and the kitchen light came on, and then the kitchen light went off too. She heard, across the yard and the county road and the still air of a valley that was not yet moving, a truck door close, and the engine start, and the slow reverse and turn onto the county road.

It was six on the clock.

The truck went east.

She watched the rear lights, red and small through the cottonwood branches, until the cottonwoods took them. She listened to the engine fade down the county road. She listened past it, until there was only the cicadas picking up for the day and the fan.

She closed her eyes.

She did not sleep.
