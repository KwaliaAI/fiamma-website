She woke up in the bunkhouse at nine-twelve on a Friday morning on a pillow she had not fully landed on, and the first thing she was aware of was that the light was wrong.

The light was nine-twelve light. She knew that before she looked at her phone. The sun had been coming in the east window of the bunkhouse at a particular angle at seven for four mornings running, and this was not that angle. This was the angle at which the pine outside the east window had already got between the sun and the window, which meant the sun was up, was well up, was up and going about its business without her.

She sat up on an elbow. Marjorie was on the rug with her chin on the baseball cap and her eyes open, watching Deb with the patient indifference of a fourteen-year-old dog who had been up at six and had chosen to say nothing about it.

Deb said, "You could have woken me."

Marjorie did not respond.

Deb reached for her phone on the nightstand. The screen came up with a bank of notifications going backward. She did not register the top three. She registered the fourth, which was a missed call at seven thirty-two that morning, and under it a voicemail at seven thirty-four, and both of them were from Pete Haverstock.

She sat the rest of the way up.

She had been rested, she did the math in her head before she meant to, three and a half hours.


Pete Haverstock ran a cow-calf outside Three Forks on ground his wife's people had been on since 1912, and she had been on a first-name basis with him for eleven years. Once in 2019 she had been forty minutes late because a mare outside Belgrade had foaled backward, and Pete had waited on the line with her technician without complaining until she came off the surgery. She had called him back from the truck before she had pulled out of the barn. She had apologised for the forty minutes. He had said the radar had been more interesting than his herd that morning.

She had not missed a call from Pete Haverstock in eleven years.

She opened the voicemail.

She listened with the phone held out in front of her on the bed because she did not want to put it against her ear yet. Pete's voice came out of the tinny speaker the way Pete's voice was, slow, flat, a vowel that put its feet down. He said hey Deb, he must have missed her, he had been trying to reach her about the new bull's left hock and the way he had been favouring it this week. He said he would try again next week, no rush on this one, she should enjoy her vacation. He said Marcie said to say hello. He said bye now. The voicemail ended.

She played it again.

The second time she sat very still while it played.

The voicemail was thirty-one seconds. It was spoken at the speed of a man who was being gracious to a woman he liked. It was also the voicemail of a man who had told her on the phone last Friday that he was going to call at seven thirty because the bull was off and he wanted to talk to her before the weekend. She had set the alarm in front of him on the call. She had repeated the time back to him to be sure.

She had slept through the alarm.

She had not heard the alarm. She had been asleep in a way she had not slept since her twenties, down to the bottom, with a man three hundred yards across the county road and her knee bruising and her dress on inside out on the floor.

She sat on the edge of the bed in the sheet she had pulled with her when she sat up. She looked at the floor. The dress from last night was on the floor inside out where she had stepped out of it at twelve-oh-five. A flip-flop was on its side next to it. Her underwear from the creek was somewhere she could not immediately see. She looked at Marjorie. Marjorie looked at her.

Deb said, "I missed Pete."

Marjorie closed one eye.


She called him back at nine-seventeen.

He picked up on the second ring.

She said, "Pete."

He said, "Hey Deb."

She said, "I owe you an apology."

He said, "No, you don't."

She said, "I slept through my alarm."

Pete was quiet for half a second. Then he said, "You're on vacation."

She said, "It doesn't matter that I'm on vacation. I told you seven thirty on the last call. I set the alarm in front of you."

He said, "I remember."

She said, "I'm sorry."

He said, "It's fine."

She was sitting on the edge of the bed in the sheet with her bare feet on the bunkhouse floor, and she had her eyes on the far wall where the knot in the pine had been winking at her for six mornings, and she did not fill the silence after his it's fine. She had been taught at twenty-two, by a vet named Wendy Groves in Kalispell who did not suffer fools, that you did not keep apologising after a man said it was fine, because the second apology was for you and not for him.

Pete said, "I'll try you next week. No rush. He's not worse."

She said, "Tuesday morning seven thirty."

He said, "Tuesday's good. Enjoy your vacation."

She said, "Thanks, Pete."

He said, "Tell Marcie hi."

She said, "Tell her yourself."

He said, "She'll like that."

The line went. She looked at the wall.


She sat in a towel on the edge of the bed for ten minutes.

The towel she had pulled off the hook last night and used on her hair was still a little damp. She had it tucked under her arms the way a woman tucked a towel in after a shower, except she had not had a shower yet. Her hair was dry on one side and flat on the other. Marjorie had her chin back on the cap.

She was thinking about Pete's you're on vacation and the way he had said it. He had been a client for eleven years and a friend for nine of them, and he had never said you're on vacation to her in his life because she had never given him occasion. She was the vet who took calls at four a.m. from a ranch in Big Timber, who called back from the truck, who did not sleep through alarms because she had set the alarm in front of the man.

She was also, as a matter of practical accounting, a woman who had spent Thursday night with a man in the cottonwood bend of a river until midnight and had brought the river home on her shoulder and gone to sleep in a bunkhouse at quarter past twelve and missed a call at seven thirty.

Both of those were true.

She did not think about what it meant. She thought about Pete in his kitchen in Three Forks at seven thirty that morning with his coffee on the counter, and the bull's left hock on his mind, and his phone ringing in his hand and going to her voicemail. She thought about him putting the phone down afterward and picking up the coffee.

She got up.

She walked to the bathroom with the towel and started the shower.


She showered. She put on shorts and a t-shirt. She put her hair up in a clip because it had dried crooked and that was the only thing to do. Marjorie followed her to the kitchen of the main house with the cap in her mouth because Marjorie was taking the cap to the kitchen now.

Wyatt was at the stove. June was at the table with a mug. Cash was at the other end of the table in a clean button-down shirt with the sleeves rolled, and he had a cup of Wyatt's terrible coffee in front of him and an expression that said he had been there since eight-fifteen and had been invited for brunch and had said yes the way a man said yes when he was not going to pretend he had not wanted to.

Wyatt, to the eggs, said, "She rises."

Deb said, "Don't."

Wyatt said, "I said she rises. That's a blessing."

June said, "Coffee."

Deb said, "Please."

June poured.

Cash said, "Morning."

Deb said, "Morning."

She sat. She did not look at him for the first second, because the first second was a second she had not yet earned, and then she looked at him and his eyes did the thing his mouth did not, the crinkle, the careful, and she drank the coffee.

Wyatt put a plate down in front of her. Eggs scrambled with a handful of the cheddar he kept in the second drawer. Bacon. Two slices of toast from the loaf June had made on Wednesday. A half a tomato from June's garden, the one plant that had not yet failed.

He said, "Eat."

She ate.

June said, toward Cash, "He invited you at seven thirty this morning."

Cash said, "He did."

June said, "He called me in the garden. I was in the garden at seven thirty. I did not know we were having brunch."

Wyatt said, "The brunch idea came to me at seven twenty-eight."

June said, "It was a revelation."

Wyatt said, "It was the spirit."

Cash said, "I was fed."

Wyatt said, "You were."

Deb said, "He fed you before or after inviting you."

Cash said, "After. He called while I was feeding the mares. The brunch had not yet been prepared."

Wyatt said, "The brunch was conceptual at that point."

June said, "The brunch was vibes."

Deb said, "And now it's eggs."

Wyatt said, "And now it's eggs."

She ate her eggs. She had eaten about half a plate when she noticed Cash had noticed she was quieter than Cash expected, and she watched him not ask. He did not ask. He refilled her coffee without asking and put the cream where her hand would find it and then he ate his toast.

She loved him a little for not asking.

She was not going to tell him.


After brunch the four of them walked to the creek because it was June's idea and June had said the word creek in a way that had caused Wyatt to put down his dishtowel. It was ninety-two outside and cloudy — the first cloudy morning in six days — and the weather radio on the counter had been promising a thunderstorm by evening for the third time in a row, and this time even Wyatt, who had been calling the forecast a lie since Tuesday, had said at breakfast "might get it today."

Marjorie stayed behind on the porch.

The creek was the ditch creek, not the Yellowstone, the little one that ran through the McClaren hay meadow from the spring up behind Cal's place. It was six feet wide and knee-deep at the deep spot and shaded the whole way by chokecherries gone past blossom and into their green berries. The four of them walked to it at a pace Deb had not walked at in a long time, which was the pace of people who were not trying to get anywhere.

At the creek they took their shoes off and put their feet in. June and Wyatt sat on a cottonwood log that had come down in April. Deb sat on the grass bank with Cash a foot off her hip. The water was cold enough on her ankles to remind her she had blood in her feet.

She had a sentence ready. The sentence was I missed a call this morning. She had been carrying it since Wyatt's eggs. She turned it over once more on the bank, and she did not say it. Cash was a foot off her hip with his feet in the creek and his forearms on his knees, and he was watching a leaf come down the current and go under his ankle, and she watched it too, and she did not say the sentence. She put it back where she was keeping it.

June, from the log, said across the creek, "The tomato was a Brandywine."

Wyatt said, "It was."

June said, "It's the only one that made it."

Wyatt said, "Next year we plant eight Brandywines and nothing else."

June said, "That's not how you garden, Wyatt."

Wyatt said, "It's how I garden."

Deb laughed once into her knees. She looked at Cash. Cash was already looking at her. He did not say anything. His eyes did the thing his mouth did not.

They stayed at the creek an hour.


At two Cash drove her back across the county road to his place because she said she was going to take a nap on his couch for twenty minutes and he said that was fine, and they both knew twenty minutes was going to be longer than twenty minutes and neither of them commented.

His couch was a brown leather couch with a quilt on the back his aunt had made in 1988, and it was long enough for her to lie on and have her feet on one arm and her head on a pillow against the other. He put the quilt over her even though it was ninety-two out, because the air conditioning in his house was the one thing about his house that worked too well, and he had a unit in the front room that had been running since June and did not know the word off.

He said, "I'll be on the porch."

She said, "With a book."

He said, "With a book."

She said, "What book."

He said, "The one Luke left on the porch chair in March."

She said, "What is it."

He said, "I haven't turned it over yet."

She said, "You've had it since March."

He said, "Yeah."

She closed her eyes.

She slept for an hour and forty minutes. She heard him once when he came in for a glass of water, at a part of the nap where she was aware she was being napped near, and she did not open her eyes because she was doing a thing she had not done in six months, which was trust the sound of a man moving through his own house to not require her attention. She went back down.

She came up at three forty because Marjorie was standing on the floor next to the couch with the cap in her mouth, and Marjorie was wheezing the particular wheeze Marjorie did when Marjorie wanted something.

Cash, from the doorway, said, "Wyatt brought her over."

Deb said, "What time is it."

He said, "Three forty."

She said, "The book."

He said, "Killing Floor. Jack Reacher."

She said, "What's it about."

He said, "A man walks into a town."

She said, "And."

He said, "I'm forty pages in. I'll tell you at seventy."


The thunderstorm rolled in at six.

They saw it coming for an hour. The clouds had been stacking west of the Absaroka since four, and by five the light had gone the particular green it went in Montana when the atmosphere had made up its mind, and by five thirty Wyatt had called across the county road to say the fix-it guy's dog was under the porch at McClaren and that was a sign. At five forty-five Cash had moved the two porch chairs in eighteen inches from where they usually sat and put the beer cooler between them. At six-oh-two the rain came down the valley in a sheet.

The temperature dropped twenty degrees in seven minutes. Deb watched the thermometer by the kitchen window do it. Ninety-one at five-fifty. Seventy-one at five-fifty-seven. She called Cash in to look. He came in, looked, and said, "Huh."

They sat on his porch under the overhang in the two moved-in chairs with the beer between them and the rain coming off the roof in a curtain eighteen inches in front of their feet. The cottonwoods in the yard went silver every time the lightning came. The thunder came at different distances, one so close it moved the beer cans on the cooler, and she had a hand over her heart at that one for a second before she put it down, and Cash said, "That one was the county line," and she said, "Feels closer," and he said, "They always do."

Luke came down from upstairs at six-twenty in a t-shirt and no shoes and stood in the kitchen doorway looking at the rain for a count of four. He said, "Sick." He went back up.

Deb said, "Was that two words."

Cash said, "One. Sick. The second syllable was air."

She said, "Pedant."

He said, "Accuracy."

She drank her beer. The thunder rolled somewhere over the Gallatin range, twenty miles out, and rolled again, and rolled again and did not quit for half a minute, and she had not heard thunder like that in five years because there was no thunder like that in Bozeman because Bozeman was on the wrong side of a mountain for thunder.

Cash said, "You staying."

She said, "Yes."

He said, "Okay."

She said, "Okay."

That was the conversation.


They slept together that night without sex.

She was in one of his t-shirts and a pair of shorts. He was in his boxers. His window was open because the air coming in was the air of a broken heat wave, sixty-four degrees, full of creek and wet cottonwood and the iron smell of a Montana rain on hot ground. His arm went across her ribs under the shirt and stayed. His hand was open on her sternum. She could feel his heart against her shoulder blade, going slower than hers.

She thought about Pete.

She thought about Pat in Helena and the text she had sent from Cash's bathroom on Tuesday morning and had still not deleted. She thought about the way Pete had said you're on vacation and the way he had meant it, and about Pat and about whether a woman could be one kind of person for eleven years and another kind of person for six days and both of those things be real at the same time. She did not know the answer. She was not going to find out tonight.

Cash's breathing evened out behind her at eleven-forty. She stayed awake.

She slept in pieces. She was up at twelve-thirty and watched the bedroom ceiling. She went down for a while. She was up at twelve-fifty and listened to the rain, which had gone light, and then gone lighter. She went down. She was up at one.

At one the thunderstorm passed the ranch. The lightning that had been walking east came back, one flash, close, the room going white, and she was awake for it with her eyes open. It lit the ceiling and the top of the dresser and his boots at the foot of the bed and the sleeve of the shirt he had put on the chair. His arm did not move on her ribs.

The thunder came five seconds after.

He slept through it.

She watched the window. The rain kept going, quieter. Somewhere on the far side of the Absaroka the storm did its last piece of weather and moved on.

She did not close her eyes for a while.
