The thermometer nailed to the post of the back porch said ninety-six at four in the afternoon, and the thermometer was in the shade, and Deb had put her feet up on the porch rail because the porch rail was cooler than her own thigh.

She had been at the ranch for about four hours and had done, she calculated, approximately nothing. She had unpacked her duffel. She had watched Marjorie move eleven inches, from the shade under the porch to the shade under the porch step, with the solemnity Marjorie had given to every choice she had made since she was nine. She had drunk two glasses of iced tea. She had sat in two folding chairs for fifteen minutes each and, for a stretch that surprised her, put her hands flat on the porch boards and felt the heat of the sun-hot wood come up through her palms and not tried to make anything of it.

She was, technically, relaxing.

Behind her through the kitchen screen she could hear Wyatt and June moving around the counter with the exhausted choreography of two people who had made a dinner plan and watched it collapse in the heat. The AC unit on the side of the house had, at approximately one p.m., made a single loud adult noise and then stopped doing anything, and Wyatt had stood in the yard looking at it for a quarter of a minute with both hands on his hips, and then he had come back into the kitchen and said, "So we're eating cold," and June had said, "Okay," and that had been the whole conversation.

Cold, in this specific kitchen, meant chicken that had been intended for the grill and now was not going to be grilled, a bowl of potato salad June had made at eleven, a tomato from Gardiner that had survived two days on the counter, and a loaf of bread Wyatt had bought at Albertson's on his way through town. Deb had looked in the fridge at three and noted, in the impassive register of a person reading a chart, that the fridge was running about six degrees warm of where it should be.

She had not said so. The fridge had been handling itself since May. The fridge did not need a diagnosis from Bozeman.

She heard the pickup before she saw it.

It came up the county road with the slow hay-weather gait of a truck a man was driving because he was not in a hurry, slowed at the McClaren gate, and pulled in, and the sound under its tires changed from gravel to packed dirt, and then Garbage came out from wherever Garbage had been and did what Garbage did.

Garbage was the McClaren chicken. She was a red hen with a walk. She had, per June, been at the ranch since before the ranch had been Wyatt and June's ranch, which put her somewhere in chicken terms between middle management and retired. She had been under the porch five minutes ago. She was at the truck now.

The truck was a green GMC, the same green GMC Deb had seen yesterday at the gate across the road, and it stopped ten feet from the porch. Garbage went directly to the front tire, looked at the tire for a beat, and bit it.

She bit it with the settled resentment of a woman who had been wronged. She closed her beak on the sidewall, held for a count of two, released, and then stepped back half a step and regarded the tire as if the tire were on notice.

The driver came around the hood.

He was six feet of tanned ranch-work in jean cutoffs and a grey t-shirt that had been soaked through at some point in the last hour and had started to dry in the heat and gone to the patchy, salt-edged condition of a shirt a man has been working in. His hair was dark blond and cut short and wet at the temples. He had a white plastic five-gallon bucket in his left hand. The bucket had a bag of ice in it and a number of cans of Coors he was making no attempt to conceal. He had on a faded blue baseball cap that said HOLLANDER across the brim in white thread gone grey with washing.

He looked at the chicken.

He said, "Yeah, the bird hates me."

Deb was halfway out of the folding chair before she had decided to stand up. She stopped at standing. She said, "Family trait."

Wyatt came out onto the porch behind her with a coffee mug in his hand, because Wyatt drank coffee at four in the afternoon, and he said, from behind the mug, "Hers was the first."

The man looked at Wyatt.

He looked at Deb.

He grinned. It was a grin that came out of his face without asking him first, and it did something crinkling and deliberate at the corners of his eyes that a man twenty years younger could not have done if you paid him.

He said, "You must be Deb."

She said, "I must be."

He said, "Cash Hollander."

She said, "I figured."

He set the bucket down on the porch step. He said to Wyatt, "I heard you needed a working fridge."

Wyatt said, "I wasn't going to ask."

Cash said, "I know. That's why I brought it."

Wyatt made a sound that was not a laugh and was also not not a laugh, and he came down one step and lifted the bucket with the flat of his hand under the bottom because fourteen cans and a bag of ice on a plastic handle was a handle problem, and he carried it up and set it on the porch floor next to Deb's folding chair. The ice collapsed inside the bag with a small shifting sound. The cans rang.

Cash had not moved. He had his hat in his hand now, and Deb had not seen him take the hat off. Her brain, doing the thing it did for a living, filed takes his hat off on a porch under habit. She told herself she was on vacation and not filing. It did not take.

She said, "Thanks."

He said, "Any time."

She said, "You say that like you mean it."

He said, "I do."

He put the hat back on. He was about her age, a year younger if she had to guess, and he carried his height with the ease of a man who had stopped being surprised by it. The t-shirt had gone translucent across the chest from the sweat. His arms were tanned to the t-shirt sleeve and then stopped in a clean line, and she looked at the line for about a second and a half and turned her face toward the yard because she was not doing that.

She said, toward the yard, "You want to come up on the porch."

He said, "I was going to wait to be asked."

She said, "Come up on the porch."

He came up on the porch.


June appeared in the doorway with a plate of something in her hands and a hand towel over her shoulder and a look on her face that said she had heard the whole approach from the kitchen window and had decided to let Deb do whatever Deb was going to do. She saw Cash. She said, "Oh, good," and that was all she said, and she set the plate on the little table next to the porch rail and turned on her heel and went back inside, and through the screen Deb heard her open the cutlery drawer with the particular energy of a woman who had been saved from something.

Wyatt handed Cash a beer. He took one for himself. He opened Deb's on the porch rail with the flat of his hand, and the beer was cold enough to ache in the tooth, and she drank an inch of it standing up.

Cash said, "Sprinklers on the east field were eating me alive."

Wyatt said, "I saw."

Cash said, "I came off that at three-thirty and I thought, all right, there are six beers in my fridge, but those people's AC died Friday."

Wyatt said, "Saturday."

Cash said, "Saturday. Anyway. I stopped at Merle's for the bucket. Merle charged me eight dollars for a plastic bucket. I am keeping the bucket."

Wyatt said, "You should keep the bucket."

Cash said, "Yeah."

They sat down.

Wyatt took the chair he always took, which was the chair with the cigarette burn on the arm nobody had ever apologised for. Cash took the chair to his left with the ease of a man who had sat in that chair before. Deb stayed in the folding chair she had been in, and her feet came down off the rail because the conversation had included her without asking her, and that was fine.

Marjorie came out from under the porch.

She came out at the speed she came out at, which was the speed of a dog considering whether her participation was required. She came up the two porch steps one step at a time, stopped at the top, looked at Cash, looked at Cash's hat, looked at Cash again, and committed.

She did not run. Marjorie did not run for anything except water coming out of a hose. She walked, three-legged with the bad hip, to where Cash was sitting, and she put her grey muzzle up against the brim of his hat where it stuck out over his right ear, and she took the hat off his head with a delicacy that was surgical. She took it like the hat had been offered. She walked four feet to the far corner of the porch, lay down with a sound that was a whole sentence, set the cap between her front paws, and put her chin on it.

Cash said nothing for a second.

He said, "Okay."

He did not get up. He did not reach. He looked at the dog with his mouth doing a thing. He said to Wyatt, without looking at Wyatt, "She does this."

Wyatt said, "Apparently."

Cash said to Deb, "Is this a thing."

Deb said, "It's a thing."

He said, "How often."

She said, "I've seen her take three hats in fourteen years."

He said, "So I'm special."

She said, "Don't."

He laughed. It was the first time she had heard him laugh, and it was a full chest laugh, quick, over in two seconds, the laugh of a man who laughed easily, and it had the quality of a door standing open.

He said, "I'll leave her to it."

Deb said, "That's your best option."


They sat on the porch for a long time.

Long, in late-June Montana terms, was about two hours, because the light did not quit until ten past nine and the heat did not quit until after the light quit, and neither of them was in any hurry. June came out with plates at five-thirty, and they ate cold chicken and potato salad and sliced tomato and bread off the cutting board in their laps on the porch because the kitchen was still the hottest room in Park County, and June sat on the top step with her plate on her knees, and Wyatt sat in his chair, and Cash sat in his, and Deb sat in the folding chair because she had decided to, and they ate.

Wyatt and Cash talked about fences and cattle and the Hollenback anniversary and the price of hay. The Hollenback anniversary was on the fifth of July, apparently, and there was going to be an accordion. Cash called the accordion a condition. Wyatt said it was true. June said, "You are going." Deb said, "Am I." June said, "You are going."

Cash said, "You can get a head start on hating it at the barbecue Saturday."

Wyatt looked at him.

Wyatt said, "You coming?"

Cash said, "You invited me in March."

Wyatt said, "I invited you in March."

Cash said, "I accepted in March."

Wyatt said, "Then you're coming."

Cash said, "Yeah."

Deb was listening to this and also not listening, and what she was doing instead was looking at Cash Hollander's forearm where it was resting on the arm of the chair, specifically at the tendon at the inside of his elbow and the way the tendon moved when he adjusted his grip on the beer can. She noticed she was looking at it. She looked at Marjorie instead. Marjorie was asleep with her chin on the cap. Marjorie was easier to look at.

She did this a second time and filed it, and she was, she knew, annoyed with herself for filing it, because filing it meant she had made it a thing.

She said nothing about it.

She did the polite mid-forties woman's work of asking him the questions a woman asks a man on a porch when she is not going anywhere with the questions. How long had he had the place across the road. (Fourteen years, his father before him, his grandfather before that.) How big was it. (Eight hundred seventy acres, nine hundred head. Smaller than Wyatt's by about a third.) Did he run it on his own. (Mostly. A kid half-time in summer from Livingston. His son for the parts the son would do, which were the parts that did not involve a fence.) What was his son's name. (Luke.) How old. (Thirteen.) He answered easily. He had answered these questions a hundred times at a barbecue and had never resented them.

He asked her about the clinic. She said three sentences. She said she co-owned with a woman named Janie Lassiter in Bozeman. She said they had been at it eighteen years. She said Janie was going to retire and she was going to sort it out in the fall, and that was all she said because the clinic was not coming to this porch, and Cash did not push. He drank his beer. He said, "Sounds like a good partner." She said, "She is."

That was the deepest that went.


At around seven the light went the colour the light went, which was the copper that came off the Absaroka range for about nine minutes before the whole valley went to gold, and June had gone inside to get a second round of iced tea, and Wyatt had gone inside to help her, which nobody believed, and Deb and Cash were on the porch.

He said, "How long you staying."

She said, "Six days."

He said, "Saturday barbecue and you go Monday?"

She said, "Tuesday."

He said, "Tuesday."

She said, "I have a herd call on Friday."

He said, "Who's the herd."

She said, "Henderson-Rowe."

He said, "Yeah, they're good people. Don't miss that."

She said, "I don't plan to."

He said, "Tuesday, then."

He said it like a man making a note and not announcing the note, and Deb heard the note being made, and she did not say anything about it because there was nothing to say yet.

She said, "You always deliver beer to neighbours."

He said, "When their fridge is warm, I do."

She said, "The fridge is running six degrees high."

He said, "I figured. I saw you eyeing it through the screen."

She said, "You were in the yard for four minutes."

He said, "I have good eyes."

She said, "Apparently."

He finished his beer, stood up, and did not ask for the cap back.


He came off the porch at twenty past seven and Garbage was not there this time, because Garbage had gone to bed with the other chickens at about six-thirty because Garbage ran on a schedule. He walked to the truck. Deb stayed on the porch. She had crossed enough lines in her head for one afternoon without letting her feet cross one.

He got as far as the driver's door and turned around.

He said, across twenty feet of yard, "Tell the dog she can keep the hat."

Deb said, "She was going to anyway."

He said, "Yeah."

He got in the truck. He backed down the drive in a slow quarter-turn. He lifted two fingers off the wheel at the screen door where June was watching him go without pretending she was not, and he lifted the same two fingers at Deb, and he was gone.

The dust took a full minute to settle. Deb stood on the porch in the last of the light, and she stood there for the minute, and she did not think about anything in particular. Marjorie had not moved. The cap was still between her paws. Wyatt came back out onto the porch with a fresh beer in each hand and handed one to his sister and sat down.

He said, "Six days."

She said, "Six days."

He said, "Not eight."

She said, "Wyatt."

He said, "I'm just saying."

She said, "Don't just say."

He drank his beer.


Across the county road at ten past nine, Cash Hollander turned off the two-lane and into his own drive and sat for a second in the cab with the engine off and the keys in his hand. He was not going to think about the woman on the porch who had been guarded and funny and had looked at her dog four times during dinner because the dog was the thing in the yard it was safest to look at. He had known Wyatt Reeves for nine years. He had known Wyatt had a sister in Bozeman for nine years. He had never once met her. He was, with the flat surprise of a man not in the habit of surprising himself in his own driveway, looking forward to Saturday.

He thought about six days.

He caught himself thinking about six days.

He got out of the truck. The porch light came on because the porch light was a motion sensor and the motion sensor did its job. He went into the house. Luke was on the couch playing something on his phone and did not look up. Cash said, "Hey, bud." Luke said, "Hey." That was the whole conversation. Cash went to the kitchen and put his empty bucket on the counter and stood at the sink with his hands on the rim for a second, and then he turned the tap on and washed them because his hands were dusty from the road.

He thought: Saturday.

He dried his hands on a dish towel and went upstairs.


At a quarter to eleven Deb was in the bunkhouse with the window open and a box fan on the sill that was pushing hot air against her shins in a way that was negotiable and nothing else. She was on top of the sheet in a tank top and cutoff sweats. Marjorie was on the rug at the foot of the bed with her nose tucked into her haunch. The window looked out on the south pasture, and the bay horse Wyatt kept out there for reasons that were not fully economic was standing hipshot at the far rail with his tail doing slow work against the flies, and the moon was up but not much, and the cottonwoods were black against a sky that was still, at this hour, a very dark blue.

She was not going to think about the neighbour.

She thought this clearly, to the ceiling boards, and she noticed that she had thought it, and she noticed that thinking it clearly was not the same thing as not thinking it.

She rolled onto her side.

She looked at the wall.

The wall did not help.

She rolled onto her back. She put one forearm across her eyes. She said, out loud, to the ceiling, "Six days." Marjorie did not comment. Marjorie had not commented on anything in eleven years, and her record was not going to be broken tonight.

Deb lay there and listened to the house. The box fan. The crickets. Somewhere, a long way off and only on the stillest minute, an accordion being practised at the Hollenback place three miles up the valley. The accordion stopped. It started again. It stopped again.

She thought: he said I'm just saying.

She thought: the bird hates me.

She thought, finally, about nothing, and she let her arm come down off her eyes and she turned her head to look at the dog, and the dog was looking at her.

The cap was between Marjorie's front paws.

Marjorie had brought it from the porch without anybody seeing her do it, which was how Marjorie did most of her work, and she had taken it to the rug and put it there and put her chin on it and closed her eyes again, and the faded blue of it was the only colour in the room the moon was touching.

Deb looked at the cap for a while, and did not reach for it, and turned her face to the window, and closed her eyes.
