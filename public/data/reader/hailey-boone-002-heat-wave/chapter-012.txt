[Deb.]

She was up before the alarm because she had been up since four, and up since four because she had set the alarm for six and her body had decided the alarm was a suggestion. The bunkhouse was fifty-eight degrees. The window was open. She could hear the owl at the creek doing the last of its shift, and somewhere past the pole barn a rooster that was not Garbage was arguing with a rooster that was Garbage about whose job the sunrise was.

She lay another minute with Marjorie heavy against her shins. Marjorie had slept through four a.m. and five a.m. and the first of the roosters, because Marjorie's policy on early rising was that it was other animals' problem. She was on her back with her head on the pillow Deb had not been using, colonised around two a.m. the way she colonised most of the things Deb did not actively defend.

Deb rubbed the top of her head with two fingers.

Marjorie exhaled through her nose.

Deb got up anyway.


She packed the duffel in the dark because turning the light on felt like a larger decision than she wanted to make before coffee. She had been living out of half the duffel for nine days. The other half had been moving between here and his place with a steadily shrinking list of items that belonged in either. Her toothbrush had been at his house since Tuesday. The shirt she was going to wear on the plane was on the chair by the door, because she had learned a long time ago that nothing made an early flight harder than deciding what to wear at five a.m.

She dressed. Clean jeans. The grey t-shirt she had worn out of Bozeman nine days ago that had somehow survived. The flannel, because airports were cold.

She folded the last two shirts and put them on top of the duffel and zipped the duffel and stood in the middle of the room with her hands on her hips and looked at it.

The room was a room again. The bed was made. The chair was empty. The stack of paperbacks on the little table by the window had gone from four to three because she had finished one — the one about the woman who ran the bookstore, which had been better than she expected, and which she had left on his nightstand on purpose.

She thought, I could.

She did not finish the sentence. She had not finished it in nine days. She was going to finish it in Bozeman, with Janie and the schedule and a cup of the coffee she made at her own house that was, on balance, not as good as Cash's.

She carried the duffel to the porch. Marjorie followed her out with the air of a dog who had heard the word truck in English without it being spoken.


Wyatt was on the main house porch with a mug when she walked up. The yard was the colour of a porch at six a.m. in late June, which was a colour the paint people did not have a word for. Garbage was on the fence post by the gate doing her morning inventory.

He said, "Coffee."

She said, "Yeah."

He handed her the mug he had been about to drink from. He went inside and came back out with another.

She drank. It was, still, the worst coffee in Park County, and it was not a subject.

He said, "Flight's at eight-fifteen."

She said, "I know."

He said, "Drive careful."

She said, "Thirty-five miles."

He said, "The thirty-five miles."

She said, "Yes, sir."

June came out of the front door in a robe over a tank top, the COUGARS mug in one hand, bare feet in boots two sizes too big that Deb was fairly sure were Wyatt's. She came down the steps and hugged Deb without setting the mug down, which meant the hug was one-armed and accurate.

June said, into Deb's shoulder, "Come back soon."

Deb said, "Labor Day."

June pulled back. She said, "Come back when you want. Don't wait for an occasion."

Deb looked at her. She nodded once.

Wyatt came down the steps and took the duffel. He walked it to the rental truck and put it on the passenger seat because the passenger seat was Marjorie's seat and Marjorie was standing next to the truck waiting for somebody to explain the running board situation to her.

He said, "Hey, old lady."

Marjorie looked up at him. She looked at the running board. She looked back at him with the pure expectant indignity of a dog who had been asked, once again, to resolve a structural problem that was not hers.

Wyatt put his coffee on the hood. He got his arms under her the way Deb had taught him on day one. He lifted her onto the passenger seat. She settled onto the seat with her front paws on the dash and her chin on her paws and did not acknowledge him.

Wyatt said, "You're welcome."

Marjorie, through her nose: mm.


Cash's truck pulled into the yard at seven-fifteen.

Deb had known it was coming because she had seen the kitchen light go on across the county road at six-forty, and had seen the porch light, and had, without meaning to, counted the nine minutes between the porch light and the engine starting — six minutes of coffee and three minutes of county road at a speed that was not slow and not fast.

He got out. He had on the trucker hat, MONTANA, faded, still a little too small. He was in clean jeans and a grey shirt, and Deb noticed she was noticing, and did not mind.

He nodded to Wyatt. He nodded to June. He did not say anything to either of them for a second, because Cash at seven a.m. was a man who arrived before he spoke.

He looked at Deb.

He said, "Hey."

She said, "Hey."

Wyatt, who had read approximately eleven thousand porch scenes in his life and knew when to walk off one, picked up his mug from the hood and said to June, "Tomatoes."

June said, "They are still not a subject."

He said, "I am going to look at them."

He walked off toward the garden. June walked after him, which was the second-least subtle thing either of them had done in nine days, and they left Deb and Cash in the yard with the rental truck and the dog on the dash and the slow bright cold of a June morning that had not yet decided what it was going to be.


Cash walked to the driver's side of her truck. She walked to him. They met at the door.

He put his hand on her jaw.

His thumb was at the corner of her mouth, his palm on her skin from a coffee mug fifteen minutes ago. She closed her eyes for half a second. She did not lean in. She let her face be where it was.

He kissed her forehead.

He said, "Drive careful."

She said, "Thirty-five miles."

He said, "The thirty-five miles."

She said, "You and my brother. Word for word."

He said, "He's got sense."

She said, "Occasionally."

He did not take his hand off her jaw for another second. His thumb moved, once, under her ear, which she felt in her ribs more than on her skin.

She said, "Labor Day weekend. Three weeks."

He said, "Three weeks."

He took his hand down. She could feel the place on her jaw where his hand had been for a slow count after he moved it, the way a boot print sits on a porch board for a minute after the boot has gone.

She got in the truck.


Marjorie moved off the dash onto the bench and put her chin on Deb's thigh the way she had put it there on the drive in. Deb scratched the top of her head once. She closed the door. She started the engine. She rolled the window down because her arm always wanted to be out, and the air was sixty-one degrees and she wanted to feel the difference from the heat-wave drive in.

Cash stood by the hood for a second. He took a step back. He put his hand on the hood once, flat, the way a man puts his hand on a horse he is letting walk, and he stepped back again.

Wyatt, from over by the garden, lifted his mug.

June lifted hers.

Deb put the truck in drive.

She did not look in the rearview until she was through the gate, and then she did, and he was still in the yard, and the trucker hat was still on his head, and Marjorie had turned on the bench and was looking through the back window at the yard receding, and Deb thought, she knows.

Marjorie put her chin back on Deb's thigh.


On Route 89 south out of the valley the radio did what radios did in Paradise Valley, which was play the one station that came in, which was the station out of Livingston that did a block of country at the top of the hour. She did not turn the radio on immediately. She drove the first ten miles with the window down and the engine the only sound and Marjorie asleep against her thigh, and the Yellowstone on her right doing the green-glass thing it did at seven-thirty in the morning when the light came down through the cottonwoods at a low angle.

At the turnoff for the swim hole she did not slow down.

She did not look at the turnoff.

She did turn the radio on, because the silence had gone from useful to loud, and the station was doing its block, and the song it was doing was one of the ones she had known since she had been twenty-nine. Shania Twain. The one about the man who didn't impress her much. Deb had heard this song at a bar in Bozeman in 1998 and at a wedding in Missoula in 2003 and she was hearing it now at seven-thirty-one in Park County and the radio was doing the tinny fiddle run and Shania was about to tell some man from the nineties what she thought of him.

Deb left the radio where it was.

She did not change the station. She did not sing along. She drove with her arm out and her dog against her thigh and the Yellowstone on her right, and the song came up through the speakers, and she drove the thirty-five miles to Bozeman with the radio doing what the radio did, and when the song ended and the next one came on she did not change that one either.

At Livingston she crossed the bridge. The Absarokas were behind her now.

Marjorie opened one eye at the change of engine sound and closed it again.

Deb said, to the dog and the windshield, "Three weeks."

Marjorie sighed through her nose.


[Cash.]

Labor Day Friday. Three p.m. The Livingston airport was one room and a baggage carousel that was a belt five feet long, and the belt had not moved yet because the flight had not landed, and the flight had not landed because the flight was four minutes late, which Cash knew because the flight was four minutes late on the board and because he had been watching the board.

Marjorie was on a lead at his left knee. She had been on the lead since eight in the morning, when Wyatt had dropped her at his house, and the lead was a formality, because Marjorie had not shown any inclination to leave his side since the moment the McClaren rental truck pulled out of Wyatt's yard and the McClaren rental truck had the woman in it who fed her. She had sat on Cash's kitchen floor for an hour this morning looking at the door. She had sat on his truck's passenger seat for the twenty-minute drive to Livingston looking at the windshield. She was, at present, sitting on the airport carpet at his left knee looking at the door through which arriving passengers came, and her tail was not moving, because Marjorie did not move her tail for hope. Marjorie moved her tail for confirmed facts.

A woman with two kids and a rolling bag walked by and said, "Good dog."

Cash said, "She's fourteen."

The woman said, "Still."

Marjorie did not look at the woman.

The PA clicked on and a voice said the flight from Bozeman was on the ground. Cash's hand tightened on the lead without his input.


He had been up since four.

He had lied to himself about this. He had told himself, last night, that he was going to sleep the eight hours a man was supposed to sleep, and he had, in fact, gone to bed at nine-forty-five, and he had read three pages of the book she had left on his nightstand in June, and he had turned out the light at ten, and he had been awake at four. He had been awake because a man who had not slept next to a woman since the last Sunday in June was not going to sleep through the Friday she came back. He had accepted this at four-fifteen and he had gotten up.

He had made coffee. He had drunk the coffee on the porch. He had re-hung the screen door on the kitchen that had been hanging wrong since August, because he had been telling himself for three weeks he was going to re-hang it before she got back and he had run out of weeks. He had done the screen door between four-forty and five-thirty. By six he had Marjorie — Wyatt had dropped her off at six, as promised, which was, by Wyatt's standards, first light, and which Cash was going to think about later.

Wyatt had come up the drive in the McClaren truck at five-fifty-eight. He had gotten out with the old duffel that was Marjorie's bed and Marjorie's bowls and the seven specific things that Marjorie considered hers, and he had set them on Cash's porch, and he had lifted Marjorie down from the passenger seat the way he had lifted her on departure morning, and he had said, "She's your problem till Friday night."

Cash had said, "Yep."

Wyatt had said, "June says hi."

Cash had said, "Tell her hi."

Wyatt had looked at him for a second and had not said any of the things he could have said, because Wyatt was, by standing-in-the-world, a man who did not say any of the things he could have said. He had gotten back in the truck and driven off across the county road at five-fifty-nine.

Cash had looked at his watch. Wyatt Reeves had driven a truck at five-forty-five a.m. The last time Wyatt Reeves had driven a truck before eight in the morning was, to Cash's knowledge, the Fourth of July in 2019, and that was because his daughter had a boat thing.

Cash had sat on the porch with Marjorie at his knee and he had thought, Wyatt drove before eight.

Then he had gone inside and made more coffee.


The door at the far end of the terminal opened.

The first person out was a man in a suit with a laptop bag. The second was a woman with a carry-on and a kid on her hip. The third was a kid on his own, about twelve, who walked past the carousel and past Cash and Marjorie and out the door and did not slow down.

The fourth was Deb.

She had on the grey t-shirt she had been wearing the morning she left. She had on jeans. She had the duffel over her shoulder and the flannel tied around her waist because the Livingston airport at three p.m. in September was warmer than the Bozeman plane had been, and she had tied the flannel off somewhere between seat 14A and here. Her hair was shorter than it had been in June by about an inch. Her arms were the brown of a woman who had been outside in Bozeman for six weeks and had stopped caring about it. Her face had the look of a woman who had slept on the plane.

She came through the door.

Marjorie saw her.

Marjorie's tail went.

It did not wag. Marjorie had given up wagging at eleven. What Marjorie did was lift her tail a single inch off the carpet and hold it there, which was, in Marjorie's personal accounting, a parade. The tail went up. It stayed up. Marjorie stood.

Deb stopped.

She was twenty feet from them. She had not yet set the duffel down. She looked at Cash. She looked at Marjorie. She looked at Cash again.

She said, "Marjorie got here at six a.m."

Cash said, "Wyatt dropped her off."

She said, "Wyatt doesn't drive before eight."

Cash said, "He did today."

She was through the rope barrier before she had the duffel off her shoulder. The duffel swung. She did not care. She got to Marjorie first, because Marjorie was closer, and she went down on one knee on the airport carpet and put her forehead on the top of Marjorie's head for a count of three, and Marjorie, for the first time in her fourteen-year life, licked somebody's face in an airport.

Deb said, into the top of her dog's head, "Yeah."

She looked up at Cash.

She stood.


He did not kiss her in the terminal.

He put his hand on the small of her back. He took the duffel off her shoulder with the other hand. He said, "Truck's out front."

She said, "Okay."

He said, "I missed you."

She said, "I know."

They walked.

Marjorie walked between them on the lead, which Deb was holding now, because Cash had handed her the lead when he took the duffel, and Marjorie's pace was Marjorie's pace, which was slow and deliberate and sovereign, and neither of them tried to hurry her. Cash's hand stayed on the small of Deb's back through the terminal and out the door and across the walkway to the short-term lot.

The sun was doing the September thing. It was not the heat-wave sun of June. It was the slant September sun of a Friday at three p.m. in Park County, hot on the shoulders and cool on the bare arms, the specific weight of a sun that was on its way out of summer and had not arrived at fall yet, and the parking lot smelled of asphalt and dust and the last of the cottonwoods giving up their green along Front Street.

At the truck she stopped.

She did not say anything for a second.

She put her face up into the sun. Her eyes were closed. The sun was on her forehead and her cheeks and the bridge of her nose and the spots she had gotten in six weeks of Bozeman August, and she stood there with her face up and her arm around Marjorie's shoulder and her other hand on the top of the truck's fender, and she breathed in once, and she held the breath, and she let it out.

Cash watched her.

He opened the passenger door. He put the duffel in the footwell. Marjorie walked to the running board and looked at it with the expression she reserved for running boards, which was the expression of a dog who had been betrayed by physics one time too many.

Cash said, "Yeah."

He got his arms under her. He put her on the bench seat.

Deb was still standing with her face in the sun.

He came around to her. He put his hand on the small of her back again. He did not hurry her. He waited.

She opened her eyes. She looked at him.

She said, "Okay."

He said, "Okay."

She got in.
