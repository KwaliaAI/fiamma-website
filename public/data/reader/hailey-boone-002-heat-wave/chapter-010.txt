[Cash.]

The gate on the north line had been wrong since April, and Cash had been telling it, and himself, that he would get to it in a week for three months running. He was getting to it now because the heat wave had broken on Wednesday night and the morning was seventy-four and a man could work again without losing his shirt to sweat in the first hour. He had the bad post out and a fresh one in the ground and the cross-piece bolted and he was squaring the hinge when he heard the McClaren rental truck coming up the track.

He did not turn. He knew the sound of that truck by now the way he knew the sound of his own, and he knew what time it was, and the two of those things together told him who was driving.

He set the wrench down.


The truck stopped twenty feet off the fence and Deb got out with a paper sack in one hand and two cans of Coke in the other. Her hair was up off her neck. She was in the same cutoff denim she had worn at the swim hole. Marjorie was not with her. Marjorie had, per a text Cash had got at ten-fifteen, stayed on the McClaren porch with a sovereign refusal to be moved.

Deb said, "June made sandwiches and I was told to bring you one or not come back."

Cash said, "That's a clean threat."

Deb said, "She is a clean woman."

She walked over and handed him the sack. He took it and turned it once to look at it and did not open it yet. He said, "You drove out for this."

She said, "I drove out for the drive. The sandwich was cover."

He said, "You admit it."

She said, "I'm on vacation."


They sat on the tailgate of his truck, parked under the one honest cottonwood on that stretch of fence, with their feet not quite touching the ground. The sky was blue in the way Montana skies were blue in September, which was not September yet but was acting like it. The meadow in front of them was going from green to the first tired gold at the edges, the way a valley did when the heat wave had taken the easy water out of the top six inches. A red-tailed hawk did a slow turn a quarter mile out and came back.

The sandwich was roast beef on the bread June had made yesterday. Cash ate half of it in three bites and said, "Tell June this was competent."

Deb said, "Competent."

He said, "I don't throw that word around."

She said, "I'll pass it on."

She was eating hers at a more reasonable pace. He watched her, not hard, the way a man watched a woman he had slept next to last night and was going to sleep next to again tonight, and she caught him watching and did not comment on it. She drank her Coke.

He said, "Remind me where Marjorie is."

She said, "McClaren porch. Wyatt was in the kitchen and she has decided the kitchen is where lunch is."

He said, "She's not wrong."

She said, "She is never wrong. She is fourteen and her error rate is zero."

He laughed once.


He told her the cattle-drive story because the red-tailed hawk had reminded him of it, or because sitting on a tailgate in the middle of a meadow in the middle of July had reminded him of it, or because he had not told the story in a year and it came up. He did not check which.

He said, "I ever tell you about the drive we did up to the Crazy foothills."

She said, "No."

He said, "Me and Wyatt and the Hollenback boys and her."

He said, "We were driving two hundred head up a piece of country that nobody had driven in about twelve years, and on day two a cow went up a draw it had no business being up, and she went after it on a horse she did not love, and the horse came back without her."

Deb said, "Oh no."

Cash said, "The horse came back at a canter, with the reins on the saddle horn, and we thought we were going to spend the next six hours looking for a body."

Deb said, "I assume you did not."

He said, "We spent about fourteen minutes. She came down the draw on foot with the cow in front of her and a switch in her hand, and she walked past us and put the cow back in the bunch and got up on the horse and said, that horse is a coward, and she rode the rest of the drive like the conversation had not happened."

Deb laughed.

He said, "The cow stayed in the bunch. The horse went back to the ranch and got sold to a guy in Billings who wanted a kid's horse and got one. She did not speak to that horse again."

Deb said, "When was this."

He said, "That was, what, 2019. Yeah, summer of 2019. We had been married about a decade."

He said it without thinking, because he was not thinking, because he was on a tailgate with a woman in cutoffs and a sandwich and a story he had not told in a year and the saying of it had the easy run a story got when you had not said it in a while and it still worked.

Deb took another bite of her sandwich. She chewed it, swallowed, and said nothing for a beat that was a little longer than a beat.


Then she said, "Cash."

He said, "Yeah."

She said, "Soft."

He said, "Okay."

She said, "That's not what you said before."

He said, "What."

She said, "The month she stopped wearing the ring. At the creek you said August. You said she had stopped wearing hers a year before you stopped wearing yours, and you said you stopped wearing yours in August. If that drive was the summer of 2019, and you had been married about a decade then, the math on the end comes out three weeks off from what you told me Tuesday."

He looked at her.

He said, "Montana winters."

She said, "What."

He said, "They run together. It's a whole thing. You lose a month and you find it in March and you put it back."

The joke came out flat. It was a joke a man made when the thing he was really doing was hoping the joke would take. It did not take. It sat on the tailgate between them in the sun, and Deb did not pick it up, and Cash heard it land and not land.

He took a drink of his Coke to cover the next second.

Deb said, "Okay."

She said it quietly. She did not say it like a joke of her own, and she did not say it sharp, and she did not say it angry. She said it like a person closing a door she was not going to open again today if the man did not want it open.

She finished the last bite of her sandwich. She folded the paper the sandwich had been in into quarters. She got down from the tailgate and walked to the trash bin chained to the post by the gate and dropped the paper in it and stood there for a second with her back to him.

He watched her back.

He watched her back, and he watched the hawk come around again, and he saw the shape of what he had just done, which was not the same shape as the thing he had done at June's dinner table on Thursday, but was cousins to it. He had made a joke at her because her question had a real answer in it and he did not want to say the real answer because he had not said the real answer to anyone in six months and he was out of practice.

The difference was that he had seen the quiet.

The difference was that he was the one who had made it.


He said, "Deb."

She turned.

He said, "Come back over here."

She did. She did not hurry, and she did not drag. She came back across the grass and got back up on the tailgate where she had been, two feet off him. She put her hands in her lap.

He said, "I don't know the date."

She looked at him.

He said, "I haven't thought about it in six months. I don't want to lie to you about it."

She nodded, and she did not speak, because she was letting him say it.

He said, "I don't know when she stopped wearing the ring. I know I stopped wearing mine a month after she did. I know the hospice was April-and-a-half. I know the funeral was Memorial Day weekend. I don't know the ring month. I made it up in bed with you on Tuesday night because you asked and I didn't want to say I don't know to you, so I picked August because August sounded right and August was a month."

He stopped.

He had not strung that many sentences together about her in a year. He could feel them on the way out, the shape of them, the small muscle in his jaw working after they had gone. He had his eyes on the hawk because the hawk was a thing to have his eyes on. He brought them back to Deb.

Deb said, "Okay."

He said, "I'm sorry."

She said, "Don't be sorry. Just be accurate next time."

He said, "Yeah."

She said, "Yeah."

He breathed out once, and he had not been aware he had been holding it until it went. He drank the rest of his Coke, looked at the empty can, and set it down on the tailgate between them. She looked at the can, then at him, and she put her hand on his knee, palm flat, and left it there for about the count of five, and took it off again.

He said, "That was quick."

She said, "You did the work."

He said, "Thin work."

She said, "Honest work. That was the part that counted."


They sat for a minute without speaking. The hawk did its circle. A grasshopper went across his boot and off the edge of the tailgate and out into the grass. He ate the second half of his sandwich because it was there and because sitting on a tailgate with a good woman and a good sandwich and a piece of honest work behind him was not a thing to waste by letting bread go stale. He was not going to make a performance of it.

After the second half he said, "I need to finish the hinge."

She said, "Go finish the hinge."

He said, "Drive careful going back."

She said, "The county road is two miles."

He said, "Drive careful the two miles."

She said, "Yes, sir."

She got down off the tailgate. She did not kiss him. She squeezed his forearm once through the shirt and walked to her truck and got in. He watched her drive down the two-track and turn onto the county road and disappear around the cottonwoods, and then he walked back to the fence post and picked up the wrench and finished the hinge.


[Deb.]

The drive back took seven minutes because she did it at twenty-five. She had her window down and her arm out, and the air on her arm was seventy-four degrees for the first time in nine days and she had not got used to it. She parked in the McClaren yard. Marjorie was on the porch, opened one eye at the sound of the engine, and did not get up.

Deb sat in the truck for another minute with the engine off.

She thought: he owned it. He had owned it at a speed that had surprised her. He had deflected once, and she had gone quiet, and he had seen the quiet and come back, and she had not had to say another word for him to say the words he had said, which was a thing she was not sure she had experienced in twelve years of marriage to a perfectly fine oral surgeon from Bozeman.

She thought: you texted Pat from his bathroom at six in the morning.

She had been carrying the text for four days. She had drafted and deleted two apology texts on Thursday night and not sent either of them. She had told herself on Friday morning, with Pete Haverstock's voicemail in her ear, that she would deal with it. She had not dealt with it.

Cash had dealt with his thing in twenty minutes on a tailgate.

She got out of the truck.


She drove to his place at seven.

She had showered. She had put on a clean shirt. She had told June, through the kitchen window, that she was going over for an hour and might be longer. June had said, "Okay," in a tone that was June's whole commentary and had been since Thursday. She had walked Marjorie across the county road on a lead because Marjorie could walk a county road if she had to, and Marjorie had accepted this the way she accepted most things, with minimum effort and maximum dignity.

Cash was on the porch. He had a beer in his hand and a second beer on the arm of the chair next to his, which was the chair his father had burned in 1996, which had the burn under Deb's elbow when she sat down and took the second beer. Marjorie went to the porch boards and flattened.

Cash said, "You came back."

She said, "I came back."

He said, "This is a social visit."

She said, "Partly. I need to tell you a thing."

He said, "Okay."

He said it the way he had said okay on Thursday at the swim hole when he had taken the beer from her on the bank, which was the okay of a man who was going to hear a thing. He did not sit up. He did not take a drink. He had his beer on his thigh and his other hand open on the arm of the chair.

She said, "On Tuesday morning in your bathroom I texted Pat Fosse, who is a grief counsellor in Helena who I have known for nine years, and I told her I had a friend who was widowed two years and might benefit from a check-in, and I asked if I could pass her number along. I sent the text before I had thought it through, which is not a thing I usually do. I knew it was wrong while the Delivered was showing up on the screen. I did not tell you. June told me on Thursday evening before dinner that Wyatt knew, because the name Pat Fosse had come up through some county thing, and I told you about it on the porch after dinner, and I told you by explaining before I apologised, which is the wrong order. I have been carrying the rest of it since. I am telling you now because you did the thing on the fence line today and it would be unserious of me not to do mine."

He listened.

He did not interrupt. He did not drink. He kept his eyes on her until she was done, and when she was done he took a small drink of his beer and put it back on his thigh.

He said, "Okay. Did Pat call?"

She said, "She called June, asking after you. June told me."

He said, "So she hasn't called me."

She said, "She hasn't called you."

He said, "Okay."

He took another drink of his beer. He watched the Absaroka range doing its evening thing. He said, "If she calls, I'll tell her I'm good. She'll know what that means."

He said it with a small edge at the corner of his mouth, because I'm good was a sentence he had said at June's dinner table forty-eight hours ago and been seen saying, and he had been the one seeing it ever since.

Deb saw the edge. She exhaled a laugh that was not quite a laugh.

She said, "That's not funny."

He said, "It's a little funny."

She said, "A little."

They sat for a while.

She said, "I shouldn't have."

He said, "I know. Don't do it again."

She said, "I won't."

He said, "I believe you."

She said, "Okay."

He said, "Okay."


They drank their beers. The sun was doing the thing it did at seven-forty in the Paradise Valley in the summer, which was the slow descent through the specific colours of the Absaroka range. Orange on the front faces. Gold on the ridgelines. Grey coming up from the draws. Marjorie on the porch boards twitched her back paw once in a dream and went still. Cash did not say anything for ten minutes, and Deb did not say anything for ten minutes, and it was not the silence of people avoiding something. It was the silence of people who had both, in the same day, said the thing and been heard saying it, and were sitting inside that weather.

He got up at eight and went inside and came back with two fresh beers.

She said, "Two-beer night?"

He said, "I'm driving nowhere and you're driving nowhere."

She said, "Fair."

He said, "Luke's at Kenny's until ten."

She said, "Is that a schedule or a report?"

He said, "Report."

She said, "Cash."

He said, "Yeah."

She said, "I'm staying."

He said, "I know."

She said, "I wasn't asking."

He said, "I wasn't either."


They sat on the porch until the sun went. The range went grey, and then the grey went dark, and then the first stars came out over the south ridge, and somewhere down on the river the owl that had been the summer's owl made its call for the night. Marjorie had not moved. Deb's second beer was half full and warm. Cash's was empty. He put his empty can on the porch boards between his boots and did not get up for another.

At nine-forty Luke's friend's mother dropped Luke off at the end of the drive, and Luke walked up the gravel with his gym bag over one shoulder and stopped at the porch step and looked at his father and Deb with the specific stillness of a thirteen-year-old boy assessing a situation that did not require his intervention.

Luke said, "Hey."

Cash said, "Hey."

Deb said, "Hey."

Luke said, "There pizza."

Cash said, "Freezer."

Luke nodded once and went inside. The door banged. The kitchen light came on. A cabinet door opened. A freezer door opened. Water ran.

Deb said, "Three words."

Cash said, "Running total?"

She said, "I have been counting since Tuesday."

He said, "That's unsettling."

She said, "Fifteen."

He said, "He's got four more in him tonight."

She said, "We'll see."

They did not see. Luke ate his pizza in front of a game on his phone in the kitchen and said exactly four more words to his father on his way upstairs, which was, "Night. Your truck smells," and Cash said, "I know it does," and Luke went up.

Deb said, "Nineteen."

Cash said, "I'm going to be proud of him."


They went inside at ten. The house was the temperature of a house that had been at seventy-four all day and not worked to stay there. She brushed her teeth at his sink with the spare toothbrush she had left on Wednesday night. He turned the fan on low and opened the window the three inches Luke would not hear it through, and he got into bed in his boxers, and she got in in his shirt, and the sheet was enough because it was seventy-four and going to sixty-five by morning.

She put her hand on his chest in the dark.

He put his hand over hers.

The fan turned. The owl did one more call down at the river. Marjorie, on the rug by the bed where she had arranged herself without input, breathed the old-dog breath she breathed. Cash's heart went under Deb's palm, and her palm stayed under his, and neither of them said anything, and in that order they fell asleep.
