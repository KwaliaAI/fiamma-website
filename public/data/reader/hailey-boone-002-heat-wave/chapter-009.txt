[Cash.]

He was awake at six-fifteen the way a rancher was awake at six-fifteen, without alarm and without effort, and Deb was asleep on his chest with her hair in his face and one hand flat on his ribs where it had landed at some point in the night. The fan on the dresser was on its middle setting, the sheet was on the floor, and the window was open. The storm had come through at two in the morning with a flash that went horizon to horizon, and an hour of rain loud enough on the metal roof to wake anyone who had not been awake already, and both of them had been awake already. Now, at six-fifteen, the light was the washed-clean light of a valley that had been at a hundred degrees for nine days and was, at last, at seventy-four. He could hear a blackbird and, under that, the gravel of his own drive drying. He could smell, through the window, the wet-sage smell of high country after rain.

He did not move for ten minutes.

He was not a man who lay still for ten minutes. He was thinking, in the way a man thinks who has decided not to think, about the fact that she had told him last night sitting on his steps with a beer that she had told Janie she was staying a couple more days, and that she had said it looking at the county road and not at him, and that he had said Okay because Okay was all he was going to say to that.

Deb shifted an inch in her sleep, made a small noise against his collarbone, and settled again. His arm around her shoulder was asleep below the elbow. He was going to let the arm go numb for another ten minutes because moving it would wake her up, and he was not ready for her to be awake yet.

At six-thirty a door opened down the hall.

Luke's door. The specific creak of the hinge Cash had been meaning to oil since Christmas. Feet in the hall. They paused outside his door, which was shut but not latched, and the pause was the pause of a thirteen-year-old boy who had heard his father moving and now heard that his father was not moving, and the boy was making a decision. Cash listened. The pause resolved. The feet went past and on down the stairs. A cupboard opened in the kitchen. A cereal box shook.

His son had not come in. His son had chosen not to come in, which was the same fact twice, and both times the fact was that his son was going to be fine.

The carpool was at seven. He had put out the clean shirt and the jeans last night and the lunch bag was in the fridge and there was nothing the kid needed from him this morning except to not be asked about the closed bedroom door, which was the one thing Cash could provide. At six-fifty Luke put the cereal bowl in the sink, at six-fifty-eight the screen door banged, and at seven sharp the Taylor's blue Civic pulled in and pulled out again two minutes later.

The house was empty.

His arm was past numb and into a buzzing he was going to regret. He moved his free hand to her shoulder blade and kept it there.


[Deb.]

"Dad! Where are my shin guards!"
