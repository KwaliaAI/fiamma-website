She woke at five forty-five in a bed that was not hers, and she knew within the first second exactly where she was and how she had got there.

Her eyes had opened onto an unfamiliar ceiling with a crack in the plaster along the east corner, and she had thought, in plain words, Cash's ceiling, and then about the walk across the county road at eleven last night, picking up her tank top and his belt off the barn floor in the near-dark with his hand at the small of her back.

He was asleep on the right side of the bed. On his stomach, one arm over his head, the sheet down at his hips. His back in the dim was the long brown plane she had learned in the afternoon. She looked at it for about four seconds and then looked at the ceiling again, because she was going to get up.

The light was just starting on the east side of the window. The hour before morning, when the sky goes from black to the deep blue it goes to before the grey starts.

She eased both legs out of the sheet and sat on the edge of the bed. The floor was old board, cooler than the air. She stood without using her hands on the mattress, which was a trick she had learned at thirty-eight. His grey t-shirt was on the floor two feet from her right foot. She picked it up and pulled it on over her head. It came down to her thighs and smelled like him and like hay, in that specific order. She had not put on a man's shirt the morning after since the oral surgeon, and the oral surgeon had been a wearer of pyjama tops, which was a different category of garment.

Cash had not moved. She went to the bathroom.


The hall was six feet long, wide pine board with a seam every third one. She stepped through the open door and closed it behind her with two fingers on the latch so the click did not carry.

The bathroom was small and tiled in a hex pattern that had to be original. Half the hexes were white and half were the pale grey the manufacturer had probably called dove, and the grout was older than she was. Clawfoot tub, pedestal sink, cotton curtain pulled to over the window. The light was not on. The light was coming in the window, and the window faced east, and the sky was doing its blue.

She used the toilet, flushed it once, washed her hands at the sink. The water was cold and then got colder, because it was a well, and a well did not care about your wrists. She splashed some onto her face, ran her wet hands through her hair, which was a hayloft-and-a-walk-and-a-bed disaster, the grey at her temples doing its own thing.

She looked at the mirror.

The mirror was rectangular and a little foxed at the bottom corner, and she had not seen herself in it before. The woman in the mirror had a grey t-shirt on that went to the top of her thighs, bare feet on cold hex tile, hair she was not going to fix without a brush, a pink mark on her collarbone she had not known was there, and a face that had slept about four hours and was not showing it the way it would have at thirty.

She thought, Well, look at you.

Downstairs the percolator started, bubbling under a blue flame. He had set it up the night before, because of course he had, which meant he had done it while she had been lying across his chest around midnight doing the minimum talking two people did after walking a quarter mile in the dark from a barn to a bed. A small luxury, waking up to it.

She put her hands flat on the sink.

He had a teenager. She had known this from Wyatt, she had known it yesterday in the water, and she had gone on ahead anyway. Luke was presumably asleep down the hall on the other side of the stairs, and Luke was thirteen, and Luke had lost his mother two years ago when the man in the bed at the other end of the hall had lost his wife at forty-four.

She had known that too, and she had walked into the loft anyway, because she was forty-five and he was forty-six and they had both put their eyes on the road with the road going where it said it was going.

Except.

The widowed-two-years-teen-son data had been parked in her head in the particular folder her head had, and the folder had been closed all afternoon and evening yesterday, and the folder was open now because she was alone in a bathroom staring at a half-inch circle on the mirror where somebody had wiped fog off with the side of a hand a long time ago.

She was thinking: has he talked to anybody.

She was thinking: Pat would be good for this.

Her phone was on the edge of the sink. The cutoffs she had shucked last night must have had it in the back pocket, and she must have grabbed it when she came in. Her hand did the thing her hand did, which was pick up a phone.

She opened Messages. Patricia Fosse, MD was the third name down. She tapped it. The thread had two lines in it from March, short and professional, a referral Pat had taken and thanked her for. She tapped the box and typed fast, the way she typed when she was dispatching a diagnosis to a client at eight in the evening from her truck and did not have time to edit.

Hey Pat. Friend of mine, widowed two years, teen son, doing okay I think but could use a check-in. I might give him your number, cool?

She read it back once. She hit send.

The blue bar went along the top of the screen and filled and the message went, and the two checkmarks came up grey under it.

She put the phone down. She turned the tap on and washed her hands again, for no reason she could have defended, and the water was still cold, and she felt the small prickle across the back of her neck a woman gets when she has done a thing that her body knows about before her head does.

The two checkmarks on the phone went blue.

She saw it out of the corner of her eye. Pat was up. Pat was a five a.m. runner, always had been, and Pat was probably standing at her own sink in Helena with a glass of water in her hand.

Deb turned the tap off.

She knew what she had done before she had finished drying her hands on the navy towel on the ring beside the sink. She had texted a grief counsellor about the man asleep down the hall. She had not told him. She had not asked. She had done it in his bathroom at five forty-nine on the morning of the first Thursday they were going to have.

The rationalisation gave itself back in the register in which she had typed the message: she was helping, Pat was discreet, it was a name and a number, he did not have to call, and if he did not call she would never tell him the text had been sent. It was the work of twenty-eight seconds between the first thought and the send, and it was the thing that should have stopped her mid-send and had not.

She put the phone face-down.

The woman in the mirror had a different face than the face of four minutes ago. The face knew.

She said, quietly, "Well."


She came down the stairs barefoot. The third one creaked and she went to the outside of it on the way down. The kitchen at the bottom was the kitchen of a man who had been running a house with a thirteen-year-old for two years, which meant everything had a place, and most of the places had been respected. A whiteboard on the fridge with SPRINKLERS under FRIDAY in his handwriting. A fruit bowl with one apple and three limes.

He was at the counter with a mug in his hand. Jeans on, no shirt, hair damp. He had already splashed water on his face, and she could see the line on his collar where he had missed with the towel.

He said, "Hey."

She said, "Hey."

She stood in his shirt in his kitchen and he took about half a second with it, and then he handed her his mug. He said, "Take this one. I'll pour another."

She took it. The coffee was black and hot and good. She drank a swallow standing up.

She said, "This is actually good."

He said, "I know."

She said, "Wyatt makes terrible coffee."

He said, "Everyone knows."

He poured his own into a white mug chipped on the rim and leaned against the counter across from her.

He said, "You sleep."

She said, "Four hours. Hard."

He said, "Same."

She said, "Toast?"

He said, "Toast exists. Eggs exist."

She said, "Both."

He pushed off the counter and got bread out of the breadbox and two eggs out of the fridge door, with the economy of a man who had done it before. She sat at the table in his shirt and watched him crack the eggs into a cast-iron pan one-handed, each, which was a small competence she had not known she was a fan of until now.

Her phone was in her other hand. She had brought it down without thinking about why and she knew why now. She was holding it like it was hot. The screen was dark and was going to stay dark until after lunch, because Pat had a ten a.m. first client on Thursdays.

He put a plate down in front of her. Two eggs over easy, two slices of wheat toast, a piece of butter on a smaller plate. He had already buttered his. He sat across from her with his coffee and his plate and he ate.

The east window had gone from blue to grey to the first colour, a pale gold about to be a peach. It was five after six.

She ate her eggs.

She said, "I'm going to walk back."

He said, "I can drive you."

She said, "It's a mile. I want the air."

He said, "Okay."

That was the whole negotiation. He was not a man who pushed a ride on a woman who wanted to walk. He finished his eggs and took the plates to the sink and ran water over them, because he was also a man who rinsed.

She set the mug down. She closed her fingers around the phone once to check. It was just a phone. It was room temperature. Her palm was making it sixteen degrees hotter than it was.

He came back to the table.

He said, "I've got to go out at the fence line by eight. Luke's up at seven-thirty. You want a water bottle?"

She said, "I'm fine."

He said, "Take one anyway."

He got a dented metal one off the counter, filled it, and set it on the table in front of her.

She said, "Your shirt."

He said, "Keep it for the walk."

She said, "I'll bring it back."

He said, "Don't. Bring yours back. I like this one."

She laughed. She had not been sure the morning was going to produce one. She held it in her chest and let it out through her nose.


At the back door he kissed her once with his hand on the side of her face. A morning kiss with the sun just over the Absaroka. He said into the kiss, "Come back later."

She said, "I'll see."

He said, "Is that a no."

She said, "That's a probably."

He said, "I'll take it."

He stepped back. She stepped into the boots she had kicked off last night on the mat and tied them. She pulled his shirt off over her head. He already had her tank top in his hand, and she had not seen him get it. She put it on with her cutoffs and the flannel, rolled his shirt, and handed it to him.

He said, "I said keep it."

She said, "For the walk, you said. I'm walking in mine."

He said, "Fine."

He did not argue. She went out the back door and down the two steps onto the cinder path.


The walk was a mile of county road through the valley at six-thirty with the sun coming up behind her over the range and the shadows of the cottonwoods laid long across the blacktop in front of her. The air smelled of grass and dust and, from a quarter mile off, the specific smell of manure that a person was either born to or never would be. She was born to it.

She walked with her hands in her back pockets. The phone was in one of them, a flat small presence against her. It had not buzzed and it was not going to.

At the half-mile mark she stopped on the shoulder. A pickup went the other way, and the driver lifted his index finger off the wheel in the county greeting, and she lifted hers back without looking at his face. She did not know who he was and he did not know who she was, and the county greeting was for both of them anyway.

She thought about texting Pat a retraction. Hey, scratch that. Never mind. Deb. Pat would get it. Pat would not ask. Pat had been a professional friend for nine years, and the nine years had included the category of never mind.

She thought about it for about twenty seconds. She did not text it, and she started walking again.

She did not text it because she could not un-read the thing Pat had already read. Pat's head now had a piece of furniture in it that Pat's head had not had at five forty-six, which was Deb's friend, widowed two years, teen son, Deb thinks might want to call, and a retraction was not going to take the furniture out. A retraction would only add a second piece, which was Deb sent the first thing without thinking, and then thought, and she did not like either piece being in Pat's head about a man she had not asked.

She walked. The laces on her boots had hay on them, and she was going to leave the hay there, because it was the hay from the loft, and she was not going to pretend anything did not happen by cleaning her laces on the shoulder of a county road at six forty.


She got to the McClaren gate at seven twenty-five.

The rental truck was where she had left it. Garbage was doing whatever Garbage did at this hour, which appeared to involve the corner of the porch and an attitude. Marjorie was on the bunkhouse porch with her chin on the boards, and she opened one eye when Deb came up the drive and did not lift her head. The dog had an opinion about the last eighteen hours and the opinion was that Marjorie had not been consulted.

Deb said, "Hey."

Marjorie closed the eye.

Deb went past the bunkhouse to the main house, because the kitchen light was on, and she could smell Wyatt's bad coffee, the specific over-extracted bad of a man who brewed the grounds twice because he was sentimental about them.

Wyatt was at the kitchen table in a t-shirt with a coffee mug and a section of the Livingston Enterprise he had been pretending to read since before she got to the porch. June was at the sink with her back to the door. They had heard the truck last night and heard no truck come back, and they had already decided what they were and were not going to say. Deb had been their sister and sister-in-law long enough to spot the decision by the set of their shoulders through the screen.

She opened the screen.

Wyatt said, without looking up, "Coffee's hot."

She said, "Wyatt, your coffee is never hot. It's tepid and tragic."

He said, "It's hot this morning."

June turned at the sink. She looked at Deb for one second, flat and pleasant, the one second a woman gives to the sister-in-law who has walked back a mile from somebody else's house in yesterday's tank top.

She said, "There's eggs."

Deb said, "I ate."

June said, "At Cash's."

Wyatt turned a page of the paper.

Deb said, "Yep."

June said, "He cooks eggs?"

Deb said, "He cooks eggs."

June nodded once and looked at Wyatt. Wyatt did not look up from the paper. June turned back to the sink and rinsed her hand and bit the corner off the toast. The exchange had taken eleven seconds and it had contained the entirety of what June and Wyatt were going to say about the night.

Deb said, "I'm going to shower."

Wyatt said, "Shower's hot."

She said, "Shower will be fine."

She went out the kitchen door and down the porch steps and across the yard to the bunkhouse, where Marjorie did not lift her head a second time. She went inside and straight to her bunk room and shut the door behind her.


She sat on the edge of the bunk.

She took the phone out of her back pocket and put it on the little pine nightstand Hal had built for this room sometime in the eighties. Face-up. The screen was dark. She looked at it for ten seconds. The screen stayed dark, and she did not touch it.

She went to the dresser and pulled a clean t-shirt and underwear and denim cutoffs out of her duffel, went into the bathroom off the bunk room, shucked yesterday's clothes into a pile on the tile, and stepped into the shower.

The water was cold first and then better than she would have called it. She stood under it with her eyes closed.

She did not cry. She was not a cryer. She did not narrate to herself what she had done, because she had already narrated it accurately, and she was not going to improve it with a repeat.

She got out, dried off, wrapped the towel around herself, and sat on the edge of the bed.

The phone was where she had left it on the pine nightstand. She looked at it. She sat there for ten minutes.

She did not pick it up. She did not open the Messages app and re-read the text. She knew what it said. She had typed it. She did not delete it either, because you could not delete a text that had been read, and even if you could she was not going to try to. A thing she had done was a thing she had done, and she was going to sit with it wet in a towel at seven fifty on a Thursday morning.

She sat.

The crickets had started up in the field outside the window. The window was open. They had the slow, businesslike pace they had at this time of year. A horsefly went past on the other side of the screen and went on. The cottonwoods made the sound cottonwoods made.

At eight she got dressed. She put on the clean tank and the clean cutoffs and the boots, because her other boots were by the door and she was not walking back into her own life barefoot. She picked up the phone and put it in her back pocket.

She opened the door and walked down the short hall of the bunkhouse and out onto the porch, where Marjorie lifted her head finally and put it down again, and she went across the yard toward the main house, because the kitchen was where the coffee was, and the coffee was what was next.
