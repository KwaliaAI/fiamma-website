I found him in the corridor outside the supply room, standing with his hands in his pockets and his linen shirt untucked, which for Edouard was the equivalent of appearing in public naked. He was looking at the stone wall. Just standing and looking at it as though the wall had said something and he was deciding whether to respond.

"You found it," he said.

I had a box of test strips in one hand and a tray of accord samples in the other. I'd been checking materials. The iris butter was holding, the labdanum was stable, and V4 was sitting on three strips in my lab, existing at a frequency that made my hands shake when I smelled it.

"Found what?"

He turned. His face was different this morning. The performance was off. Edouard's face was always on, always broadcasting, a one-man variety show of opinion and commentary and wit, and this morning the broadcast had gone quiet and what was underneath it was a seventy-one-year-old man who looked tired.

"The thing in the formula that scares you. I can see it from here. It's in your posture." He gestured at me. "You're carrying those strips like they contain evidence."

I set the tray down on the stone ledge that ran along the corridor wall. "I found something. I don't know what it is yet."

"You know exactly what it is. You're afraid of what it means." He leaned against the wall. His shoulder on the stone, his body at an angle, and the angle was wrong for Edouard. Too casual. He was using the wall because he needed it, and Edouard never needed walls. "I made a formula once. For my wife. My first wife. Isabelle."

I waited.

"1994. We'd been married for six years. She asked me for something she could wear every day. Something that was hers. I said, 'I've been making perfume for thirty years, you've never asked.' She said, 'I'm asking now.'" He smiled at the memory, and the smile was smaller than his usual production. Private. "So I made it. Took me four months. The longest I've ever spent on a personal commission, and I'd built for Dior, for Chanel, for half the houses in Paris. Four months for one woman."

"What was it?"

"Rose absolute. Moroccan. With a musk base I'd been saving for something I couldn't name, and when she asked, I knew that was what I'd been saving it for. The rose did what rose does. The musk made it hers."

He was quiet for a moment. The corridor was cold. Morning light through the cloister windows, thin and blue.

A door opened somewhere deeper in the building. The kitchen, probably. The sound of a tray being set on stone, and then Gianluca's radio, faint, some Italian ballad with a tenor who sounded personally wounded. Edouard waited for the singer to hit his note and miss it.

"Flat," he said. "Every morning. The man is consistently flat."

He picked up the thread as if the interruption had been scheduled.

"She wore it for twelve years. Every day. I could smell it on her coat, on her pillow, on the letters she left on my desk. The formula lived on her skin and became part of her body chemistry and after twelve years I couldn't tell where Isabelle ended and the perfume began."

"Edouard."

"We divorced. She moved to Lyon with a cellist. A cellist." He said this without bitterness, or with a bitterness so old it had oxidised into something else. Amusement, maybe. Or just tiredness. He'd told this story enough times that the edges had smoothed. "And she kept wearing it. She still wears it. I saw her two years ago at a funeral. Our friend Michel. I walked into the church and I could smell it from the third pew. The rose. The musk. My formula. On the skin of a woman who left me for a man who plays the cello."

I shifted against the wall. "She kept wearing it? After she left you for someone else?"

"You think she should have stopped?"

"I think most people would burn it."

"Most people have not spent four months making something for a woman they loved. The burning instinct belongs to the audience. The creator does not burn." He said this with more force than anything before it. A crack in the telling. "The creator watches someone else wear the fire."

He straightened up. Took his hands out of his pockets. Put them back in.

"The best thing I ever made lives on the skin of a woman who left me. That is perfumery, Margot. You build something true and you give it away and it becomes someone else's and you don't get it back. Your formula scares you because it should. If it doesn't scare you, it isn't yours."

From the courtyard, Elena's clipboard hit a table. The sound was sharp and specific and broke the corridor's hush like a plate on tiles. We both looked toward the archway. Nothing. Elena conducting her morning logistics with her usual acoustic signature.

"She has dropped that clipboard seventeen times," Edouard said. "I am counting."

He picked up one of my test strips from the tray. Held it to his nose. Closed his eyes. Three seconds. Put it down.

"Yes," he said. "Be afraid of that." And he walked away down the corridor, his untucked shirt catching the light from the cloister windows, his armour slipping back on with every step.

* * *

The lab. V4 on the bench. Two strips side by side.

The first strip was the safe version. The formula I could have made. The one that balanced the labdanum and the iris with a textbook bridge, clean transitions, professional proportions. It smelled like desire with a seat belt on. Controlled and technically excellent, the kind of formula that wins prizes by not offending anyone on the panel.

The second strip was V4. The formula I was making. The one where the labdanum fought the iris and the iris fought back and the centre held like a breath. The chinotto opening bit. The oud underneath was doing something slow and irreversible.

I held the safe version to my nose. Pretty. Accomplished. A formula that would get me through the panel and the commission and Bergmann & Cole's supply terms and Ferranti's emails and the number with the comma.

I held V4 to my nose. My chest opened. My fingers tightened on the strip. The formula climbed through my sinuses and sat in my sternum and said hello, you know me, you've been building me your whole life and you're only just meeting me now.

I put the safe version in the bin. Picked up my pipette. Committed.

* * *

The courtyard. Afternoon. The lemon tree was doing its thing, existing in a state of perfection that I found personally offensive given the general state of my life. Sun through the branches, the fruit impossibly yellow, the leaves glossy and dark. I was sitting on the bench with my notebook open and a pen in my teeth and my brain running calculations I should have been running in the lab but couldn't, because sometimes a formula needs you to leave the room before it'll tell you what it wants.

Leo came through the archway carrying a wrench.

I took the pen out of my mouth. "What broke?"

"Shutter hinge. Second floor. Elena asked." He sat on the other end of the bench. Set the wrench between us. Looked at the lemon tree.

I waited. With Leo, you waited. Talking to Leo was like coaxing a cat off a high shelf. Any sudden movement and he'd retreat into competence and monosyllables.

"I don't like competitions," he said.

I looked at him. He was looking at the lemons.

"You're in one."

"I know." He stretched his legs out. Work boots, laces untied. "You're asking five people to stand next to the same sunset and describe different colours. Someone wins. The sunset doesn't care."

"That's surprisingly poetic for someone who communicates in tool-repair."

He almost smiled. The left side of his mouth shifted a millimetre. His version of a standing ovation. "The coffee shop," he said.

"The six years?"

"Brooklyn. Red Hook. A roaster on Van Brunt Street run by a woman named Pia who weighed green beans like they were gems. I pulled shots for six years. Learned extraction curves, water chemistry, dial-in protocols. All of it."

"You liked it."

"I liked it too much." He picked up the wrench, turned it in his hands. Long fingers, blunt nails, a scar across the right knuckle that looked old. "I was building a life around a job that smelled good and paid badly. I needed to build a life around what I was actually good at."

"And what are you actually good at?"

He looked at me. "Finishing things."

The sentence sat in the courtyard air. Finishing things. He didn't mean coffee. He didn't mean shutter hinges. He meant: I start something and I stay with it until it's done, and the staying is the skill, and everything else is decoration. I thought about V4 on the bench. The commitment I'd made an hour ago. The safe version in the bin.

"Your review of DIRTY," he said.

My stomach did a thing. "You read that?"

"Nez magazine. Two years ago. Issue forty-one." He set the wrench down. "You wrote that the drydown punished the wearer for enjoying the opening."

I had written that. Fourteen hundred words for Nez about a fragrance called DIRTY by a perfumer identified only by initials. L.H. I'd praised the opening, dissected the heart, and taken the drydown apart because it betrayed the promise of the first thirty minutes. The piece was honest. The piece was possibly cruel. I'd filed it at midnight and thought nothing of it until now, sitting on a bench in Positano while the man who made DIRTY quoted my sentences back to me.

"That made me angry for two days," he said. "Then I smelled it again and you were right."

"I was hard on it."

"You were accurate. Hard and accurate aren't the same thing, but in that review they were."

A pigeon landed on the fountain rim. Looked at us. Left.

"What's in your formula?" he said. "The heart."

I could have lied. I could have deflected, the way you do in competitions, guard your materials, keep the recipe behind the wall. But Edouard had just given me his wound in a corridor, and Leo was sitting three feet away with untied boots and a wrench between us and his own formula threading through my lab every night through a medieval ventilation system, and the walls were already down.

"Labdanum," I said. "It's the anchor. Everything else orbits it."

He nodded. Slowly. He'd suspected. Now he was deciding how much of that confirmation to show. It confirmed the grille. The air between our labs. The nights I'd spent breathing in his formula while he breathed in mine. He knew what labdanum smelled like coming through old iron, and now he knew it was the centre, and his nod said yes, I thought so.

"And yours?" I said.

He picked up the wrench. Stood. "Not yet." He walked toward the archway, the wrench swinging from his right hand, and I watched him go and I didn't push it. He'd given me more words in fifteen minutes than in the previous five days combined. The formula could wait.

* * *

Evening. The stairs. Seventy-three of them, and by Day 6 we had a system. Elena pressed button one. Leo pressed button two at twenty-three. I pressed button three at forty-nine. Edouard provided the soundtrack.

"And the Frenchman," Edouard announced, starting his descent with one hand on the railing, narrating himself like a nature documentary, "weakened by wine and age, begins his nightly migration to the feeding grounds. He travels in a herd for protection. His natural predators include steep gradients, insufficient lighting, and the hubris of medieval architects."

"You haven't had wine yet," Elena said from below.

"I am preparing. The body must anticipate the wine. This is French science."

Yuki was already at the bottom. She was always already at the bottom. I'd stopped trying to understand how she moved through space. She simply departed and arrived, and the interval between the two events contained no observable effort.

The timer ran out at step thirty-eight. Darkness. Leo's phone torch came on. Elena found the button at forty-nine. Sixty-two seconds. She'd been right about the timer.

"Sixty-two," she said. "I told Chiara. She said she'd look into it."

"Chiara will look into it," Edouard said. "With a clipboard and an expression that suggests looking into it is beneath her."

We reached the bottom. The town opened up. Vespa exhaust and jasmine and frying oil and the sea underneath all of it, the base note of Positano, salt and depth. The alley. The Christmas lights blinking their irregular schedule. The doorway.

Paola pointed at our table. "Sit."

We sat.

Luca brought wine. Red, the carafe, same as every night. He set it down and his hand brushed Elena's shoulder and it was brief, the smallest contact, but Elena's face changed for half a second, a softness that arrived and departed so quickly I almost missed it. They had their own thing now. It was new and it was quiet and neither of them was performing it for the table.

"Tonight," Edouard said, pouring wine like a sommelier, loudly, because Edouard has never poured anything quietly, "I want to hear about failure."

"Why?" Elena asked.

"Because we have spent six days being excellent. Excellence is boring. Failure is instructive, humiliating, and pairs better with wine."

He looked around the table. The candles. The photographs on the walls. Paola's family across decades.

"I'll start," I said. "Two years ago. Department store commission. They wanted something for their fragrance counter. Clean, modern, accessible. I made it in three weeks, fastest I've ever worked. Delivered it. They tested it in-store."

"And?"

"Under fluorescent lights, the top notes shifted. Something in the formula reacted to the ambient temperature of the retail floor. The sales staff said it smelled different from the sample. The client called it 'institutional.' Like a hospital. They shelved it."

"Did it smell like a hospital?"

"It smelled like a hospital lobby where someone had spilled expensive perfume. Which is worse than a hospital, because a hospital has an excuse."

Edouard laughed. He drank. "Good. Good. My turn." He set his glass down. "1987. I am thirty-four. I have been commissioned by a house in Paris to make a fragrance for their autumn launch. The brief is 'sophisticated evening.' I interpret this as: smoky, dark, a fragrance that enters a room before the wearer and apologises to no one. I work for five months. I present it to the creative director. A man named Philippe who wore his scarves indoors."

He paused. Edouard's pauses were always scored.

"Philippe smells it. He puts down the strip. He looks at me. He says: 'Edouard. This smells like a fireplace in a brothel.' I say: 'That was the intention.' He says: 'The intention was sophisticated evening, not arson in a sex hotel.' I lost the commission. Philippe gave it to a twenty-six-year-old from Versailles who made something that smelled like talcum powder with ambition. It sold three hundred thousand units."

"Did you keep the formula?"

"I kept the formula. I wear it every November. It still smells like a fireplace in a brothel. Philippe was right. I was also right. These are compatible facts."

He turned to Leo. The table turned to Leo. Leo was holding his wine glass with both hands. Holding it like he needed something to hold.

"A formula I spent two years on that someone else registered."

The table went quiet. Candle flames moving. Paola's kitchen sounds behind the door.

Edouard set his glass down. "That is not failure. That is theft."

"It's both," Leo said.

His voice was flat. The same flat delivery he used for everything, but underneath it something had shifted, a tectonic plate moving half an inch, and the half inch was enough to change the landscape. He didn't elaborate. He picked up his wine and drank and the drinking was a period at the end of a sentence and the sentence was closed.

Edouard looked at him for a long moment. Then he picked up his glass and raised it, slightly, a gesture so small it could have been a tremor, and it was the most respectful thing I'd seen him do in six days.

Yuki put down her fork. "A scent I made for my mother. She said it smelled like someone else's memory." She picked up her fork. That was the entire story. The table absorbed it in silence, with the understanding that elaboration was not available and would not be offered.

Luca arrived with plates. Sea bass again, Paola's, roasted, the skin crisped and the flesh white. He set Edouard's plate down with an extra serving of pasta on the side, penne with oil and garlic, placed without comment, without being asked.

Edouard looked at the pasta. Looked at Luca. Looked back at the pasta.

"He is trying to seduce me with carbohydrates," he said to the table. "It's working."

Luca grinned and went back to the kitchen. At the doorway he turned and caught Elena's eye, a quick look across the room, and Elena held it for one second before looking down at her plate. A look that had a history. Six days of history, new but not nothing.

Edouard ate his extra pasta with deliberate pleasure. For Edouard, food is a moral act. The table settled. The failure conversation had done what failure conversations do when they're real: it had stripped a layer. We were closer to the surface of each other now. Leo's stolen formula hung in the air like a note I couldn't place. DIRTY. The drydown that punished the wearer. Two years of work registered by someone else. The pieces were there. I didn't assemble them.

* * *

The stairs. Going up. Elena pressed the button. We climbed.

I fell in beside Leo on the second flight. His stride was longer than mine and he shortened it without being asked.

"The formula someone registered," I said.

He stopped. One step above me, his face half in the timer light, half in shadow. The light gave him edges. The shadow took them away.

"Not tonight."

Two words. He said them without anger. Without the flat deflection he used when Edouard probed or when the table got too close. He said them like closing a door he intended to open later. Carefully. Making sure the latch caught, making sure I heard it click and understood that the click was not a lock. A request.

"Okay," I said.

We climbed. The timer ran out on the third flight and we stood in the dark for four seconds until Elena found the button, and in those four seconds Leo was one step above me and I could hear him breathe and the breathing was steady and close and the darkness held us in it without asking us to perform anything for it.

The lights came on. We climbed. Sixty-seven, sixty-eight. Edouard counting behind us, his voice carrying the numbers like a man reading a psalm he's memorised but no longer believes. Seventy-one. Seventy-two.

Seventy-three.

The courtyard. The lemon tree and the stars. Edouard lowered himself onto the wall and put his hands on his knees and looked at the sky, same as every night, as though the sky owed him an explanation for the stairs.

We dispersed. Yuki gone. Elena toward her office. Edouard still sitting, still looking up.

I walked toward the east wing. Leo walked beside me. The corridor was dark and our footsteps echoed on the stone and neither of us spoke. He turned left toward his room. I turned right toward mine. He didn't say goodnight. I didn't say goodnight. The corridor swallowed the sound of his boots and then it was just my footsteps and the cold stone and, through the grille above my door, faint and constant, his formula moving through the building in the dark.

I went inside. Sat on the bed. The sea through the window. V4 on the bench in the lab, doing its slow irreversible thing. Edouard's wound in my chest. Leo's closed door in my mind.

I lay down. Sleep came at midnight. The base note came through the grille, his formula, persistent, patient, and I breathed it in and let it go and breathed it in again.
