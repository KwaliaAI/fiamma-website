The central lab has been emptied.

Chiara's people took out the benches, the stools, the glass cabinets, the source naturals. What's left is a long table with five chairs on one side and nothing on the other. Four strips in numbered holders. Five glasses of water. Five notebooks. A room that smells like nothing. The hardest thing to achieve in a building made of porous stone and perfumers' ambitions.

Elena walks me through it at seven in the morning because Elena walks everyone through everything and this is her Sistine Chapel.

"Strips are numbered one through four. The judges test in sequence. Each formula gets twenty minutes on skin plus fifteen on strip. Two hours minimum, possibly three."

"Where do we go?"

"Courtyard. I've put water and coffee out. Lemon tree bench." She holds her clipboard against her chest like a shield. "I've also put out biscotti because Edouard gets philosophical when he's hungry and I can't take it before nine."

"Elena."

"What?"

"You're going to hold that clipboard so hard you bend it."

She looks down. Her knuckles are white. She loosens her grip by a fraction.

"I've been organising this for three weeks. The logistics. The timing. The strip preparation. I know which formula is which and the judges don't and that knowledge is sitting in my sternum like a trapped bird."

"Which number am I?"

"I can't tell you that."

"Can you tell me it's not thirteen?"

"It's not thirteen."

"That's all I needed."

She pulls the clipboard back to her chest and walks toward the central lab and I watch her go and I think: this woman applied as a competitor two years ago. Promising instincts and insufficient technique. She framed the rejection letter. And now she's carrying the entire operational weight of a blind panel on a clipboard she's about to snap in half, and she's doing it because being near the work matters more than being in it.

* * *

The courtyard. Eight fifteen. Bench under the lemon tree. Biscotti on a plate. Coffee in a thermos that Elena has labelled COMMUNAL in black marker. Peak Elena. Nineteen days of Elena objects and this one crowns them all.

Edouard arrives first. White shirt, linen trousers, loafers without socks. He sits on the bench like a man arriving at a café where he's a regular. He picks up a biscotto, examines it, eats half.

"The biscotti is acceptable," he says.

"I'll tell Elena."

"Don't. If she knows I approved, she'll stop bringing them."

Yuki arrives second. She walks across the courtyard at her usual pace. Exact number of steps between two points. Not one extra. She sits on the far end of the bench. She does not take a biscotto.

Leo comes last. Water bottle. Dark shirt. His hair is doing the thing it does when he hasn't slept, the same thing it does when he has slept, existing on his head in a way that I'm not going to think about because I have larger concerns.

He sits next to Edouard. Our eyes meet for a second. He lifts his chin. The smallest acknowledgment.

On my wrist, wine-coloured silk. Edouard's tie. His first wife's gift. Thirty-two years and seventeen victories and eleven losses and a funeral. I tied it this morning and the silk was cool against my skin.

My hands are shaking. I put them in my pockets.

"Waiting," Edouard says, settling into the bench, "is the only part of this profession I have never mastered."

Yuki looks at him. "You have not mastered the waiting because you fill it with talking."

"Talking IS my waiting."

"This explains much."

Edouard takes another biscotto. "She wounds me with economy. I require paragraphs to achieve the same damage."

"Yes," Yuki says.

Leo is drinking water. Nineteen days and he has not once deviated from water and the consistency of it is either a moral position or a personality disorder and I find both options attractive. Says more about me than him.

"They've started," Elena says from the doorway. Clipboard in hand. She says it and then she leaves.

* * *

The first forty minutes are the worst.

I know this because I've done a competition before. Manchester, 2016. I came second to a formula that smelled like a Selfridges lobby. I told Nadia I was never competing again, and she said "fine" in the voice that meant "you'll compete again," and here I am.

Edouard tells a story. He tells stories the way other people breathe: continuously, without apparent effort, and with the strong implication that stopping would cause a medical event.

"In 1997," he says, "I submitted a formula to a competition in Geneva. Very prestigious. Very Swiss. The judges took four hours. FOUR. I sat in a corridor that smelled like hospital and ate from a vending machine and by the third hour I had consumed six chocolate bars and made friends with the security guard, who was from Portugal, who told me about his mother's garden in Faro, who described a jasmine hedge in such detail that I went home and used his description as the basis for a fragrance."

"Did the fragrance win?" I ask. Because someone has to keep him going or he'll stop and the silence will eat me.

"The fragrance was terrible. The story was magnificent."

"And the Geneva competition?"

"I won. The chocolate bars were a strategic choice. Sugar feeds confidence. The Swiss understand this. It is their primary national contribution."

"Watchmaking," Leo says.

"Chocolate," Edouard says. "Watchmaking is an anxiety."

"Banking," Yuki says.

We all look at her. She doesn't acknowledge it. She looks at the lemon tree.

Elena appears in the courtyard. Clipboard. Face doing absolutely nothing.

"They're on formula three."

I'm on my feet. "How did they react?"

"They're professionals, Margot. They don't react."

"Everyone reacts."

"They write things down."

"What do they write?"

"I don't read over people's shoulders."

"You read over MY shoulder last Tuesday."

"That was a scheduling conflict. This is judging. There's a distinction."

She leaves. I sit. My hands come out of my pockets. Still shaking. Back they go.

"The English," Edouard says, "respond to stress by hiding their hands. The French respond by using them. We gesture. We cook. We make love."

"The Japanese," Yuki says, "respond by sitting quietly and waiting for the French to stop talking."

"And the Americans?" I look at Leo.

"We drink water and say nothing useful."

"That is the first honest thing an American has said in my presence," Edouard says.

Leo raises his water bottle. A toast. Edouard clinks his coffee cup against it. The sound is small and absurd and it makes something loosen in my chest.

* * *

Fifty minutes. Elena.

"They're still on three."

"Three is taking long."

"Three is complex. They're discussing."

"They discuss? Out loud?"

"In whispers. Professional whispers."

"What does a professional whisper sound like?"

"Like a whisper, Margot, but with better posture."

She's gone. I pick up a biscotto and put it down. I pick it up again. I eat it without tasting it.

Edouard is watching the lemon tree. He has gone quiet. More alarming than his talking.

Yuki has not moved in thirty minutes. I've changed position eleven times. I've walked to the fountain and back. I've looked at the sky. Blue. Offensively, categorically blue, a sky that has no business being this untroubled while five people in a room are deciding my future.

Leo is sitting with his elbows on his knees, water bottle between his feet. Every few minutes he looks up and his eyes find me before they find anything else. Then he looks back at the stone. He's not being careful about it and he's not being reckless about it and the combination is doing something to my stomach that I'd like to attribute to the biscotti.

* * *

An hour and twenty. Elena.

"They've moved to four."

My stomach drops. Or rises. Or exits my body entirely and goes to sit on the bench next to Edouard where at least it would hear a good story.

"How many strips did they use on three?"

"Several."

"Several is not a number."

"Several is a professional quantity."

I'm going to kill her. Her and the clipboard and the succulent she's been watering on her desk for three weeks.

She catches my face. Something softens.

"Breathe," she says. Just to me.

I breathe.

She goes back inside.

* * *

Two hours. They're still in there.

Edouard has exhausted his stories and moved to silence. It looks good on him, like a suit he never wears. Yuki has not moved. Leo is standing by the fountain, one hand on the stone rim, his back to me. He made a formula in the room next to mine for nineteen days and I made mine next to his and both of us built something that reached for the other through medieval ventilation and neither of us knew what we were doing until we did.

The tie on my wrist is soft against my pulse point. I can feel my heartbeat through the silk.

Elena. In the doorway. Her face is still doing nothing. She is the most disciplined woman I have ever met.

"Chiara would like you to come in."

* * *

The central lab. Four of us in a row. Standing. The table is in front of us with its four strip holders and five notebooks and the judges behind the table with their faces doing exactly what Elena said they would do, which is nothing. Professional nothing, accomplished nothing, a nothing that has been practised for years.

The room smells like four formulas and stone and the nothing that Chiara's team achieved this morning, already losing the battle against nineteen days of accumulated ambition.

Chiara stands at the end of the table. She's holding a piece of paper the same way she holds everything: as if the object is secondary to what it represents.

My hands are in my pockets. I keep them there.

"The panel has reached a decision."

Edouard is standing to my left. Yuki to my right. Leo at the far end. I can feel all three of them without looking. We're a unit. Whatever happens next, we're a unit.

"The decision is unanimous."

Five-zero. Whatever it is, five-zero.

Chiara looks at the paper. Then she looks at us. She takes her time. She has earned the right to take her time.

"The commission is awarded to formula number four. Submitted by Margot Crane."

My face doesn't move.

I know this because I can feel it from the inside, the muscles of my jaw and my cheeks and my forehead all holding exactly where they are, like a building whose foundation has just shifted and the walls are waiting to see which direction to fall.

Chiara reads the citation. "For a formula of uncommon bravery that asks a question of the wearer and trusts them to carry the answer."

Behind me, Elena makes a sound. It comes from behind the clipboard. Small and compressed and containing three weeks of strip preparation and the application she submitted two years ago and the rejection she framed.

Edouard puts his hand on my shoulder. Heavy. He's lost and won enough times to know that what a winner needs in the first five seconds is someone else's hand. Heavy and still.

Yuki nods. A millimetre. From Yuki, this is a standing ovation.

Leo doesn't speak. He's at the far end of the row and he looks at me and his face does something I don't have a word for. Pride and tenderness and the specific pain of a man who knows what the formula is reaching for and knows she chose not to grab it and knows why. His face holds all of it without performing any of it. I will keep this face for the rest of my life. I will carry it like Edouard's tie carries thirty-two years. In the body. In the material.

* * *

The corridor. Alone.

I walk until I'm around the corner and out of sight of the central lab. The stone is cool and the motion sensor clicks on, one pool of light in an empty hallway.

I put my hands over my face.

The fist unclenches. Eighteen years. The Bermondsey bench at seventeen. The fiancé who left when the money did. Two hundred and three thousand, four hundred and seventeen pounds and sixty-two pence. Ferranti Capital. Martins & Hogg. The oud in 14B wrapped in socks on Argos shelving. Nadia saying win and hanging up.

I lean against the wall and I breathe and my hands are wet against my face and the corridor is quiet and the motion sensor gives up and the light goes off and I stand in the dark and let the fist open.

* * *

I call Nadia from the courtyard. The lemon tree. The bench that held four people for two hours of terrible waiting. My phone rings twice.

"Tell me."

"I won."

Nadia is crying before I finish. I can hear it, the specific sound of Nadia crying. She does it rarely and badly. Two years of making spreadsheets and deferring her salary and fielding solicitors' letters while her business partner is in Italy making perfume.

"Oh thank god." She's laughing through the crying. She does both at once and neither well. "Margot. Oh thank god."

"Fifty thousand euros. It's roughly forty-three thousand pounds. It doesn't clear the debt."

"I know what it doesn't clear. Do you know what it does?"

"Tell me."

"It gives us time. That's Ferranti and Martins & Hogg. That's us walking into the High Court response with a Radici commission and a cheque and a lawyer who can finally say 'my client has assets' without lying."

"Your client has always had assets. The oud."

"The oud is not liquid. The oud is the opposite of liquid. The oud is a deeply illiquid substance in a climate-controlled case on Argos shelving in a storage unit in Bermondsey. You have turned your entire financial life into a SMELL, Margot. And I love you for it. And the smell just won."

I laugh. Actually laugh. Sitting under the lemon tree in Positano at eleven in the morning with tears on my face and my business partner crying in Bermondsey and forty-three thousand pounds that won't fix everything but will fix enough.

"I love you too," I say.

"I'm hanging up. I'm going to call the solicitor. Then I'm going to call Ferranti's solicitor. Then I'm going to have a glass of wine at eleven in the morning because I've earned it."

"It's ten in London."

"Even better."

She hangs up. I put the phone on the bench. The lemon tree is above me and the courtyard smells like stone and coffee and I sit there with my hands in my lap, palms up, open, and I don't move for a long time.

* * *

The trattoria. Eight PM. Everyone.

The long table. Everyone. Bread and wine already down, Luca moving between chairs with the carafe.

Paola comes out of the kitchen carrying a bottle. She holds it like she holds everything: as if the object should be grateful for the transport. She sets it on the table.

"This is from 1998."

The table goes quiet. Paola does not make announcements. Paola gives instructions disguised as hospitality.

"My husband made it. He is a terrible man." She uncorks the bottle with a single motion, no ceremony. "The wine is excellent. These things, they go together."

She pours. Six glasses. Then a seventh. Then she pours an eighth, and the eighth glass she keeps.

She pulls out a chair. She sits.

The table watches Paola sit like you'd watch a mountain decide to relocate. She has been standing, serving, cooking, directing for nineteen days. She has never once been at the table. She has been the table. The gravity that pulled everyone into the same room each night and fed them and sent them back to work and did it again the next day.

Her sitting is the largest thing in the room.

"Salute," she says, and raises her glass, and drinks.

The wine is extraordinary. I'm not a wine person, I'm a nose person, but this wine doesn't require expertise. It requires a mouth and the willingness to shut up and listen. Dark fruit, earth, something tannic and ancient that tastes like a grudge held long enough to become beautiful.

"Your husband is a terrible man," I say.

"The worst."

"The wine is magnificent."

"Yes." She drinks again. "Marriage is complicated. Wine is simple. This is why I prefer wine."

Edouard is looking at her with something close to awe. I've never seen that expression on his face. Edouard's face believes it has already seen everything worth seeing.

"Signora Paola," he says. "You are a force."

"I am a cook. Eat your fish."

He eats his fish.

Elena's clipboard is on the chair next to her, face down. First time in three weeks.

Edouard stands. He has his glass in his hand and the room adjusts itself toward him because rooms do that when Edouard stands.

"To formula number four."

The table lifts its glasses.

"Which I would have been furious about if it had been anyone else." He looks at me. His eyes are bright. Seventy-one years old and French and his eyes are bright. "Since it is Margot, I am merely devastated."

He drinks, and the table drinks, and I drink.

He puts his glass down.

"I am also proud." His voice changes. The performance falls away. Fifty years of making perfume, and he has decided the woman sitting across from him with a wine-coloured silk tie on her wrist might be the future of his art. "Which is worse."

He sits. Picks up his glass. Puts it down without drinking. Picks it up again.

His hand is shaking.

Edouard Blanchard's hand is shaking around a wine glass in a trattoria in Positano and nobody at the table is looking at it because we all see it and we all know what it costs him for us to see it.

"Fifty years," he says. Quietly. To the glass. "Fifty years of formulas and the bravest one was made by a woman half my age with oud in her socks." He drinks. The shaking doesn't stop. "I should have made your formula. I had the materials. I had the technique. I didn't have the nerve." He puts the glass down too hard. Wine sloshes onto the tablecloth. He doesn't notice. "That is not a graceful thing to say and I am saying it because I have been graceful about losing for forty years and tonight I find that I am tired of it."

Paola, who misses nothing, puts her hand on his. Briefly. The way you'd steady a glass on a shelf. Then she lets go and picks up the bottle.

She refills his glass. He wraps both hands around it. The shaking stops.

Leo is sitting next to me. His hand is under the table. His fingers find mine. I hold on.

Outside, the sea is doing what the sea always does: being enormous and indifferent and present. Positano is lit up, gold and white, cascading down the cliff. Inside the trattoria, Paola is sitting and Elena's clipboard is face down and Luca is pouring from behind the bar and Leo's hand is holding mine under the table and the wine is excellent and the fish is excellent and the debt is still there but the fist is open and the formula won and the fist is open and the formula won.

I drink the terrible man's excellent wine. I hold Leo's hand. I let the evening be what it is.
