V4 is too heavy. I know it before I even sit down, before I uncap anything, because I dreamed about it and in the dream the base was a hand pressing on my chest. Oud does that when you let it run the room. You think you're using it and then you wake up and it's using you.

I strip the base back by a third. Rebuild. Leaner, more air between the notes. The centre holds its shape. I don't know what fills it yet. But the architecture is getting precise, and precision is what lets you leave something unfinished without the whole thing collapsing.

By six o'clock I've done what I can. My nose is shot. I wash my hands, change my shirt, and go down to dinner.

* * *

Luca is clearing the antipasti plates when he mentions it.

"The festa di San Giovanni is tonight. Down at the harbour."

"What kind of festa?" Elena says, already sitting up straighter.

"Lanterns. Music. Food. Same every year."

"Food," Edouard says.

"Street food," Luca says.

"I heard you."

"Fried dough. Grilled octopus. Things on sticks."

"I have eaten at three-star restaurants in eleven countries." Edouard pushes his chair back and stands. "Take me to the things on sticks."

Yuki looks at Luca. "Will there be crowds?"

"Thousands."

"I'll find a bench."

"There are no benches."

"I'll find a wall."

Luca almost smiles. He looks tired tonight. Not his usual languid tiredness but something underneath it, something that's been sitting in his face all through dinner. He clears the last plate and disappears into the kitchen.

We take the lift down. Except Edouard.

"I will walk," he says at the top of the stairs. "The lift lacks character."

"The lift has tremendous character," I say. "It shakes."

"Trembling is not character. Trembling is infrastructure failure."

"You're seventy-one."

"Which is why I know the difference."

He takes the stairs. We take the lift. He beats us to the bottom.

* * *

The harbour is unrecognisable.

Paper lanterns strung between the boats. A band on a wooden platform near the water, playing something with an accordion and a lot of commitment. Food stalls along the quay, smoke rising from flat grills, the air thick with garlic and hot sugar and cheap wine.

Elena vanishes toward a stall with hand-lettered signs advertising limoncello tastings. Edouard finds a man selling straw hats and buys one immediately. Yuki finds a low wall near the water, sits, and becomes invisible.

Leo and I end up walking together.

Not by design. A group of teenagers barrels through the crowd going the wrong direction and we both step the same way and then we're side by side and it would be weirder to separate than to keep going.

"Octopus," he says, pointing at a stall.

"Fried dough," I say, pointing at the next one.

"Octopus first."

"Dough first."

"Why."

"Because if I eat octopus first, the dough tastes like octopus."

"And?"

"And dough should taste like dough."

"That's not how taste works."

"I'm a perfumer."

"That's not how perfume works either."

"How would you know?"

"I wouldn't. Get the dough."

I get the dough. He gets the octopus. We trade bites standing in the middle of the crowd while someone's child runs between our legs.

"Good?" he says.

"The octopus is fine."

"Fine."

"Fine is a compliment from me."

"Is it."

"It is. Ask anyone."

"I don't know anyone here."

"Ask me."

"I just did."

The band finishes one song and starts another. Or possibly the same song in a different key. It's either traditional Neapolitan folk music or an extremely earnest cover of something that was in the charts in 2019.

"Do you think they know what they're playing?" I say.

"Does it matter?"

"It matters to me. I like to know if I'm witnessing art or an accident."

"Aren't those the same thing?"

"No."

"Your formula. The gap in the middle. Was that art or an accident?"

I look at him. "Art."

"You sure?"

"I'm sure."

"Because from what you've described, it sounds like something you didn't plan."

"The best art is something you didn't plan but recognised when it showed up."

He takes this in. Eats his last piece of octopus.

"That's good," he says. "You should put that on a T-shirt."

"Shut up."

The fireworks start without warning. A bang, then a bloom of red and gold over the water, and the crowd surges backward and Leo steps into me, his hand catching my waist to keep me upright.

It stays there. One second. Two.

"Sorry," he says.

"You're fine."

He drops his hand. We watch the fireworks.

"Art or accident?" I say.

"Controlled explosions with aesthetic intent."

"So violence with a colour scheme."

"Isn't that literally what perfumery is?"

I open my mouth. Close it.

"Fair point," I say.

He almost laughs. Almost. The corner of his mouth, that's all. We watch the rest of the fireworks in a silence that isn't empty.

* * *

We circle back toward Paola's around ten. The trattoria is supposed to be our default, the place where we end up when everywhere else has too many strangers.

Tonight, everywhere else has come to Paola's.

The place is rammed. Every table, every chair, people standing two-deep at the bar. The queue goes out the door and halfway up the street. Through the window I can see Luca moving between tables at a speed I didn't know he had. Four plates on one arm. He looks like he's been running for an hour.

We push through the door. Inside it's hot, loud, chaotic. Paola's voice comes from the kitchen in a stream of Italian that doesn't sound like instructions. It sounds like threats.

Luca passes us with six water glasses gripped in his fingers.

"Where's Maria?" I say.

"Festa."

"So you're—"

"Me and Mamma. That's it."

He's gone before I can answer.

I watch him weave through the room, set down the glasses, pick up two empty plates, dodge a man standing in the middle of the aisle with a beer, and disappear into the kitchen. He comes back eight seconds later with bread.

I put my bag under the bar. I pick up an apron from the hook by the kitchen door.

"What do you need?" I say when he passes again.

"Hands."

"Done."

Elena appears beside me. "What are we doing?"

"Working."

"I don't work here."

"Neither do I. Grab menus."

"There are no menus."

"Exactly. Go stand at the door and tell people that."

Elena's eyes go bright and focused in a way I haven't seen since she lost her clipboard during the harbour walk. Within twenty minutes she has a new clipboard. I don't know where it came from. I don't ask.

Edouard pushes into the kitchen.

"No," Paola says.

"Yes."

"This is my kitchen."

"And you are alone in it."

"I am fine alone."

"The pasta water is boiling over."

"It is supposed to boil."

"Over?"

A pause. Paola turns down the flame.

"I have cooked for three wives and the ambassador to Morocco," Edouard says. "Move."

"The ambassador to Morocco has low standards."

"The ambassador to Morocco had excellent standards. His wife had low standards. That is why she was my second wife."

"Touch my fish and I'll kill you."

"I don't want your fish. I want the pasta."

"Fine. The pasta."

"Fine."

They find a rhythm within five minutes. It shouldn't work. Edouard handles pasta and sauces. Paola handles fish, meat, and the rage. They argue about everything. Cooking times. Salt quantities. Whether the colander is in the right place. But the plates keep going out.

Edouard, from the kitchen: "THE PASTA IS NOT AL DENTE. IT IS AL MORTE."

Paola: "AL DENTE IS FOR NORTHERNERS."

Edouard: "AL DENTE IS FOR CIVILISATION."

Paola: "YOU ARE FRENCH. YOU HAVE NO AUTHORITY ON CIVILISATION."

A plate of spaghetti comes out. It's perfect.

Yuki installs herself at the end of the bar with a glass of water and begins counting. Plates out. Plates back. Clean glasses. Dirty glasses. Clean forks.

"Fourteen clean forks," she says when I pass.

"Thank you?"

"You'll thank me when they run out and I'm the only one who knows."

She's right. Twenty minutes later, we're down to six. Yuki has already moved the backup cutlery from the drawer under the register.

Leo rolls up his sleeves and takes the sink.

I notice this because I'm carrying a tray of dirty plates toward the kitchen and he's suddenly there, hands in hot water, a towel over his shoulder, and he's not asking anyone what to do. He just knows. Plates here. Glasses there. Silverware soaking. Stack, rinse, wash, rack.

"You're good at this," I say, setting down the plates.

"Six years."

"Of washing dishes?"

"Of cleaning up after people who don't see the work."

I stop. He keeps washing.

I carry the next tray out.

I am, for the record, terrible at this. The first tray I carry to a table, I hold it wrong and three plates slide and I catch them with my hip and a sound that is not dignified. A bread basket hits the floor. A child at the nearest table laughs so hard her father has to hold her drink. I curtsy. The child claps.

Elena, from the door: "Table four wants to know if we have a menu."

"We don't have a menu."

"We have Paola."

"Tell them that."

"I did. They want specifics."

"Fish. Pasta. Whatever Edouard hasn't ruined."

"I HEARD THAT," Edouard shouts.

"GOOD," I shout back.

"Table four is very happy," Elena says, making a note on her clipboard.

It goes on like this for an hour. Ninety minutes. The rhythm of the room takes over and you stop thinking about it. Plates out, plates back. Bread. Wine. Water. More wine. Edouard and Paola fighting about garlic. Yuki calling fork counts like a croupier. Elena running the door like a customs checkpoint. Leo at the sink, steady, quick, his forearms wet to the elbow.

And Luca, who has been doing this alone for hours, moving through the room like he's been poured into it. At one point I see him lean against the wall by the kitchen for three seconds with his eyes closed. He looks his age. He looks younger than his age. He looks like someone carrying something that isn't just plates.

The crowd thins after eleven. The last tables settle their bills. Paola comes out of the kitchen wiping her hands and looks at the room and looks at us and says nothing for a long moment.

Then she brings out a bottle of something unlabelled and pours six glasses and sets them on the bar.

"Grappa," she says. "My husband made it. Before."

We drink. It's awful and perfect. Edouard sips and says, "Your husband was not a subtle man."

"No," Paola says. "That's why I married him."

Luca leans against the bar next to me. "Thank you."

"Don't."

"I mean it."

"I know. That's why I said don't."

He almost smiles. That tired face again. Something in his eyes that isn't about tonight.

"Your mum all right?" I say. Quiet.

"She's in Sorrento."

"I know."

"She's fine."

"Okay."

He doesn't say anything else. I don't push.

* * *

We take the lift back up. Everyone takes the lift. Nobody has the energy for a character debate with seventy-three stairs.

My feet hurt. My shirt smells like olive oil and someone else's fish and cheap wine that isn't mine. I go to the lab and sit at my bench and uncap V4 and try to work.

I last forty minutes. The formula is close. The oud is better, leaner, the new balance is right, but I keep stalling on the same transition and I know why. My brain keeps going back to the sink. To his forearms. To the way he said "six years" like it was nothing and then said the next sentence like it was everything and then kept washing dishes.

This is absurd. I'm distracted by dishwashing.

I cap everything. Clean my bench. Walk to the corridor.

Leo's light is on.

His door is open. He's at his bench, back to the door, writing something in a notebook. The lab smells like vetiver and something citrus and the soap from Paola's sink still on his skin.

He turns when he hears me. Looks up.

I walk in. I close the door behind me.

I kiss him.

His mouth tastes like Paola's grappa and bread, and for a half-second he doesn't move, his hands still holding the notebook, and then the notebook hits the bench and his hands are on my waist, my back, pulling me in, and it turns out eleven days is exactly as long as it felt.

His workbench. His stool. I push him back and his thighs hit the bench and he sits and I step between his knees and his hands pull my shirt up and my hands are in his hair and a rack of test strips falls sideways and scatters across the bench.

"Your test strips," I say against his mouth.

"Don't care."

"They're everywhere."

"Still don't care."

His mouth on my neck. My hands on his belt. The stool scrapes against the floor tiles as he shifts and I climb onto him and the angle is wrong, the bench is too high, my knee hits something glass.

"Wait," he says. "Here."

He lifts me. Turns. Sets me on the edge of the bench and half the test strips crinkle under me and a vial rolls and his mouth is on my collarbone and I pull his shirt off and he pulls mine off and skin against skin in a cold lab at midnight with the fluorescent light buzzing overhead.

His body is lean and his hands know where they're going without asking and I wrap my legs around him and pull him in and there's a moment, one moment, where we both go still. Both knowing. What this is. What it changes. The question hanging in the two inches between us.

I answer it by pulling him closer.

The fluorescent light hums. The bench creaks. Bottles rattle on the shelf behind me and one of them tips and rolls in a slow circle and doesn't fall. His breath against my shoulder, my hands on his back, finding the muscles, the ribs, the ridge of his spine, and his hands on my thighs and the sound he makes when I shift my hips.

Somewhere in the middle of it, the filing system stops.

It stops without announcement. Starts again before I'm ready.

&nbsp;

Then my brain comes back and I can smell everything: his sweat, which is sharp and clean. The vetiver on his bench. The iris absolute from the vial that rolled. My own skin: olive oil and fish and a night I wasn't expecting.

The stool breaks.

One leg just gives. We crash sideways and his elbow catches the bench and I catch him and we hit the floor and a vial of iris absolute rolls off the edge and bounces once on the tile and doesn't shatter, because iris is bottled in heavy glass, because nothing about iris is fragile.

"Ow," he says.

"Sorry."

"Was that the stool?"

"That was the stool."

"Okay."

We're on the floor. The broken stool is on its side next to us. Test strips everywhere. The iris vial between us.

"I knocked a vial of iris off your bench."

"I know. I heard it."

"Should I—"

"Leave it."

"Iris is patient."

I laugh. Actual, real, full laughter, sitting on the floor of Lab 2 in nothing but his shirt draped over my shoulders, my back against the bench leg, his hand on my ankle.

He smiles. A smile I haven't seen before. Open, crooked, surprised by itself.

My stomach growls.

Not a polite rumble. A full, aggressive, audible demand that echoes off the lab floor like a small animal trapped in my torso.

"Oh God," I say.

"Was that—"

"Shut up."

"When did you last eat?"

"The dough. At the festa. Seven hours ago."

"You worked a trattoria for two hours and didn't eat?"

"I was CARRYING the food, not EATING the food."

"That's a design flaw."

"In what?"

"In you."

He reaches behind him, grabs his jacket off the stool that still works, and digs through the pocket. He pulls out a piece of bread. Slightly crushed. From Paola's kitchen.

"You have bread in your pocket."

"I have bread in your pocket."

"Why do you have bread in your pocket?"

"Because I worked in restaurants for six years and you always take bread."

"Always?"

"Always."

He breaks it in half. We eat stale bread on the floor of Lab 2, surrounded by scattered test strips and a broken stool and a vial of iris absolute neither of us picks up. He's in his trousers and nothing else. I'm in his shirt and nothing else. The fluorescent light is still on and it makes everything ugly and honest.

"This is the best bread I've ever eaten," I say.

"It's stale."

"I know."

"And cold."

"I know."

"And you spent thirty minutes tonight arguing with Edouard about the correct temperature for focaccia."

"Twenty minutes."

"It was thirty."

"It was twenty, and I was right."

"You were right."

He finishes his half. Brushes the crumbs off his trousers, misses most of them. The fluorescent light hums.

"I keep thinking I'll go back to New York," he says. Then stops. Like the sentence walked out of a room he meant to keep locked. He looks at the bread in his hand, the last crust of it, and puts it down on the bench beside him. "I won't. I know I won't. I don't know where I'll go after this and it's the first time in my life that's true."

I don't say anything. He isn't asking me to.

A test strip is stuck to my thigh. I peel it off and read it. Vetiver, cedarwood, something I can't parse. His handwriting, small and certain. I set it on the floor between us.

His thumb traces a circle on my ankle. Small. Absent. Like he's not thinking about it. He's thinking about it.

"I should go back to my room," I say.

"Probably."

"Because if I stay here we'll end up on the floor again and that floor is very hard."

"It is."

"And the other stool might not survive."

"It might not."

I pull his shirt tighter around me. Stand up. My knees complain. The floor was, in fact, very hard.

"Goodnight," I say.

"Goodnight, Margot."

He says my name like it has weight.

I take my clothes and walk back to my room smelling like vetiver and iris and bread and the soap from Paola's sink and underneath all of it, him. His base note. On my skin, tangled with the residue of V4 that lives in my fingertips permanently now.

V4 is closer tonight. I don't name what it needs. I go to bed.
