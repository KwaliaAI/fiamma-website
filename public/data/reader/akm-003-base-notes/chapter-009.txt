The neroli bridge is wrong by two degrees.

I stand at the bench, strip in my left hand, and the problem is so precise I could draw it. The heart accord blooms beautifully for eleven seconds and then the neroli pushes too hard into the transition, shouldering the oud aside instead of handing off. A relay runner snatching the baton.

I adjust. Reduce neroli by 0.3 percent, add a fractional lift of petitgrain to thin the citrus without losing the bitter edge. Dip a fresh strip. Wait.

Better. The bridge holds. The oud comes through underneath like a voice in a cathedral, present before you locate it. And then the base, that strange hollowed-out space where something should anchor and doesn't. Where the formula asks its question.

I mark the modification on the worksheet. V4.1. The handwriting is getting worse, which means I'm getting closer.

Two more strips. The second one I hold against the inside of my wrist, below the pulse point, and let body heat do its work. The sillage changes. Rounder. The base thins where my skin is warmest, the formula pulling apart at the seams, showing its construction.

I cap the neroli, rack the pipettes, and leave the strips standing in their holder like tiny surrender flags. The lab smells of everything I've done this week. Nine days of work layered in the air, a geological record.

It's nearly one. I'm hungry.

* * *

The lift still surprises me.

I press the button and the door opens with a pneumatic sigh, as if the building has been holding its breath. Thirty seconds later I'm at street level, stepping onto warm stone, and the four hundred stairs I've been climbing twice daily don't exist.

Elena is waiting outside with her clipboard and a canvas bag.

"You look like you've solved something," she says.

"The neroli bridge. Maybe. Ask me again tomorrow."

"I'll put it in the schedule." She taps the clipboard. "One-fifteen: ask Margot about neroli bridge."

We walk down toward the town. The afternoon is absurdly bright. Positano stacks itself against the cliff in that way it has, all pastels and terracotta and bougainvillea doing exactly what postcards promise. I've stopped resenting how beautiful it is. Day nine, and I've surrendered to the geography.

"I need a bookshop," Elena says.

"You need a bookshop or the programme needs a bookshop?"

"I need a bookshop. Elena. The person." She pauses. "The programme needs nothing. The programme is a clipboard and a succulent and the persistent fiction that I am organised."

The bookshop is on a side street below the church, crammed between a ceramics place and something selling lemon everything. Inside it smells of old paper and dust and the sweetness of glue losing its grip. Elena disappears into Italian poetry. I drift.

There's a perfume shop three doors down that I almost miss. The sign is hand-painted, faded, the kind of place that's either been here forty years or is pretending it has. Inside: shelves of old bottles, ambers and clears, some with labels in copperplate, some with no labels at all.

The owner is a woman in her sixties with reading glasses on a chain and no interest in selling me anything. She watches me pick up bottles, sniff, put them back.

One shelf near the back, unlabelled vials. I work through them. Citruses, florals, two attempts at chypre that smell like 1987 in a good way. And then one that stops me. Green, sharp, with an animalic undertone that shouldn't work and does. Something in the drydown that I can't place. Moss, but from somewhere specific. A hillside, a season, a time of day.

"Quanto?" I ask.

"Dodici euro."

I pay. I slip the vial into my bag and I don't know why it matters but my hands are careful with it.

Elena's bought a book of Montale poems. We find a café on the piazza and order espressos that arrive in thirty seconds because this is Italy and coffee is not a production, it's a fact.

"How do you know the application process so well?" I ask. The question has been sitting in my mouth for three days. "You talk about the Radici like someone who's read the manual, but you talk about the work like someone who's done it."

Elena puts her cup down. Picks it up. Puts it down again.

"I applied. As a competitor. Two years ago."

The espresso machine behind the counter hisses into the silence.

"They said my formula showed 'promising instincts and insufficient technique.'" She looks at me steadily. "I framed the rejection letter. It's in my office."

"Why?"

"Because 'promising instincts' is the nicest thing anyone's said about my nose."

She drinks her coffee. Finishes it, actually, in one precise motion. The clipboard, the schedule, the succulent she carries from room to room. The way she arrives first and leaves last and knows where every pipette is and which competitor's extraction is running and when the grilles need cleaning. She came back because being near the work was better than not being near it. She carries things because carrying things is what you do when your own art wasn't good enough to carry you.

I don't tell her I'm sorry. She hasn't asked for sorry.

"Your instincts ARE good," I say. "The button timing alone."

"The button timing?"

"The lift. You pressed it like you already knew it worked. You walked straight to the panel and pressed the right button. No hesitation. That's a nose, Elena. Different instrument, same instinct."

She laughs. Full and sudden, surprising both of us. Two men at the next table look over. Elena covers her mouth, which makes it worse, and then she gives up and lets it happen and for about six seconds she is a completely different person.

"A lift button," she says. "That's your evidence."

"I've built cases on less."

* * *

The courtyard catches the four o'clock light like a palm catching rain. Flat, golden, everywhere at once.

Edouard has wine. Of course. Yuki has tea in a ceramic cup she brought from Kyoto, which she told me on Day 3 and I believed completely. Leo has water. I have cheap white from Paola's in a plastic bag, which I pour into a monastery glass with what I choose to regard as panache.

We're sitting on the low wall by the lemon trees. All four of us, for the first time outside the labs, and the conversation has that loose quality of people who've been working in adjacent rooms and are remembering how speech functions.

Leo sniffed the air. Frowned. "Jasmine."

I breathed in. Let my nose do its work. "Pittosporum."

"That's not pittosporum."

"It's pittosporum. The hedge kind. Tiny cream flowers. I tracked it my first day in the taxi."

He breathed again. Deeper. His frown deepened with it. "Huh."

"You're wrong about a smell," I said. "I want to mark this occasion."

"I'm wrong about a smell once a year. You've used yours."

"I'll treasure it."

Edouard starts it. Edouard starts everything.

"Who made you?" He addresses this to the air. "I have been thinking about influence. We are four people in a monastery making perfume for a prize, and somebody made each of us. Who?"

Yuki answers first. "Tadao Ando. Jun Aoki. Kengo Kuma. And Toshio Nagata."

"Three architects and a perfumer," Edouard says. "Which is Nagata?"

"The perfumer."

"I have not heard of him."

"No," Yuki says. And that's it. That's her entire contribution. She drinks her tea.

"For me," Edouard says, settling himself, "my greatest influence was realising that nobody alive could teach me." He pauses for the beat. He always pauses for the beat. "This sounds arrogant. It was survival. I was twenty-four in Grasse and every master I met was making the same twelve fragrances with different labels. They were brilliant and they were finished. I had to leave the room to find the room."

"The room being yourself," I say.

"The room being the space where nobody is telling you what jasmine means. Yes."

Leo hasn't spoken. He's sitting with his back against the wall, legs stretched out, the water glass balanced on his thigh.

"Leo," Edouard says. "You are exempt from nothing."

"Mr. Avedian," Leo says. "My upstairs neighbour in Astoria. Eighty-three, could identify seventeen different roast profiles by smell. Completely deaf. The two things were unrelated. He roasted coffee on the fire escape and the whole building knew what he thought about every batch by which window he opened afterward. Good batch, kitchen window. Great batch, bedroom window. Bad batch, he'd roast again at six AM and nobody complained because the man had earned the right to wake people up with the smell of getting it right."

"And?" Edouard asks.

"And my mother's window box. Three square feet of soil. The apartment always smelled like something growing."

A pause. Edouard nods slowly. I think he recognises something in that answer that he didn't expect.

"Margot."

"A supplier in Bermondsey," I say. "Jean Firth. She let me have a bench in her stockroom when I was seventeen because I was too stubborn to leave and too broke to rent. She told me: 'Your nose is better than mine. Your discipline is worse. Fix one of those.'"

"Which did you fix?" Yuki asks.

"Neither. I just kept showing up."

Edouard laughs. Refills his wine. The light is moving off the courtyard now, the gold turning amber, and I think we could sit here all evening and talk about the people who broke us open and it would stay exactly this easy, this careful, this much about the past and nothing about the thing we're all doing right now in these labs, the thing that will end in two weeks when one of us wins.

But we don't. Yuki stands, nods once, and walks inside. The conversation dissolves the way conversations do when one person leaves and the geometry changes.

Edouard follows. Then Leo. I stay for a minute to finish the wine. It's terrible. Paola charges three euros and it tastes like three euros.

* * *

The terrace faces west and the sunset is doing absolutely nothing subtle.

Leo is already there when I come up. Standing at the stone railing with his water glass. Still water. The man drinks still water at sunset on the Amalfi Coast.

"You're drinking water," I say.

"I'm drinking water."

"At sunset."

"The time of day doesn't change what's in the glass."

"It absolutely changes what should be in the glass. There are rules. Sunset requires wine. Or at minimum an Aperol Spritz. You're at a monastery in Positano and you're drinking water that you probably poured from a tap."

"I filtered it."

"Oh, you filtered it."

"The monastery has a Brita."

"The monastery has a Brita and you chose it. Over wine. At sunset. On the Amalfi Coast." I sit on the railing two metres from where he's standing. "This is a moral failing."

"This from the woman drinking three-euro wine from a plastic bag."

"My wine has personality."

"Your wine has a personality disorder."

I almost spit. I actually almost spit the terrible wine across the terrace railing and down the cliff face into the Tyrrhenian Sea, which would have been a waste of three euros but possibly an improvement to the ocean's flavour profile.

"Fine," I say. "You drink your filtered tap water. I'll drink my unhinged wine. We'll see who's still standing at the end of the residency."

"I'll be standing because I'm hydrated."

"You'll be standing because you've never taken a risk in a beverage."

He turns. Leans his back against the railing. Looks at me.

"I took a risk in a beverage once. After DIRTY, a commercial house offered me a contract. Beaumont Fragrances. Big brief, big budget. They wanted to 'mainstream' my aesthetic." He does the air quotes. They look wrong on his hands. "They gave me a brief that said 'accessible.' I made something that smelled like an apology."

"You're comparing a fragrance to a beverage."

"I'm comparing playing it safe to playing it safe. Your three-euro wine is braver than anything I made in eight months at Beaumont."

"Did they produce it?"

"They produced it. They called it 'Midnight Coast.' It sold in airports."

"Oh no."

"Duty-free. The glass counter between the Toblerone and the inflatable neck pillows."

"Oh no."

"Three hundred thousand units. I saw it at Newark Liberty between gates B and C with a cardboard display of a woman running on a beach."

"Running toward the ocean or away from it?"

"Does it matter?"

"It profoundly matters. Running toward is aspiration. Running away is the feeling of wearing a fragrance that smells like an apology."

He laughs. A real one. Short, surprised, through his nose. "She was running toward. The tagline was 'Discover your horizon.'"

"Kill me."

"I lasted eight months. The apartment went. The roaster went. The coffee setup went into storage in a friend's garage in Gowanus, and I applied for the Radici from a motel in New Jersey that smelled like chlorine and someone else's cologne."

"What cologne?"

He looks at me. "You want me to identify a stranger's cologne from a motel bathroom?"

"You're a nose. I'm asking professionally."

"Cool Water. Davidoff. 1988. Dihydromyrcenol, amber, sandalwood."

"You identified it from a motel bathroom."

"I identified it in two seconds. I spent the rest of the night being offended."

I'm grinning. I can feel it on my face, the stupid wide grin that I can't do anything about because this man just identified a stranger's cologne from a New Jersey motel and was offended by it and I understand this complaint on a molecular level.

"I reviewed DIRTY," I say.

The grin is still on my face but the words change the air. He straightens slightly.

"For Nez. Two years ago. They commissioned a piece on independent launches. I spent a week with your formula."

"I know. I read the Nez piece."

"That wasn't the review."

His hand tightens on the water glass. One squeeze. Release.

"I wrote a longer version. Unpublished. For myself. Three thousand words. You want to hear the line I couldn't publish?"

"Yes."

"'The base note operates as a question the formula asks without answering. The drydown is the sound of a door closing in a house you're still inside.'"

He doesn't move. The sunset is doing its thing to his left and he's not looking at it.

"You wrote that."

"I wrote that."

"That's what I was trying to do."

"I know."

We sit with this. The violet is coming in over the water. Positano is starting to light up below, house by house, like someone is going through the town with a match.

"Why unpublished?" he says.

"Because the published version was eight hundred words and said 'innovative use of oud with unexpected citrus counterpoint,' which is true and also means nothing. The real review was the one where I spent three paragraphs on your base note and couldn't figure out why I cared so much." I pick at the stone railing. "I figured it out about four days ago."

I look at the sea.

"Your water is getting warm," I say.

"Your wine was never cold."

"Goodnight, Leo."

"Goodnight."

But neither of us moves for another thirty seconds, and the thirty seconds are loud, and the sea doesn't care, and the sunset doesn't care, and I care so much my teeth hurt.

* * *

"Fish," Paola announces, as if contradicting someone who suggested otherwise.

The trattoria is full. A family of six has colonised the corner table and two of their children are playing under it while Paola ignores them. Children beneath tables are furniture. Luca navigates around them carrying plates above his head. He catches my eye and grins. Easy, uncomplicated. I grin back. We had a good night. We are having a good evening. The distance between those two things is comfortable.

The fish is branzino, whole, with lemon and oil and something Paola won't name.

"Capers," Edouard says, tasting.

"No," Paola says, already walking away.

"Capers," Edouard insists to the table.

"Anchovies," Yuki says, without looking up.

Edouard takes another bite. His face goes through several nationalities of expression. "Anchovies. Damn."

Luca brings Edouard a second piece of fish. Unprompted. Edouard looks at it, looks at Luca, looks at the fish again.

"He is feeding me into submission," Edouard says. "I accept."

"I once entered a fragrance competition in Tokyo," he says, refilling everyone's wine. "I got lost. I was in Shibuya and my Japanese is non-existent and I followed a group of people who seemed purposeful into a building and up a flight of stairs and sat down at a table and it was not a fragrance competition. It was a sake judging."

"Did you leave?" Elena asks.

"I judged. Four rounds. My notes were, I have been told, impressively wrong. I gave the highest score to something they were using to demonstrate a fermentation defect." He pauses. "I was magnificently qualified for neither."

Leo catches my eye across the table. I don't look away. He doesn't look away. Edouard says something about fermentation and the moment has a soundtrack now, the trattoria noise and the children and Paola's kitchen and the sound of my own pulse.

* * *

My phone rings at eleven. I'm in my room, on the bed, still dressed, reading the Montale that Elena lent me. The Italian is beyond me but the shapes of the poems are beautiful. Short lines, long lines, short again. Like breathing.

Nadia's name on the screen.

"Tell me," I say.

"Ferranti are sending a solicitor's letter. Twenty-one days to respond or they begin enforcement proceedings."

I sit up. The Montale slides off the bed.

"Enforcement meaning what?"

"Meaning a County Court judgment. Which means it goes on your credit file for six years. Which means you won't be able to rent, borrow, or open a business account until you're forty-two."

"Nadia."

"I know."

"I have two hundred and three thousand pounds of debt and I'm in a monastery in Italy making perfume for a prize I might not win."

"You might win."

"Winning pays fifty thousand euros. That's roughly forty-three thousand pounds. That's twenty percent of what I owe."

"Twenty-one percent. I checked."

"You're not helping."

"I'm helping by being accurate. They've put a clock on it, Margot. Twenty-one days."

The line is quiet. I can hear Nadia's office in the background. She works late. She always works late.

"Twenty-one days," I say.

"Twenty-one days."

"Right."

I hang up. The ceiling is old stone, cracked in three places. The big one looks like the Thames if you took out all the bends.

Twenty-one days. The Radici presentations are in thirteen.

The vial from the perfume shop is on the desk. I uncap it. The green note, the animalic, the drydown I can't name. I cap it and leave the window open and go to bed.
