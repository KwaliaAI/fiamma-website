The lab at seven in the morning smelled like yesterday's work and the sea and the faint trace of Leo's formula drifting through the grille. I'd slept hard and woken hungry and come straight here without breakfast because V3 was on the bench and V3 had questions and I was the only person who could answer them.

Two test strips on the counter. I'd made them both last night before bed, drying overnight to let the base notes settle and the heart find its final shape.

Left hand: the safe version. Ambergris anchoring the base, a synthetic accord filling the centre. I held it to my nose. Complete. Every transition accounted for, every question answered. The chinotto opening bit through to the bergamot, the bergamot softened into the heart, the neroli bridge held the labdanum and iris in place, and at the centre: a clean synthetic musk doing a competent impersonation of the thing I couldn't find. A formula that had decided not losing was the same as winning.

Right hand: V3. The brave version. I held it up and breathed.

The labdanum reached. The iris floated above it. And between them, the thinning. Still there. Still shaped like something I could almost name but couldn't, a keyhole that had narrowed overnight but refused to close. The neroli bridge held the two sides in proximity and the pull between them was audible, almost. Like a pause in a sentence, like the rest between two notes in music that makes you lean forward, that makes you lean forward.

I stood at the bench with both strips and I knew.

The safe version was a perfume. The other one was the truth.

I set the safe strip down. Picked up a pipette. Pulled out a fresh blotter sheet and wrote V4 at the top and started building.

The labdanum went on first. Higher concentration than V3, pushed up by two percent, enough to give the base more reach without tipping it into sweetness. The iris next, cold and clean, hovering above. I didn't fill the centre. I adjusted the neroli bridge instead, pulling it back, giving the two sides of the formula more room. More air. Letting them lean toward each other without touching.

My hands were fast. Looser than yesterday, looser than any day this week. The pipette moved between vials and strips without the hesitation that usually sat between intention and action like a customs officer checking passports. Today the customs officer was on break. Today my hands were doing the thinking and my brain was keeping up.

The chinotto opening needed recalibrating for the new base. I adjusted it. Sharper. More bitter. The bergamot transition got two drops less than V3 because V3 had been over-smoothing the handoff and smoothness was a kind of lying.

By ten I had the full structure on a strip. I held it to my nose and waited.

The opening bit. Good. The bergamot caught it. The heart opened and the neroli bridge held and the labdanum reached through the gap and the iris floated above and the absence at the centre was there, alive, shaped and intentional, a space that the formula wanted to fill and couldn't and the wanting was the whole point.

V4 was the best thing I'd made.

It was incomplete and it was brave and it was mine in a way the safe version would never have been, because the safe version was a formula that protected itself and V4 was a formula that risked something. The incompleteness wasn't a flaw. It was the thing the perfume was about.

I sat on the stool and breathed it in and my eyes were hot. I blinked. Once. Twice. Picked up my phone.

How's the formula?

I'd been ignoring Nadia's messages for two days. She'd sent four. The first was about Bergmann & Cole. The second was about Ferranti. The third was a photograph of her cat sitting on a tax return. The fourth said Margot I know you're alive because your phone is reading these and also because you haven't died because I would feel it in my accounts receivable.

I'm building it around a hole.

Three dots. Nadia typing. Nadia typed like she did everything: fast, decisive. Fifteen years of managing other people's financial disasters had given her opinions.

That's either genius or a cry for help.

Yes.

How big is the hole?

Big enough to be the point.

I'm choosing to interpret that as genius. Bergmann & Cole rang again. I told them you were in Italy making something extraordinary and that their supply terms would be repaid with interest once you'd won fifty thousand euros. I may have implied you were further along than you are.

How much further?

I may have used the word "finalist."

Nadia, there are no finalists. It's four people in a monastery.

Semantics. You're in the final four. That's a finalist. I went to Cambridge, Margot, I know how words work.

You went to Cambridge for three terms and got sent down for running an unlicensed bar out of your room.

The bar was profitable. The college lacked vision. Call me when you win.

I put the phone down. Stared at V4 on the strip. The formula was still talking.

I went back to work.

* * *

The afternoon was layering. V4's structure was right but the transitions needed hand-finishing, the small adjustments between notes that turned a formula from a sketch into a composition. I worked on the bergamot-to-heart handoff for an hour. Too sharp. Too soft. Too sharp again. I found the point where it stopped being a transition and started being a conversation and I marked it and moved on.

The oud. My oud, the Kannauj oud that had come here in socks and survived the stairs and the taxi and every other indignity I'd subjected it to. I tested it against V4's base. The labdanum accepted it. The oud sat underneath, dark and patient, and the two of them together turned the formula's centre from something hollow into something that waited. Like the breath before someone says the thing they've been holding.

I was testing V4 on skin when I heard the grille.

Leo's formula. Faint, carried on the ventilation, the green note and the resin and the base I still couldn't name. It drifted through the grille and mixed with V4 on my wrist and for three seconds the two formulas existed in the same air and the combination made something I hadn't expected. A third thing. The complement. The echo of what had happened during the skin study in this same room two days ago, when his wrist was ten centimetres from my nose and the formulas spoke to each other in a language neither of us had built.

I closed the grille.

I opened it again forty seconds later because the ventilation was better open and that was the only reason. That was the reason I was going with.

* * *

Evening. The walk down was becoming routine. Elena pressed the first button and we moved. Leo pressed the second at twenty-three. I pressed the third at forty-nine. Edouard complained on every flight. Yuki was already at the bottom.

"Sixty-two seconds," Elena said as we reached the second landing. "The timer. I counted today. Sixty-two seconds, not ninety. I've been wrong this whole time."

"Twenty-eight seconds of error," Edouard said. "In the dark. On a cliff. I could have died."

"You didn't die."

"It was a near thing."

The trattoria. Same table. Same candles. Same bread and oil appearing before we'd sat down, Paola placing the carafe. She had already decided what we were eating. Our presence at the table was a formality she was willing to tolerate.

Luca served. He brought the wine and the water and he caught my eye and I caught his and we exchanged the look of two people who had thoroughly enjoyed each other's company and were now back in the room where plates got carried and that was fine. Easy. Done.

Elena was wearing a dress. Navy blue. Simple. I'd only ever seen her in polo shirts and practical trousers for carrying clipboards and lemons and other people's logistical problems up and down a cliff. The dress was different. The dress was a choice made for someone or for herself and either way it changed the angle of her shoulders, how she held her wine glass, how she looked at the table and sometimes past the table toward the kitchen door.

Edouard noticed. His eyes moved from Elena's face to her dress to his wine. He was choosing not to comment, which for Edouard was the equivalent of a religious vow.

Yuki noticed. She looked at Elena, looked at the kitchen door, looked back at her bread. Filed it.

I noticed. Elena caught me noticing and gave me a look that said don't and I gave her a look back that said I'm not saying anything and she gave me a third look that said your face is saying it and I drank my wine.

Leo was oblivious. Leo was eating fish and talking to Edouard about a cedar supplier in Morocco and he could have been sitting in a room full of fireworks and he would have been talking about cedar. The man had a gift for not seeing the things that were happening six inches from his plate.

He had a piece of bread in his left hand that he kept tearing into smaller and smaller squares and arranging on the tablecloth in a grid pattern. He wasn't aware he was doing it. The grid was precise. Four by four. When he ran out of bread he picked up another piece and started a second grid next to the first.

"You're building something," I said.

He looked down. Saw the bread architecture. His ears went red.

"Habit," he said, and swept the squares into a pile with his palm, and the pile was less interesting than the grid and I felt a small loss on behalf of the bread.

Paola's food was lamb tonight. Slow-roasted, falling apart, with potatoes that had been cooked in the fat until they stopped being potatoes and started being a reason to live. I ate and the lamb was extraordinary and the wine was good and Edouard told a story about a perfume conference in Dubai where the air conditioning failed and forty noses were trapped in a ballroom at thirty-eight degrees with two hundred open testers.

"The room smelled like the collapse of Western civilisation," he said. "Every note at once. Oud fighting jasmine fighting vetiver. A woman fainted. I have never been more alive."

"Which note won?" Yuki asked.

"The sweat," Edouard said. "The sweat always wins. This is the great secret of perfumery. You can spend ten thousand euros on materials, but the human body will override all of it by three o'clock in the afternoon."

We stood up. Chairs scraped. Edouard kissed Paola on both cheeks. Paola tolerated it. Forty years of running a trattoria. Every variety of charming Frenchman, seen and survived. She was no longer susceptible.

At the door, Elena hung back. "I'm going to stay for a drink," she said. Casual. Light. A sentence that meant nothing unless you'd spent the evening watching her sit in a navy dress at a table where a twenty-five-year-old waiter kept finding reasons to refill water glasses that were already full.

I caught her eye. Elena shrugged. One shoulder. A motion that contained more information than most people's paragraphs.

I grinned at her. She pointed at the door. Go.

We walked to the stairs. Four of us now. The group felt different without Elena. She was the one who knew the buttons, the timing, the logistics of getting five adults up a cliff in the dark. Without her we were expedition without command.

"I know the buttons," Leo said, as though he'd heard the thought.

He pressed the first one. The lights came on. We climbed.

"Elena is not coming?" Edouard asked at step twelve.

"She's having a drink."

"Ah." He climbed three more steps. "That boy is going to work his way through the entire Radici programme."

"Is that a complaint?"

"It is an observation made with complete professional neutrality and considerable personal envy."

I laughed. My laugh bounced off the stone walls and the sea below swallowed it. Leo pressed the button at twenty-three. The lights reset.

Step thirty. Thirty-one. Edouard counting under his breath. Yuki ahead of us, silent, climbing at her constant efficient pace.

Step thirty-eight. The lights went out.

Total darkness. The sea. Our breathing. The stone under our feet. Edouard stopped.

"This is very stupid," Yuki said.

I stopped. Leo stopped. Edouard made a sound. Something between reverence and cardiac arrest.

Then Edouard laughed. A real laugh, big, the kind that starts in the stomach and doesn't care where it ends up. He laughed so hard he had to sit down, and I heard him find his spot, step forty-four, the stone under him, laughing in the dark on a cliff face in Positano because Yuki Tanaka, who spoke in single words and occasional pairs, had just produced a complete sentence and it was the funniest thing any of us had heard all week.

"She speaks," Edouard said from step forty-four. Wheezing. "She speaks and she's right. It IS very stupid."

Leo's phone torch came on. He found the button at forty-nine. Pressed it. Light.

Yuki was four steps above us. Her face was the same as always. If the laughter or the darkness or the sentence had cost her anything, she wasn't showing it. She turned and kept climbing.

Edouard stayed on forty-four for ten more seconds, collecting himself. "I am going to remember that for the rest of my life," he said, and stood up, and we climbed.

* * *

The top. The courtyard. Lemon tree and night air. Yuki gone already, vanished to her room or her lab or whatever quiet space she occupied after dark. Edouard heading to his room, still chuckling, shaking his head, trailing the echo of Yuki's sentence behind him like a man carrying a gift.

Leo and I walked toward the east wing. Side by side. The cloister was dark and the stone was cool and our footsteps fell into an accidental rhythm.

"You changed something today," he said.

"What?"

"Your formula. You changed something."

I looked at him. "How do you know?"

"The ventilation."

I stopped walking. He stopped.

"You've been listening to my work through the grille."

"Smelling it. The ratios shifted around midmorning. The labdanum came up. The synthetic accord dropped out." He said this the way someone would report the weather. Factual. Unashamed.

"That's my formula."

"I know."

"You've been monitoring my formula through a ventilation grille."

"I've been working in my lab. The grille is open. Your formula comes through. I'm a nose. I notice."

I should have been angry. He was smelling my work. He was tracking my changes. He was sitting in the next lab with his grille open and his nose running its programme on my formula while I ran mine on his. The professional violation was clear and the personal violation was something else, something closer to the feeling of being watched through a window you'd left open on purpose.

"You dropped the safe version," he said.

"How could you possibly know there was a safe version?"

"Because there's always a safe version. And you were using it until today. The accord changed. Whatever you replaced it with has more space. More air." He paused. "It's braver."

We stood in the cloister. The lemon tree above us. The sea below. His face in the dark was half-lit by the courtyard lamp. He'd just told me something true and he was waiting to see if I'd let it land.

"Goodnight, Leo."

"Goodnight."

He didn't move. I walked away and I could feel him standing there in the cloister behind me, under the lemon tree, in the dark. I didn't turn around.

I went to my lab. I didn't turn on the overhead light. The bench was visible in the light from the courtyard, the test strips lined up, V4 on skin on my wrist still talking, the labdanum still leaning toward something it couldn't find.

I stood at the grille. Through it, faint: his formula. He'd gone to his lab too. Of course he had. The base note drifted through, the dark resinous thing I couldn't name, patient as ever.

Why did he leave the grille open?

Any serious competitor would seal it. The ventilation carried everything: concentrations, ratios, materials, the entire architecture of a formula laid bare for anyone with a trained nose and a willingness to listen. He knew I could smell his formula. I'd been smelling it since Day 1. He knew this because I knew his, and if I could hear him through the wall then he could hear me, and any perfumer with fifty thousand euros on the line would seal the grille and work in silence.

He'd closed it once. I'd heard the click, Day 3, an afternoon. The air in my lab had gone flat and private and I'd known instantly what had changed. Twenty minutes later: another click. Open again. His formula drifting back in, the green and the resin and the base note resuming their slow conversation with my walls.

He was careless. Or he didn't care. Or the ventilation was genuinely better with the grille open and he'd made a practical decision and I was constructing a narrative out of airflow.

The grille was open. His formula came through. My formula went through to him. Two compositions breathing the same air through six hundred years of stone, and neither of us had shut it.

I picked up V4's strip. Held it to my nose. I set it down and went to my room.
