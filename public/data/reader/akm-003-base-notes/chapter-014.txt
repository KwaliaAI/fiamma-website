The competition brief is forty-seven pages. I know this because I have read forty-six of them.

I've been ignoring Section Four since Elena handed out the printed copies on day one. Section Four is Production and Commercialisation. Section Four is what happens after you win, which, when you're sleeping on Nadia's sofa and calculating whether a tube of cadmium yellow from the perfume-shop curiosity shelf is a business expense or a cry for help, feels like planning your Nobel acceptance speech while failing A-levels.

But V4 is done. V4 is the best thing I've ever made. And this morning, sitting in Lab 3 with the windows open and the monastery doing its best impression of a place where nothing bad has ever happened, I open the brief to page thirty-one and read Section Four.

Most of it is standard: licensing structures, revenue sharing, production timelines. The usual bureaucratic furniture you'd expect from a company whose letterhead costs more than my rent.

Section 4.2 stops me.

I read it twice. Then a third time. Then I put the brief down and pick it back up because maybe I read it wrong.

The winning formula shall be submitted in full to Radici's production partner, including all material specifications, proportions, and architectural notes, within fourteen days of the panel's decision. This submission shall constitute the basis for commercial reproduction and is subject to proprietary review by Radici and its designated partners.

Full formula disclosure.

Every material. Every proportion. Every architectural note. The whole map.

V4's architecture is a map. The labdanum leans one way, the iris leans the other, and between them the formula thins to nothing. Anyone reading the full disclosure will see exactly where the bridge should be, exactly what shape it should take. A trained nose at Radici's production partner will look at V4 and ask: what goes here? What were you reaching for?

And the answer points directly at Leo's base note. At DIRTY. At the work he's spent two years protecting since Daniel Reeves walked into a boardroom and used his architecture against him.

I sit in Lab 3 for four minutes. The brief is open on the bench. My formula is capped on the shelf. The ventilation grille hums. Same hum as every day, his air and mine mixing in the duct, except the sound that was beautiful yesterday is evidence today.

I take the brief and I run.

* * *

Lab 2's door is open. He's at the bench, pipettes out, working on something I can't see because I don't slow down long enough to look.

"Read this."

I put the brief on his bench. Open to page thirty-one. My finger on Section 4.2.

He looks at my face first. Then he looks at the page.

I watch him read it. His eyes move once across the paragraph. Then back to the beginning. Then through it again, slower, like re-reading a medical result.

His face goes flat. The expression doesn't disappear into composure or anger or any of the things I've learned to read on him over the past two weeks. It just leaves. Like someone pulled a plug.

"Full disclosure," he says.

"Full disclosure."

"All material specifications."

"And proportions. And architectural notes."

He puts the brief down and lays his hands flat on the bench, fingers spread, and looks at the wall.

"The formula," he says.

"The formula."

"Anyone reading V4's architecture will see where it thins."

"Yes."

"And what's missing is shaped like my work."

"Yes."

He's quiet. His hands are still flat on the bench. I can see the tendons in his forearms, taut, like he's holding the wood down rather than himself up.

"Don't win," he says.

"What?"

"Don't win. Reformulate. Make a V5. One that doesn't point at me."

"Every version that doesn't point at you is dead. I've tried fourteen of them. Fourteen, Leo. V1 through V3, all the branches, all the blind alleys. The formula only works when it's reaching for something it can't have."

"Then build a different formula."

"There isn't time. We're three weeks out."

"There's always time if the alternative is—"

"Is what?"

He looks at me. "You know what."

"Say it."

"I'm asking you to say it out loud because I need to hear you say it."

"If the alternative is handing a production lab a map that leads straight to my base note. To the thing I've spent two years protecting. To DIRTY."

His voice is even. Controlled. How he gets when something matters enough to hold very still around.

"I would never—"

"You wouldn't have to. The formula speaks for itself. That's what makes it good. That's what you told me last night."

"I know what I told you last night."

"So don't submit it."

"I can't afford not to submit it. You know what I owe."

"Two hundred and three thousand—"

"Four hundred and seventeen pounds and sixty-two pence. Yes. And Nadia's holiday and Nadia's salary and Ferranti's solicitor and enforcement proceedings in twenty-one days minus whatever days have passed since the letter which I've stopped counting because counting makes it worse."

"So this is about the money."

"This is about the money and the formula and the money and the fact that the best thing I've ever made needs something I have no right to give away and the only alternative is making something worse on purpose."

"Then make something worse."

"Did you hear me? I can't afford—"

"I heard you. I'm saying there might be a version that's worse and still wins."

"There isn't. I've been inside this formula for weeks. The open centre IS the formula. Remove it and you've got a very competent fragrance with no soul. Radici doesn't give the prize to competent."

He picks up the brief again. Reads the clause one more time. Puts it down.

"I thought about your formula," he says. "When you first told me about V4. The architecture. The way it leans toward something it can't reach. I thought about it and I thought about what it meant and I didn't think about production. I didn't think about Section 4.2 because I stopped being careful."

"Leo—"

"I stopped being careful because of you. Because you sat in my room and told me about Pradip and the oud and Unit 14B and I forgot that people use formulas against other people because the last person who did that was Daniel Reeves and I've spent two years making sure it can't happen again."

"I am not Daniel Reeves."

"I know you're not Daniel Reeves."

"Then stop treating me like I am."

"I'm not treating you like Daniel Reeves. I'm treating you like someone who's about to hand a production lab full of trained noses a document that says 'here is the exact shape of the thing Leo Vance has been protecting for two years, enjoy.'"

"That's not what the disclosure says."

"It's what the disclosure means. Any decent production chemist reads V4's architecture and sees where the formula thins. They see the shape and they ask what goes there. And what goes there is me."

"You're not in the formula."

"I'm all over the formula. You told me that. Last night. In my bed. You said you built the best thing you've ever made around me."

I don't have an answer for that because it's true and he's using the truth like a scalpel and I'd respect the precision of it if it didn't cut so cleanly.

"That was in confidence."

"I know it was in confidence. That's what makes this worse. I didn't take it as intelligence. I took it as you telling me something real. And now the real thing is the thing that puts me at risk."

"So what do you want me to do?" I say.

"I want you to protect me. Like I would protect you."

"I'm trying to protect both of us. That's the problem. I can't do both."

The room is very quiet. Outside, the sea does its thing. Neither of us is looking at it.

"I can't afford to need you," I say. "And the formula can't afford to need you."

He lets out a breath through his nose. Sharp. Like a door closing inside his chest.

"You're saying I'm a risk to your business."

"That's not what I said."

"You said it exactly like that."

"I said I can't afford—"

"You said the formula can't afford to need me. Which means I make it more expensive. Which means I'm a liability."

"That is a profoundly unfair reading of what I just said."

"Is it wrong?"

I open my mouth. Close it. Open it again.

"I don't know."

He nods. Once. Like that was the only honest answer and he'd been waiting for it.

Then he walks to the wall.

The ventilation grille is iron. Old, monastic, ornamental because it's been functional for four hundred years and four hundred years of function becomes decoration whether you intend it or not. It sits between Lab 2 and Lab 3 at shoulder height. It has been open since day twelve. My formula drifts through it into his lab. His base note drifts through it into mine. Two labs breathing into each other. He told me once that he could smell when I was working late because the vetiver came through around midnight.

He opens the supply drawer under his bench. Takes out a roll of gaffer tape. Grey, industrial, the kind that holds things shut.

He tears off a strip. Presses it across the bottom of the grille. Smooths it with his thumb.

Tears off another strip. Presses it above the first.

Another. Another. Methodical. Strip by strip, from the bottom up. Each one smoothed flat with his thumb, edges pressed tight against the stone. Same hands as the bench. Same precision. Unhurried. Complete.

I stand in the doorway and watch him seal the air between us.

He doesn't look at me while he does it. His face is the flat face from before, the one where the expression has left and whatever remains is structural rather than emotional. He reaches the top of the grille. Tears one final strip. Presses it into place.

The hum stops.

I have been hearing that hum for two weeks. The low, constant movement of air between Lab 2 and Lab 3. Formulas traveling. His work and mine, mixing in the ducts. I'd stopped noticing it the way you stop noticing a heartbeat. And now it's gone, and the silence is a sound.

He puts the gaffer tape back in the drawer. Closes the drawer.

He doesn't slam the door. He closes it. Quietly. Handle turned, latch engaged, released. A deliberate close. Someone who thought about how to close a door and chose the version that makes no noise.

Which is worse.

* * *

The afternoon is long.

I sit in Lab 3 and I try to work and V4 sits on the shelf and the grille on my side is silent. I hold a strip under my nose. Between the notes: the same thinning, the same quiet. It smells like yesterday's triumph and today's problem and I can't tell the difference because they're the same thing.

I re-read Section 4.2 four more times. It doesn't change. Legal language has the decency to mean what it says.

At half five I shower and change and go down to dinner because Paola's trattoria does not negotiate with feelings.

* * *

The table is set for six. Paola counts heads like a general counting soldiers.

Edouard is already seated, napkin tucked, wine poured. He's telling Elena about a vineyard in Burgundy that he either owns or is lying about owning, and Elena is tolerating the story because Edouard tells his lies with enough conviction that they function as entertainment.

Yuki arrives. Takes her usual seat. Says nothing. Unfolds her napkin with the precision of someone performing origami.

I sit.

Paola brings bread. Then olives. Then a carafe of white wine that nobody ordered because Paola doesn't accept orders, she accepts gratitude.

Luca is carrying plates from the kitchen. He puts mine down with a small nod. His face is tired. The Sorrento tiredness, the mother tiredness, the thing he carries without mentioning and I carry without asking because we've agreed on that much.

Leo's chair is empty.

Edouard notices first, because Edouard notices everything except his own volume.

"Where is the American?"

Elena looks at the door. Then at me. She says nothing. Elena's silence is diagnostic.

Luca pauses with a plate. "He said he wasn't hungry."

"Not hungry." Edouard says it like someone has reported a violation of natural law. "The man has been working since seven. He ate an apple at lunch. I know this because I saw the core in the courtyard bin. I observe things. One cannot subsist on a single apple. This is science."

"It's not science," Yuki says.

"It is nutritional fact dressed as science. Close enough."

"He said he wasn't hungry," Luca repeats.

I look at my plate.

The bread is excellent. I tear off a piece and put it in my mouth and chew. I am absolutely fine.

Edouard, for once, does not comment. He picks up his wine. He drinks. He turns to Yuki and says, "This white is very ordinary," and Yuki says, "Good," and they begin a conversation about ordinary wine that is technically about wine and practically about giving me something to listen to that isn't the empty chair.

I could hug him for it. I won't, because he'd be unbearable, but I could.

Paola brings the fish course. She looks at the empty chair. She looks at Luca. She makes a plate and hands it to him.

"Take it up."

"He said—"

"I heard what he said. Take it up."

Luca takes the plate and goes. Paola watches him leave and then she puts another plate in front of me. It has more fish on it than anyone needs.

"Eat," she says.

I eat.

The conversation moves around me. Elena mentions the weather forecast: rain tomorrow. Edouard says rain is good for perfumery because humidity clarifies the nose. Yuki says this is not true. Edouard says it is poetically true, which should count. Elena says it shouldn't.

"I once composed an entire accord during a thunderstorm in Grasse," Edouard says. "The humidity brought forward the jasmine absolute in a way I had never experienced."

"You composed an accord," Yuki says. "During a thunderstorm."

"I am a man of passions."

"You were probably drunk."

"Also true. But the accord was excellent."

Elena writes something on her clipboard. Edouard peers at it.

"Are you taking notes on my thunderstorm?"

"I'm updating the schedule. But if you'd like me to note your thunderstorm, I can create a subcategory."

"Please do. Under 'Moments of Genius.'"

"I'll file it under 'Unverified Claims.' Same section as the Burgundy vineyard."

Under the table, my hands grip my knees.

Luca comes back with the plate. Empty.

"He ate?" Paola says.

"He ate."

Paola nods. Turns back to the kitchen. The matter is resolved. Paola has run this trattoria for thirty years. She has outlasted three mayors, two economic crises, and a nephew who once tried to put pineapple on a pizza in her kitchen. She has fed everyone under her roof and whether they wanted to be fed is irrelevant.

* * *

After dinner, the lane.

The streetlamp at the corner is still out. The others are ahead. Elena is explaining something about tomorrow's schedule. Edouard's hat catches the moonlight.

Yuki falls into step beside me. She's quiet for twenty paces, which, for Yuki, is simply walking.

"He closed it," she says.

"What?"

"The air between your labs. It's gone quiet."

I look at her. Her face is unreadable. It's always unreadable, but tonight it's unreadable with purpose.

"I don't know what you—"

"I'm in Lab 4. I hear everything. The grille between Two and Three has been humming for two weeks. Formulas carry. I could smell your vetiver and his base note mixing in the duct above my ceiling." She adjusts her collar. "This afternoon it stopped."

I don't say anything.

"The monastery breathes," she says. "All of these old buildings do. You block one passage and the air finds another route or it doesn't move at all."

She walks ahead. Her back is very straight. She says nothing more. She doesn't need to. Yuki has said the thing that needed saying and she's finished with it. Cleanly, without excess, without waiting for me to respond.

I stand in the lane and let the others get ahead of me. The air smells like stone and lemon trees and the sea. Absolutely nothing from Lab 2.

* * *

Lab 3. Eleven at night.

The grille on my side is silent. On his side, the tape will be smooth and grey and complete, every strip pressed flat by his thumb.

V4 smells the same. The labdanum, the iris, the oud underneath, the formula thinning at the centre where it always thins. But tonight it feels different. Yesterday that thinning was a doorway. Tonight the room on the other side is dark and there's no one in it.

I hold a strip under my nose. The formula develops. Top notes lifting, heart opening, the base arriving with that slow certainty that makes V4 what it is. The labdanum leans. The iris floats. Between them, the formula does exactly what I built it to do. Fourteen versions. Fourteen failures. One success that costs more than I can pay.

I put the strip down.

I think about reformulating. A V5. Something that doesn't point at him, something with a different absence, an absence shaped like nothing in particular. I pull out a blank evaluation card and start mapping it and I get three notes in before I stop because the architecture collapses without the specific gravity of what's missing. The gap doesn't work as a gap. It works as a Leo-shaped gap. Remove the shape and it's just a hole.

I walk the corridor toward my room. Lab 2's door is closed. There's a line of light underneath it. Thin, yellow, the kind that comes from working late with a single desk lamp.

I walk past it.

My room is cold. The window is open because I left it open this morning, when the monastery was still breathing, when the grille was still open, when V4 was the most beautiful thing I'd ever built instead of the most expensive.

I sit on the bed. The competition brief is still in Lab 3. Section 4.2 is still on page thirty-one. The sixty-two pence is still sixty-two pence.

I close the window.
