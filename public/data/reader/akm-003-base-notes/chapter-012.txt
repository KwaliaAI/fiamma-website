The camping stove is the size of a paperback novel. He's crouched over it in the corner of Lab 2 like a man performing surgery on a hamster, adjusting the flame with a concentration I recognise from watching him work with volatile compounds.

"You're joking," I say from the doorway.

"Good morning to you too."

"Is that a camping stove?"

"It's a perfectly functional single-burner."

"In a fragrance laboratory."

"The espresso machine was a professional courtesy." He doesn't look up. "This is how I actually make coffee."

I step inside. The lab smells like last night. Like us, and the bread, and something green I can't name. The stool is still broken in the corner, one leg at an angle that suggests a catastrophic structural failure rather than gentle wear.

"How long have you been hiding a camping stove in Lab 2?"

"Since week one."

"And the espresso machine upstairs?"

"Elena insisted. I let her believe it converted me."

He pours something dark and furious into a ceramic cup that has no business being in a professional setting. It looks like he stole it from a motorway service station. He hands it to me.

I drink it.

It is, without question, the worst coffee I have ever had. It tastes like a tyre fire in a forest.

"You're not saying anything," he says.

"I'm drinking your coffee."

"You're not saying anything about the coffee."

"It's coffee. I'm drinking it."

"Margot."

"It's very hot."

"It tastes like shit and you're being polite."

"I'm never polite. I'm being British, which is entirely different."

He almost smiles. Almost. The composure is different this morning. It's still there but there's a crack running through it, a hairline fracture where something shifted in the night and didn't shift back. He pours himself a cup and drinks it standing, leaning against the bench where, twelve hours ago, things happened to that bench that it was not structurally designed for.

Neither of us mentions it. We drink our terrible coffee.

"I owe you something," he says.

"A better cup of coffee?"

"An explanation."

"You don't owe me anything."

"DIRTY," he says. "You've been circling it for two weeks. You figured out the architecture. You identified the structure. You earned the rest."

I set the cup down. "All right."

He doesn't start right away. He refills his cup, adjusts the flame down, turns it back up. Buying time with his hands.

"Her name was Mara," he says. "She was a photographer."

"Was?"

"Is. She's alive. She's in Portland now. She was in Astoria. Same as me."

"Queens Astoria or Oregon Astoria?"

"Queens."

"Good. The other one sounds made up."

"She had a darkroom in the bathroom. I had a lab bench in the kitchen. We were very compatible on paper."

"On paper."

"On paper we were two people who'd turned every room in a one-bedroom apartment into a workspace and called it intimacy."

"She used this shampoo. Cedar and something synthetic, something laundry-clean. I never figured out the synthetic and it drove me crazy. I could break down a forty-ingredient commercial fragrance in an afternoon but I couldn't figure out her shampoo."

"That's either romantic or deeply obsessive."

"Both. That's the problem."

"How long?"

"Three years. She left in March. I came home and the darkroom was gone but the bathroom still smelled like fixer solution. And the hallway. The hallway smelled like her shampoo for weeks. The building had that shared hallway carpet, the kind that holds everything."

The camping stove guttered. The flame shrank to a blue ring, barely there, and Leo reached down and adjusted the valve without looking, the way you adjust something you've fixed a hundred times. The flame came back.

"Sorry. The valve sticks when it's cold."

"It's twenty-six degrees."

"The valve disagrees."

"The shampoo ghost."

He looks at me. "Yeah. Exactly that."

"So DIRTY was about Mara."

"DIRTY was supposed to be for Mara. A personal fragrance. Presence. What she smelled like when she was there. I'd been working on it for months."

"And then she left."

"And then the gift became the thing I was trying to describe the absence of."

"You rebuilt it."

"I inverted it. Everything I'd built to capture presence, I restructured around what was left when someone goes. The cedar's still there but it's the memory of cedar. The synthetic I never identified became the centre of the whole thing."

I think about V4. The centre I've been trying to fill for days.

"There's something else," I say.

"What makes you say that?"

"Because Mara explains DIRTY but she doesn't explain you. The way you work. The fortress."

He puts his cup down.

"Daniel Reeves," he says.

"Who's Daniel Reeves?"

"Before Mara. Before DIRTY. My collaborator in Brooklyn."

"Collaborator how?"

"We had a shared workspace. Not a company, nothing formal. Two noses with one rent cheque. We were developing a base note together. An accord. Three notes functioning as one."

"A chord."

"A chord. Yes." He looks at me differently when I use the right language. "It took us eight months. And it worked. It actually worked. This dense, unresolvable thing that read as a single note to anyone who smelled it but was three distinct materials in conversation."

"That's beautiful."

"It was. I went home. Family thing, two weeks in Michigan. When I came back, Daniel had filed a proprietary blend registration."

"Under both your names."

"Under his name."

The camping stove hisses between us.

"He sold it," Leo says. "Mid-tier house, exclusive license. By the time I found out, the paperwork was done."

"That's theft."

"That's fragrance. There's no copyright. No patent protection for a scent composition. You can trademark a name. You can patent a delivery mechanism. But the smell itself? Belongs to whoever files first."

"Did you fight it?"

"With what? We had no contract. No written agreement. Just two guys in a rented space with a handshake and a shared gas chromatograph."

"So he got away with it."

"He got away with it and got a career out of it. He's at a house in New Jersey now. Does fine."

"Does fine," I repeat.

I picked up my cup and the coffee had gone cold, which somehow made it worse. Cold terrible coffee is a different category of terrible than hot terrible coffee. The hot version has conviction. The cold version is just sad.

"And you never confronted him?" I said.

"I saw him once. Industry event in SoHo. He was wearing a suit that cost more than my first lab bench." Leo's mouth tightened. "He waved."

"He WAVED?"

"Across the room. Like we were old friends who'd lost touch."

"What did you do?"

"Left. Went home. Rebuilt the chord."

"The worst part wasn't the accord."

"The worst part was that I'd shown him how I built things. The method. How I layer. How I think about negative space. How I approach the structure before the materials. And he used the seeing."

"The seeing."

"The way into my process. I'd given him the way I see. And he used it."

Somewhere in the corridor, a pipe clanked. The monastery's plumbing announcing itself, the way it did at unpredictable intervals, medieval infrastructure reminding everyone that hot water was a privilege and silence was temporary.

Leo looked at the ceiling. "That pipe has been doing that since week one."

"Elena says it's the boiler."

"Elena says everything is the boiler. It's her answer to every mechanical question."

He says this without drama. Without raising his voice. Without the tremor that would make this easier to categorise. He says it the way you'd describe a car accident that happened years ago to someone who wasn't there.

"That's why DIRTY is a fortress," I say.

"The base note. Yes."

"You rebuilt the chord alone."

"In my kitchen. Took fourteen months. Made it so dense, so layered, so structurally illegible that nobody could reverse-engineer it. Not even me, sometimes."

"The base note isn't a formula. It's a defence system."

He doesn't confirm this. He drinks his terrible coffee and looks at the camping stove and says nothing, which confirms it completely.

I reach into my bag.

The vial is small. Green glass, no label, twelve euros from a cluttered shop on a side street in town. I've been carrying it for three days. I set it on the bench between us.

"What's this?" he says.

"I bought it at that perfume shop. The one past the church with all the steps."

"Ferretti's."

"I've been trying to pull it apart all week. Green top note, animalic heart, moss drydown I couldn't place. But the structure underneath. Three notes functioning as one."

He picks up the vial.

"It's degraded," I say. "Old stock, probably years on the shelf. But the architecture is there. It's an earlier iteration. A precursor."

"Where did you find this?" His voice has changed.

"On a shelf between a lavender room spray and something called Notte di Passione that smelled like a carpet shop."

"Margot."

"Twelve euros."

He holds the vial up to the light. Turns it. His thumb runs over the glass the way I've seen him touch test strips, reading through his skin.

"This is the version before Daniel," he says. "Before either of us. This is the sketch."

"I know."

"How did it end up in a shop in southern Italy?"

"I have no idea. Supply chains for discontinued compositions are a mess. Could have been a sample run, a regional distributor, a dead-stock wholesaler."

He's still holding it. Not smelling it. Just holding it. A ghost in green glass. Something that existed before the theft and the rebuilding and the fortress. The original impulse, twelve euros on a dusty shelf.

"You should keep it," I say.

"Why?"

"Because it's yours. It was always yours. Twelve euros and some questionable shelf life doesn't change that."

He closes his hand around the vial. Puts it in his shirt pocket. Doesn't say thank you, which is right. Thank you would be too small and too formal and would close something that should stay open.

He makes another cup of coffee. Hands it to me.

"This is genuinely terrible," I say.

"I know."

"Like, aggressively terrible. It's not bad in a charming way. It's bad in a way that suggests hostility."

"Do you want me to use the espresso machine?"

"God, no. Pour me another."

* * *

The grille is open.

Neither of us opened it this morning. It was open last night and it stayed open through the night and now it's early afternoon and my formula sheets are on my bench and his are on his and between us is six feet of open air where a locked metal grille used to define the boundary.

I'm working on V4. The centre is still open. But I understand it differently now. An open centre isn't always a problem. Sometimes it's architectural. Sometimes the incompleteness is the point.

I make a modification. Swap the bergamot for a bitter orange I haven't used before. Write it down.

From Lab 2, the sound of Leo's pen on paper. The camping stove clicking off.

"Try reducing the musk by a third," he says. Not looking up.

"I didn't ask."

"I know."

I reduce the musk by a third. It's better. I don't tell him.

He knows anyway. I can hear it in the way his pen doesn't stop.

Yuki appears in the corridor. She's carrying a single test strip, which for Yuki constitutes a full workday's materials. She stops outside my door. Looks at me. Looks at the open grille. Looks at the bench where my formula sits with its new bitter orange and its reduced musk.

"Your formula has changed," she says.

"It has become personal."

She walks away. Her footsteps are the only sound in the corridor, precise and unhurried, and then they're gone.

I sit with it. The observation. Not advice. Not permission. Not judgment. Yuki, three materials, telling me she can see what's happened to mine.

It lands harder than anything she could have said with more words.

* * *

The trattoria is different tonight.

I know it as soon as we sit down. The tables are the same, the bread's the same, Edouard is in his usual chair with his usual glass of wine. But the frequency is off. The room is running at the wrong speed.

Luca brings the bread.

His hands do the work. Set the basket down. Straighten the cloth. Place the olive oil where Edouard can reach it. But his face is doing nothing. Not sad. Not performing not-sad. Just neutral in a way I've never seen on him. The twenty-five-year-old whose entire professional skill set is charm and light has turned the light off.

"Luca," Edouard says.

"Sì?"

Edouard looks at him. Luca looks back. Whatever passes between them doesn't need language or translation. Edouard nods. Luca moves to the next table.

From the kitchen, Paola's voice. Rapid Italian, low, the tone pulled tight. I've heard her on the phone before but this is different. She's receiving information she already expected and didn't want confirmed.

Leo looks at me.

"His mother," I say quietly.

"I know."

"Sorrento?"

"I think so."

Paola comes out of the kitchen. Her face is composed, which is the most alarming thing I've ever seen on her, because Paola's face is never composed. It is always in motion, expressing fury about the bread or joy about the wine or contempt about the way Edouard holds his fork. Composure on Paola is wrong.

She puts an extra plate of pasta in front of Edouard. No commentary. No explanation. No opinion about whether he deserves it or has earned it or is eating too fast or too slow. She sets it down and goes back to the kitchen.

Edouard looks at the pasta. He looks around the table. For the first time since I've known him, he doesn't say anything. He doesn't tell a story about a similar plate in 1987. He doesn't compare the sauce to something his second wife made. He picks up his fork and eats.

We all eat.

I catch Luca's eye across the room when he passes with a carafe of water. He shakes his head. Small. Private. A tiny motion that says: not now.

I look away.

Dinner is shorter than usual. The pasta is very good, because Paola's pasta is always very good, and tonight she's cooked it with furious precision. She can't fix anything except what's on the plate. Edouard eats his extra portion. Nobody orders dessert. Leo's hand finds mine under the table at some point and stays there, and I let it, because sometimes the only useful thing a hand can do is be where someone else can reach it.

Luca clears the plates. Efficient, quiet, professional. He is twenty-five and his mother is in a hospital in Sorrento and he has tables to clear and a cloth to fold and he does all of it without the smile and without the flirtation and without any of the things that made him easy to dismiss as a beautiful uncomplicated man, because it turns out the uncomplicated part was performance and underneath is just a person.

We leave early. The walk back to the residency is quiet. Edouard walks ahead, which he never does. Leo's hand is still in mine.

"Water or wine?" Leo says at the door.

"Water."

"Okay."

We go inside.
