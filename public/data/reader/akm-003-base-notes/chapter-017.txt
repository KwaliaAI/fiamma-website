The grille is open.

I hear it before I'm fully awake. A scrape. Iron slats adjusting after two days of silence, the medieval ductwork resettling into its original position, and through the grille comes the first thread of Leo's formula: green, resinous, the base note underneath like a pulse. The monastery exhales through Lab 3's brass grate and I lie in bed and listen to a building remember how to breathe.

He peeled the tape before sunrise. I know this because the quality of the air changed at five-something, when the sky was still grey and the sea was still flat and the only sound in the east wing was stone cooling. I was half-asleep. The scrape woke me. I smiled at the ceiling. All dignity gone. Something better in its place.

I get up. Shower. Dress. Lab 3 first, because Lab 3 is always first.

V4 is on the shelf. I hold a strip under my nose. The gap is there, the same architecture, the same absence, and through the reopened grille his base note arrives like a sentence finishing itself.

I cap the vial. I am submitting V4 as it is. This was decided last night, on the terrace, with Leo's arm around me and Positano going dark below and the word "disgraziata" hanging in the air between fish jokes. The disclosure clause is a problem for today. The formula is a fact.

I take my tea to the corridor.

* * *

Edouard is standing by the courtyard fountain, fully dressed at seven in the morning. Blazer. Tie. The wine-coloured one. He's holding an espresso cup in his left hand. He's been there a while. I can tell from how far down the cup is.

"You made a decision," he says.

I stop walking.

"Your face," he says. "I have been married three times. When the decision is made, the jaw does a particular thing. Your jaw is doing it."

"I've always had this jaw."

"You have always had this jaw. But today it is doing something different." He puts the espresso cup on the fountain edge. "Come with me."

"Where?"

"Up."

"That's not a destination."

"It is the best destination. The only one that matters. Stairs."

"It's seven in the morning."

"Stairs do not respect the clock."

He's already walking. I follow because Edouard in motion is a force that resists argument, and because the espresso cup on the fountain edge is still full, which means he was waiting for me specifically, which means this is not spontaneous, which means Edouard has a plan. Edouard with a plan is either magnificent or catastrophic and I haven't had enough tea to calculate which.

He leads me past the east wing, past Lab 4, past the storage room where Elena keeps the emergency clipboard supply. A narrow stone staircase I've never seen. Tucked behind a column, covered in wisteria so thick the first two steps are invisible unless you know where to put your feet.

"How long has this been here?"

"Since the fourteenth century, I imagine. The monks needed somewhere to dry their laundry."

"You don't know that."

"I know it with the certainty of a man who has invented it. This is sufficient."

We climb. The stairs are steep and uneven and the wisteria closes overhead like a tunnel of purple and green and the light comes through in pieces. Twelve steps. Fifteen. The stone is worn in the centre of each tread, centuries of feet, and Edouard takes them steadily, one hand trailing the wall, breathing audibly but not stopping because Edouard does not stop on stairs. This was established in week two and it is constitutional.

At the top, an iron door. He pushes it open.

A flat terrace. Stone. The whole of Positano below, tumbling down the cliff in whites and yellows and terracottas, the sea beyond it blue and enormous and still. The morning light is coming in from the left, low and gold, catching the rooftops and the boats in the harbour and the curve of the coast stretching east toward Amalfi.

"Oh," I say.

"Yes."

"This has been here the whole time."

"This has been here the whole time."

I walk to the edge. Low wall. Waist height. The view is absurd. Cinematic. Something you'd see on a postcard and think: nobody actually lives here.

"My secret," Edouard says behind me. "My only one." He comes to the wall and puts his hands on it and looks out. "I am too old for most secrets, but a view I keep."

"How long have you been coming up here?"

"Every morning. Since day three. I wake at five because at seventy-one the body has decided that sleep is a luxury and has revoked it. I make espresso. I climb. I stand here. I watch the light arrive."

"Alone?"

"Alone. I have been alone on this roof for fifteen mornings and I have not been lonely once, which is the difference between solitude and sadness, and that difference took me sixty years to learn."

He says this looking at the sea, not at me, and the weight of it arrives sideways, how Edouard delivers everything, wrapped in a story so you don't see the feeling until it's already inside you.

"Sit," he says. There's a bench. Stone. Ancient. He sits first and I sit next to him and the bench is cold and the view is ridiculous and Edouard smells like espresso and linen and his own formula, technically his competitor's advantage, but at seven in the morning on a secret roof I can't bring myself to care.

"Your formula," he says.

"What about it?"

"I have been smelling it through the ventilation for eighteen days. Everyone has. The monastery is not subtle about these things. Air moves. Stone carries. I have smelled your formula develop from V1 to V4 without opening your lab door, which is either a testament to medieval engineering or to the fact that you use an extraordinary amount of oud."

"The oud ratio is correct."

"The oud ratio is audacious. This is what makes it correct."

He crosses his legs and adjusts his blazer. Takes a breath.

"It will win."

I look at him.

"I tell you this as a competitor and as a man who has been smelling formulas for fifty years. It will win because it asks a question, and the panel will not be able to stop asking it long after the strip is cold. A formula that answers a question is beautiful. A formula that asks one is dangerous. Yours is dangerous."

"Edouard—"

"I am not being generous. I am being accurate. Generosity would be telling you it needs improvement so I could maintain the pleasant fiction that I might beat you. I have decided against this fiction. At seventy-one, the fictions one can maintain become limited. I am conserving."

"What about yours?"

He looks at the sea. His hands are on his knees, fingers spread, the same posture he has at dinner when he's about to say something he means.

"Mine is beautiful. It is technically flawless. Every transition is clean, every material at its optimal concentration, every structural choice defensible. I could present it to any jury in the world and they would admire it."

He pauses.

"It is also safe. And I am too old to pretend otherwise."

"Edouard."

"I made the safe version because the brave version is yours and I recognised it. Around day eight. Your bergamot was doing something through the vent that I had considered doing and decided against, because doing it required tolerating an opening in the formula that I could not justify technically. You tolerated it. You built around it. You made it structural. I heard you do it and I knew."

"Knew what?"

"That you had made the choice I wouldn't make. The brave choice. The one where the formula risks being ugly in order to be true." He smooths his tie. "My formula will never be ugly. This is its limitation."

I don't know what to say. There is nothing in the professional vocabulary that covers a competitor telling you he recognises your courage because it's the courage he chose not to have. There's no protocol. There's no banter. There's just Edouard on a stone bench at seven in the morning, telling the truth on a roof nobody else knows about.

"It's still a brilliant formula," I say.

"It is a brilliant formula made by a man who has won seventeen competitions and lost eleven and tied two and buried one colleague and married three women and been brave approximately four times in seventy-one years. Four times is not enough. But it is what I have."

He reaches for his tie.

The wine-coloured one. Silk. I've seen it every day since day one. Linen blazer and silk tie, Edouard's uniform, the Frenchness of it so consistent it became invisible, like the stairs, like the fountain.

He undoes the knot. Slowly. Pulls it free from his collar with the deliberate care of a man handling something that has a history longer than most marriages.

"My first wife gave me this tie on the day I won my first commission. Thirty-two years ago. In Lyon. The commission was for a department store fragrance. Terrible brief. I made something that smelled like rain on hot pavement because I was thirty-nine and believed that rain on hot pavement was romantic." He holds the tie in both hands. "She said, 'Wear this to every competition. It will remind you that the first one was the hardest.'"

"Was it?"

"It was the easiest. She was wrong about that. She was right about everything else." He folds the tie once. "It has been on my neck for seventeen victories and eleven losses and two ties and the funeral of a man I respected more than anyone alive."

He holds it out to me.

"Wear it. To the panel."

"Edouard, I can't—"

"You can."

"It's your wife's tie."

"It was my wife's gift. It became my tie. Now it can become yours." His hand is steady. The silk catches the morning light, deep red, the colour of wine that costs more than I can afford. "Everything holds what it loves, even after the love is gone. Let it hold something new."

I take it.

The silk is lighter than I expected. Thirty-two years of competitions and funerals and seventeen victories and it weighs almost nothing. That seems wrong. It should weigh more. But silk doesn't work that way. Silk holds everything in the weave.

I wrap it around my left wrist. Three loops. I tie it with my right hand and my teeth, inelegant, practical, how I tie everything, and the knot holds. The silk sits against my pulse point, where the skin is thin and the blood is close.

Edouard watches me tie it and his face does something I haven't seen. His eyes are bright and his mouth is set and the expression is neither happy nor sad but something older, something that has to do with giving a thing away and watching it land.

"Good," he says.

"Thank you."

"Do not thank me. Win."

"That's what Nadia says."

"Then Nadia is correct. What else does Nadia say?"

"Mostly profanity."

"I like Nadia already."

* * *

We sit on the roof for another few minutes. The light changes. Positano wakes up below us, shutters opening, a moped somewhere on the cliff road, the first tour boat pulling away from the dock with a diesel grumble that carries up the stone face.

"We should go down," I say.

"We should. But the view is better than the descent."

"The view is always better than the descent. That's the problem with views."

"That is not a problem. That is the function." He stands. Adjusts his collar, which looks strange without the tie. Incomplete. Like a sentence missing its verb. "I will require a replacement. Elena will have an opinion. Elena always has an opinion."

"Buy one in town."

"In Positano? They will sell me linen. I am French. I require silk."

"You require therapy."

"Also true. But silk first."

We go down the wisteria stairs. He lets me go first. Chivalry, or strategy, or the practical reality that if he falls on a fourteenth-century staircase someone should be below to break the impact.

* * *

Chiara calls the competitors for a schedule meeting at nine. The courtyard. Elena has the clipboard out, which means this is official.

Panel in two days. Final lab time tomorrow. No lab access on panel day. Submissions by six PM the evening before. Elena reads the logistics in her brisk, competent voice, the voice that carries schedules the way cathedrals carry sound.

The four of us sit in a row on the fountain edge. Edouard, me, Yuki, Leo. Like students waiting for exam instructions. Edouard has his legs crossed and his collar open and looks distinguished in a way that only Frenchmen over seventy can manage while missing a tie. Yuki has her hands around her green tea. Her face is still. Ready. She hasn't wasted a single gesture all morning and she won't start now.

Leo is three feet to my right.

I haven't seen him since last night. Since the terrace and the floor and "LITERALLY medieval" and his shirt around my shoulders. He's showered. Clean shirt. He's slept, and slept well. I can smell the difference in his formula on his skin, how the base note sits when the body isn't tense. It's fuller this morning. Easier.

He looks at me.

I look at him.

It's the briefest thing. Half a second. His eyes on mine, mine on his, and I don't smile and he doesn't smile and neither of us moves because the courtyard has four competitors and one coordinator and one clipboard and this is not the place. But the half-second holds everything. The terrace. The floor. The tape. The grille that's open again because he peeled it before sunrise while I was half-asleep.

I look away first. The tie on my wrist presses against my pulse.

Chiara finishes the logistics. Elena makes a note. Edouard stands, adjusts his tieless collar, and surveys both of us. He has been paying attention for eighteen days. He is about to prove it.

"Well," he says. "The ventilation is working again."

The courtyard goes very still.

Elena's pen stops. Yuki's tea pauses halfway to her mouth. Leo's face does nothing. His own confession. My face does nothing, and that's worse.

Yuki sets down her tea.

"It was always working," she says. "You were not listening."

"I was listening to other things."

"Yes." She picks the tea back up. "To yourself."

The silence lasts three seconds. Then Edouard laughs. A short, sharp bark. He's been hit cleanly and he knows it. He looks at Yuki with something that might be love if love is what happens when someone sees you clearly and you're grateful instead of afraid.

"She's right," he says. To no one. To everyone. To the courtyard and the fountain and the four competitors who have been breathing each other's formulas through medieval stone for eighteen days. "She's always right. This is insufferable."

Yuki says nothing. She drinks her tea.

I look at the wine-coloured silk on my wrist. Thirty-two years. Seventeen victories. A first wife who could smell a lie in a formula. A brave choice he gave to me because he wouldn't make it himself.

Leo catches my eye across the fountain. His mouth moves. The smallest change. A fraction of a smile directed at my wrist, at the silk, at Edouard, at the ventilation that is working again because he climbed on something before sunrise and peeled tape off a grille while the monastery slept.

I wrap my fingers around the tie. The silk has taken my temperature.

Two days.

* * *

I find Chiara in the central lab at half past ten. She's alone. She's standing at the long bench with a strip in each hand, held at chest height, eyes closed.

I wait in the doorway. A perfumer with closed eyes and two strips is in the middle of a sentence, and you don't interrupt a sentence. You wait for the full stop.

She opens her eyes. Puts the strips down. Looks at me.

"Ms. Crane."

"Can I talk to you? About the submission."

"Close the door."

I close the door. The lab is clean. Morning light through the east windows catches the glass cabinets and the rows of source naturals in their amber bottles with handwritten labels. Chiara's reading glasses sit folded on the counter next to a single espresso cup, empty and perfectly aligned with the bench edge. Everything Chiara touches acquires alignment.

She doesn't sit. She leans against the bench, crosses her arms, and waits. She expected this conversation days ago. She's been keeping a space for it in her schedule.

"Section 4.2," I say.

"Yes."

"Full formula disclosure to the production partner."

"I know what Section 4.2 says. I wrote it."

Right. Of course she did.

"My formula has an open centre. Deliberate. Load-bearing. It's the architecture that makes V4 work. If the production partner receives the full disclosure, a trained nose will read the architecture and know whose method it points at. Another competitor's proprietary method."

Chiara's expression doesn't change. Her arms stay crossed. She looks at me the way she looked at the puttanesca at Paola's that first night: tasting the components individually before deciding whether the whole thing holds.

"Which competitor?"

"I can't tell you that."

"You're asking me to amend a contractual clause and you can't tell me whose work you're protecting."

"If I name them, I've already done the thing I'm trying to prevent. You'd know whose method the architecture points at. That's the disclosure."

She uncrosses her arms. Picks up the espresso cup, looks into it, puts it back down. The cup returns to its exact position on the bench. I'd need a ruler to confirm this but I'm confident.

"The open centre," she says. "The architecture of your formula."

"Yes."

"I've smelled it. Through the ventilation. Through the stone. Everyone has. Edouard told me last week your bergamot was doing something criminal. He meant it as a compliment."

"He tends to."

"That open centre is what makes V4 exceptional. You know this. I know this. A formula built around what isn't there is either a failure or a provocation, and yours is a provocation."

She says provocation the way she said continue at the check-in. As a verdict that needs no ornament.

"If I submit the full architectural notes, the formula becomes a map. A production chemist reads the map and sees where it thins. They see the shape of what should be there and they ask what goes in it. And what goes in it is someone else's work. Someone in this building."

"I'm aware of how architecture reads."

"I know you are. That's why I'm here and not with a solicitor."

She pushes off the bench. The lean was listening. The stand is deciding. I've watched Chiara long enough to know the difference.

"What are you proposing?"

I reach into my back pocket. The napkin. Creased, wine-stained at one corner where Paola refilled my glass without asking. The words are in black biro, my handwriting, which Nadia once described as "early-onset doctor."

I put the napkin on the bench between us.

Chiara picks it up. Her lips move slightly, tasting the language the way she tastes formulas.

The space constitutes a proprietary architectural element whose disclosure would compromise a third party's trade methods.

"You wrote this on a napkin."

"At the trattoria. Paola asked what I was doing. I told her it was a love letter. She said love letters should be shorter."

"Paola is correct about most things. Including this." She puts the napkin down and smooths it flat with two fingers. "You want to submit V4 with the materials, proportions, and build notes intact, but withhold the structural annotation that identifies the open centre."

"Yes. The production partner gets everything they need to reproduce the formula. They get the what. They don't get the shape of the why."

"And you believe this protects the third party."

"I believe it makes the architecture illegible. A production chemist looking at the formula without the structural map will see an opening and assume it's an error or a stylistic choice. With the map, they'll see it's shaped around something specific. Something with a signature."

Chiara is quiet. She picks up one of the strips from the bench and holds it at arm's length, looking at it the way you look at something you've already decided about and are revisiting because you enjoy being right.

"I smelled V4 at the bench review," she says. "Thursday. When you left Lab 3 for lunch. I do this with every competitor. I go in and I smell the current state and I form my own assessment, because the panel is external but the house is mine and I will not send work forward that I have not smelled myself."

I didn't know this. I sit with it. The thought of Chiara in Lab 3, alone, nose over V4, reading the architecture.

"That open centre is the formula," she says. "Filling it would make V4 technically complete and artistically dead, and I did not build Radici's commission to produce technically complete work. The clause was written for formulas that contain their entire architecture on the page. Yours doesn't. The most important part of yours is what isn't there, and forcing it onto the page would destroy the thing that makes it worth producing."

She turns back to the bench. Opens a drawer. Takes out a single sheet of paper. Radici letterhead. The kind that costs more than my rent.

"I will amend Section 4.2 for your submission. Full material specifications, proportions, and build notes proceed to the production partner. The structural annotation stays with me. Filed under my editorial authority as artistic director of the commission."

"Not legal. Editorial."

"The open centre is an artistic decision. It gets an artistic protection. If the production partner has questions about the architecture, they come to me. I'll tell them the architecture is the point, and the point is not their concern."

She writes. Three lines on the letterhead. Signs it. The signature is small and sharp, like everything Chiara does, occupying the minimum necessary space and filling it completely.

She slides the paper across the bench.

I read it. Three sentences. Material disclosure proceeds in full. Structural and architectural annotations withheld under the artistic director's editorial authority. Effective immediately upon countersignature.

Paola was right. The love letter should have been shorter.

"The person whose work you're protecting," Chiara says. She's watching me. "I will not ask the name. I do not need it. I have been in this building for three weeks with four perfumers and functional ventilation and I am not stupid."

"I never thought you were."

"Good. Then we understand each other." She hands me the pen. "Sign."

I sign. My signature is worse than hers, larger and messier, the handwriting of shipping invoices and warehouse receipts in Bermondsey. It looks ridiculous next to Chiara's elegant notation. It looks like exactly what it is.

Chiara takes the paper back. Puts it in the drawer. Closes the drawer.

"Your formula is exceptional," she says. Quietly. The way you'd say it about weather, or distance, or anything that simply is. "The panel will see it. I wanted you to know that I saw it first."

I stand up. My legs forget how joints work. I grip the bench edge for half a second. Chiara sees it. Chiara sees everything. She doesn't mention it.

"Thank you," I say.

"Don't thank me. Win the commission. Make the formula worth the amendment."

She puts her glasses on. Picks up the pen. Returns to the document she was reading before I walked in, and I am dismissed, and the dismissal is the kindest thing she could do because it means this is finished and it's finished cleanly and I don't have to stand here performing gratitude while my hands shake.

I close the door behind me.

The corridor is empty. Hot stone and the hum of ventilation through the walls, carrying air from lab to lab, formula to formula, the monastery breathing how it has for six hundred years. I lean against the wall. The stone is cool against my shoulder blades. Edouard's tie is on my wrist, silk against pulse, and the napkin is in my back pocket, eighteen words I didn't need because Chiara had already smelled the answer.

I am very tired. The kind of tired that lives behind your eyes and in the specific muscles you use to hold your face still while someone is deciding your future.

I stand there for a while. The corridor. The stone. The air.

Then I push off the wall and walk toward Lab 3. The amendment is signed. The submission is at six. The formula is safe.
