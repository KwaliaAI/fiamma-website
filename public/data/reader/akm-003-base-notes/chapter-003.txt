Chiara delivers the news at breakfast as though announcing a minor schedule change rather than the total collapse of our dining infrastructure.

"Gianluca's mother is unwell. He's taken the ferry to Naples. The kitchen is closed until further notice."

She says this and picks up her espresso and drinks it and the five of us sit with the information as it settles across the table like weather.

Edouard puts down his bread. "Define 'further notice.'"

"When his mother is better."

"And when will his mother be better?"

"When she is better, Edouard."

"So we are to starve. Beautifully, I assume. In a former monastery. With excellent ventilation and no dinner."

Chiara does not dignify this. Chiara has a talent for not dignifying things that borders on a performing art. She sets her cup down, aligns it with the saucer, and says, "There is a trattoria in lower Positano. Elena has the details. I suggest you eat there tonight." She stands. Leaves. The door closes behind her with the finality of a woman who has communicated everything she intends to communicate and considers questions a character flaw.

"Lower Positano," Elena says, pulling out her phone. She's already working. Elena is always already working. "The trattoria is called Da Paola. It's family-run. The barista at the café on the second landing told me about it yesterday. Good food, cheap, the owner decides what you eat."

"The owner decides," Edouard repeats.

"Apparently."

"I think I'm in love."

"You haven't met her."

"This is immaterial."

Yuki has said nothing. She is looking at the stairs visible through the refectory window. The seventy-three stone steps that connect the monastery to the town below. She looks at the stairs. She looks at her shoes. She stands up, goes to her room, and returns four minutes later wearing different shoes. Flats. Rubber-soled. She sits back down and resumes eating as though she has not just conducted a complete risk assessment and wardrobe intervention in the time it took Edouard to finish a sentence.

"The walk is about fifteen minutes once you reach the bottom," Elena says. "The stairs take five if you move quickly."

"I have never moved quickly on stairs in my life," Edouard says. "I am French. We descend with purpose."

"You descend with commentary," I say.

"Commentary is purpose."

Leo has been quiet through all of this. He's holding his coffee, watching the table process the news from the side, with the focused calm of someone taking notes in a language the rest of us can't read. He asks the only question that matters.

"How long?"

Elena checks her phone. "Chiara didn't say. His mother is in hospital. Could be days. Could be weeks."

"Weeks," Edouard says. He gives the word the weight of a medical diagnosis.

"The trattoria is good. The barista was very clear about this."

"The barista was clear."

"The barista knows food."

Edouard looks at the ceiling. The ceiling, being fourteenth-century stone, offers no comfort. He looks back at Elena. "I will die on those stairs."

"You won't die."

"Coming up. After wine. In the dark. On medieval stone. In shoes that were not designed for mountaineering because I am a perfumer, not a sherpa."

"It's seventy-three steps."

"Seventy-three opportunities for catastrophe."

Elena puts her phone down. Slowly. Deliberately. A general putting down a map. The reconnaissance is over. The campaign begins. "I've timed the buttons," she says.

"The buttons?"

"The timer lights on the staircase. You press the button, the lights come on. They stay on for ninety seconds. Then they go off."

"Ninety seconds."

"We need to move faster than Edouard."

"Everyone moves faster than Edouard," Edouard says. "This is by design."

I spend the afternoon on V2. The neroli bridge I've been avoiding for three days because it felt too obvious, too much like the answer a different perfumer would reach for. I try it anyway. Sometimes the obvious answer is obvious because it works, and pride is expensive when you owe two hundred thousand pounds.

The neroli slots between the labdanum and the iris and holds. It holds better than it should. The centre is still thin, still wanting. But the neroli gives it edges. I can feel where the missing note sits, can almost taste the weight of it, and the formula around it is tighter, braver, closer to the thing I'm reaching for. I work for two hours and leave V2 on a strip to develop and go to my room and change my shoes because tonight we descend.

Six thirty. The courtyard. The five of us assembled at the top of the stairs like a party preparing for an expedition that none of us signed up for and one of us is treating as a funeral.

Elena goes first. She's carrying her phone in one hand and the knowledge of every timer button location in the other. "First button is at the top. I'll press it. We have ninety seconds to reach the second button at step twenty-three. Leo, you're tall. You press that one."

"Why does height matter?"

"The button is higher than it should be. Someone installed it for a giant." She looks at Edouard. "Edouard, you're in the middle. Don't stop."

"I will stop if the spirit moves me."

"The spirit should move you downward. At pace."

Yuki is already three steps down. She has not waited for instructions. She does not appear to need instructions. She descends with the efficient silence of a woman who has calculated the optimal speed for seventy-three stone steps and is executing without consultation.

Elena presses the button. The lights come on. Fluorescent, ungenerous, light that exists to prove that light exists rather than to actually illuminate anything. The staircase unfolds below us: narrow, stone, a wall on one side and a railing on the other with the sea beyond it going dark.

"Go," Elena says.

We go.

I am wearing my Primark sandals. Three pounds fifty from a bin in Peckham, bought because the Ferragamos committed structural suicide on Day 1 and a woman needs something between her feet and medieval stone. The Primarks have no arch support. They have no ankle support. They have, if I'm honest, no support of any kind. They are two pieces of rubber connected by straps that aspire to the concept of a shoe without fully committing. On flat ground they are functional. On seventy-three stone steps going down, they are a test of faith.

"Step twelve," Elena calls from below. "There's a loose one. Left side."

"Thank you for the advance warning," Edouard says from behind me. He is descending at a pace that suggests each step is an individual decision requiring careful deliberation. "I am treating this as a pilgrimage. In Compostela they walk for months. I am merely walking for my dinner."

"In Compostela they don't have timer lights."

"In Compostela they have faith. Which is what I am operating on, because I cannot feel my left ankle."

The lights go out.

Total darkness. The sea is still there, audible, indifferent to our situation. The stone is still under my feet. But the visual world has ended and we are standing on a cliff face in the dark and someone, somewhere, failed to press a button.

"Elena."

"I know."

"The button, Elena."

"I am looking for the button."

"You are looking for the button in the dark."

"Yes, Edouard, that is the nature of the problem."

Leo's phone torch comes on. A single cone of white light sweeping the wall. He finds the button panel at step twenty-three. Presses it. The lights return. We are standing in a staircase barely wide enough for two, Edouard frozen mid-step with one hand on the railing and the other pressed to his chest, performing cardiac arrest, and Yuki fifteen steps ahead of everyone, already at the next landing, waiting. She'd arrived on time. The rest of us were still failing at it.

"Charming," Edouard says. "Reminds me of my first marriage."

We reach the bottom in eleven minutes. Elena says it should have taken five. Edouard says five minutes is a number invented by people who don't have knees. The town is below us: lights, voices, the sound of a Vespa somewhere, the sea spreading out dark and flat beyond the harbour. We walk ten minutes through narrow streets. Leo keeps his phone torch on at the corners. Elena navigates from her phone. Yuki walks ahead of everyone. I walk behind Edouard, whose commentary on the gradient of each street is continuous and inspired.

The trattoria is at the end of an alley too narrow for a car. We almost miss it. A doorway, an awning, a string of Christmas lights blinking at irregular intervals as though they're running on a schedule only they understand. Inside: six tables, candles in wine bottles, walls covered in photographs of someone's family across what appears to be several decades and at least one papal visit. It smells like garlic and bread and olive oil and the sea coming through the back door.

Paola appears.

She is in her sixties. She is wearing an apron. She has the energy of a woman who has been deciding what people eat for forty years and has not once been wrong and does not intend to start. She looks at the five of us standing in her doorway and her face does something that is either a smile or a verdict.

"You sit." She points at a table. "I bring."

Edouard sits first. "I am deeply in love," he says to the table.

"You've known her for four seconds," I say.

"Four seconds is enough. I knew my second wife in three."

"Your second wife left you."

"She left magnificently. I've never been left so well."

Paola brings bread. Oil. A carafe of red wine placed in the centre of the table without explanation or ceremony, as though wine is a fact of life rather than a decision. The bread is still warm. The oil is local. My nose catches it without being asked: early harvest, peppery, a grassiness that says it was pressed within the week.

A waiter comes through the kitchen doorway carrying a second carafe. Young. Mid-twenties. Dark hair. He sets the carafe down and smiles and the smile is easy and open and carries nothing underneath it. I notice him the way you notice a painting in a room you've just walked into. Present and filed. I'm hungry and my feet hurt and the wine is already doing something to my shoulders that three hours in the lab couldn't achieve, and I reach for the bread.

Edouard stops mid-sentence. Watches the waiter walk back to the kitchen. Picks up his wine. A long sip.

"That," he says, "is an unreasonable face."

"Edouard."

"I am making an observation. One is allowed to observe a face."

"One is allowed to eat dinner."

"These are compatible activities." He tops up his glass. "I once lost a client because I spent an entire meeting looking at his assistant. The assistant was Sardinian. I have no regrets."

Yuki is studying the menu. It's handwritten in Italian and she's reading it like a legal contract. She does not appear to have noticed the waiter. She also does not appear to have not noticed him.

Paola's food arrives. Pasta with anchovies and breadcrumbs, the breadcrumbs toasted dark and the anchovies melted into the oil until they've stopped being fish and become something closer to salt and the sea. A salad that is leaves and oil and salt and should not be as good as it is and absolutely, unreasonably is. Fish from this morning. Paola sets it down without telling you where it came from because she was there when it was caught.

The table loosens. Outside the monastery, away from the labs and the competition and the ventilation carrying everyone's formulas through stone walls, we become people.

Edouard tells a story. "Marrakech. 1992. I am there to source cedarwood. Atlas cedar, the good kind, from a man named Hassan who claims his trees are two hundred years old. Hassan is a poet. I mean this literally. He reads me poetry about cedar while I am trying to negotiate a price. The poetry is terrible. The cedar is magnificent."

He pauses. Drinks.

"Three days of poetry and cedar. By the end I am paying forty percent more than I intended and I have memorised a sonnet about bark. Hassan throws in dinner. His wife's tagine. The tagine is so good I forget the price. I sign the contract. Hassan reads me one more poem. About the contract."

"Was it a good poem?" I ask.

"It was a catastrophic poem. But the cedar lasted me four years and the tagine changed my understanding of apricots. In business, as in perfumery, you must know what you are actually paying for."

Elena is sitting with us. At the table. Just sitting. With wine in her hand and her phone face-down and her clipboard nowhere in sight. She looks different like this. Younger. Her shoulders have dropped two inches from their usual position near her ears.

"Elena," I say. "When was the last time you sat at a dinner table without a clipboard?"

She thinks about it. "February."

"Of this year?"

"Of last year."

Leo is watching me eat. I don't know how long he's been doing it. When I look up he doesn't look away. Either confidence or a failure to understand how being caught works.

"The chinotto opening," he says. "How long did it take?"

"The opening?"

"The decision to lead with something bitter."

I put my fork down. "It wasn't a decision. It was the formula telling me what it wanted. I just stopped arguing."

He nods. He holds this like it's useful. Then: "I argued with mine for six months. The base note. Everyone told me to cut it."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because they were wrong." He picks up his water. "And because cutting it would have made the formula polite. Polite formulas get bought. They don't get remembered."

I hold the sentence. He's right. I know he's right. And the fact that he told me this, here, unprompted, at a trattoria table after two glasses of wine, is either a professional courtesy or something I'm going to think about later when I'm lying in bed and can't sleep.

Edouard looks between us. Drinks his wine. For once, says nothing. The restraint is causing him visible physical distress.

The waiter clears plates. Paola returns with limoncello she pours without asking. "You finish," she says, gesturing at the glasses. "Then you go. I close. My husband snores and I have been here since five and the fish will not catch itself tomorrow."

The bill comes. Twenty-two euros. Nineteen pounds. I do the conversion before I can stop myself. Nineteen pounds I shouldn't spend on dinner while owing two hundred thousand, but the wine has softened the arithmetic and the fish was extraordinary and I am sitting at a table with people who are becoming something other than competitors, and the number sits in my stomach for one swallow of wine and then the wine covers it.

We stand. The logistics of leaving a small trattoria: chairs scraping, coats gathered, Edouard telling Paola about jasmine cultivation in Grasse while Paola tells Edouard about her daughter in Milan who married a man shorter than his photograph. Neither of them is listening to the other. Both of them are enjoying it enormously.

The stairs.

Seventy-three steps going up. After wine. In the dark.

Elena takes command. "Same system. I'll press the first button. Leo, button two at step twenty-three. There's a third button at step forty-nine. Margot, you take that one."

"I'll take it."

"Press it before the lights go off."

"I understand how buttons work, Elena."

"You'd be surprised how many people forget under pressure."

She presses the button. The lights come on. We climb.

Going up is different. Going down, gravity helps. Going up, gravity has opinions. My calves announce themselves at step eight. My Primarks offer the traction of a bar of soap. Edouard is behind me, narrating.

"Seventeen. Eighteen. In my youth I climbed Montmartre daily. Montmartre has three hundred steps. But Montmartre has boulangeries at the top. Montmartre has motivation."

"Twenty-one," Elena calls from ahead. "Twenty-two. Leo, the button."

Leo presses the button. The lights reset. We keep climbing.

"Twenty-seven," Edouard says. "Twenty-eight. I would like to register a formal complaint with the medieval architects."

"They're dead," I say.

"Then I will register it with God, who also chose to put a monastery on top of a cliff."

"Thirty-four," Elena calls. "Thirty-five. Why couldn't they build this place at sea level."

"Monasteries require suffering," Edouard says. "It's in the brochure."

Step forty-two. The lights go out.

I missed the button at forty-nine because we haven't reached forty-nine yet, but something else has happened. The timer ran out early. Sixty seconds, maybe seventy. Elena's ninety-second calculation betrayed by old wiring or divine intervention or the fact that nothing on the Amalfi Coast operates on the schedule you expect it to.

Darkness. Total. Dark with texture, with weight. I can hear Edouard breathing behind me. Elena ahead, her phone torch sweeping the wall. Yuki somewhere above us, silent, probably already at the top. Leo to my left, close enough that I can hear his feet on the stone.

"Nobody move," Elena says.

"I am not moving," Edouard says. "I am experiencing a religious conversion. This is where I live now. Tell my family I died doing something dignified."

Leo's torch finds the button at forty-nine. He's moved ahead in the dark, sure-footed, navigating by touch. He presses it. The lights come on.

Edouard is sitting on step forty-four. Both hands on his knees. His face says the human race's decision to build upward was a fundamental error of civilisation.

"Get up," I say.

"I am resting."

"You're sitting on a medieval staircase in the dark after one carafe of wine. Get up."

"One and a half carafes."

"Edouard."

He stands. A monarch rising from a lesser throne. Dignity. Reluctance. The clear implication that the standing is a favour he is granting to the concept of forward motion.

We climb. My Primarks slip on step fifty-six and I grab the railing and my hand hits Leo's hand because he's grabbed it at the same moment from the other side. Contact. One second. Knuckle to knuckle on cold iron. We both let go. We keep climbing.

"Sixty-one," Elena says. "Sixty-two. Sixty-three. I'm counting so I don't scream."

"Screaming would also be acceptable," Edouard says.

"Sixty-seven. Sixty-eight."

"Almost," Leo says. The first word he's said on the climb. His voice is steady. The man is not winded. I hate him for this. I hate him for this specifically, with my burning lungs and my war-crime sandals and my hand still feeling the cold iron where his knuckle was.

Seventy-three.

The courtyard. The lemon tree and the night air, cool after the enclosed staircase, carrying the scent of the terrace rosemary and the sea below and, faintly, from the open ventilation of the east wing, someone's formula drifting through old stone.

Edouard sits on the courtyard wall. He puts both hands on his knees. He looks at the stars.

"I am never doing that again," he says.

"Dinner's there tomorrow," Elena says.

"Then I will starve."

"You won't starve."

"I will starve beautifully. In a former monastery. As God intended."

He will not starve. He will do it again tomorrow. He will do it every night for the rest of the residency, and he will complain every step, and the complaining will be half the reason we go. But he doesn't know this yet.

We disperse. Elena to her office. Yuki to her room, already gone, silent as arrival. Edouard to his room, trailing the last of his commentary like a scarf behind him. Leo toward the labs, because of course Leo is going to work at ten thirty at night after seventy-three stairs and a carafe and a half of wine.

I stand in the courtyard. The lemon tree and the rosemary. Stone still holding the day's heat. Through the ventilation grille in the east wing wall, faint and persistent, carried by channels that have been moving air through this building since it held monks instead of perfumers: Leo's formula. The green, the resin, and underneath, the base note I can't name.

I breathe it in. I let it go.

Something has changed. The group has changed. The stairs did it. The wine did it. Paola's food and Edouard's stories and Elena counting steps out loud and the darkness at step forty-two when we all stood together on a cliff face with nothing but each other's voices and the sound of the sea below. I go to my room. My feet are sore and my legs are tired and my face hurts from laughing on the stairs, from Edouard's funeral commentary and Elena's step-counting and Yuki already at the top, waiting, as though she'd been there for hours and the rest of us had simply been slow to discover what she already knew: walk.

I lie on the narrow bed. The sea comes through the stone walls. His formula comes through the ventilation. And through the open window: the lemon tree and the night and the town below, where a trattoria with blinking Christmas lights is closing up and a woman named Paola is telling someone about her terrible grandmother, and tomorrow night we'll be back, all of us, at the same table, eating whatever she decides.

I close my eyes. I am still smiling.
