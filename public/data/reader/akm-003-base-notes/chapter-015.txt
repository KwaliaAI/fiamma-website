The kettle makes a sound like the espresso machine dying.

I brought it from the kitchenette because I can't face the kitchenette. The kitchenette is in the south wing corridor where Leo's room is, and the corridor goes past his door, and the door is shut. I'm not going to walk past a shut door at six in the morning. I've been that woman, in other corridors, with other doors. The standing never worked. The learning was expensive.

So. The kettle. I filled it at the lab sink, the utility sink meant for pipettes and glass rods, and plugged it in, and it made the sound. Identical to the espresso machine on the morning Leo fixed it. Four seconds. Panel on the side. The grind adjustment slipping. My mouth almost does something and then doesn't.

Day fifteen. The competition has three days left. The corridor outside Lab 3 is dark. Motion-sensor lights off because nobody moves in the east wing at six in the morning except me, and the building is in its nocturnal register: stone and damp and lemon trees from below and from Lab 2, through the brass ventilation grille above my workstation, nothing.

The grille is sealed. Silver tape. Industrial. I know because I went into the corridor at midnight on day thirteen, like a person with no self-respect, and saw the edge of it on the brass grate. Sealing a duct. Preventing the movement of air between two spaces.

I make tea. The water smells like mineral deposit and neglect. I put the teabag in a white mug with a chip on the rim, left side, where my mouth goes, and the ceramic edge is rough against my lip and I don't turn the mug around because the roughness is a sensation and sensations are what I have.

I open the fridge.

V4. Standard distance. Inhale.

The formula is still extraordinary. I need to say that clearly because the last two days have produced a fog and the assessment has to be clean. V4 is the best thing I've ever made. The chinotto-pink pepper opening, bright and strange and honest. The neroli-petitgrain bridge. The labdanum and orris doing their beautiful war. The oud holding everything. And at the centre, the formula still reaching for something it can't name.

I put V4 on a strip and set the strip on the bench and sit on my stool and look at it.

The leaning is the problem and the point. The formula's heart leans toward something it can't have, and the leaning is extraordinary, and the leaning is also a portrait of Leo's base note rendered in negative. Section 4.2 says I hand the full formula to Radici's production team. The architecture would tell them what V4 is leaning toward. A skilled perfumer could reverse-engineer the shape.

I have three days. I'm going to try again.

* * *

I pull materials from the shelf. Cedarwood absolute. Synthetic sandalol. Ambergris. Muskenone. Cashmeran. Ambroxan. Civettone. I line them up on the bench like applicants at a job interview. Competent. Qualified. Wrong.

Version one. Cedarwood absolute, low concentration. Enough depth to fill the centre, enough resonance to hold the base together. I blend it into V4's architecture carefully, slowly, watching for rejection. Strip on the bench. Fifteen minutes. Inhale.

The cedarwood fills the centre. The formula is complete. Every note has a partner. Every transition lands. The structure is sound and the composition is professional and the whole thing is dead. A beautiful corpse. A perfume with all the right organs and no pulse.

I cap the vial and write V4.1: cedarwood. complete. dead. on the blotter strip.

Version two. Sandalol. Creamy, soft, a base note that makes everything else feel held. The formula accepts it politely. The orris likes it. The labdanum tolerates it. The centre closes and the closing is painless and the result is a perfume you'd spray on your wrist in a department store and say "that's nice" and never think about again.

V4.2: sandalol. nice. which is the worst word in perfumery.

Version three. Ambergris, the real stuff, the last of what I brought from London in a vial wrapped in three layers of tissue paper in my carry-on because checking forty grams of ambergris is a gamble I will never take. The ambergris is magnificent on its own. In V4 it's a guest at the wrong party. The formula goes soft and round and the softness covers the centre like a blanket over a hole in the floor. You can't see the hole. You can still fall through.

V4.3: ambergris. blanket. hole still there.

I'm working fast now. Muscle memory. The body knows what it's doing even when the mind is arguing with it. Strip, blend, wait, inhale, write. The rhythm of a perfumer solving a problem she already knows the answer to.

Version four. Muskenone and cashmeran together, a synthetic backbone, trying to build a floor where the lean used to be. The floor holds. The formula stands on it. The standing is the problem. V4 was never supposed to stand. V4 was supposed to lean.

Version five. Civettone at trace amounts, an animalic note so low it's almost subliminal. The formula shivers. Gets interesting for three seconds. Then the civettone and the oud start arguing over territory and the whole base goes muddy and I lose the labdanum's drydown.

Version six. Ambroxan. I know before I finish blending that this is wrong. Ambroxan is a crowd-pleaser, all amber and musk and crowd-friendly haze, and V4 has never been a crowd-pleaser and the combination is like putting a pop chorus on a string quartet. I cap it anyway because discipline means documenting your failures.

V4.6: ambroxan. absolutely not.

* * *

By noon I've done nine versions. Every one is complete. Every one is dead. I have nine blotter strips pinned to the corkboard above my station in a row and they look like a medical chart of a patient who keeps stabilising and keeps not waking up. I eat an apple from the bowl Elena leaves in the corridor. It tastes like an apple. I eat it standing at the bench because sitting down feels like an admission.

The door to Lab 3 is shut. I shut it on day thirteen and I haven't opened it except to go to the bathroom and once at midnight to check the tape on the grille. Pathetic. I am not going to think about it.

I can hear the building through the stone. Edouard is playing something French on a small speaker in Lab 5. Yuki's lab is silent but it's a working silence, the specific quiet of concentration, not absence. Leo's lab is silent too, and his silence is a different species entirely. I can't hear his pipettes. I can't hear his fridge opening. The sealed grille means I can't smell him. Two days of nothing from Lab 2 and the nothing has a texture. Dense. Intentional.

I go back to the bench.

Version ten. I try something lateral. I pull the orris back by a third and expand the labdanum into the vacated territory, trying to restructure the formula so the centre reads smaller, less legible. If the architecture is tighter, the negative shape of the base note is harder to read, and maybe partial disclosure becomes viable. The reformulated version is interesting. Architectural. Cold. The orris was doing more than I thought. Without the full orris, the formula loses its grief, and the grief was the thing that made V4 feel human. I undo it.

Version eleven. I try layering two materials in the centre, sandalol and cedarwood together, one for depth and one for body, and the combination creates a base note that is technically functional and emotionally insulting. Two substitutes pretending to be a thing that was never a substitute. I cap it with more force than the vial deserves.

Version twelve. I go back to the original Bermondsey formula. Pre-Italy. Pre-Leo. Pre-everything. The version I packed in London and brought on the plane as a safety net. I pull it from the back of the fridge where it's been sitting for two weeks, untouched. I put it on a strip.

It's fine. It's a fine fragrance by a skilled perfumer and it will not win anything because it does not want anything. V4 wants. The Bermondsey version is satisfied with itself. Satisfied kills art.

I throw the strip in the bin and immediately take it out because waste is waste and also because the Bermondsey version doesn't deserve the bin. It deserves indifference. Far worse.

Version thirteen. I experiment with ISO E Super at the threshold of perception, the concentration where it's felt more than smelled, a phantom presence. It's clever. The formula becomes mysterious. But the mystery is a trick, a sleight of hand, and V4's mystery was never a trick. V4's mystery was honest. V4 says: I am incomplete. I know it. The ISO E says: look over here while I distract you. Completely different.

Version fourteen. The last. I sit at the bench for ten minutes before I start because I know this is the last one for the day and I want it to be good and I know it won't be and I start anyway because that's what work is.

I use iso-ambrettolide. A macrocyclic musk with a clean, almost watery quality. If anything can hold that centre without lying about it, it's this. Transparent. Honest about what it is. I blend it in at low concentration and put it on a strip and wait the full fifteen minutes for the development and I don't cheat and sniff early because this one matters.

Fifteen minutes. Inhale.

The formula is the closest any version has come. The iso-ambrettolide doesn't pretend to be anything it isn't. It simply occupies the centre. A good house-sitter in someone else's home. Everything is maintained. The structure is sound. The formula breathes.

It is not V4.

V4 with the gap filled is a perfume. V4 with the gap open is art. I cannot make it both.

I write V4.14: closest. still dead. I pin the strip to the corkboard with the others. Fourteen strips in a row. A census of failure.

* * *

The lab is dark when Elena comes in. I don't know what time it is. Late. The overhead fluorescent went off an hour ago when the motion sensor gave up on me and I didn't get up to trigger it again because the bench light was enough and movement felt optional.

"You haven't eaten."

"I'm not hungry."

"That's not what I asked."

She puts something on the bench. I hear the clink of ceramic on stone. A plate. She pulls a stool from the corner, the one that still has all four legs, and sits.

Bread. Pecorino. Two slices of something pink and cured. A glass of water.

I eat because Elena is sitting there and Elena sitting there is a force of nature that does not accept the word no. She doesn't ask what happened. She doesn't look at the corkboard with its fourteen strips. She sits on the stool with her hands in her lap, not the clipboard for once, just her hands, and she watches me eat the bread and the cheese and the thing that might be bresaola.

"Is this from Paola?"

"The bread is from me. The cheese is from Paola. The bresaola is from Luca, who said 'give this to the English one.'"

"I'm flattered."

"He said it while washing dishes. I think it was mostly convenience."

I finish the bread. I drink the water. Elena takes the plate.

"Tomorrow the fish is good," she says. "Luca told me to tell you."

"Is it actually good?"

"It's adequate. But he's worried about you and this is how he worries."

She leaves. The door closes. The lab is quiet and the corridor is quiet and I'm sitting in the dark with fourteen failed versions and an empty plate and the best thing I've ever made sitting in the fridge being unreasonable about its own architecture.

* * *

My phone rings at eleven. Nadia.

"They filed."

I close my eyes. "Filed."

"High Court. This afternoon. I got the notification from our solicitor twenty minutes ago."

"Ferranti."

"Ferranti. They're not threatening anymore, Margot. They've filed. We have fourteen days to respond or it's a default judgment."

"What does default mean?"

"You know what default means."

"Tell me anyway."

"Asset seizure. The business. The studio lease. Any materials held in your name. The oud in 14B. Everything."

The oud. Forty thousand euros. Fourteen months sourcing. Three trips to Kannauj. Pradip's handshake. Nadia's flat in the Algarve that she'll never buy. The oud that cost me a fiancé and a flat and a year of my life. The oud that sits in Unit 14B in Bermondsey on Argos shelving in a climate-controlled case that I built myself because I couldn't afford a proper one. The oud that is, in purely financial terms, the single most valuable thing I own and, in every other term, irreplaceable.

"If you don't win this, we're done." Nadia's voice is flat. She's not performing. This is just the information, delivered cleanly, because Nadia delivers everything cleanly. "Done done. The business closes. I take the second job. You start over with nothing."

"I know."

"Do you? Because I need you to say it back to me so I know you heard it."

"The business closes. You take the second job. I start over with nothing."

"Win."

She hangs up. I put the phone face down on the bench. The screen glows through the gap between the phone and the stone and I flip it over and turn it fully off because the glow feels like an accusation.

* * *

I get up from the stool because the stool is in the lab and the lab has the phone and the phone has Nadia's voice in it and I need to not be near the phone. That's the whole thought. That's as far as the planning goes.

The corridor is dark. My feet are doing the walking. I'm going toward the bathroom or maybe just going, just the motion of a body with somewhere to not be, and I get as far as the junction between the east wing and the south and then something happens to my legs. They stop. They just. I put my hand on the wall and the stone is cold and I'm looking at the floor, the slate, the grout line, and my breathing is doing something I didn't ask it to do. Fast and shallow and wrong. The oud. The flat I sold. Pradip's hand shaking mine in Kannauj while the rain came in sideways through the warehouse door. Nadia's second job. My hands are shaking. My hands are. I sit down. I sit down on the corridor floor because standing stopped and I press my back against the wall and I'm trying to finish a thought and the thought won't. It just. Fourteen days. Default. The word seizure keeps arriving and I can't make it leave. I can't. I'm pressing my palms flat on the slate and my face is wet which means I'm crying which I did not agree to.

Light from Lab 4, a thin stripe across the floor. A door opening wider. Feet. Yuki stands in the corridor in her lab coat and socks and she looks at me on the floor with my wet face and my shaking hands and she doesn't say anything. She doesn't step forward. She stands there for maybe four seconds. Then she sits down. On the floor. Against the opposite wall, a metre away. She puts her hands on her knees.

She doesn't ask. I can't talk. The corridor is dark except for the stripe of light from her lab and I'm sitting on a slate floor at half one in the morning falling apart in front of a woman I've known for fifteen days and she is sitting across from me like this is just where we are now. Like the floor is as good a place as any. After a while my breathing slows. After a while the shaking stops. She doesn't comment on either. When I wipe my face with my sleeve she looks at the wall to give me that. Then she stands. Holds out her hand.

"Tea is bad here," she says.

I take her hand. I stand. My legs work again, which feels like a separate surprise.

She leads me into Lab 4. I sit on the stool by the door. Her lab is immaculate. Every vial aligned, every strip labelled, the bench wiped clean except for the semicircle of discarded blotters. The room smells like hinoki and something citric and green and alive.

I don't ask. I wait. Two minutes. The stone is cold through my trousers. Three minutes. Somewhere in the building a pipe ticks.

"I don't know if it's good enough."

Five words. She says them to the bench, to the blotter strips, to the room in general. She says them like something that's been sitting in her mouth for days, compressed, taking up space, and the saying is a release and also an exposure and the exposure costs her. I can see the cost. It's in her shoulders.

I don't comfort her. Comfort would be an insult. Yuki doesn't need someone to tell her it's going to be fine. She needs a conversation about materials.

"What's the structure?"

She looks at me, then pulls a strip from the semicircle, the freshest one, and holds it out.

I smell it. Hinoki, unmistakable, the Japanese cypress that smells like a temple at dawn. Behind it something floral and dark, not jasmine but adjacent, tuberose maybe, with a bitter green note underneath that I can't place.

"Kuromoji?"

"Yes."

"In the base?"

"In the heart. The base is hinoki and vetiver and a synth I'm making from shiso oil."

"You're synthesising your own material?"

"The commercial shiso extracts are flat. They lose the aldehyde character above 0.3 percent. I needed the green bite without the sweetness."

She's building her own molecules. She just told me she doesn't know if her work is good enough. The distance between those two things is the distance of every perfumer I've ever respected.

"The kuromoji in the heart is doing something," I say. "It's bridging the hinoki and the shiso. If you pull it, what happens?"

"The formula collapses inward. The hinoki and the vetiver fuse and the shiso sits on top and you get a very expensive air freshener."

I almost laugh. "And if you raise the kuromoji?"

"It becomes a kuromoji perfume. Everything else becomes accompaniment. The hinoki loses its lead."

"So the kuromoji concentration is the hinge."

"The kuromoji concentration is everything. Too low, the formula is furniture. Too high, the formula is a solo. I need it at the exact point where it's a conversation."

"How close are you?"

She looks at the strips. All of them. The semicircle of attempts.

"Very close. And very close is a specific kind of terrible."

I know. Very close means you can see where the formula needs to go and you can't quite make your hands take it there. Very close is the bench where skill runs out and something else has to carry you the rest of the way and you don't know what that something else is, and calling it instinct feels like a lie and calling it luck feels like an insult.

"Your formula has a space," she says.

I look at her.

"I heard it. Before the grille closed. The ventilation carried it from your lab through the corridor and I caught it once at the junction. The base thins where it shouldn't. Deliberately. Architectural."

"Yes."

"You are keeping it."

"Yes."

She nods. Slow.

"Then I will keep mine as it is."

She picks up a pipette. She pulls a vial from the rack. She leans over the bench and starts working. The conversation is over. The conversation was exactly long enough.

I stand. At the door I stop.

"Yuki."

She looks up.

"The kuromoji is the right call. The formula just needs to catch up with you."

She holds my gaze for one second. Then she goes back to work.

* * *

Lab 3. Two in the morning. I sit at my bench with V4 in its vial and the fourteen dead versions on the corkboard and the sealed grille above me and Nadia's voice in my head saying win.

Fourteen versions. Fourteen ways to fill the centre. Fourteen confirmations of what I already knew. The formula that leans toward something is alive. The formula that doesn't is product.

I'll submit V4 as it is. Section 4.2 says full disclosure. I'll go to Chiara and negotiate. Partial disclosure. Redacted architecture. I'll explain that the formula's structure contains proprietary information about a competitor's work and that full disclosure would constitute an ethical breach. There are no precedents for this. Leo said so and he was right. I'll make the precedent.

I don't know if Chiara will agree. I don't know if partial disclosure is even legally viable for a house commission. I don't know if I can protect Leo's base note and win the competition and save the business and keep the oud in 14B. I don't know any of it. The formula doesn't change.

Win, Nadia said.

I pick up the vial. I hold it to the light. Amber liquid in glass, the colour of strong tea, of the canal water in Bermondsey at sunset, of the whisky I can't afford. Inside this vial is the best work I've ever done and the work is built around Leo, sitting in a sealed lab two walls away, his door shut and the grille is taped and none of that changes the formula because the formula was built around the specific ache of something incomplete that knows it's incomplete and refuses to pretend otherwise.

I put V4 back in the fridge.

I walk down the corridor. It's dark. The motion sensors have long given up. Past Lab 4, where Yuki's light is still on, her pipette still moving, the kuromoji still finding its concentration. Past Lab 5, dark, Edouard sensibly asleep. Past the junction where the east wing meets the south.

There is one line of light under Leo's door.

I walk past it. I don't stop.
