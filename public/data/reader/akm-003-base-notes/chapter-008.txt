V4 was fighting me on the mid-notes. The neroli bridge from V3 had held, the keyhole had tightened, but somewhere between the bergamot opening and the heart there was a wobble. A frequency clash. The labdanum and the iris were having a conversation I hadn't authorised, and the chinotto was sitting on top pretending it wasn't involved.

I swapped the Calabrian bergamot for the Tunisian. Sharper, less round, a citrus with an opinion. The wobble moved. Didn't vanish, but moved, which meant it was structural and not chemical. So I tried something I hadn't tried before: a micro-dose of the violet leaf absolute I'd been hoarding for two years. The one I'd brought from London wrapped in tissue in its own separate pouch because it was expensive and irreplaceable and I'd been waiting for the formula that deserved it.

Violet leaf at that concentration doesn't smell like violet. It smells like cold metal and wet garden earth and the first morning after heavy rain. It's a bridge material nobody uses as a bridge because everyone thinks it's a heart note, and they're wrong.

I put it on a strip. Waited sixty seconds. The bergamot opened, the chinotto bit, and then the violet leaf caught the labdanum and the iris at the exact point where they'd been colliding and pulled them apart by half a frequency. The wobble stopped. The mid-notes aligned.

I sat on my stool and held the strip to my nose and breathed and V4 was better than anything I'd made in my life.

The centre was still open. What had been a door, then a keyhole, was now the keyhole with the right key half-inserted. Something was missing, but the formula around it was ready. The house was built. The room was furnished. One chair was empty.

My phone buzzed.

Nadia.

Margot. Ferranti are threatening county court. I need a number I can give them.

I put the strip down.

How much buys us three months?

Four thousand.

Four thousand pounds. I had six hundred and change in my account, and that was before the flight home, and before Nadia's own invoice, and before the electricity bill I'd left on the kitchen table in Peckham with a Post-it that said PAY THIS which I'd written to myself as though I were a separate person who could be delegated to.

I don't have four thousand.

I know you don't have four thousand. But I need to tell them something. County court means public records. Public records means CCJ. CCJ means no supplier will extend terms. You know this.

I knew this. County court was the line. Below the line: ugly, stressful, survivable. Above the line: credit destroyed, business finished, Bergmann & Cole pulling the iris butter supply, V4 collapsing into a formula I couldn't source for.

Can you stall them?

I've been stalling them for three months. The man on the phone today used the word "proceedings." When they use "proceedings" it means a solicitor has typed something.

The prize is fifty thousand euros.

When do you find out?

End of the residency. Twelve days.

I will tell them you're expecting income. That buys maybe two weeks. Win faster.

I'll try.

Don't try. Win.

I put the phone face-down on the bench. V4 was on the strip in front of me. The violet leaf bridge holding, the formula tighter than it had ever been, and I was sitting in a fourteenth-century lab in a former monastery on a cliff in Italy with six hundred pounds and twelve days and a debt collector who'd learned the word "proceedings" and I breathed. In. Out. The formula on the strip. The sea through the grille. The faint green trace of Leo's base note drifting through the ventilation like it always did.

I picked up the strip. I breathed V4 again. It was still good.

I cleaned the bench. I labelled the strip. I locked the lab and went to get ready for dinner.

* * *

Six thirty. The courtyard. Night five.

Edouard appeared in a linen shirt that suggested a previous life in which laundry was performed by someone who loved him.

"Migration," he announced. "Night five. Survivors: all."

"You say that every night," I said.

"And every night it remains a miracle."

Elena was already at the stairs, phone in hand, clipboard under arm. The system was routine now. She'd mapped every timer button, timed every interval, identified the dead bulb on the second flight and the loose step at twelve and the railing that wobbled at thirty-eight. The stairs had been conquered. They were now merely endured.

Yuki passed us without breaking stride. She was already four steps down before anyone had spoken to her. Her descent was a masterclass in efficiency. No commentary. No hesitation. Just a woman and seventy-three steps and the absolute certainty that both would survive.

Leo came out of the east wing. Grey t-shirt. He fell into step beside Edouard and the two of them began the descent, Edouard talking about a restaurant in Lyon he'd visited in 1994 that no longer existed but that lived on in his memory as a benchmark against which all subsequent meals would be measured and found wanting.

And then Chiara came through the door.

She was wearing a dark blue dress instead of the cream linen. No clipboard. No reading glasses on their chain. Hair down. She looked like a different person, or the same person with a different agreement about what the evening required.

"I thought I'd join you tonight," she said.

The five of us looked at her. Edouard's mouth opened, closed, opened again. Elena's clipboard dipped two inches. Leo nodded. Yuki, four steps down, turned back and looked at Chiara for three full seconds, which from Yuki was the equivalent of a standing ovation.

"Wonderful," Edouard said. He offered his arm. Chiara did not take his arm. She walked past him and started down the stairs and Edouard followed her, delighted. A new audience. He intended to perform for it with everything he had.

The descent was the usual controlled catastrophe. Elena pressed buttons. Leo pressed the high one at twenty-three. I pressed the one at forty-nine. Edouard paused at step forty-four, his step, the one he'd claimed on night one, and Chiara walked past him without stopping and Edouard looked at me with an expression of pure delight and mouthed: She is magnificent.

The trattoria. The table. Paola appeared with bread and oil and wine and then she saw Chiara and stopped.

"Ah," Paola said. "You are new."

"I am Chiara."

"You have not been eating here."

"I've been eating at the monastery."

"Alone?"

"Yes."

Paola made a sound. A single syllable, somewhere between disapproval and diagnosis. Eating alone was a correctable error and Paola had appointed herself the correction. She poured Chiara's wine first. Chiara accepted this as a natural sequence of events.

Luca brought the second carafe. He set it on the table and smiled at the group and caught my eye for one beat and moved on. Professional. Easy. The same compartmentalisation he'd shown since our afternoon. He understood which room was which.

The food arrived. Pasta with puttanesca. Olives, capers, anchovies, tomato, garlic. Paola placed it in the centre of the table. A verdict, not a suggestion.

Chiara took one bite. Chewed. Swallowed. "The olives are Gaeta."

Paola stopped walking. Turned. "Yes."

"And the capers are Pantelleria."

"Yes."

"But the anchovy is wrong."

The table went quiet. Paola came back to the table. She placed both hands on the edge. She leaned in. The physical dynamics of the scene had shifted from a restaurant to a tribunal.

"The anchovy," Paola said, "is from Cetara."

"I can taste that it's from Cetara. It's too salty for this sauce. You need the Sicilian. Smaller fish, longer cure, less salt."

"You are from Napoli?"

"Roma."

"This explains your error."

Chiara put down her fork. "It's not an error. It's a preference based on thirty years of eating puttanesca in a city that invented it."

"Roma did not invent puttanesca."

"Roma claims it."

"Roma claims many things. This does not make them true." Paola drew herself up to her full height, which was considerable in spirit if not in centimetres. "The anchovy is from Cetara because my husband's cousin catches the anchovy in Cetara and my husband's cousin has been catching anchovy for forty years and the anchovy is correct."

"The anchovy is loyal. That doesn't make it correct."

Edouard was vibrating. He'd set down his wine. His eyes were moving between the two women. The finest sporting event of his life. Elena was eating bread calmly. Years of event management had taught her that the best response to conflict was continued consumption.

"In my restaurant," Paola said, "the anchovy is the anchovy I choose. If you want a different anchovy, you cook your own puttanesca."

"I do cook my own puttanesca. It's better."

The pause that followed could have sustained weather systems. Paola stared at Chiara. Chiara stared at Paola. Two Italian women in their respective decades of authority, separated by a bowl of pasta and a philosophical disagreement about small cured fish, and neither of them was going to blink first.

Paola smiled. It broke across her face like sunrise through a grudge.

"You come to my kitchen," she said. "Tomorrow. You bring your Sicilian anchovy. We cook. We see."

"Fine."

"Fine."

Paola swept back to the kitchen. Chiara picked up her fork and resumed eating the puttanesca she had just declared inferior, and she ate every bite, and the eating was its own kind of respect.

"That," Edouard said, reaching for his wine, "was the most erotic thing I have witnessed in a decade."

"It was a conversation about anchovies," I said.

"It was a conversation about AUTHORITY. Conducted by two women of extraordinary conviction over a bowl of pasta in a restaurant on a cliff. I am moved. I am profoundly moved."

"You're drunk."

"These are compatible states."

The wine kept coming. Paola brought a second bottle without being asked, and then a third, and the table opened up as it did on nights when nobody was tired and nobody was guarding anything. Edouard told a story about sourcing cedarwood in Morocco that he'd told before but told differently this time, because Chiara was the audience and Chiara listened differently. She listened with her whole face. At the monastery she listened with her clipboard and her glasses and the professional distance of a woman running a competition. Here, with wine in her glass and the Christmas lights blinking their irregular rhythm, she listened like a person.

"Why Radici?" I asked her.

She turned to me. Set her glass down. "The industry lost its nose."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning. Twenty years ago I sat in a boardroom and watched a committee approve a fragrance that had been optimised by focus group data. They hadn't smelled it. They'd seen the numbers. The numbers said it would sell. It sold. It sold enormously. And it smelled like nothing. Like a spreadsheet that someone had liquefied and put in a bottle."

"Which brand?"

"I'm not going to tell you which brand. But you've smelled it. Everyone has smelled it. It's in every airport on earth. That was the day I decided I wanted to find the people who still cared about whether the thing smelled like a thing. People who made formulas because the formula demanded to exist. I wanted to put them in a room and give them materials and time and see what happened."

"And what happens?"

She looked around the table. At Edouard, mid-pour. At Yuki, cutting her fish into precise segments. At me, with wine on my lip and a strip of V4 in my bag because I'd brought it to dinner to test its development at four hours. At the empty chair where Leo had been sitting before he'd gone to the bar for water.

"This happens," she said.

Luca cleared the plates. He came to Chiara's end of the table and she looked up at him and said, "The puttanesca was excellent. Don't tell Paola I said that."

Luca laughed. "She already knows. She watched you eat the whole plate."

"A woman can disagree with an anchovy and still eat the dish."

"In Positano, this is how we give compliments."

He stood there. She looked up at him. One second too long for plate-clearing. His question came out casual, easy. Local boy, simply curious.

"The monastery. You did the restoration?"

"I oversaw it. Four years of work. The building had been empty since the 1960s."

"I remember. I was a kid. We used to throw stones at the windows. There were no windows to break, but we threw them anyway."

Chiara smiled. A real smile, not the professional adjustment she gave to contestants when their formulas met her standards. A smile that used muscles near her eyes.

"The perfume labs were my idea," she said. "The monks made perfume here in the sixteenth century. I found their records in the archives in Naples. Recipes for orange blossom water, rose otto, a tincture of something they called 'fiore di pietra' that I've never been able to identify. Flower of stone. I think it was a lichen."

"My grandmother talks about the monks. She says they could cure headaches with their perfumes."

"They probably could. Half their ingredients are used in medicine. The other half are used in church incense. There's less distance between healing and worship than people think."

Luca leaned against the back of the empty chair. Listening. His body language was open and unhurried and he was looking at Chiara with the genuine interest of a person hearing something new about a place he'd known his entire life. And Chiara was talking. Chiara, who communicated in single sentences and clipboard annotations and the word "Continue," was talking about archives and monks and lichen, and her hands were moving when she spoke, and the reading glasses on their chain swung with the movement, and she was a person at a table telling someone about the thing she loved.

I caught Edouard's eye across the table. He raised one eyebrow. I shook my head.

"We should go," Elena said. She'd been watching the time. She always watched the time. "The last flight has that dead bulb and I don't want to do it in full dark with Edouard."

"I resent the implication that I am a liability," Edouard said.

"You sat on step forty-four for three minutes last night."

"I was reflecting."

"You were panting."

We stood. Chairs scraped. The check-clearing logistics: Paola refused to let Chiara pay. "First time is free. Next time you bring the anchovy." Chiara accepted this with a nod that carried more mutual respect than most peace treaties.

The group assembled at the door. Coats. Phones. Elena doing her pre-climb inventory. And Chiara, still at the table, wine glass in hand.

"I want to finish my wine," she said. "I'll walk up alone. I know the stairs."

The silence was specific and total. Elena looked at her phone. Yuki looked at nothing. Leo looked at the door. I looked at Edouard, who was looking at Chiara with an expression that combined scholarly appreciation and absolute glee.

"Of course," Edouard said. "We'll see you at the monastery."

We left. The door closed behind us. The narrow alley, the stars, the sea below, the start of the climb.

Edouard made it to step eleven before he spoke.

"The boy is a phenomenon."

"She's an adult," I said. "She can finish her wine."

"Of course she can finish her wine. I am merely observing that the wine will take approximately forty-five minutes and a cigarette to finish. The cigarette is my assumption. The fingers suggest she quit smoking ten years ago and occasionally relapses under conditions of..."

"Edouard."

"...extraordinary..."

"This is gossip," Yuki said.

We all stopped climbing. Yuki was ten steps above us. She had turned around. She was looking down at Edouard. She had identified the precise category of behaviour she was witnessing. She disapproved of it completely.

"This is OBSERVATION," Edouard said. "There is a distinction. Gossip is conducted behind closed doors. Observation is conducted on a staircase in the open air."

"No."

"No?"

"No."

Yuki turned and continued climbing. The conversation was over. Yuki had ruled. The ruling was final. There was no appeal.

Edouard looked at me. I shrugged. Elena pressed the button at twenty-three. Leo was already ahead of us, climbing steadily, saying nothing.

We climbed the rest in near-silence. Edouard managed only two observations, both about the quality of the evening air. At the top, the courtyard, the lemon tree, the monastery holding the day's heat and releasing it into the night. Yuki was already gone. Elena said goodnight and went to check something in the supply closet. Edouard kissed my hand and departed, humming.

Leo stood in the courtyard. He was looking back down the stairs. At the lights going off, section by section, as the timers expired.

"Goodnight, Leo," I said.

"Goodnight, Margot." He turned. Paused. "V4. How is it?"

"Better than anything I've done."

He nodded. Something moved across his face, there and gone. "Good."

He went inside. I stood in the courtyard for thirty seconds and watched the last set of timer lights go dark.

Morning. Day eight.

Elena appeared at breakfast with a face like Christmas morning. She was carrying her clipboard and her phone and a torch, and her hair had cobwebs in it, and she was smiling so hard her cheeks had to be hurting.

"I found a lift."

Edouard set down his bread.

"Behind the east wing," Elena said. She was breathless. "A service lift from the restoration. It's behind a tarp. I pulled the tarp off. It's industrial, it's ugly, it goes down to a lane that connects to the main road. Five-minute walk to the trattoria. I tested it. It works."

The table absorbed this at different speeds.

"No stairs," I said.

"No stairs."

Chiara, who had arrived at breakfast before us and was sitting at the head of the table with coffee and her composure making it clear that no one was going to ask any questions and she was not going to answer any questions that no one was going to ask, said: "I'd forgotten about that lift. The contractors installed it for the heavy materials. The marble, the lab equipment."

"It WORKS," Elena said. "I pressed the button and it came up and I got in and I went down and there's a lane and the lane goes to the road and the road goes to the town and the town goes to PAOLA." She held up the torch. "The shaft is dark. You need a torch. But there are no stairs."

"No stairs," Edouard repeated. He looked at the table. He looked at his bread. He picked up his coffee and drank it slowly, absorbing the news that a fundamental premise of his existence had been altered.

"I was developing a relationship with step forty-four," he said.

"You were developing a cardiac event on step forty-four."

"These are compatible outcomes."

Yuki ate her toast strip. The lift was information. She'd received it. She'd stored it. If the lift worked, she'd use it. If it didn't, she'd walk. The existence or non-existence of the lift had no visible effect on her toast or her morning or her understanding of the universe.

Leo reached for the coffee pot. "Good work, Elena."

Two words. Elena glowed. Approval from Leo, who did not distribute approval freely, who fixed grinders and pushed in chairs and said "Good" when he meant it and nothing when he didn't, and he'd said it and she'd heard it and the cobwebs in her hair looked like a crown.

He poured himself water from the jug. The jug was the monastery's pride: ceramic, hand-painted, lemons and vines, a souvenir shop's idea of the Amalfi Coast. Leo held it at arm's length like it had personally insulted him.

"This jug," he said.

"What about it?"

"The spout doesn't pour. It dribbles. I've used it every morning for eight days and it dribbles every time."

"It's decorative."

"It's a jug. A jug has one job." He set it down with more force than a water vessel required. "Decorative is a word people use when something doesn't work."

Edouard looked at the jug. Looked at Leo. "The American has opinions about crockery. I am charmed."

"The American has opinions about function." Leo mopped the water off the table with his sleeve. "This jug is a liar."

"Does this mean no more stair commentary?" I asked Edouard.

"The stair commentary will continue. It will simply be retrospective. Nostalgia is commentary's highest form."

"I'll miss the dead bulb on the second flight," I said. This was a lie. I would not miss the dead bulb.

Chiara drank her coffee. "I'm glad it's been found. My knees will appreciate it."

This was the only reference Chiara was going to make to last night, to the stairs, to the wine she'd stayed to finish, to however she'd got back to the monastery and at what hour and in whose company. She drank her coffee and that was the full extent of the available data.

Edouard opened his mouth. Yuki looked at him. He closed his mouth. Picked up his bread. The restraint was going to leave a bruise.

* * *

The lab. Afternoon. V4 on my wrist, four hours into its development, the formula unfolding as a formula unfolds when the architecture is right and the materials are talking to each other instead of past each other.

I'd been here seven days.

Seven days. I'd arrived barefoot with broken sandals and oud in my socks. I'd built a formula around a hole. I'd slept with a waiter and left a sandal under his bed and retrieved it in the morning and drunk coffee on his balcony while a cat judged a woman's laundry. I'd climbed seventy-three stairs drunk in the dark more times than I could count. I'd heard Leo's voice catch on the word "both." I'd watched Edouard sit on step forty-four and refuse to move and then move. I'd watched Yuki shut down an argument with a single syllable. I'd heard Chiara talk about monks who made perfume from lichen, and I'd watched her composure come apart over a bowl of puttanesca and a boy with a face that should've been illegal.

I picked up the strip with V4. Held it to my nose.

The formula was good. The formula was close. The opening worked, the mid-notes aligned, the violet leaf bridge was the bravest choice I'd made in years. And at the centre, something still missing. The formula was asking for something.

It was asking for a base note.

A dark, resinous base note I'd been smelling through a ventilation grille for seven days. On a man's wrist in a skin study that had ended with both of us standing too close in a lab that smelled like his formula and mine and the third thing that belonged to neither of us. In a corridor at two in the morning, a line of light under a door.

The formula was asking for Leo's base note. And I was starting to know this. I was starting to know it the way you know a word you can't say yet, the shape of it in your mouth, the weight of it on your tongue.

I put the strip down. I looked at the bench. The vials, the pipettes, the oud in its rack, the iris butter, the violet leaf absolute with its tissue wrapping. All of my materials. Everything I'd brought.

The thing the formula was asking for wasn't in my case.
