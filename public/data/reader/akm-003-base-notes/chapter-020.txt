The suitcase is the same one I brought from London.

The wheel is still broken. The left rear, which died somewhere between Gatwick and the Naples ferry terminal, a quiet mechanical surrender that I chose to ignore because I was twenty hours into a journey and my Ferragamos were dying their own slow death and I didn't have the bandwidth for an intervention on behalf of luggage. Three weeks later the wheel is still broken and I am not the same person who dragged it up seventy-three stairs in the dark.

I fold the last blouse. Place it on top of the materials case, which takes up half the suitcase and has taken up half the suitcase for fourteen years because the materials case was here before the clothes and will be here after the clothes and the hierarchy has never been in question. Seven vials in foam slots. The chinotto oil. The pink pepper CO2. The neroli. The last of my ambergris, which survived a carry-on from Bermondsey and a monastery fridge and is now going home in a suitcase with a broken wheel. It deserves better. So does the formula it helped build.

The Ferragamos are in the bin. They've been there since Day 3, when the left heel separated from the sole on the courtyard stairs and I performed a brief private funeral involving the monastery bin and a single moment of silence. The Primarks are on my feet. Loyal. Indestructible. Nineteen pounds from Oxford Street and they have outlasted Italian leather by seventeen days.

The room is almost empty. My room. Lab 3's resident quarters, third floor, east wing, stone walls the colour of old paper and a window that frames the sea like it's doing it on purpose. Three weeks of my life in this room. The tea stain on the desk from the kettle I stole from the kitchenette. The pillow I folded double every night because monastery pillows were designed for monks with smaller heads and firmer convictions. The hook on the back of the door where I hung Edouard's tie on the night he gave it to me, wine-coloured silk catching the lamplight, thirty-two years of someone else's victories and losses and one funeral, and I looked at it and thought: that's a lot to put on a piece of fabric.

The tie is on my wrist now. I put it there yesterday and I haven't taken it off.

I check the bathroom. Empty. My toothbrush is in the side pocket of the suitcase. My shampoo ran out on Day 16 and I've been using Elena's, which smells like coconut and administrative competence. The towel on the hook is monastery-issue, white, thin, a towel that communicates clearly that comfort is not the priority here. I fold it and leave it on the bed because someone taught me manners and because the towel didn't do anything wrong.

I zip the suitcase. It makes the sound of leaving.

* * *

The monastery in the morning is different from the monastery at any other time. Cooler. Stiller. The stone holds the night longer than the air does, so the corridors are cold even when the light through the windows is already gold. I walk the east wing slowly. My hand trails the wall.

Lab 3. The door is open. My bench is clean. I cleaned it last night, after midnight, after everyone had gone to bed, because I wanted to do it alone. The bench where I built V4 and all fourteen of its failed siblings. The corkboard where I pinned the blotter strips in a row like a medical chart. The strips are gone. I took them down, rolled them into my notebook, packed the notebook. The corkboard has fourteen tiny holes where the pins were.

I cross the corridor. The ventilation grille is between Labs 2 and 3, set into the stone about seven feet up, brass slats gone green at the edges. I reach up and touch it. Cold iron under my fingers. The grille that carried his formula to me and mine to him for eighteen days. Through stone and air and medieval engineering and the improbable genius of whoever designed a ventilation system in the fourteenth century that would, six hundred years later, allow two perfumers to fall in something without leaving their labs.

It's just a grille now. The air moves through it, neutral, carrying nothing personal.

I let go. My fingers are cold.

The courtyard. The lemon tree is doing its lemon tree thing, producing fruit with an indifference to human drama that I've found increasingly admirable over three weeks. The fountain is running. It has been running since I arrived and presumably ran for the six hundred years before that and will run for the six hundred after. The bench under the tree, where I sat with Edouard on Day 2 and he told me I was talented and terrified and both were useful. The stone where Yuki stood on Day 7 and said "Your bergamot is too loud" and was correct. The patch of sunlight where Elena spread out her scheduling matrix on Day 10 and the wind took half of it and she chased paper across the courtyard with the succulent still in her arms.

Three weeks. I've lived here for three weeks and now I'm leaving and the courtyard doesn't care, the same way the sea doesn't care, and the not-caring is the kindest thing a place can do when you're going.

* * *

Edouard is in the courtyard.

He's standing under the lemon tree, hands behind his back, looking at the sea. He's wearing a new tie. Pale blue. Silk, obviously, because Edouard doesn't acknowledge the existence of other fabrics. His suit is impeccable and his shoes are polished and his posture is immaculate. Fifty years of arriving and departing from beautiful places and he treats each one with the same ceremony.

"New tie," I say.

He turns. "This one is from my third wife." He adjusts it. "It holds less. But it's newer."

"How is your third wife?"

"Magnificent. Terrible. These are the same quality in a woman."

He looks at my wrist. The wine-coloured silk, knotted twice, the ends hanging past my thumb. His face does something complicated and quick, like weather passing over a mountain.

"Keep it," he says. "It belongs with a winner."

"Edouard—"

"No. Do not argue with me. I am too old to change my mind and too French to want to. The tie is yours. It will hold your victories now. Treat it better than I treated the marriage."

He steps forward and kisses both my cheeks. Left, right. His cologne is cedarwood and bergamot and something old-fashioned that smells like the inside of a very expensive shop in a very small town.

"If you ever need cedarwood," he says, stepping back, "I know a man in Morocco. He will read you poetry. The poetry is terrible." He pauses. "The cedar is magnificent."

"I'll take the cedar."

"You will also take the poetry. He does not separate them. I have tried."

He smiles and turns and walks across the courtyard toward the stairs, and I watch him because I know what he's going to do before he does it.

He reaches the top of the staircase. Seventy-three steps. The same seventy-three I climbed on Day 1 with a broken suitcase and dead shoes. The same seventy-three he has climbed and descended every day since he arrived, refusing the lift. Vertical transport is a matter of principle.

"For old times," he says over his shoulder.

He starts down. He does not look back.

I watch until his grey head disappears below the wall. Step by step. All seventy-three.

* * *

The corridor back through the east wing. Yuki at the fountain with her case already latched, handle upright. She extends her hand. I shake it. Her grip says everything her mouth won't. She picks up her case and walks away and her footsteps on the stone are precise and evenly spaced and I will miss them.

Elena is crying in the doorway of the administrative office, clipboard in one arm, succulent in the other. "Allergic reaction to departure," she says. I hug her. The clipboard digs into my ribs. She laughs through it, wet and sudden, and tells me to get a succulent and to go before she starts again.

Luca, leaning against the wall by the lift, catches my eye. "Come back," he says. "Bring the ugly shoes." He kisses my cheek, one side, and goes through the service door toward the kitchen. He goes back to work.

* * *

Leo is at the top of the stairs.

The seventy-three steps are behind him, falling away down the cliff toward the harbour and the sea and the town. The morning light is coming from behind him and his face is half in shadow and he's holding a folded piece of paper.

I walk to him. My suitcase is at the bottom of the stairs. I left it there because I wanted to say goodbye to him without the broken wheel scraping stone between every word.

He holds out the paper.

I take it. Unfold it. A name. An address. Eleni Andreadis. Heraklion road, 14 km marker, south. The farm with the blue gate.

I look up.

"I thought you should have this," he says.

"This is where the labdanum comes from."

"This is where it starts."

The sea behind him. The lemon tree behind me. The monastery holding its breath or maybe that's just me holding mine.

"The blue gate," I say.

"The blue gate."

"And Eleni Andreadis."

"She's seventy-three. She's been harvesting labdanum since she was fourteen. She'll tell you the resin is alive. She's right."

He's looking at me. I'm holding the paper with both hands because one hand isn't enough for what this is. He's giving me the source. The place where his base note begins and my base note begins and the connection between our formulas starts, before the architecture and the reaching and the space I chose to leave empty. Before any of it. The goats and the rock roses and the resin and a woman in Crete who has been pulling labdanum from the hills for fifty-nine years.

I fold the paper. Put it in my pocket. Left side. Over whatever the organ is that's doing this to my chest.

He steps forward. His hand goes to the back of my neck, his fingers in my hair, and he kisses me. Brief. Unhurried. His mouth tastes like coffee and his skin carries the base note at zero distance, the full architecture, and my brain does what it always does. Reaches. Tries to hold every molecule, name every component, solve the last unnamed fraction of the fortress he built.

And I let it reach. And for the first time I don't need the answer.

He pulls back. His hand stays on my neck for one more second. His thumb traces my jaw.

"Call me from Crete," he says.

"I'll call you from the blue gate."

* * *

The broken wheel scrapes the stone.

Seventy-three stairs. I'm going down them for the last time, in the Primarks, with the suitcase bumping behind me on every step, and my calves remember every single one. The burn on step twenty from Day 1. The false landing on step forty-one where the staircase pretends to end and doesn't. The view from step thirty-four where the trattoria roof appears, terracotta tiles and the edge of the pergola where I sat with Luca on a night I'm not going to describe to anyone but will keep in the part of my memory that doesn't require language.

Positano unfolds below me. The sea. The pastel stack of the town falling toward the water. The harbour where the ferries come and go. The bougainvillea on every wall, absurd, relentless, purple. A scooter is winding up the coast road, the sound thin and mechanical against the sea. Somewhere below, a dog is barking at a boat. Somewhere above, the monastery is already becoming the place I used to be.

I reach the bottom. My calves are shaking. My suitcase is behind me, patient, broken, loyal.

I sit on the low wall by the harbour road. I take out my phone. I open the airline app.

London. Gatwick. Home. Nadia. The lab on Bermondsey Street with the window that doesn't close properly and the radiator that costs eleven pounds a day and the table where I'll put V4 and look at it and know exactly what it's worth.

I type the destination. Delete it. Type again.

London Gatwick, via Heraklion.

Via Crete.

I book it. The confirmation comes through in four seconds. Departing Naples 14:20, arriving Heraklion 18:55, connecting London Gatwick tomorrow morning. One night in Crete. One visit to a farm with a blue gate on the Heraklion road, fourteen kilometres south. The ticket costs more than the direct flight by forty euros. Forty euros I don't have. Forty euros that Nadia would call reckless and that my bank account would call fictional and that I'm spending anyway because the formula started somewhere and I want to stand there.

I'm going to the source before I go home.

The taxi comes. I get in. The suitcase goes in the boot and the driver says nothing about the broken wheel. Politeness or indifference. Both are fine.

The harbour. The ferry ticket. The metal ramp under my feet and the deck rail under my hands and the engine starting low in the hull. The ferry pulls out and Positano begins to shrink behind me, the cliff face and the stairs and the monastery at the top, stone and iron and three weeks of my life held in medieval walls.

The sea is flat. The sun is high. The air carries lemon and salt and stone and rosemary from the coast, fading as the distance opens, and underneath it, faint, the base note I can name but won't.

The ferry turns south. The wake spreads white behind us. Positano disappears.

I'm carrying a formula with a hole in it and a tie on my wrist and a piece of paper in my left pocket and a flight to Crete and a broken suitcase and the scent of a man I chose not to solve.

The sea ahead is wide and blue and I am going toward it.
