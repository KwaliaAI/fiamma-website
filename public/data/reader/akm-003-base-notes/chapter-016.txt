The formula is as done as it's going to be.

I've been staring at it for six hours. Version fourteen. Or version four-point-fourteen, if we're being technical, and I've stopped being technical because being technical requires a brain that hasn't eaten bread from a paper bag in two days and slept on a lab bench in yesterday's clothes.

The formula holds. The oud holds. The bergamot transition that broke my soul on version eleven has finally, grudgingly, decided to behave. Everything I can control, I've controlled. Everything I can't control is shaped like Leo Vance and I can't think about that right now.

I cap the last vial, put my notebook in the drawer, and go up to the terrace.

* * *

Eleven PM. Positano is lit up below, a cascade of gold and white falling down the cliff to the water. The sea is black and flat and enormous. The terrace railing is cool under my hands and the stone is still holding the day's heat through the soles of my bare feet. Someone — Elena, probably — has left a glass on the wide part of the wall. Empty. A ring of condensation where the ice was.

I lean on the railing and let the air move over me and try to feel something other than tired.

Two days. Two days since the gaffer tape. Two days since "You're saying I'm a risk to your business" and his face when I said it and the door he closed and the silence that followed, the kind that fills a building from the inside out.

Fourteen versions. Most of them terrible. Three of them good. One of them close. And then today, this afternoon, Yuki in my doorway with her hands in her pockets saying "I don't know if it's good enough" about her own formula, which was as vulnerable as Yuki gets, and me saying "It's good enough" because it was, and her nodding once and leaving and neither of us mentioning what we were actually talking about: the competition, and the panel, and the fact that both of us are running out of days.

Elena brought bread at midnight, two nights running. I ate it because she stood there watching until I did.

Nadia said "Win." Just that. One word. Then she hung up.

I lean further over the railing. The sea doesn't care. Good.

The door opens behind me.

I know it's him before I hear his voice because the air changes. I've spent seventeen days breathing his formula through medieval stone and my body has its own filing system for him now, independent of my brain's, and it says: that. Here.

He doesn't sit. He walks to the railing, four feet from me, and leans. His eyes are on the sea, or the lights, or the middle distance where feelings go when you're not ready to aim them at a person.

"The ventilation is off," he says.

"You taped it."

"I know."

The sea does something reflective below and neither of us acknowledges it.

"I peeled the tape," he says. "This afternoon. Most of it. There's a piece at the top I couldn't reach."

"You could've used a chair."

"I could've used a chair."

"I didn't want to use a chair. I wanted to tell you I peeled the tape."

"You're telling me."

"I'm telling you."

The first time we stood here was Day 9. He had water. I had three-euro wine in a monastery glass. He told me about Beaumont Fragrances and a perfume called Midnight Coast that sold in airports. I told him his water was a moral failing. It feels like a year ago. It was eight days.

"You're drinking water," I say, even though he isn't holding anything.

He glances at me. The corner of his mouth. A flicker.

"I'm not drinking anything."

"Even worse."

He exhales. It's almost a laugh. We're standing four feet apart on a terrace in Positano and neither of us can cross the four feet and we're both trying and the trying looks like jokes about beverages and taped ventilation because that's what we have. It's what we've always had.

"I need to tell you about the base note," he says.

"You've told me about the base note."

"I told you the story. I didn't tell you the door."

He turns slightly. Still not looking at me. Looking at his hands on the railing.

"When Mara left, the hallway smelled like her shampoo for three weeks. Cedar and something synthetic. The shampoo ghost. I told you that."

"You told me that."

"The base note was supposed to be a gift. Before she left. A personal fragrance. Something private, just for her. What she smelled like when she was there — the darkroom chemicals on her hands, the shampoo, the film developer, and underneath it all, her skin. Presence. That's what I was building."

"And when she left, it became the wound."

"When she left, everything I'd built to capture presence inverted. I rebuilt it around the absence. The ghost. DIRTY was supposed to be about someone being there. It became about someone being gone."

"You told me this. In the lab. The morning with the camping stove coffee."

"I'm telling you the part I didn't."

I wait.

"The base note. The foundation of the gift. Three notes, one chord. I designed it to be the most private thing I'd ever made. Something nobody could find, because finding it would mean finding Mara, and Mara was mine. So I buried it. Layer after layer after layer. When it became DIRTY, I kept building walls. I made it so no one could find the door."

I look at him. He's still looking at the railing.

"I found the door," I say.

He goes still.

"My formula. The centre of it. I didn't design it to be open. I tried to fill it fourteen different ways in the last two days and none of them held because the formula knows what it wants. It has an architecture and the architecture is yours."

He turns. Looks at me. Full-on, no angle, no railing, no sea view to split the attention.

"The formula reached for something and what it reached for was you. I didn't build that. It grew."

The silence between his exhale and his inhale lasts a long time.

"How long have you known?"

"Known is the wrong word. Suspected since version six. Admitted it around version eleven. Tried to fix it for two days straight, which brings us to version fourteen and me on this terrace at eleven PM talking to a man I taped out of my ventilation system."

"Technically I taped myself out of your ventilation system."

"Technically we both behaved like idiots."

"Agreed."

The word hangs. It's the first thing we've agreed on in two days and it's about our mutual idiocy, which feels right.

"What are you going to do?"

"Submit V4 as it is. Negotiate with Chiara on the disclosure clause." I straighten up. I've been practising this. "The formula's architecture constitutes a proprietary element whose disclosure would compromise a third party's trade methods."

He stares at me.

"You've been practising that."

"I wrote it on a napkin at the trattoria. Paola asked what I was doing. I told her it was a love letter. She said love letters should be shorter."

He laughs. Actually laughs. Full sound, full throat. His head drops forward and his shoulders move and the laugh bounces off the stone walls behind us and comes back fuller and I stand there watching Leo Vance laugh on a terrace at eleven PM and something in my chest unclenches that has been clenched for two days.

He sits on the stone wall next to me.

His arm goes around my shoulders and I lean into it and his hand is on my upper arm and we sit there looking at Positano and the lights and the sea and neither of us says anything for a long time.

"Come to dinner tomorrow," he says.

"I've been eating bread in my lab for two days."

"I know. Paola is offended."

"Paola is always offended."

"Paola is offended specifically. She told Elena, who told me, that anyone who eats bread alone in a laboratory when there is fish is making a statement about her fish."

"I'm not making a statement about her fish."

"Tell her that."

"I will. Tomorrow. I'll bring flowers."

"She doesn't want flowers."

"What does she want?"

"For you to sit at the table and eat the fish and tell her it's good."

"The fish is always good."

"Then it shouldn't be hard."

Below us, Positano is settling into its midnight quiet, the one that only happens in a place that runs on tourism and early mornings. A few lights going off. A few staying on. Somewhere down the cliff, music from a bar that hasn't given up yet.

His thumb moves on my arm. Once. The same gesture as the lane after the trattoria, after Edouard and Yuki and the fennel argument and the walk up the hill. Small and deliberate.

I reach for his hand.

His fingers close around mine.

I pull him in. Or he pulls me in. Or we both move at the same time, and his mouth is on mine and the kiss is not slow, not careful, not a terrace-at-eleven-PM-reconciliation kiss. This kiss is two days of sealed grilles and dead air and fourteen failed versions and bread from a paper bag. His hand is in my hair and my hand is on his jaw and his teeth catch my lower lip and I don't care. I pull him closer.

"Lab," I say against his mouth.

We walk. Through the stone corridor, barefoot, and the motion sensors click on as we pass, one by one, a trail of light behind us like something from a film neither of us would admit to watching. Lab 2. He unlocks the door. I pull him in. He locks it behind us.

The lock makes a sound. Definitive. Nobody has locked a lab door in this building in seventeen days. The click says: this is private.

The bench is three steps away. We don't make it.

I pull his shirt over his head and he pulls mine and we're on the floor. Stone. Cold. Seven hundred years of cold stored in this stone, patient and absolute, and it shocks my back and I gasp and he's over me and the weight of him is what I want. The press of his body against mine on this ancient freezing floor. Gravity. Substance. Proof.

He's on top. He hasn't been, before. In Lab 2 I was on the bench, on the stool, over him. In his room it was face to face, side by side, the narrow bed dictating geometry. But tonight I want to be underneath. I want the weight. I want to feel held against something solid while the rest of the world does whatever the rest of the world does.

He enters me and I make a sound I don't plan. Half relief, half breath. The grille was sealed. His formula was gone from my lab for two days. The air was dead. And now he's inside me and his base note is everywhere, in his skin and his breath and the air from the reopened grille above us where both our formulas are threading through the stone channels again, mixing.

I wrap my legs around his back and pull him deeper. My hands on his shoulders. My nails dig in and I feel the skin give and I don't stop. Two days of careful distance. Two days of silence and paper-bag bread and Elena's midnight deliveries and Nadia saying "Win" and fourteen versions that broke because they were missing him. I am done with careful.

He's not careful either. His hands on my hips, pressing me into the stone, and the pace is hard and the floor is unforgiving and my spine is registering every ridge and groove of fourteenth-century masonry and I could not care less.

My nose is wide open.

I don't fight it. I don't dismiss it. I don't coexist with it. I use it.

I can smell everything. His formula on his skin, fading in layers as his body heat rewrites the sillage. My formula drifting through the reopened grille, fainter, the bergamot just arriving. Stone floor: mineral, damp, iron trace, cold. His sweat: sharp, clean, different from the last time, because the last time was urgency and this time is hunger and the body knows the difference. The ghost of gaffer tape adhesive, chemical and faint, on the iron slats above us. And underneath all of it, underneath the sweat and the formula and the stone, the base note.

I reach for it. Deliberately. My brain opens every channel and I lean into him and breathe him in and the base note is closer than it has ever been. I can feel the architecture. Three notes. One chord. The construction is almost familiar. Labdanum, something. Animalic, something. And a third element that sits between them like mortar between stones, binding the chord into a single sound.

I almost have it. The third note is right there, at the edge of recognition, the way a word sits on the tip of your tongue. I can feel its weight, its shape, the space it occupies in the chord. I just can't hear it clearly enough to name it.

The identification slips. I'm at the door he built to keep everyone out. The door is locked. But I can see the keyhole now. And I know, with a certainty that lives in my body and not in my brain, that I am two days from holding the key.

I pull his head down and bite his neck. Hard. A mark. My teeth on his skin and his groan against my collarbone and the stone floor pressing patterns into my back that I will carry for days.

I come hard. Full body. Off the stone. My back arches and my legs tighten around him and the sound that comes out of me is nothing like the scream at Luca's flat and nothing like the silence in Lab 2. A single sustained note. Serious. A sound that means something specific and I don't know what it means and I don't need to.

He follows, his forehead dropping to my collarbone, his breathing ragged against my skin, his hands still holding my hips to the floor.

We lie there on cold stone. Through the grille above us, both formulas thread through the channels again, his to my lab and mine to his. The air is moving.

* * *

"The stone floor was a mistake."

"Agreed."

"My back has the texture of a fourteenth-century monastery."

"Medieval."

"LITERALLY medieval."

I'm laughing and he's laughing and his face is different in this light, the desk lamp casting everything amber, his composure gone entirely, replaced by something open and wrecked and pleased.

We move to the bench. Sitting, not — the bench has served that other purpose and I think it's earned a rest. He picks his shirt off the floor, shakes it out, and puts it around my shoulders. His hands on the collar, pulling it closed. The same gesture as the first time. Right here. After the stool, after the iris vial, after the bread in his pocket.

"You do that every time."

"You're always cold after."

"Maybe I'm always cold and you're the variable."

"Maybe."

"Or maybe you just like putting your shirt on women in laboratories."

"Only the ones who've been on my floor."

"How many of those are there?"

"Including you?"

"Including me."

"One."

"Good answer."

"It's also the true answer."

"Even better."

He sits next to me. His hand on my knee. My head finds his shoulder at exactly the right height, geometry I didn't ask for and am grateful for anyway.

Through the grille, I can smell both formulas. His: green, resinous, the base note thick and present. Mine: the bergamot, the oud, and at the centre the formula leaning toward the base note I almost identified three minutes ago with my back on the floor and my legs around him and my brain running wide open.

Two days from holding the key. I can feel it.

"Come to dinner tomorrow," I say. "I mean it."

"Paola's fish."

"Paola's fish."

"She'll want an apology."

"From you?"

"From both of us. She takes absence personally."

"I've been eating bread."

"I know. She told me. She used the word 'disgraziata,' which I think means 'woman who has insulted my fish.'"

"That's a very specific insult."

"Italian has a word for everything."

His arm tightens around me. I close my eyes. The lab smells like both of us and stone and sex and two formulas meeting in the air above us, and the base note is there, present, unnamed, two days from being known.

I keep my eyes closed. I breathe him in. The base note. The formula that grew toward him like a plant toward light, without permission, without plan.

Close.
