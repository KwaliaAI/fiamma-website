I woke up lighter.

Not a metaphor. My body felt different. Muscles I'd been clenching for months had let go in the night and hadn't clenched back. My feet were sore from the barefoot climb and there was a bruise on my left shin I didn't remember getting and my dress was still inside out on the floor where I'd dropped it and I didn't care about any of it. I lay on the narrow bed with the sheet tangled around one leg and the sea coming through the window and I felt, for the first time since the debt crossed the comma, like my body belonged to me.

I padded to the bathroom. Caught my reflection in the mirror above the sink. Hair wrecked. Mascara migrated. A red mark on my collarbone from his mouth. I smiled at it. Just smiled. No analysis, no projection, no calculation of what it meant or what came next. I'd had excellent sex with a beautiful waiter and I was going to shower and go to work.

The shower was monastery plumbing: forty seconds of scalding followed by a temperature drop that could have been used to interrogate prisoners. I stood under the cold blast and felt every nerve ending in my body announce itself. Good morning. Welcome back. Where have you been.

My good sandals were under Luca's bed. I'd left them there at some point between the second orgasm and the part where I made the crucifix rattle against the wall. The Primarks were by my door. Three pounds fifty from a bin in Peckham. I put them on. Edouard was going to have something to say about this. Edouard always had something to say about footwear.

* * *

The lab smelled like yesterday. My formula on test strips, the labdanum still talking, the oud underneath doing its slow patient climb. I set up my bench and started V3 where I'd left it. The neroli bridge was the problem. It had been the problem for three days: too timid at one concentration, too sweet at another, jamming itself between the labdanum and the iris like a mediator nobody had asked for.

I tried it again. Higher ratio. And this time my hands moved differently. Looser. Faster. Less hesitation between the pipette and the strip, less second-guessing, as though the part of my brain that usually stood between intention and action had taken the morning off. The neroli went on and I held the strip to my nose and the bridge held. It held in a new way. Tighter. The labdanum and iris were still fighting, but the neroli was letting them fight while keeping them in the same room. Not a referee. A wall between two neighbours who needed the wall to exist so they could shout over it.

I tested it on skin. Left wrist, inner. Waited ninety seconds. The opening bit, the chinotto doing its bright aggressive thing, the bergamot softening it, and then the heart opened and the neroli was there, holding, and the centre of the formula had changed shape. Smaller. Tighter. A keyhole instead of an open door.

V3 was better than anything I'd made so far.

* * *

Breakfast. I was late. The refectory was already full of conversation when I pushed through the door, and the conversation didn't stop for me.

Elena slid a coffee across the table without comment. This was her gift. The logistics of other people's needs, handled silently, without requiring gratitude or acknowledgment. I picked it up and drank and it was perfect.

Edouard was mid-story. "...the man had never been to Corsica. He was selling Corsican immortelle from a warehouse in Belgium. I asked him: 'Have you smelled real immortelle?' He said: 'I've smelled the spec sheet.' I left. I walked out. You cannot build a fragrance from a spec sheet any more than you can build a marriage from a questionnaire, and I have tried both."

"How many marriages?" I asked, sitting down.

"Three. Each one taught me something. The first taught me patience. The second taught me that patience has limits. The third taught me to read contracts more carefully."

Yuki was eating toast. She was cutting it into strips with a knife and eating each strip individually, the same methodical approach she brought to everything. Her hinoki notes from the skin study would have faded overnight, and she'd be rebuilding this morning, and I wondered if she'd slept or if she'd gone straight to the lab. With Yuki it could be either and she'd give you no evidence for either.

"New shoes," Edouard said.

I looked up. He was looking at my feet. Of course he was.

"Same shoes," I said. "The Primarks. From Day 2."

"Ah. The return of the Primarks." His tone suggested a diagnosis. "And the Ferragamos?"

"Dead. You were at the funeral."

"I was. It was moving. Brief, but moving." He drank his coffee. "And the replacement pair? The ones you wore last night at dinner?"

The ones currently under Luca's bed. "Elsewhere."

Edouard let this sit for exactly the amount of time it took him to understand everything it meant. He picked up his bread. "Elsewhere. Of course."

Elena refilled my coffee from the pot. "Chiara wants us in the central lab at eleven for check-in notes. Individual meetings, fifteen minutes each."

"Will there be feedback?" Edouard asked.

"She said 'observations.'"

"Observations is feedback without the courage to call itself feedback. I've been married three times. I know the genre."

I ate. The bread was from yesterday, a bit stale, better toasted. Gianluca's absence was showing in the small things. The butter was still in its foil. The fruit bowl had the look of something Elena had assembled from the pantry with good intentions and limited materials. An orange, two bananas going brown, and an apple that Yuki was eyeing with what I could only describe as professional assessment.

Leo came in.

He came through the door without announcement, without adjusting his entrance for the room. Grey t-shirt, jeans, coffee already in his hand because he'd made his own in the kitchen. He sat down across from me and said good morning to the table and the table said good morning back and that was the full extent of the ceremony.

He was looking at me differently. His eyes moved over my face and stopped and moved again, and I could see him trying to place what was different. Same person, same seat, different energy. I was taking up more space. I was sitting with my elbows on the table and my shoulders down and my body present in a way it hadn't been yesterday morning, when I'd been coiled with the skin study and the grille and the debt and the noise of all of it. Today I was quiet inside. Today the body was running the show and the body was satisfied.

Edouard said something about Belgium and I laughed. A real laugh, loose, and Leo's expression tightened for a fraction of a second. Just the jaw. A small muscular adjustment that could have meant anything and that I caught because I'd been watching his face for four days and I knew its resting state and this was a departure from it.

"I calculate cost-per-millilitre before I calculate beauty," I said. We'd been talking about sourcing. Edouard had been complaining about a supplier who charged by the gram and rounded up. "I wish I could say that was a creative choice."

"It's an honest one," Leo said.

"Honest and creative aren't mutually exclusive," Edouard said. "My first wife was both. My second wife was neither. My third wife was creative about her honesty, which is worse."

Yuki put down her toast strip. "Leo," she said.

He looked at her. We all looked at her. Yuki addressing someone directly was an event.

"He is careful," she said.

Edouard waited. I waited. Leo waited.

There was no more.

Yuki picked up her next toast strip and ate it. The observation was complete. It required no elaboration. Yuki had spoken and the speaking contained everything she intended and if we needed more it was our own deficiency, not hers.

Leo reached for the coffee pot.

His hand caught the milk jug. The jug was small, ceramic, and positioned exactly where his hand didn't expect it to be. It tipped. Milk spread across the table in a white rush, heading for Edouard's sleeve with the inevitability of a liquid that has identified its target and committed.

"Shit. Sorry." Leo grabbed a napkin, mopping, but the milk was faster than the napkin and the napkin was thin and the mopping was making it worse, spreading the spill wider, soaking into the bread basket. His face went red. Actual red, a flush that started at his neck and moved up to his ears, and he kept mopping and the mopping kept failing and he looked, for five full seconds, like a man who had never successfully interacted with a table in his life.

Edouard lifted his sleeve with theatrical precision. One inch above the advancing tide. The timing was perfect. "The Americans are now attacking."

Leo half-laughed. The laugh was thin, embarrassed, and the flush didn't fade. He mopped. He used a second napkin. Elena reached across and moved the bread basket out of the flood zone. Quietly, automatically. She'd been cleaning up after other people's disasters her whole career.

He didn't recover smoothly. That was the thing. This was the man who fixed the espresso machine in four seconds, who adjusted the salt grinder without commentary, who left the ventilation grille open on purpose and said I wanted to know what you'd build next through a stone wall calmly, as though he'd rehearsed it in his sleep. And here he was turning crimson over spilled milk, making it worse with every swipe, unable to find the version of himself that handled things. The competence had left the building. What remained was a thirty-eight-year-old man who'd knocked over a milk jug in front of four people and couldn't make it smooth.

I liked him more in that moment than I had during any moment of capability. My chest did a thing. I ate my bread.

Elena mopped up what Leo's napkins had missed, quietly, efficiently, and the table reset itself. Small disasters have short memories.

"Elena," Edouard said, pouring himself more coffee from the pot Leo hadn't knocked over. "I know nothing about you. This is unusual. I normally know everything about everyone by Day 2. You have been resistant to my interrogation techniques."

"You haven't interrogated me."

"I've been subtle."

"You've been talking about yourself."

He paused. Considered this. "These are the same thing."

Elena smiled. It changed her face. She was usually in motion, carrying something, managing something, her features arranged around the next task. The smile was still, and young, and surprised.

"I'm from Milan," she said. "I studied art history. Ended up in event management."

"That's a journey."

"It's a detour. I took this job because I wanted to be near something creative without the pressure of being the creative one." She looked at her coffee. "I carry things. I'm good at carrying things."

"That's not a small thing," I said.

Elena looked up. The surprise again. As if being told her skill mattered was a sentence she hadn't heard before, or hadn't heard from someone she expected to say it.

"Thank you," she said. And then, because Elena's sincerity came in short bursts followed by immediate retreat: "Also, someone has to know where the timer buttons are."

* * *

The afternoon was mine. Chiara's check-in was brief and clinical. She smelled V3, wrote something on her clipboard, said "Continue," and moved on to the next lab. Continue. The highest Chiara compliment. Continue meant she hadn't told me to stop, and in Chiara's vocabulary the absence of criticism was endorsement.

I needed my sandals back. The good pair, the ones I'd bought in a market in Bermondsey with money I should have spent on rent, the ones that were sitting under Luca's bed in a flat above a trattoria in lower Positano because I'd left them there while being too well-fucked to care about footwear logistics.

I took the stairs down. Seventy-three. In daylight they were less dramatic. Positano spread below in its cascade of pastel and bougainvillea, the sea flat and bright, tourists moving through the narrow streets with the unhurried purpose of people who had nowhere specific to be and budgets to prove it. My Primarks slapped on the stone. Edouard would have wept.

The trattoria was closed for the afternoon. Shutters down. The string of Christmas lights off. I went around the side. A narrow door, a narrower staircase. Up.

Luca opened the door in shorts and nothing else. Sleep-rumpled. Half his hair going one way and the other half going somewhere it would regret. He smiled when he saw me, and the smile was the same one from last night: open, easy, carrying nothing underneath it.

"I left my shoes," I said.

He laughed. "I know. I found them." He stepped back. "Come. I make coffee."

The flat was the same small clean room from last night, now in daylight. The crucifix. The footballer poster. Sheets on the bed, fresh. He'd changed them. The window was open and the alley was below and across the alley a woman was hanging laundry on a line and a cat was sitting on a wall watching the woman with the focused disapproval of an animal that has opinions about domestic labour.

He made coffee on the stove. A moka pot, the kind every Italian kitchen has, the aluminium ones that cost four euros and make better coffee than any machine. I watched his hands. Broad, brown, competent from doing one thing a thousand times. He poured two cups and handed me one.

We sat on the small balcony. Two chairs, barely room for both. Our knees almost touching. The alley below, the laundry, the cat. The coffee was strong and good.

"Your shoes." He pointed inside, under the chair by the window. Both sandals, paired neatly. He'd found the second one. The one I'd lost in the dark under the bed. He'd found it and placed them together. Clean sheets, paired shoes. He tidied up after guests without being asked.

"Thank you."

"You are welcome." He drank his coffee. Looked at the alley. Then: "Your work. The perfume. It goes well?"

I weighed what to say. Testing how much I could explain to a civilian. "I'm building something. It's got a gap in the middle. Like a bridge that's missing a section. You can see both sides but you can't cross."

He thought about this. "You're making something that misses something?"

I looked at him. "That's actually perfect."

He grinned. Big, pleased. He'd accidentally said something right and he knew it. "I don't know perfume. But I know about missing things."

"What do you miss?"

"My mother's cooking. She lives in Sorrento now. I eat my own food. It's terrible." He tipped his cup back. "Everything else I have."

This was the thing about Luca. He was complete. Twenty-five, living in a flat above his aunt's trattoria, carrying plates up and down a cliff face, sleeping deeply, wanting simply. A man who had filled his life with the available materials and found them sufficient.

The coffee finished. The cup was empty. The afternoon light was shifting in the alley and the woman had finished her laundry and the cat had relocated to a higher wall for better surveillance.

"I should go," I said.

"Okay." He stood when I stood. Kissed my cheek. Simple. "Come eat tonight. Paola makes the fish."

"I'll be there. With the others."

"Yes. With the others." He smiled. He understood. We both understood. The thing was the thing and the thing was done and the done was fine.

I left with my sandals. The good ones, back on my feet. Down the narrow stairs, out the side door, into the alley. The afternoon was bright and the stone was hot and my feet felt like they belonged to me again.

* * *

The seventy-three stairs going up were a different negotiation in the heat. I climbed them with my calves burning and my lungs working and V3 waiting in the lab, and the lab pulled me. The neroli bridge. The keyhole at the centre. I wanted to get back to it. I wanted to test it again with a clear head and clean hands.

I was on step forty-six when my phone buzzed.

Nadia.

Margot. Bergmann & Cole. They'll extend your supply terms if you can show income coming. Existing contract or prize award. Something on paper.

I stopped climbing. A man with a hiking pole came down past me, nodding, and I pressed myself against the wall and read the message again.

Income coming meant Bergmann & Cole wanted proof that Margot Crane was still a going concern and not a woman with six figures of debt and a prayer. They supplied my iris butter. Without them, V3 lost its heart.

What counts as income?

Prize award would work. Commission letter. Something with a number on it.

The Radici commission is fifty thousand euros.

That would VERY MUCH count. Win it.

Thanks Nadia, very helpful, I'll just win it.

You're welcome. Also: Ferranti rang my office. Asking if I have current contact details for you. I told them I was your accountant, not your mum, and that their calling my office was inappropriate and possibly actionable. I was bluffing about the actionable part but the man on the phone didn't know that.

I love you.

You owe me. Literally. My invoice from January is still outstanding.

How much?

£340.

I leaned against the sun-heated stone wall. Added it to the number. £203,417.62 became £203,757.62. The comma was still there. The comma wasn't going anywhere.

I'll pay you when I win.

You'll pay me when you can. Win anyway.

I put the phone away. Climbed the remaining twenty-seven stairs. My calves screamed. My Primarks held. The afternoon sun pressed on the back of my neck and Positano spread below me, beautiful and expensive and indifferent to the financial details of one British perfumer who needed to win a prize she couldn't afford to lose.

The lab. V3 on the bench. I picked up my pipette and went back to work.

* * *

Evening. The trattoria. Candles, string lights blinking their irregular rhythm, Paola appearing at the table with bread and oil and wine she placed without ceremony. We sat. The same table as every night. The same carafe. The same bread that Paola baked herself and would serve until we stopped coming or she stopped baking and I suspected both events were equally unlikely.

Luca served us. He brought the wine and the water and he caught my eye for one second and then moved on, professional, easy, as though last night and this afternoon existed in a different room and this room was the one where he carried plates. I respected this. Compartmentalisation is an undervalued skill.

Edouard noticed my feet. "The good sandals have returned from Elsewhere."

"They have."

"Elsewhere must be a forgiving place."

"Elsewhere was very hospitable."

He raised his glass. "To Elsewhere."

Paola's fish arrived. Sea bass, whole, roasted with lemon and rosemary. The skin crackling and the flesh white and the lemon charred at the edges. She served it without comment. The fish was perfect. Your opinion was a formality.

We ate. The table did its thing. Edouard told a story about a perfume launch in Milan where the model allergic to the featured fragrance sneezed on the front row of journalists. Yuki asked a single question about the sneeze: "Which note?" Edouard didn't know. Yuki was disappointed. Elena laughed and the laugh was bigger than yesterday, less guarded. The stairs were doing their work. Four nights of climbing a cliff in the dark together will break down boundaries that professionalism spends years constructing.

Leo ate his fish. He was quiet tonight, but it was a different quiet from the skin study quiet. Settled. Working on something internal, his fork moving steadily, his eyes on his plate, occasionally lifting to the table, to the conversation, to me and then away. After the milk spill this morning his composure had rebuilt itself slowly across the day, layer by layer, a formula finding its base after the top notes burn off.

We climbed the stairs. Elena pressed the first button. Leo pressed the second. I pressed the third at forty-nine. Edouard provided commentary on every flight. Yuki was already at the top.

In my room, I sat on the bed and did the maths. The Radici prize: fifty thousand euros. Roughly forty-three thousand pounds. Enough for Ferranti and three months of minimums and Nadia's invoice and maybe, maybe, a new pair of shoes that weren't from a bin in Peckham.

Without it: Bergmann & Cole pulled the supply terms. No iris butter. V3 lost its heart. The formula collapsed and I went home with nothing and Ferranti's emails moved to the font size I found unnecessary.

I had to win.

I lay on the bed. The sea through the window. Through the ventilation grille, faint and persistent: Leo's formula. The base note I couldn't name, doing its quiet patient thing in the dark.

I closed my eyes. V3 was better. The body was looser. Tomorrow I'd push it further.

Sleep came at midnight. Fast, for once. A body that has been thoroughly used and then put back to work does not argue with sleep. It goes.
