Six a.m. and the lab smells like yesterday's trials: the labdanum still hanging in the air, a ghost of the accord I built at midnight and dismantled by one. I set my materials out in the order I always use. Oud far left, labdanum centre, iris and the bridge candidates on the right. Pipettes in a glass, cleaned, dried, lined up like surgical instruments. My hands know this choreography. They've been doing it since I was seventeen, when I got my first bench at a supplier in Bermondsey and spent four months learning that organisation is not a personality trait, it's a survival mechanism. The lab is the one place where every object has a location and every location makes sense. The rest of my life has not historically offered this service.

I start V2. The chinotto opening is too aggressive. It did something last night that made me flinch, a bitterness that crossed from interesting to antagonistic in the first thirty seconds, and a perfume that picks a fight with its own wearer is a perfume that gets sprayed once and lives in a drawer. I dial it back. Add bergamot at a ratio that should soften the landing without killing the bite. Test strip. Wrist. Wait.

Through the wall: the sound of glass on glass. A cap being unscrewed. The scratch of a pen.

Leo is in Lab 2.

I knew this because I passed his door on the way in and the light was already on. I also know his schedule now because I've been here thirty-six hours and apparently that's enough time to memorise a stranger's work patterns. Perfectly normal competitive awareness that means nothing beyond its professional utility.

The ventilation grille is set into the stone above my bench. Old, iron, the slats angled downward. Medieval engineering designed to move air through the monastery, and the monks who built it were not anticipating a situation in which the air would carry fragments of someone's formula from Lab 2 to Lab 3 and that this would present a problem for the person in Lab 3 that she had not budgeted for.

I can smell it. His. Threading through the grille. The green top, the dark resin, and underneath both of them, faint, the base note I can't identify. My brain reaches for it automatically. Reflex. Like a hand going to a railing. It reaches and misses and reaches again.

I close the vent.

I work for ten minutes. The chinotto-bergamot balance is better. The formula is approaching something. The labdanum holds underneath, patient, doing the heavy lifting while the top notes sort themselves out. But the bridge between labdanum and iris is still missing. A note should live there and doesn't. I can feel the shape of what's missing. I just can't fill it.

I open the vent.

There it is again. The base note. Quiet, present, doing something to the air in my lab that I'm going to classify as a ventilation issue and leave at that.

Breakfast is in the refectory at eight. Bread, butter, coffee, fruit. Gianluca has laid everything out and gone back to the kitchen, where I can hear him singing something in Italian that sounds like it's either about love or sausages. Possibly both.

Edouard is seated at the head of the table. He has been sitting at the head of tables since before most of us were alive and his composure says so. He's eating bread with butter applied in a thin, even layer that speaks of decades of practice and a deep philosophical position on the correct ratio of fat to carbohydrate. His coffee is black. His posture is immaculate. He's wearing a linen shirt that probably cost more than my rent.

Yuki is eating an apple. Just an apple. She cuts it into precise slices with a small knife she appears to have brought from Japan. Each slice is identical. I find this calming and menacing in equal measure.

The espresso machine makes a sound. A grinding, mechanical protest, like something inside it has given up on life and would like us all to know about it. Elena approaches from the supply closet with both hands raised, the way you'd approach a horse that's about to bolt.

Leo stands up. He walks over to the machine, looks at it for four seconds, opens the side panel, does something with his hand, closes it. The grinding stops. Espresso flows into his cup, dark and perfect and silent.

"The grind adjustment slips when the hopper vibrates," he says to Elena. "Common on this model."

He picks up his cup and sits back down.

Edouard stares at him. "How do you know this?"

"I worked in a coffee shop."

"For how long?"

"Six years."

Edouard takes a slow sip of his coffee. Processes this. "Americans," he says, as though the word explains a geological formation he's been puzzling over.

Chiara arrives. Cream linen, clipboard, reading glasses on a chain. She outlines the schedule while standing at the end of the table in a way that makes it clear she's not joining us. She's briefing us. Lab access from six a.m. to midnight. First formal check-in at the end of week one. Final panel at the end of week three. The Radici Prize is judged blind by a five-person panel. The commission for the winner is fifty thousand euros.

Fifty thousand euros. I do the maths without wanting to. Forty-three thousand pounds. That covers Ferranti and leaves enough for the next three months of minimum payments on the rest. It's a quarter of the debt. It buys time, not freedom. But time is the only currency I can currently afford, and this is the exchange rate.

"Questions?" Chiara says.

Yuki puts down her apple knife. "What are the evaluation criteria?"

"Originality and technical mastery. Emotional coherence." Chiara counts them on her fingers. "The panel will smell your formulas blind. No names, no context. The work stands alone or it doesn't."

"And if it falls down?" Edouard asks.

"Then it wasn't ready." Chiara picks up her clipboard and leaves.

After breakfast I walk the cloister. The stone holds the morning cool, the light coming through in slanted bars that hit the floor and make it glow. There's a courtyard at the centre with a lemon tree and a dry fountain and a bench that faces the sea, and I sit on the bench because my body has been in a lab since six and it wants a sky. My nose picks up the lemon tree, the hot stone, the wisteria on the wall. Brief. Automatic. I let it run and don't engage.

Leo comes through, heading to his lab. He's carrying a coffee and walking with the focused quiet of a person going to do the thing they're built for. He sees me on the bench. Slows.

"Your formula is coming through the grille," I say.

He looks at me. "Is that a problem?"

"It's distracting."

A beat. The lemon tree moves in the breeze. Somewhere below us, Positano is happening.

"You could close it," he says.

"I did."

Another beat.

"And then you opened it."

He knows. Of course he knows. The vent is old iron and it makes a sound when you move the slats, and Lab 2 is right there, and he has ears and presumably a functioning awareness of the acoustic properties of medieval monastery ventilation systems, and I closed it and I opened it and he heard both.

"The grille is shared infrastructure," I say. Possibly the least romantic sentence ever spoken under a lemon tree on the Amalfi Coast. "I'm not going to seal it because your formula is opinionated."

The left side of his mouth does something. "Opinionated."

"It has a lot to say."

"So does yours." He starts walking again. Then, over his shoulder: "The oud is better this morning. You changed the ratio."

He's gone before I can answer. A mercy. The answer would have involved my face doing something I would have needed several minutes alone to recover from.

I go back to the lab. I work. The bergamot is cooperating. The chinotto has calmed down. But the bridge note is still missing, and I try neroli at two different concentrations and it's close, closer than anything else, but close is the most frustrating distance in perfumery. Close means you can see the door. Close means the door is locked.

My phone vibrates. Email from Ferranti Capital. Subject line in capitals. I put the phone face down on the bench and leave it there.

Lunch is casual. Gianluca has made a frittata and left it in the refectory with bread and salad. Edouard is explaining the history of Grasse to Elena, who is listening patiently. She has heard this before. She suspects she will hear it again. Yuki is eating her frittata with the precision of a surgeon. Leo is not here. Through the corridor, from the direction of the labs, I can hear glass on glass.

Gianluca's phone rings. He answers it in the kitchen. His voice changes. I can hear it through the door. Softer, worried, rapid Italian. Elena glances toward the kitchen. "Is he okay?" she says to no one. She gets up and goes to check. Comes back. "Family thing," she says. "His mother." She picks up her fork and doesn't eat.

Afternoon. The lab. The vent is open. His formula comes through in waves. The base note stronger now, or I'm more attuned to it, or the afternoon air moves differently through the channels. It sits in the room while I work. Present. Unhelpful.

I catch myself thinking about his hands at dinner. Around the salt grinder. Four seconds, no comment. Hands that fix things without announcing it. I redirect to the formula. The formula needs my attention. The formula doesn't care about anyone's hands.

I try violet leaf as a bridge. Wrong. Too green, too thin, pushed the formula in a direction that had nothing to do with where it wanted to go. Ambrette: too sweet. The fight between the labdanum and the iris needs a referee, not a mediator. Something that holds the tension without resolving it.

Then, through the grille, his voice.

"It's brave."

I stop. My pipette is mid-air. His voice comes through the iron slats, slightly distorted by the stone, lower than it should be, as though the building itself has dropped it half a register.

"What?"

"Your formula. The chinotto opening." A pause. Glass on his side, a cap being replaced. "Most people wouldn't lead with chinotto. It's aggressive."

"It's honest."

"I didn't say it wasn't." Another pause. I can hear him moving. A stool scraping. "I said it was brave. Those are different things."

I set my pipette down. I'm talking to a wall. To a wall with a man behind it and a ventilation grille between us, and the fact that I can't see his face is doing something to the conversation. Making it easier. The wall is allowing a kind of honesty that his dark eyes and his quiet focus and his hands would complicate.

"I can smell yours too," I say. "Through the grille. It's been in my lab all morning."

"I know."

"You left it open. Your side."

"I did."

"Why?"

The longest pause yet. I can hear him breathe. Through iron and stone, his breathing. Considering what to say. Not hurrying toward it.

"Because I wanted to know what you'd build next."

I sit with this. I sit on my lab stool with my unfinished formula on the bench and his voice coming through a medieval ventilation grille and I sit with the fact that he left it open on purpose. That he wanted to smell my work as it developed. That this is either the most flattering professional compliment I've ever received or something else entirely, and the ventilation grille is not going to help me sort the difference.

"The Lefevre limited," he says.

My hands go still.

"Cedar and violet. Two hundred bottles. The house closed the year after. The magazine that reviewed it folded six months later." His voice is steady, factual, the same flat delivery he uses for everything. "I found a bottle at a flea market in Brooklyn. Thirty dollars. No box, no label, just the juice in the bottle. I knew within ten seconds."

The Lefevre limited. I made it when I was twenty-six. Lefevre was a tiny house in Hackney, two people and a dream, and the dream lasted fourteen months and the house dissolved and the two hundred bottles scattered into the world and I assumed they'd disappeared. Into bathroom cabinets and charity shops and the back corners of people's lives, sprayed twice and forgotten. The best thing I ever made, made recklessly, without a commercial brief, when I still believed that if the formula was good enough the rest would follow.

It didn't follow. The formula was good and the house collapsed and the bottles vanished and I carried the memory of that work like a photograph of a room you once lived in that no longer exists.

He found one. In a flea market. For thirty dollars.

"How did you know?" I say. My voice is different. I can hear it.

"The violet." Through the grille, through the stone. "The way you let it fight the cedar instead of smoothing it out. Nobody does that. Everyone softens it. You let it be angry."

Something in my chest is doing a thing I am going to attribute to the ventilation and to the acoustics of a fourteenth-century building and to the fact that I haven't eaten enough today. All three attributions are available and none of them are accurate and I'm going to use them anyway.

"I thought it disappeared," I say. "All of it. I thought nobody kept it."

"I kept it." A beat. "I've used half the bottle. The other half I'm not going to use."

The grille sits between us. Iron and stone. I can smell his formula through it, the base note threading into my lab, and I can hear his breathing, and I am standing in my workspace staring at a wall and the wall is the only reason I'm able to have this conversation, because if I could see his face right now I would have to do something about what I'm feeling and I do not have a plan for that.

"Your base note," I say. "I still can't identify it."

"Good."

"That's what you said last night."

"It's still what I mean."

The grille goes quiet. I hear him pick up his pipette. The scratch of a pen. He's gone back to work, and I'm standing at my bench with the ghost of his voice in the air and the bridge note still missing and the Lefevre limited alive in a bottle in Brooklyn when I'd thought it was dead. My hands are shaking. I blame the caffeine.

Evening. Dinner. Gianluca has made risotto that tastes like butter's argument with saffron, and butter is winning. The refectory is candlelit, the stone glowing. Edouard holds court.

"I once judged a competition in Lisbon where the finalist presented a fragrance made entirely from Portuguese cork oak. The judges were polite. The fragrance smelled like a wine barrel. The man was furious." He drinks. "He's now very successful making candles."

"Good pivot," I say.

"All great careers are pivots. I intended to be a poet." He pauses. Lets this land. "Grasse saved me from myself. And from French literary criticism, which is worse."

Yuki has been reading the wine label for three minutes. She appears to be memorising it. She puts the bottle down, drinks, and says: "Volcanic soil. Aglianico. 2019. They over-irrigated."

Everyone looks at her.

"I read the label," she says. Goes back to her risotto.

Leo eats. He's quiet tonight. When Edouard addresses him directly, he gives short answers that are exactly sufficient and not a syllable more. He's thinking about something. I know this because I've been watching him think about things for thirty-six hours and I can now distinguish between his regular quiet and his working quiet and this is working quiet and I need to stop tracking the internal states of a man I met yesterday.

Dinner ends. I go to my room. Text from Nadia.

How's the Italian prison camp for nose nerds?

Good. I'm making progress on the formula.

Scale of 1-10, how pretentious are the other prisoners?

Edouard is a 9 but he's earned it. Yuki is a 0 but in a way that's more intimidating than a 10.

And the American?

I stare at my phone. The cursor blinks.

Quiet.

That's not a number, Margot.

Goodnight, Nadia.

THAT'S NOT A NUMBER.

I put the phone down.

I can't sleep. This is becoming a pattern, and the pattern has a cause, and the cause is a ventilation grille. I lie in my bed and stare at the ceiling and the base note is there, faint, coming through the grille above my headboard. He's working. At this hour, in his lab, and his formula is travelling through stone channels that have been moving air since the fourteenth century and arriving in my room like a letter I didn't ask for and can't stop reading.

I get up. One-thirty. Joggers, t-shirt, bare feet. The stone corridor is cold. The motion sensors click on as I walk. A procession of light marking my progress through the sleeping building.

The lab. My lab. I push the door open and the grille is right there, above the bench, and through it: his formula. Richer at this hour. The base note is fuller in the quiet, when there's nothing else competing for the air. Or I'm less defended at one-thirty in the morning. Both of these are true and the second one is the one that matters.

I sit on my stool. I don't turn on the bench light. The room is dark except for the moonlight through the high window, and his formula fills the lab, and the base note sits in my chest the way it did in the corridor last night. The same question I can't answer. The same empty hand.

I can hear him through the grille. Working. Glass, pipettes, the quiet rhythm of a person deep in their process. He doesn't know I'm here. Or he does, and he's choosing not to mention it, which with Leo amounts to the same thing.

I stay for twenty minutes. I sit on my stool in the dark and breathe in his formula and let my brain try to identify the base note and fail, over and over, reaching and missing. It's maddening. My nose can identify three thousand compounds and this one sits outside all of them, in a space my training didn't build a shelf for.

The formula through the grille shifts. He's added something. The heart opens, the dark resin moving outward, and the base note deepens and I can feel it now in my sternum, in the space where the debt usually lives, and I realise that I am sitting in a dark lab at two in the morning breathing in a man's formula through a wall and this is either a professional exercise or it is something else and I need to leave before I find out which.

I leave.

The corridor. Cold stone. His door, further down the hall, shows a line of light underneath. I walk past it. I don't stop. I go to my room and I lie down and the base note is still there, faint, through the grille above my bed, and I close my eyes and I can still smell it and I can still hear his voice saying I kept it and the other half I'm not going to use and my hands are still shaking and I'm still blaming the caffeine and the caffeine was twelve hours ago.

Sleep comes at three. It takes its time. It always does.
