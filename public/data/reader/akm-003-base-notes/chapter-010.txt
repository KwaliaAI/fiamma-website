I knock on Luca's door at half two in the afternoon. Sober. Showered. Wearing both sandals.

He opens it in shorts and nothing else, towel over one shoulder, hair damp. He smells like Castile soap. My nose opens the drawer, sees the file from three weeks ago, closes the drawer. Done.

"I have an hour before I should be working," I say.

"Okay," he says.

He steps back. I step in. The flat is one room with a kitchenette, a bed that's been made badly, and a window that overlooks the back of the trattoria kitchen. There's a cat on the windowsill that I don't remember from last time.

"You have a cat now."

"She is not mine. She comes and goes."

"Very Positano."

"Very Italian." He drops the towel on a chair. "Do you want water? Coffee?"

"No."

"Okay," he says again, and this time he's smiling, and I'm already crossing the room.

I kiss him standing up, both hands flat on his chest, pushing him backward. His back hits the wall. I pull the shorts down. He laughs.

"You are in a hurry."

"I'm efficient. There's a difference."

"Is there?"

"Efficiency means I know what I want. Hurry means I'm afraid it'll disappear."

"I am not disappearing."

"Then I'm efficient."

I drop to my knees.

I'm good at this. I know I'm good at this because I pay attention and because I genuinely like it, and those two things together are worth more than any technique article in any magazine. His hand goes to the back of my head, gentle, not steering. I look up once and his head is tipped back against the wall and his mouth is open and his breathing has gone ragged and I feel the specific satisfaction of doing a thing well.

The cat watches from the windowsill with the blankest expression I have ever seen on a living creature.

I pull back. "Your cat is judging me."

"She is not my cat."

"She's staring."

"She stares at me when I eat cereal. This is not personal."

"It feels personal."

"Go back to what you were doing."

"Don't tell me what to do."

"I am asking. Please go back to what you were doing."

"Better."

I go back to what I was doing. Luca makes a sound that starts in his chest and ends somewhere lower and I feel it in my knees, and then his hands are under my arms, pulling me up.

"I want—" he starts.

"Wall?"

"Wall."

"You read my mind."

"I read your body. It is not subtle."

He picks me up like I weigh nothing, which I don't, not for him, not after ten years of carrying plates up seventy-three steps. My back hits the plaster and I wrap my legs around him and we're both laughing because the logistics of this are genuinely ridiculous and anyone who tells you wall sex is effortless has never tried it with a twenty-five-year-old Italian in a flat the size of a parking space.

"Left," I say. "Shift left."

"Your left or my left?"

"My left. Your right. Just—that direction."

He shifts. Plaster dust falls into my hair.

"Your wall is falling apart."

"My wall is fine. You are squirming."

"I'm ADJUSTING."

He gets the angle. I stop adjusting. I stop talking.

"There," I say.

"Yes?"

"Yes. There. Don't move. Just—"

"LUCA!"

We freeze.

Paola's voice comes through the floorboards like the wrath of a benevolent God, if God ran a trattoria and needed someone to carry the bread delivery.

His hand covers my mouth. My hand covers his. We are pinned to the wall, joined, silent, shaking with held laughter, and I can feel his heartbeat everywhere. Through his chest. Through his stomach. Lower.

"LUCA! IL PANE!"

Three seconds. Four. Five. The voice doesn't come again.

"Your aunt," I whisper against his fingers, "is going to kill me."

"My aunt has survived worse."

"Has she survived THIS?"

"She survived my uncle. This is nothing."

I laugh so hard he nearly drops me, and his nose is running, because physical exertion does that, because bodies are stupid and graceless and perfect, and he can't wipe it because both arms are holding me against a wall, so he turns his head and drags his nose across his own shoulder while still inside me. There's a visible smear on his skin and I start laughing again and I genuinely cannot stop. My back is sliding down the plaster. My legs are shaking. He's laughing too, trying to hold me, and we are four seconds from a genuine injury.

"Bed," I say. "Bed bed bed."

He carries me the three steps. We fall onto the badly made sheets and I push him onto his back and climb on top.

This part is mine.

I set the pace. Slow. Eyes open. His hands on my hips, thumbs pressed against the bones. I grind against him and watch his face and his face is extraordinary right now. Unguarded. Present. His eyes are brown and slightly crossed with effort and his lips are parted and every performance has stopped and he is just here.

"Don't close your eyes," I say.

"I wasn't going to."

"You were."

"Maybe."

"Keep them open."

He keeps them open. I come watching his face. It rolls through my spine and into my jaw and I don't look away and he comes right after with a sound that's more surprise than anything, like he didn't expect to, like I caught him off guard.

I lean down and bite his shoulder. Hard enough to leave a mark. I don't apologise.

The cat has moved to the foot of the bed.

"Your cat."

"Not my cat."

"She's on the bed."

"She does this."

We lie there. The ceiling has a crack that runs from the light fixture to the corner.

"Your ceiling is terrible," I say.

"My ceiling is fine."

"There's a crack."

"That crack has been there since 1987. It is structural character."

"That's very philosophical for a man who was inside me thirty seconds ago."

"Italians are philosophical. It's in the water."

"Everything is in the water with you people."

"This is why we don't drink wine. We don't need to." He turns his head. Grinning. "We have water."

"Don't you start. Leo already has that position."

"Leo drinks only water?"

"At sunset. It's a moral stance. We've argued about it."

"You argue about water."

"We argue about everything."

"This sounds like more than arguing."

"It is very precisely arguing."

Luca props himself up on one elbow and looks at me. Fond, uncomplicated. He has no stake in being right. "It is clearly not arguing."

"It is clearly none of your business."

"This is true." He kisses my forehead. "You should shower. You smell like sex."

"I'm a perfumer. I can fix it."

"You are a perfumer who smells like sex and has plaster dust in her hair."

I reach back. White powder. I look like a haunting.

"Use the shower. The pressure is bad."

"I remember."

I shower in his tiny bathroom with the handheld nozzle that dribbles rather than sprays, and I put on my dress, and I put on both sandals, and I check the mirror. Presentable. Settled.

At the door I turn back. "Thank you."

"For what?"

"The hour."

"It was forty-three minutes."

"Overachiever."

The cat is already asleep.

* * *

Edouard finds me by the fountain at half four. He's wearing a linen blazer and a tie, because he is always wearing a linen blazer and a tie, because he is Edouard, because France is a country that produces men who treat neckwear as identity.

"Margot. Sit. I have a story."

"I'm already sitting."

"Sit more attentively."

I adjust my posture. He nods, satisfied.

"In 1987, a man named Claude Arnault worked for a house in Grasse. Respectable house. Fourth generation. He was their nose, their star, their future. He won the Fifi twice. He consulted for Guerlain. He was, by every measure, legitimate."

"But?"

"But he was a fraud. His entire career, every composition that bore his name, was built on three synthetic accords. Three. Galaxolide, Hedione, Iso E Super. He rotated the proportions. He changed the top notes. He told stories about sourcing trips to Madagascar and extraction techniques he'd invented and rare absolutes he'd discovered in a monastery in Calabria. None of it was real. The accords were basic. A second-year student could have mixed them."

"How long did it last?"

"Twenty-three years."

"Twenty-three YEARS?"

"He was very confident. Confidence is the most expensive ingredient."

"How did they catch him?"

"They didn't. He retired. Full honours. He told the truth at his retirement dinner because, and I quote, 'the champagne was mediocre and someone had to say something interesting.'"

"That's not a punchline, Edouard. That's a horror story."

"It is both. The best stories are."

Yuki arrives from the direction of the lab, carrying a cup of green tea in both hands like a small ceremony. She sits on the fountain edge. She doesn't greet us. She doesn't need to.

Edouard resumes. "My point, and I do have one, is that the line between confidence and competence in this profession—"

"You tell stories so you don't have to say what you mean," Yuki says.

The courtyard stops.

The fountain sounds louder. A bird somewhere pauses mid-phrase. Edouard opens his mouth and for the first time in ten days, nothing comes out. His jaw works. His hand moves toward his wine glass and stops. I have known this man for ten days and I have never seen him without a sentence loaded.

Five seconds. I count them.

He picks up his wine. Drinks. Sets it down carefully.

"She's right. This is insufferable."

A longer beat.

"My greatest influence was my first wife. She could smell a lie in a formula the way some people hear a wrong note." He's looking at the tablecloth. "I have spent forty years trying to be as honest as she was. I am not there yet."

Yuki nods. Once.

Then Edouard straightens his tie, lifts his chin, adjusts his blazer, and says: "Now. Dinner."

* * *

Dinner is Paola's decision, as always. Tonight it's orecchiette with broccoli rabe, then branzino, then the tiramisù she made this morning and has been guarding in the kitchen like a state secret.

We're loose. Week two. The politeness burned off days ago. Elena has abandoned the clipboard for the evening and is wearing a polo shirt that might technically be inside out. Leo is drinking water. I am drinking whatever red Paola poured, and I've decided I don't care.

"You're drinking the red," Leo says.

"I am."

"Is it good?"

"It's wet and it's alcoholic and the sun is setting."

"That's not an answer."

"That's the only answer wine needs."

"Water doesn't need any answer."

"Water doesn't need anything. Water is the absence of personality."

"Water is the absence of pretension."

"Those are the same thing."

"They're not."

"They're close."

"You keep saying that about things," Leo says. "Close."

"Because things keep being close."

Elena leans across the table. "Are you two going to do this all night?"

"Do what?" I say.

"Fight about liquids."

"We're not fighting. We're having a philosophical disagreement about hydration."

"You're flirting," Yuki says, not looking up from her branzino.

"We are NOT—"

"We're not," Leo says, at exactly the same time, and our simultaneous denial is so perfectly timed that Edouard laughs so hard he chokes on an olive.

"Proof," Edouard wheezes. "Proof of flirting. Synchronised denial."

"That's not proof of anything," Leo says.

"Synchronised denial is proof of everything," Edouard says. "Ask any divorce lawyer."

"That is a bleak reference," I say.

"I am French. All our references are bleak."

Luca brings the tiramisù. He sets each plate down with that grace he has, the one that lives in his wrists, and the table goes slightly quieter because we've all noticed it at various points across the last ten days and nobody has talked about it except through glances. When he leans across to place Edouard's plate, Edouard puts his hand on Luca's forearm.

The table goes fully quiet. Not tense. Anticipatory.

"Young man." Edouard's voice is steady. Three glasses of wine steady, which on him means precise, not sloppy. "I am seventy-one years old and French and I have been watching you serve pasta for ten days and I need you to know that if I were forty years younger and any nationality other than my own, I would make you very happy."

Luca doesn't flinch. Doesn't stiffen. Doesn't pull his arm away. His face opens into a grin so genuine it's almost unbearable to look at directly, and he puts his other hand on top of Edouard's.

"You are very kind," he says.

"I am not kind. I am declaring."

"Then I accept."

He picks up the empty wine carafe and goes to the kitchen. Easy. Graceful. Like someone just told him the weather was nice and he agreed.

"This is ridiculous," Yuki says.

"This is ITALY," Edouard says.

I'm laughing. Elena is laughing, her Aperol spritz sloshing dangerously close to the rim. Even Leo is smiling, and it's not his usual careful, calibrated smile. It's a real one. The kind that changes his whole face from composed to young, and for a second I can see what he must have looked like at twenty-five behind a bar in Red Hook, pouring bad espresso with too much foam, before whatever happened that made him so precise about everything.

"Everyone," I say. "Every single person at this table and probably half the kitchen staff. He's a weather system."

"I have filed a formal objection," Yuki says.

"Noted," I say. "Denied."

"Not denied. Tabled."

"Yuki is immune," Edouard says. "This is her superpower."

"I am not immune. I am busy."

"She's busy," Elena repeats, grinning. "The rest of us just have no standards."

"I have standards," Edouard says. "They are simply French."

"I had standards," I say. "Then I came to Italy."

"I never had standards," Elena says, and she's laughing before she finishes the sentence, and I think this might be the first time I've seen her genuinely drunk, and it suits her.

Leo pushes his water glass forward. "I'd like the record to show I've maintained complete neutrality."

"The record shows you're drinking water at a table full of wine," I say. "That's not neutrality. That's dissent."

"Dissent is underrated."

"Dissent is lonely."

"Who said I was lonely?"

I don't have an answer for that.

"Eat your tiramisù," Yuki says. "Both of you."

We eat our tiramisù. It's perfect. Of course it is. Paola doesn't make imperfect tiramisù. That's not how Paola works.

* * *

After dinner, Edouard says, "The stairs."

"We have a lift," Elena says.

"We have always had a lift. But we also have stairs. And tonight we have dignity."

"Dignity?" I say.

"Dignity. We are artists. We climb."

"You've had three glasses of wine and an olive that nearly killed you."

"I have had three glasses of courage and one olive of truth."

"That's what wine IS," I say, looking directly at Leo. "That's literally what wine does. Courage."

"That's literally what wine pretends to do," he says. "Courage comes from somewhere else."

"Where?"

"Stairs, apparently."

Edouard hits the first timer button. The light clicks on. Sixty-two seconds.

We climb.

Elena goes first, taking them two at a time, Aperol spritz metabolism burning off with each step. Yuki follows at her own pace, unrushed. Edouard is next, one hand on the rail, breathing audibly, refusing to slow down.

"Step twenty-three," he announces, hitting the second timer. Fresh light. "I climbed these for love. Let the record show."

"The record shows you climbed these for tiramisù," Yuki calls down.

"Same thing."

"Very much not the same thing," Yuki says.

"Yuki. You wound me."

"You'll recover."

Leo and I are behind them. Not touching. The staircase is narrow and our shoulders are near but never quite colliding and I can smell his formula through the grille between Labs 2 and 3. I've been tracking it for days now. It's different tonight. Something in the base has shifted. Heavier. More committed.

"Your formula," I say.

"What about it?"

"It's getting braver."

He glances at me. Half a step above. "Or more desperate."

"Those sound the same."

"They're not." His foot hits the next step. "But they're close."

"There's that word again."

"There's that word again," he agrees.

We climb. Above us, Edouard is giving a running commentary on each step like a sportscaster covering an endurance event. Below us, the timer on the first button has run out and the lower stairs have gone dark.

"Step forty-nine!" Edouard calls. The third timer clicks.

"Margot," Leo says.

"What?"

"Your formula's changing too."

"I know."

"Do you know how?"

"I know exactly how."

He nods. We climb the last twenty-four steps without talking.

At the top, Edouard is leaning against the wall, hand on his chest. He has made a point. He is now paying for it.

"Are you dying?" Elena asks.

"I am savouring."

"He's fine," Yuki says.

"I am magnificent," Edouard says. "Good night."

He goes to his room. Elena follows. Yuki is already gone, because Yuki is always already gone, because efficiency is silence and Yuki is very efficient.

Leo and I stand in the corridor.

"Good night, Margot."

"Good night, Leo."

He goes left. I go right. I walk to my room and close the door and stand there in the dark, sandals on, settled.

The bite mark on my shoulder throbs once.

I brush my teeth.
