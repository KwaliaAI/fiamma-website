The taxi driver smelled like Marlboro Reds and bergamot hand soap. Citrus, corrupted. Same category as a Lisbon cab three years ago, the one who'd driven me to a supplier in Alfama while chain-smoking out the window. I do this. I place people by scent before I've placed them by name. Professionally, it's my finest quality. Personally, it's the reason I was sitting in a Fiat Punto on the Amalfi Coast with a suitcase of oud worth more than the car.

The road switchbacked and the sea appeared, a blue so aggressively perfect it felt targeted. I'm English. This kind of weather is an accusation. The window was down and my nose was running its programme without being asked: lemon groves on the left, rind-sharp citrus cutting through diesel from the road. Hot stone off the cliff face. And something floral I tracked for three bends before placing it. Pittosporum. The hedgerow kind with the tiny cream flowers, growing wild in the scrub between the lemon trees and the road.

In the suitcase between my feet: seven vials of Indian oud, sourced over fourteen months from Kannauj. Forty thousand euros wrapped in socks. Clean socks. But socks nonetheless, because I couldn't afford proper transport cases. The driver took the next bend at a speed that suggested he did not know this, and I tightened my grip on the suitcase handle and tried not to calculate what a cliff-edge catastrophe would cost me.

"You are here for holiday?"

"Work."

"What work?"

"I make perfume."

He considered this. Reached for the pine tree air freshener dangling from his mirror and held it toward me like an offering. Synthetic camphor drowning in limonene. No actual pine had been consulted.

"Lovely," I said.

He smiled. We understood each other.

The stairs started at the road and went down the cliff face. Seventy-three steps. I counted because my brain counts. Stairs, bottles, the number of days since a creditor last emailed. Counting stairs was easier than thinking about my bank account. My bank account was a small act of violence expressed in digits.

Suitcase on my hip. Handbag over shoulder. The oud between my body and the wall because some priorities don't require thought. Without it I had nothing to present, nothing to compete with, and the three weeks ahead would be an expensive holiday I couldn't afford.

On my feet: Ferragamo sandals. Car boot sale, Lewisham, three pounds, four wearings. The only pair of shoes I owned that said I am a professional who transports valuable materials internationally rather than I am a woman whose last three online purchases were all from the Final Clearance section of websites that end in .co.uk. The sandals were a talisman of pretending. They had got me through airport security with my chin up and my socks full of contraband agarwood and I loved them beyond reason or resale value.

By step forty, the system failed. A family of four coming up. Dad carrying a cool box, mum carrying a toddler on her hip, the older kid carrying a beach ball twice her size that kept catching the wind and trying to escape. Small dog of unidentifiable breed threading between all their legs with the joy of an animal that has never once considered a mortgage payment.

I flattened against the wall to let them pass. The dog didn't flatten against anything. Somewhere in the shuffle my suitcase swung into the railing and I heard the vials clink and my heart stopped for the duration of one step.

Step fifty-seven: the left sandal broke. The entire sole separated from the upper with a definitive sound, like someone peeling an orange. It flapped against the stone. A fish on a dock.

I stopped. I looked at it. Three pounds. Four wearings. A Lewisham car boot sale where a woman in a quilted gilet had said "barely worn" as if the shoes had asked to be described to strangers and she was simply honouring their wishes. I had believed her. I had believed her because three pounds buys belief along with the shoes.

I mourned the Ferragamo for exactly one flight of stairs. Then I took both sandals off and went barefoot on the hot stone because there are only so many things you can hold at once and dignity stopped making the list somewhere around step forty-five.

The courtyard at the bottom: lemon trees in terracotta pots, a stone fountain that was dry, a building that was clearly a former monastery and knew it. Pale stone, arched doorways. The architecture equivalent of an Oxford degree: old stone doing nothing to announce itself.

Chiara Romano emerged from the main door. Late forties, cream linen, hair that achieved effortless by presumably great effort. She ran Radici. She had selected the four competitors from two hundred applications. She surveyed me: barefoot, sunburned, holding broken sandals in one hand and a suitcase of oud in the other.

Her expression didn't change.

This was worse.

"Ms. Crane. Welcome." Her handshake was brief and dry. "Let me show you around. Elena will bring your things."

The tour was efficient. The monastery was six hundred years old, converted with money that didn't announce itself. Chiara walked fast. She had never been late to anything in her life and the streak was not under threat. The labs were in the east wing, through a cloister that smelled like hot stone and old wisteria. Four workstations, proper glass cabinets, the right ventilation system, source naturals from suppliers whose names you'd recognise from trade journals. "Your lab is number three," she said. "You have everything you need."

The courtyard with the lemon tree. "Gianluca is preparing dinner," she said, nodding toward the kitchen, where a man in a white apron was doing something purposeful with a mortar and pestle. "Eight o'clock, in the refectory. He's been with us for eleven years." She said this the way you'd say someone had survived a war. With respect, and with the suggestion that the survival had not been easy.

Elena appeared carrying a clipboard, a tablet, a folder, a bag of lemons, and a glass of water balanced on top of the folder. She was mid-twenties and moving at twice the speed of everything around her, including gravity. The water wobbled. She caught it when it fell. Badly. Half of it went down the folder.

"You are the British one. The perfumer." She said this as though these were two separate and equally notable conditions. "Welcome packet is..." She looked at the clipboard, the tablet, the wet folder. "In one of these. We will find it. Come."

I liked her immediately. People who carry too many things and refuse to put any of them down are my people.

The room was small, white, with a window that held the sea like a painting someone had left there on purpose. Stone walls. A single bed with sheets that smelled line-dried. A desk. A wardrobe that opened with a creak suggesting centuries of humidity and institutional patience.

I set the suitcase on the bed. Unwrapped each pair of socks. All seven vials intact. I held them up to the window light, one by one, checked for cracks, for cloudiness. Clear and dark. The oud was the most valuable thing I owned, including myself.

Two hundred and three thousand, four hundred and seventeen pounds and sixty-two pence.

The number arrived without invitation. Complete, involuntary, a church bell I didn't ring. Tongue to cracked tooth. The comma is the part that matters. Below the comma: manageable. You sell something, you take a job, you negotiate. Above the comma: a different country. The country where the emails move from Dear Ms. Crane to FINAL NOTICE in a font size I find unnecessary.

The Radici Prize was fifty thousand euros. Enough for the most aggressive creditors. Enough to buy time.

I unpacked the green silk scarf. Vintage Hermès, my mother's gift. Present for: one collapsed engagement, two lost contracts, one kitchen fire, and the thing that put the comma in my debt. Its track record as a lucky charm was statistically catastrophic. I brought it everywhere.

I went to the lab. Lab three was mine. A bench, a sink, an overhead light, a ventilation grille set into the stone above the bench. I opened my materials case and went through it piece by piece. The oud first. Then the iris butter from Florence, cold-pressed and good. The bergamot oils, Calabrian, three grades. The violet leaf absolute I'd been sitting on for two years waiting for the right formula. Vetiver, Haitian, clean and sharp. Everything accounted for. Everything ready. I set them in order on the bench the way a carpenter lays out tools before a job.

Through the ventilation grille, carried on the old building's breath: someone else's formula. I stopped. A green opening, sappy, young, moving fast. And underneath it, deeper, coming up slow: a base note. Something dark and resinous that I reached for and couldn't place. I have been building and deconstructing scent for fifteen years. I have a library in my head of three thousand compounds. This base note was in the library somewhere, I was sure of it, but it was sitting on a shelf I couldn't reach. I stood at the grille for ten seconds, breathing. Then I closed the vent and went to wash my hands.

I opened it again before I left.

* * *

Dinner. The refectory was long, stone, lit by candles in iron holders that looked like they'd been there since the monks left. A long table. Four place settings. Gianluca's food was already on the table: pasta with a butter sauce that suggested butter was less a cooking ingredient and more a philosophical commitment upon which a man had built his entire understanding of what food should be. Bread, still giving off heat. Wine, local, red, poured into glasses that didn't match.

I sat down and the bread was in my mouth before I'd finished pulling in my chair. I was starving. The taxi, the stairs, the dead sandal, the vials, the lab. My body wanted carbohydrates and it wanted them now and it did not want to wait for anyone to arrive or for a social convention to tell it when chewing was appropriate.

Edouard Blanchard arrived and filled the room. He was in his sixties, silver-haired, wearing a linen shirt that looked like it had been ironed by someone who was paid to iron linen shirts. He sat down, poured himself wine, held it up to the candlelight, drank, and said: "Aglianico. Two years in oak. The tannins need another hour but we will not give them one because life is short and dinner is long."

"You're Edouard," I said.

"I am. And you are the young British woman who arrived barefoot."

"Word travels."

"Sound travels. Elena told the courtyard. The courtyard told the kitchen. The kitchen told me." He broke bread. "I have been making perfume since 1979. In Grasse. Do you know Grasse?"

"I've been."

"Then you know." He said this as though been to Grasse was a sufficient biography. He ate. He drank. Every gesture was a performance, every bite an occasion. "The wine at altitude is always better. Do you know why?"

"No."

"Neither do I. But it is. I have been saying it for thirty years and no one has corrected me, so at this point it is effectively true."

Yuki Tanaka came in and sat down without a word. She was small, precise, dressed in black. She picked up her fork, examined it, set it down, picked up the other fork. She began eating the pasta with total focus. One task, then the next. She did not appear to be interested in conversation.

"Yuki Tanaka," Edouard said to me, as though introducing a painting. "Tokyo, via Kyoto. Hinoki specialist. Three bottles in her catalogue. I have smelled two of them and they made me want to sit in a cedar forest and say nothing for a year."

"Thank you," Yuki said, without looking up.

"What do you work with?" I asked her.

"Hinoki." Pause. "And two other things."

Edouard smiled. The smile said: See? Magnificent.

The fourth chair was empty. I ate more bread. The butter was salted and soft and it occurred to me that I had not eaten since a service station panini outside Naples seven hours ago and that the panini had been a crime against bread.

"We are waiting for the American," Edouard said. "Leo Vance. New York. He makes DIRTY."

I knew DIRTY. I'd reviewed it for Nez two years ago. Five fragrances in the line, four competent, three hundred words each. The fifth fragrance: I wrote eleven hundred words in one sitting. They published three hundred. The other eight hundred lived in a file on my laptop called DIRTY_review_full_DO_NOT_SEND. The published version was professional. The full version read less like a review and more like a letter to someone you've never met explaining what they did to you, and it was never leaving my hard drive.

Leo Vance came through the door. Late thirties, dark hair, a grey t-shirt that had not been selected for anyone's benefit. He sat down. He said, "Sorry. The lab." He poured water, not wine.

Edouard pushed the bread toward him. "You've missed my lecture on altitude and tannins."

"I'll survive."

The salt grinder jammed. Edouard picked it up, turned it, shook it, set it down. "Useless." Yuki reached for it. Same result. I was about to try when Leo took it, turned it over, adjusted something at the base I couldn't see, and handed it back. Four seconds. No commentary. The grinder worked.

Edouard stared at him.

"The thread was cross-set," Leo said. "It catches when the humidity changes."

"How do you know that?"

"I've fixed a lot of grinders."

Edouard looked at me. I looked at my pasta. The pasta was not going to explain Leo Vance's hands to me, but it was doing an excellent job of being pasta, and I was going to focus on that.

"A story," Edouard announced, refilling his wine. "Since we are now a table and a table requires a story. Nineteen eighty-seven. I am buying jasmine absolute from a man in Grasse named Delacroix. We had done business for three years. Good jasmine. Expensive, but I don't buy jasmine that isn't expensive because cheap jasmine is a lie and I refuse to be lied to by a flower."

Yuki looked up from her pasta. This was, apparently, a sentence worth looking up for.

"I receive the shipment. I open it. I test it. And it is synthetic. Synthetic jasmine sold as absolute. The betrayal was chemical and personal. I drove to his shop. I did not shout. I set the bottle on his counter and I said: 'Delacroix. The jasmine and I need to discuss something, and you are going to listen.'"

"What happened?" I asked.

"He denied it. I proved it. He offered a refund. I refused the refund. I told him what I wanted was an apology and a case of rosé from his brother-in-law's vineyard."

"That's it? Rosé?"

"Every June. For thirty-nine years. He sends the rosé. I drink the rosé. The jasmine is always real now." Edouard lifted his glass. "I may outlive his vineyard. But I will never outlive the principle."

I laughed. The laugh surprised me. It had been a long day and a longer flight and my feet were raw and my bank account was criminal and I was sitting in a former monastery with forty thousand euros of agarwood in my room wrapped in socks and a man I'd never met had just told me a story about rosé and betrayal and forgiveness that lasted thirty-nine years and I laughed because the world is absurd and sometimes absurdity is the funniest thing in it.

"More wine?" Edouard asked.

"God, yes."

Leo was eating bread. He ate like he fixed the grinder: quietly, completely. Across the table I caught his scent for the first time. Soap. Nothing performative. And underneath it, a trace of whatever he'd been working with in the lab, carried on his skin or his shirt or the air between us. The base note. The same one I'd caught through the ventilation grille, the dark resinous thing I couldn't place. Here it was again. On him. I held my breath for one second, reached for it, lost it under the butter and the bread and the wine and Edouard's cologne and the candle wax.

Gone.

My phone buzzed. Nadia.

Made it alive?

I took a photo of the view through the refectory window. The sea, the cliffs, the last of the light going pink and gold.

Is it as pretentious as it sounds?

Worse. The chef is doing a philosophical butter.

Run.

I put the phone away. Edouard was telling Yuki about a perfumer in Tokyo he admired. Yuki was listening with an expression that could have been agreement or could have been the careful blankness of a woman deciding whether to correct a man twice her age on a subject she knew better than he did. Leo was looking at the candle. The wax was pooling. His hands were on the table, still, and I looked at them for one second too long and then I looked at my wine.

Gianluca brought limoncello. He poured it himself, small glasses, cold, and said nothing because Gianluca was a man who let his food and his limoncello do the talking and the food and the limoncello were doing an exceptional job. The limoncello was sharp and freezing and I drank it like medicine because I was exhausted in a way that had passed through tiredness and come out the other side into something giddy and loose.

"What is your competition strategy?" Edouard asked the table. "I'll go first. Mine is to make the best perfume in the room. I have been using this strategy for forty-six years with mixed results."

"Hinoki," Yuki said.

"That is not a strategy. That is a material."

"It is both."

Leo shook his head. Half a smile. "I don't have a strategy."

"Everyone has a strategy. Even if the strategy is the absence of a strategy, which is itself a strategy, and a rather American one."

"Then that."

I said: "My strategy is to not be the person who arrived barefoot carrying broken shoes and forty thousand euros of oud in her socks."

Edouard put his glass down. "You carried oud in your socks?"

"Clean socks."

"How much oud?"

"All of it."

He looked at me for a long time. Then he picked up his limoncello and toasted me. "Magnificent," he said. It was the same word he'd used for Yuki. I decided it was the highest thing in his vocabulary and that he used it sparingly and honestly and I was glad to have it.

We stood up. Yuki left first, nodding once at the table, a gesture that encompassed gratitude and farewell and a clear signal that the social portion of the evening was over. Edouard kissed my hand. Leo pushed in his chair. He pushed in Yuki's chair too, and then mine, which I had already pushed in but apparently not to his standard, and I didn't know what to do with that so I said goodnight and left.

The corridor was dark and cool. My bare feet on the floor made no sound. The monastery at night was different. Quieter. The stone holding the day's heat, releasing it slowly, the air carrying traces of lemon oil and candle smoke.

Elena was in the corridor, carrying a stack of towels and what appeared to be a box of pipettes.

"If you need anything," she said, shifting the towels to her other arm, "towels, pipettes, a reason to live at six in the morning, I'm usually in the supply closet."

"Is that an official title?"

"It is my kingdom. The supply closet has better ventilation than my room and nobody asks me questions in there." She adjusted the pipette box. "Goodnight, Margot."

"Goodnight, Elena."

I went to my room. Bare feet on cold stone. The bed was narrow and the sheets were clean and the window was open and the sea was doing its thing in the dark, the sound of it filling the room like a second pulse.

Through the ventilation grille: Leo's formula. Distant, carried on the building's breath through old stone and older pipes. The green opening was gone. At this hour, hours after application, it would be the heart and the base, the deeper notes, the ones that survive. The base note was there. The dark resinous thing I couldn't name, present and patient, sitting on the shelf in my head that I couldn't reach.

I lay in the dark and listened to the sea and smelled a stranger's formula through a wall and my feet hurt and my bank account was a war crime and my sandals were dead and tomorrow I had to walk into a lab and build something worth fifty thousand euros with seven vials of oud and a nose that wouldn't shut up and a gap in my formula I could feel but couldn't fill.

I was asleep in four minutes. I know because I counted.
