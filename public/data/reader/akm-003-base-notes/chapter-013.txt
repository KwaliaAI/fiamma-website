V4 is done.

I hold the strip at arm's length and the formula opens exactly where I built it to open. A doorway you walk through and the room on the other side is bigger than the room you left. The vetiver holds the floor, the bergamot cedes at the right second, and in between there's a silence that smells like standing very close to someone in the dark.

I cap the vial. My hands are shaking.

My phone buzzes. Nadia.

"Ferranti's solicitor sent a follow-up. Fourteen days."

"Fourteen days until what?"

"Until they escalate to enforcement. Which means—"

"I know what it means."

"Do you? Because you're in Italy making perfume and I'm in Bermondsey making spreadsheets and one of us should be panicking."

"I'm panicking elegantly. You can't see it because it's a phone call."

"Margot. Two hundred and three thousand, four hundred and seventeen pounds. And sixty-two pence."

"The sixty-two pence is the part that really stings."

"I've deferred my salary again. I need you to know that."

"Nadia—"

"Not for sympathy. For the record. When you win this thing, my holiday to the Algarve comes first. Before rent. Before creditors. Before God."

"Before God."

"I'll send you the letter. Don't read it before bed."

She hangs up. I read it immediately. The language is surgical. _We write to inform you that in the absence of a satisfactory arrangement within fourteen calendar days from the date of this correspondence, our client intends to pursue all available remedies._

I put the phone face-down on the bench.

V4 is done. V4 is the best thing I've ever made. V4 doesn't pay Ferranti Capital.

* * *

The courtyard is doing that thing where the light hits the lemon tree and everything looks like a painting you can't afford. I'm sitting on the low wall with my sandals off because one of the Primark straps has given up entirely and I respect its decision.

Leo comes out with two cups. He hands me one.

"Water?"

"Lemon water. Elena's making everyone drink it. Something about electrolytes."

"She's running a wellness retreat now?"

"She's running everything. Always has been."

He sits. The cup is warm. There's an actual slice of lemon in it, which feels aggressive.

"V4's done," I say.

"Yeah?"

"It's good. It might be the best thing I've ever made."

"That's a big claim."

"I know."

He drinks. I drink. The lemon tree drops a leaf onto the flagstones and neither of us mentions it because we are adults.

"You look like someone just told you your dog died," he says.

"I don't have a dog."

"That's probably why you look so sad about it."

I laugh. It comes out wrong. Too much air in it.

He waits.

This is the thing about Leo since the lab, since the stool, since yesterday morning and Daniel Reeves and the vial. He waits. He doesn't fill the silence with questions or reassurance or any of the things that make it easy to deflect. He just sits there with his lemon water and his patience and eventually you hear yourself talking.

"I owe two hundred and three thousand pounds."

He doesn't move.

"Four hundred and seventeen. And sixty-two pence."

"To who?"

"Ferranti Capital. Most of it. Martins and Hogg for the rest."

"What happened?"

"Oud happened."

He turns his cup in his hands. "Tell me."

"You know what Kannauj oud costs."

"I know what good Kannauj oud costs."

"This was extraordinary Kannauj oud. Fourteen months sourcing. Three trips. A distiller named Pradip who wouldn't sell to anyone he hadn't met in person, and I don't blame him because the last European buyer tried to pass off synthetic as his product and he nearly lost his reputation."

"How much?"

"Forty thousand euros."

He breathes out. Slow.

"And I didn't have forty thousand euros."

"Who does."

"Nadia did. Some of it. She'd been saving for a flat. A two-bedroom in the Algarve. Sea view, terracotta, the works. She'd had the listing saved on her phone for a year."

"You borrowed from Nadia."

"She offered. I said no. She transferred it. I said I'd pay it back in six months because the oud was going to anchor a collection and the collection was going to sell and I believed that the way you believe in weather. Completely. Stupidly."

"And it didn't sell."

"The collection was brilliant. The timing was catastrophic. The distributor collapsed. Three months of stock sitting in a warehouse in Rotterdam while lawyers argued about insolvency provisions. By the time I got the product back, the window had closed and I'd borrowed more to cover operating costs."

Leo reached across and took my cup. Sniffed it. Put it back.

"The lemon slice is dead," he said.

"It's been dead since Elena squeezed it."

"You're still drinking it."

"I'm British. We drink things after they've died. It's cultural."

He almost smiled. The left side of his mouth. I kept going.

"How much more?"

"All of it. All the more. Every kind of more. Credit. A business loan from Ferranti at an interest rate that should be illegal and probably is in some countries, just not the one I live in."

"And the fiancé?"

I look at him. "Who told you about the fiancé?"

"You mentioned him. Day three. Passing reference. You said 'before the fiancé' like it was a geological era."

"It was."

"What happened?"

"He wanted me to sell the oud."

Leo goes still.

"To cover the debt. Liquidate the asset, pay down the balance, start fresh. Very sensible. Very reasonable. He had a spreadsheet."

"He made a spreadsheet about your oud."

"He made spreadsheets about everything. Our holiday budget. Our grocery rotation. He had a tab for condiments."

"Margot."

"He wasn't wrong about the maths. Selling the oud would have cleared about a third of the debt. But the oud wasn't maths."

"No."

"The oud was mine. The work was mine. The debt was a fact but the work was who I am. And he said, 'You're choosing a smell over our future,' and I said, 'Yes,' because I was angry enough to be honest, and three weeks later he was gone."

"He left over the oud?"

"He left over all of it. The oud was the last thing, but there were plenty of things before it. I think he'd been leaving for a while and the oud gave him a reason that sounded noble. _She wouldn't compromise. She wouldn't be practical. I tried._"

A gust came through the courtyard and the lemon tree dropped three leaves at once, a coordinated resignation. One of them landed on Leo's knee. He picked it up, turned it over, set it on the wall between us. He didn't throw it away. He just put it somewhere.

"Did you love him?" he said.

The question landed harder than I expected.

"I loved who I was when I was with him. That's different. I didn't figure out the difference until he was gone."

"Where'd you go?"

"Nadia's sofa. For a month. Then Unit 14B."

"What's Unit 14B?"

"Storage unit. Ground floor. Bermondsey. Forty-seven pounds a month."

"A storage unit."

"Concrete floor. No windows. One overhead light that buzzed at a frequency that I'm fairly certain was designed to induce madness. Damp in the corners. I put my materials on shelving from Argos and slept on a camping mat."

"For how long?"

"Three months."

"Three months in a storage unit."

"I rebuilt my entire materials library in that unit. Every accord. Every base. Every reference strip. I cut my knee on a metal shelf the second week and it got infected because the nearest A&E was forty minutes on the bus and I kept telling myself it would heal on its own."

"Show me," he said. But when I pulled up the trouser leg and he saw the scar, his jaw set. A muscle at the hinge.

"Three months," he said. "In a storage unit."

"It had electricity. And a toilet. In the cafe next door."

"Margot."

"I know."

He was angry. At the situation, at the storage unit, at the infected knee, at whatever combination of institutional failure and personal stubbornness puts a person with a £200,000 nose on a camping mat in Bermondsey. I could see it in the way he held his cup, knuckles pale, and he didn't try to hide it.

"And Nadia?"

"Nadia hasn't taken a holiday in two years. The money that should be her holiday is covering my minimum payments to Ferranti. She deferred her salary again this month. She's looking at a second job. She won't say it, but she's exhausted, and every month I don't win or sell or land something big enough to clear the balance is another month she loses."

"That's a lot to carry."

"The sixty-two pence is the worst part. If it were a round number I could abstract it. But sixty-two pence means someone calculated it to the penny and that means it's real."

"It's real either way."

"I know. But round numbers feel like stories. Specific numbers feel like invoices."

He's quiet for a long time. The lemon tree does its thing. Somewhere inside, Elena's clipboard hits a table.

"I built the best thing I've ever made around you," I say. "And I can't unbuild it."

He reaches across the space between us. His hand closes over mine. I squeeze. He squeezes back.

"I'm not going to use it against you," he says.

"I know. That's the problem."

* * *

Paola's trattoria is loud tonight. Edouard is arguing with Yuki about whether fennel belongs in a fish broth and Yuki is winning by saying nothing.

"Fennel is a gift," Edouard says.

"Fennel is a crutch," Yuki says.

"You wound me."

"Good."

I leave them to it and take my wine to the bar. Luca is polishing a glass, thinking about something else entirely.

"Can I buy you a drink?"

"You're drinking my aunt's wine in my aunt's restaurant. With my aunt's money, essentially."

"Technically it's the competition's money. They're feeding us."

"Technically." He puts the glass down. Picks up another. Same polish. Same nowhere stare.

"How's your mum?"

His hand stops. "She's in Sorrento. The hospital."

"I know. Yuki mentioned it."

"Paola won't let me go. We're short-staffed and the competition is—" He gestures at the room. "She says she needs me here."

"What do you say?"

"I say my mother is in a hospital and I'm polishing glasses."

His voice is flat. Twenty-five and sounding fifty. I sit on the bar stool next to the till.

"What's your mum's name?"

"Giulia."

"Nice name."

"She'd like you. She likes difficult women."

"I'm going to take that as a compliment."

"It is."

Paola comes out of the kitchen. She sees us at the bar. For a second her face does something complicated and private and then it goes back to Paola. She puts two glasses of something dark and sweet on the bar between us. She doesn't say a word. She goes back to the kitchen.

Luca stares at the glasses.

"She makes that with walnuts," he says. "She only brings it out when she's sorry."

We drink. It tastes like Christmas in someone else's house. The good kind.

At the table, Paola is telling a story. "A man came in last year and told me the fish was overcooked. I asked him to leave. He comes to the door sometimes. I wave. He knows."

Edouard raises his glass. "You are a benevolent dictator."

"I am a woman with a kitchen. Same thing."

* * *

The lane between the trattoria and the lift is dark. The streetlamp at the corner is out again, or still, or forever.

The others are ahead. Elena's clipboard catches the moonlight. Edouard is humming something French and probably inappropriate.

Leo's hand finds mine. His fingers lace through and hold. His palm is dry and calloused at the base of the thumb where he grips pipettes.

We walk. Fifteen steps. Twenty. His thumb moves once across my knuckle.

The lift light spills into the lane. He lets go before anyone turns around.

* * *

His room is smaller than mine. Monastery cell. Single bed pushed against the wall, window open to the sea, and his materials case on the floor by the nightstand, every vial upright in foam slots, labelled in his handwriting, which is neater than mine. Neater than most people's.

"Your materials are neater than mine."

"Everything is neater than yours."

"That's rude."

"You left a pipette cap in the courtyard."

"I did not."

"Under the lemon tree. Tuesday."

"Maybe it was Edouard's."

"Edouard has never dropped anything in his life. Except two marriages."

I throw a pillow at him. He catches it, one-handed. Puts it back on the bed. Straightens it. Because of course he does.

"You're insufferable."

"You're in my room."

"I can leave."

"You can."

He's standing by the window. I walk to him and I put my hands on his chest and I look at him.

"I don't want to leave."

"I know."

I unbutton his shirt. One button. Two. He stands still and lets me. The fabric falls open. There's a tattoo on his ribs, left side, just below the heart. A molecular structure. Precise black lines.

"What is it?"

"Calone. First synthetic I loved."

"You tattooed a molecule on your body."

"You carry forty thousand euros of oud in your socks."

"Fair."

I kiss the tattoo. His breath catches. I feel the catch against my mouth, the ribs expanding under my lips.

He unzips my dress. It's the green one, the one with the broken zip that only works if you pull it at an angle, and he gets it on the first try, which means he was either watching when I got dressed or he's done this before, and I don't ask which because his hands are on my waist and his mouth is on my neck and the question doesn't matter.

We get to the bed. It's narrow. Absurdly narrow. He pulls me down and his foot catches in the sheet and he kicks it off and the kick sends his shoe across the floor and it hits the materials case and we both freeze because if a single vial is broken we will never forgive the shoe. Three seconds of silence. The case is fine. We laugh. His forehead against mine, both of us shaking with it.

"If you'd broken the calone—"

"I would've deserved it."

"You would've deserved worse."

He kisses me. Slow. His hand on my face, thumb at my jaw. Mine on his, fingers at his temple where his hair is starting to grey.

Face to face on the narrow bed. Slow. Eyes open.

I smell everything. His soap. Something clean and woody. The base note still on his skin from the morning's work. Formula residue in the creases of his fingers. Old stone from the walls. The sea through the open window. My brain files it all, categorises, cross-references, the way it always does, the automatic taxonomy that never stops. But my body is here too. His weight against mine. The sound he makes against my throat. And for the first time the filing system and the body run at the same time. They don't compete. The perfumer and the woman coexist. I don't choose.

Every point of contact is deliberate. His hand on my hip. My hand on his ribs. The tattoo under my palm. The scar on my knee against the sheet.

He moves slowly. We're close. His eyes are open and so are mine and I've never found it harder to hold someone's gaze and I don't look away.

I come while looking at his face. He follows immediately, with a sound like someone saying something important very quietly.

After. The bed is too narrow for two people and we're in it anyway, my head on his chest, my leg thrown over his because geometry demands compromise.

"I built the best thing I've ever made around you."

"I know."

"Is that a problem?"

"Yes." A beat. "I don't care."

Through the wall, faint and unmistakable: my formula. Drifting through the ventilation grille from Lab 3 into his room. Two labs breathing into each other all night.

His hand is in my hair. My hand is on the calone tattoo.

I close my eyes.
