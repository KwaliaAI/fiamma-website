6:43 a.m. The porch.

I had been up since six and the kitchen had not yet warmed to the hour. The coffeemaker, which was a Mr. Coffee from a decade I did not want to assign, had produced a pot of the wrong drip-coffee with the kind of confidence that machines have about things they are bad at. I had poured it into a Libbey mug with a chip on the rim and brought it out to the porch and sat in the rocking chair the previous renter had put there, and the smell of the coffee was the smell of a gas-station vestibule in November.

I drank it anyway.

The screen had a tear at the bottom corner mended with what I had decided yesterday was surgical tape and was today, I could see in better light, plain electrical. The wind was on Bellevue at six in the morning the way the wind is on a coast at the back end of August, which is to say it remembered being a different wind in March and did not want to commit. The screen ticked. The screen ticked again. Between ticks the magnolia hedge made its own small sound, which was a sound I did not have a name for, and which I had spent the first cup of bad coffee deciding I was not going to find a name for.

The porch table had three things on it. A magazine from June with a brown ring on its cover where some renter had put a cup of tea. Two cold slices of pizza on a paper plate, the pizza I had not eaten last night and which now sat under cling film with the cling film failing at the edges. And a racquet.

The racquet was not mine. Mine was on the floor beside the chair in its bag.

The racquet on the table was a Babolat in a black overgrip, frayed at the butt-cap, the strings unstrung. The strings were not entirely unstrung. They were strung, slack, the way an instrument is strung when somebody has been twisting the pegs without intending to play. The gut was the colour of an old piano key. I knew the gut. The gut was his.

I picked it up.

The smell came up off the gut the way the smell comes up off a man's old jersey when you find it in a drawer in a house you used to live in. Locker rooms in my twenties. The Bollettieri court six in 2004. The hot string-machine room at Wimbledon qualifying in 2008 where the Frenchman who did my match racquets used to put a cigarette out in a tin lid on the windowsill and pretend, when asked, that he had not. The gut smelled, specifically, of all of that, and the porch in Newport at 6:51 a.m. on a Thursday in August had no business smelling of any of it.

I set my mug down on the table next to the pizza. The mug's chip caught my thumb. I drank around the chip.

Upstairs, water ran. Then it stopped. Then a floorboard.

He came down at 7:08 a.m. with his own mug. I knew it was 7:08 because I had looked at my phone when I heard the second-from-top stair, the one that did not creak, and I had counted the creak of the lower five against the digital reading on the lock screen. I knew this about myself. I let it pass.

He saw the gut in my hand before he saw my face. His own face did a thing. The thing his face did was not a flinch and was not a smile and was not anything I had a word for, which is the thing his face had always done in the year I had known him in 2011 when I caught him at something he had not meant to be caught at, like sitting on the changeover bench at 4 all in a tiebreak and humming the chorus of a Hall and Oates song.

"Morning," he said.

"This was on the table."

"I know it was on the table."

"Whose."

"You know whose."

"Yes."

He pushed open the screen door and came onto the porch in socks. The screen door's broken hinge made its small clicking sound. He set his mug down on the table beside mine. His mug was the matching Libbey, also restaurant-stock, also chipped, the chip on his on the opposite side from mine the way two coins out of the same roll wear differently. He sat in the wicker chair across from the rocker. The wicker chair gave one beat of complaint and rested.

I held the racquet up by the throat. The gut sagged where my finger pressed.

"Fifty-six," I said.

"I have not strung it. That is why it is slack."

"It is your backup."

"My third backup. The string broke at the academy on the eighteenth and I have not got around to it."

"It would string at fifty-two."

"It would."

"You travel with three polyester match racquets and a fourth that is your gut backup."

"I travel with four match racquets, three at fifty-two polyester and one at fifty-six gut. The gut is the racquet I would use if my polyester strings broke and the stringer was busy. The gut is also the racquet I bring when I am being honest with myself about the fact that I learned the game on gut."

"Mm."

"Mm."

I put the racquet back on the table. My bare feet were on the floorboards and the floorboards at the back of the porch were cold from the wind through the screen and my feet registered the cold the way feet register the cold, which is to say my whole spine registered it. I tucked my left foot under my right thigh.

He saw the tuck. He looked away from the tuck. He took a sip of his coffee and made the face. The face was the face of a man making peace with the fact that the coffee was not coffee, and the face was a face I had seen him make in 2011 at a Holiday Inn Express in Flushing, and I did not know what to do with the recognition of the face, which had not changed when the rest of him had changed, and which was therefore an object that had survived the fifteen years without my permission.

"You strung your gut at fifty-six in 2011," he said.

"I did."

"You have strung your gut at fifty-six since."

"Yes."

"Why."

"Why what."

"Why fifty-six and not fifty-four."

"Because I can feel the ball."

"You can feel the ball at fifty-four."

"I can feel the ball at fifty-six the way I want to feel the ball. At fifty-four the ball feels like a ball that has been negotiated with. I am not interested in negotiating with the ball."

"You are interested in negotiating with the ball. You just want the ball to come to the table with a smaller offer."

"That is a difference that matters."

"That is a difference of vocabulary."

"Then we are arguing vocabulary."

"We are arguing vocabulary."

"Fine."

"Fine."

He took another sip. I watched his hand on the mug. The hand was the hand from yesterday, and the hand from the Subaru, and the hand from the airport, and the hand from 2011, and the hand had not changed and would not change, and his thumb sat on the rim of the mug the way it had always sat on the rim of a mug, which is to say flat, not curled. His ring finger had a small white scar across the knuckle I had not noticed before. The scar was new in the sense that it had not been there in 2011. The scar was old in the sense that it was healed and faded and probably from a forehand-drill collision with a fence-post in 2014, which was the kind of injury he had collected on the doubles tour the way other people collect parking tickets.

I set my mug down.

"You are looking at my hand," he said.

"I am."

"You can stop."

"I cannot, but go on."

He laughed once. It was the short laugh. The laugh that had paid the deposit on the deck I had not built. I did not look at him while he laughed. I looked at the magnolia hedge through the screen, where a single porch-light moth from the night before had survived into the morning, against the rules, on the inside of the screen, alive but slow.

The moth tapped the screen. The screen ticked. The wind kept its old promises.

"Why fifty-two," I said.

"Because my shoulder."

"Your shoulder."

"My shoulder makes its decisions for me. I am forty-four. Polyester at fifty-two does less work to the shoulder than gut at fifty-six. Polyester at fifty-two does less work to the elbow than polyester at fifty-six. Polyester at fifty-two lasts twice as long as gut at fifty-six, which means I restring half as often, which means the academy pays half the stringer's bill on the racquets that go out the door to my fourteen-year-olds whose parents have already paid for the lessons and would like to feel that the lessons were the expensive part."

"You are running an academy."

"I am running an academy."

"At forty-four."

"At forty-four."

"You did not used to talk about elbow longevity in 2011."

"I had two arms in 2011."

"You have two arms now."

"I have one arm now that has, for fifteen years, hit ten thousand more forehands than the other arm. The right arm and the left arm in this body are not the arms of one man anymore. They are the arms of one man and one indentured servant. The polyester is the negotiation with the indentured servant."

"That metaphor is doing too much."

"I am aware. I have not had enough of this coffee."

"The coffee is what it is."

"The coffee is a war crime."

"It is."

"Tess."

"Mm."

"Gut at fifty-six in late August in Newport on grass is the wrong tension."

"I know."

"You know."

"I have known."

"Then why."

I looked at him. He was sitting forward in the wicker chair with his elbows on his knees and his mug in both hands and his face was the face of a man who had been arguing this argument since he was twenty-nine years old and had finally, at forty-four, decided to argue it through to the end, and the face was therefore not, on first read, the face of a man arguing about strings, and I, who am paid eight hundred dollars an hour to read faces, registered this and decided not to address it.

"Because I am forty-one," I said, "and the body that hit gut at fifty-six in 2010 is the body the brain still believes in, and the brain is wrong, and the brain knows it is wrong, and the brain has a vote anyway."

"That is honest."

"I have not had enough of this coffee either."

"The argument is."

"Stupid."

"The argument is stupid."

"We are still arguing it."

"We are still arguing it."

The screen ticked. The moth tapped. The slice of pizza under the failing cling film looked at me with the kind of indifference that cold pepperoni has at seven in the morning. I considered eating it. I did not eat it. I picked up the unrestrung gut again instead, the way a person picks up a thing they are not done with, and I turned it on its head and rested the butt on the floorboards between my bare feet, and I let the strings, slack as they were, sag toward me. The hand. The hand was always doing the work first. My right hand on the throat of his backup racquet, on his porch, in his rental, in his August.

I gave the racquet a quarter-turn. I did not say what the quarter-turn meant. He did not ask.

I put the racquet back on the table.

"I am going to get more coffee," I said. "Of the bad kind."

"I will join you in a minute."

"Mm."

I left the porch.

7:34 a.m. The kitchen.

She had gone in for more coffee and had not come back. I stayed on the porch for two minutes with my mug and the bad coffee and the magnolia hedge and the moth which had, in the last ninety seconds, decided to die against the inside of the screen with what I thought was unnecessary commitment to a bit. I poured the rest of my coffee onto the porch floorboards through the gap at the edge of the screen, which is a thing I had done in 2002 in a hotel in Stuttgart and had not, between 2002 and this morning, done again. I came inside.

The kitchen was cold the way the kitchen had been cold in Stuttgart, which is to say it was not cold and I had brought the cold in from the porch in my skin and was, momentarily, surprised by the kitchen for being a room and not a porch. She was at the counter at the Mr. Coffee. The Mr. Coffee was wheezing toward a second pot. She had her hand flat on the yellow tile beside the burner. The hand at the yellow tile was the hand from yesterday on the steering wheel and the hand from the deli counter and the hand from the changeover-bench yesterday at four-thirty.

I went to the cupboard above the sink and got a glass and ran the tap on the cold side, which I now turned on without thinking about it, and which had stopped being stiff in the night, which is what the cold side does. I filled the glass. I drank the water. I did not look at her left hand on the yellow tile. I had been not-looking at her left hand on the yellow tile for forty-two seconds when she spoke.

"I am sorry I was sharp."

"You were not sharp."

"I was sharp about your elbow."

"You were honest about my elbow. The honesty is not sharpness."

"I know the difference. I am still sorry."

"Noted."

"Jude."

"Mm."

"You have not been honest about your elbow."

I did not say anything. The Mr. Coffee did its slow honest grind. She turned to face me with her back to the burner and her hands behind her on the yellow tile and her bare feet on the kitchen floor and the kitchen floor was linoleum that had been laid in 1973 and you could see, under the pattern, the ghost of a different pattern from a linoleum that had been laid in 1962. The two patterns made a third pattern that I had been looking at for ten minutes the previous night before bed because the third pattern was the pattern the room actually had. I looked at the third pattern now.

"Show me your right shoulder," she said.

"What."

"Show me your right shoulder. Lift the sleeve."

"Tess."

"Lift the sleeve. It will not bite."

I lifted the sleeve. The shoulder had ten thousand serves in it and was admitting nine thousand of them. The deltoid had gone, in the last six years, from the deltoid of a man who hit a hundred and forty serves an hour for twenty years to the deltoid of a man who coaches a hundred and forty serves an hour of fourteen-year-olds. There was a faint surgical line at the top of the front delt from the labrum repair in 2019 that I had been told would be fine if I quit serving and which I had not, since 2019, served competitively until last week. There was a knob at the back, a knot the trainer in San Diego pretended not to see and treated quietly with the dry-needling I pretended not to schedule.

She looked at the shoulder. She did not touch it.

"That is the shoulder," she said.

"That is the shoulder."

"The shoulder is the reason."

"The shoulder is a reason."

"The other reason."

"The other reason is the academy and the elbow and the bills and the math."

"The other reason."

"Tess."

"The other reason is that you have spent fifteen years not letting yourself want the things gut at fifty-six lets you want."

I let the sleeve down.

The Mr. Coffee finished its wheeze. The carafe was full. She did not move. I did not move. The hallway light upstairs was off because we were both down here and the morning light off Bellevue was coming in through the kitchen window at the angle that comes in at the angle in August at seven thirty-six and the angle hit the yellow tile and made the yellow tile look briefly like the yellow of an egg yolk and then briefly like the yellow of a wasp and then went back to the yellow it had been since 1962.

"That is a reach," I said.

"It is."

"It is also not nothing."

"No."

"Tess."

"I am not asking you to restring at fifty-six."

"I know."

"I am not even asking you to restring at fifty-four."

"I know."

"I am pointing at the shoulder. I am a person who points at the shoulder. The pointing is what I do."

"I know."

"I will stop now."

"You will not."

"I will stop pointing at the shoulder. I will not stop pointing."

"Right."

The carafe sat. Neither of us reached for it. She had her hands behind her on the tile and I had my hands on the rim of the sink behind me. The half-bath door was three feet from her right hip, ajar an inch, the way she had left it last night when she came down for water. The third pattern on the floor was the pattern of two linoleums that had been put down by two different people in two different decades to make a kitchen that was somebody else's idea of what a kitchen on Bellevue should be. I had been looking at it for ten minutes the night before because it was a pattern that admitted the work it had taken to make it. The shoulder was a pattern. The polyester was a pattern. The fifty-two was a pattern. She was, I thought, looking at the third pattern in me, and her question about the shoulder was the question of someone who had been looking at it.

"Coffee," I said.

"Yes."

I poured. I poured hers first because she had got up first and made it. I handed it to her. Her fingers came around the mug and brushed mine for a half-second on the handle and I registered the brush and registered myself registering it and the kitchen, which was small, did not get any smaller.

"Thanks."

"Mm."

We drank the second cups standing up at the counter. The coffee did not improve on the second cup. We did not improve on the second cup. We did, between the second sip and the third, manage to talk about the bagel place, and the time it opened (7:30), and the bagel I would order (an everything, plain cream cheese, no tomato, which she registered without comment), and the bagel she would order (a sesame, cream cheese, tomato, which I registered with a face), and the walk over (eight blocks, all flat), and the fact that we had a rest day, the kind of day a doubles team is supposed to use to rest, and the fact that neither of us was going to rest.

"Walk," she said.

"Walk."

I went upstairs to put on shoes. I did not look at the wood of the doorframe. I had been not-looking at the wood of the doorframe for thirty-six hours now and the not-looking was, in the way of not-looking, work.

9:42 a.m. Bellevue, walking back.

We walked. The bagel place was a deli with a different paper-hat woman behind the counter from the sandwich deli of Tuesday noon, and the bagels were what bagels are in New England in August, which is to say loyal but not Manhattan, and we ate them on a bench three blocks from the Casino and watched a man in salmon shorts try to parallel-park a Mercedes G-Wagen and fail at it twice. Jude did not say anything about the parking. I did not say anything about the parking. We watched. The man in the salmon shorts saw us watching at the second failure and we both, in a small synchronised gesture neither of us discussed, looked at our bagels.

The walk back was along Bellevue under the magnolias that were past peak and into the bend where the houses changed from rented Capes to summer manors and back again. The wind had stayed honest. The Atlantic, at this hour, was not visible from Bellevue but was audible as a low constant against the wind, the way an old refrigerator is audible in a house once you have noticed it.

"You were quiet at the bench," he said.

"Mm."

"Mm."

"The man in the salmon shorts had a wife in the passenger seat."

"He did."

"She was on her phone."

"She was on her phone."

"He failed at the parking and she did not look up."

"She did not."

"That is a marriage."

"That is a fifteen-year marriage."

"Right."

At Pelham Street he stopped. He did not turn. He had his hand on the lamppost for no reason a lamppost needs a hand and he stopped and stood with his back three-quarters to me. A car came past. The car was a Volvo wagon. The Volvo wagon went up Pelham toward the water and was gone.

"You looked at the bridge in Brooklyn the night you did not show up at dinner," he said.

"Jude."

"You looked the way you looked this morning when I came onto the porch and you were holding the gut."

I did not say anything.

"That is the only thing I am saying about it," he said.

"Right."

"I am not raising it."

"You are raising it."

"I am noting it."

"That is raising it."

"Then I am raising it. I am raising it once. The raise stands. It does not require a response."

He took his hand off the lamppost. He walked. I walked. The porch screen of the Cape four blocks up came into view at the end of the next block.

We reached the Cape at 9:58 a.m. He went up the porch steps first and held the screen door open with his right hand. I went past him under his arm. The screen ticked closed behind us. The unrestrung gut racquet was still on the porch table where I had left it. The cling film over the pizza had failed at one more edge. My mug from this morning was still on the table beside his.

He stood in the doorway between the porch and the kitchen. One hand on the frame. The same frame he had had his hand on Tuesday at noon when the landlord had given us the key on the wooden tag. I sat back down in the rocking chair. I did not look at his hand on the frame.

The chair gave one beat of complaint and rested.

The slack gut racquet sat on the table. The strings sagged in the middle, the way I had left them. The mug rim on my mug had its chip catching the angle of the light through the screen.

Neither of us moved.

The screen ticked once.

The moth on the inside of the screen had stopped tapping at some point in the last forty minutes and was now, I could see, dead at the bottom corner against the electrical-taped tear, having committed entirely to the bit.
