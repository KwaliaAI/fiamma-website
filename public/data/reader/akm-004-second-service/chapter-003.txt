8:02 a.m. Practice Court 4.

The grass at eight in the morning at the Hall of Fame had a smell I had forgotten in the way I had forgotten the hand. Cut at six. Watered at five-thirty. The wet still on the seam of my shoe when I stepped onto the surface and the wet went into the canvas at the toe in the small dark stripe wet does in canvas.

"Serves first," he said.

"Mm."

"Then returns. Then two-up two-back. Ninety minutes. I want to be off court at nine-thirty so we have the locker before they push us out."

"Yes."

He fed me a basket of practice serves from the deuce side. His tossing arm went up at the same speed every time, the kind of speed a man develops who has fed twelve thousand baskets of serves to teenagers in San Diego. I stood at the T and put the ball where I had been putting the ball since 2003. Wide on the deuce. Down the middle on the ad. The body and the hand. The hand did the work. The body was the meeting the hand had to attend.

I missed two of the first ten. The third missed wide and I said *come on* under my breath in the way you say it when you are coaching yourself and the coach is twenty-six and not patient. Jude did not say anything. He tossed the next ball. I put it on the line.

We hit returns at eight-twenty-six. The intern on the umpire chair was a different intern from yesterday, a girl with a clipboard and a bottle of green juice on the floor of the chair's footrest. The green juice was the cold-pressed kind in the glass bottle that costs eleven dollars at the Whole Foods in San Diego and which I had no theory about how a tournament intern in Newport had acquired. She was reading the broadcaster's call sheet and not watching the practice. I appreciated it.

Jude's first-serve toss at eight-thirty-two was an inch further out in front than the one at eight-oh-seven. I noticed it on the second serve of the game and not on the first. By the third game his toss had walked back. The body of a forty-four-year-old man takes ten minutes to remember it has a body in the morning. The body of a forty-one-year-old woman, who is on the second day of a six-day tournament and the seventh year of a sabbatical, takes longer than that. I did not mention the toss. He would not have wanted me to. The not-mentioning was the morning practice's actual practice.

Two-up two-back at eight-fifty. He poached on my third deep return and the ball came back so flat into the deuce-court alley I had to laugh, short, into my own elbow. He gave me the look I remembered from the Pell Bridge yesterday, the look that meant a man had pleased himself and would rather not say so.

"Coffee," I said, after.

"Coffee is food on Wednesdays. Sandwich at noon."

"Coffee is the rumour of food."

"Coffee at the lounge is the rumour of the rumour of food."

"Are you going to feed me."

"Sandwich at noon."

"Right."

We came off the grass at nine-thirty-one. The intern shut her clipboard and did not look up. The grass behind us at the baseline had two long brown scuffs in it, his and mine, where the foot lands at the second-serve return. The groundsman would brush them out by ten.

I had used, I counted while we walked off the court, six sentences this morning. Five of them were words like *coffee* and *yes*. The sixth was *come on* and I had said it to my own elbow. The arithmetic pleased me. I had been on planes for fifteen years with men who needed to fill silence; tennis silence was the silence I had moved to California to remember.

11:43 a.m. The Cape. I lay flat on the bedroom floor with my feet up on the bedframe for ten minutes the way the physio in San Francisco had told me to and listened to Jude downstairs not talking on the phone to anybody. The Cape's bedroom floor was pine and had been finished once in polyurethane and then never again, the kind of floor that had absorbed three decades of a different family's bare feet before some grandchild put it on a rental site. The pine had a knot near my right hip. I could feel the knot through the rug, which was thin, the kind of rug a landlord buys at HomeGoods on the way to the property.

The body had its memory of the morning. It would have a memory of the afternoon. The right hip held. I had been told in 2017 it would hold and I had not believed the man telling me and the man had been correct, which was a thing I tried to remember when I was being told things in 2026 by men of the same age and the same calm.

I got up at 11:54 and went downstairs.

Jude was at the kitchen table with the racquet bag open and a small white towel folded into thirds beside it. He had the polyester match racquet across his thighs and was wiping down the throat with the towel. He did this the way he did the breakfast sandwich, the way I had seen him do everything in two days, without commentary. The towel went over the throat. The towel went over the grip. The towel went back into the third-fold. The racquet went into the bag.

"Sandwich," he said.

"You're going to say the same order."

"Same place. Same order. We have committed to a Newport deli on the strength of two sandwiches."

"That is how loyalties form."

"Tomato again."

"Don't let them put the onion on mine."

"Mm."

"Tess."

"Jude."

We walked the three blocks. The woman in the paper hat was the same woman in the same paper hat. She did not say hello and we did not say hello. He paid. I let him. He registered me letting him, and pretended not to, and counted on me not to mention it. We had done the same arithmetic at the porch table yesterday.

On the sidewalk table I watched him fold the wax paper into a rectangle when he was done.

"Hand," I said, before I could not say it.

"Mm."

"Your hand on the throat of the racquet when you wipe it. You did the same thing in 2011 in the locker before the Hungarians."

"I was warming up the grip."

"You were not. You were doing the thing you do when you do not know what to do with your hand."

"And you were watching."

"I watched you in 2011 too. This is older than you'd like."

"Is it."

"By a margin."

"Eat your chip."

He ate his chip. The chips today were the same chips as yesterday. He did not push me on the hand and I did not push him on the not-pushing. Doubles is a relationship. The hand was a doubles relationship. The hand had said good morning to me at twelve on the steering wheel and the hand was, at lunch on Wednesday, the hand it had been, plus fifteen years.

I let the noticing go. It did not let me go, but it could move into a back room for the afternoon. I had a 2 p.m. match. The back room would do the work the front room could not.

12:42 p.m. We walked back to the Cape. He changed upstairs. I changed in the downstairs bedroom I had not slept in. I tied my shoes twice. The right one was already tied; I retied it for the body's reason, not the shoe's.

We walked to the venue at 1:14 p.m. He carried the racquet bag with both racquets in it, his black and my white-with-blue, the way he had carried it on the practice court at eight and the way he would carry it on the changeover at three. The walk took eleven minutes. We did not say anything along it. The Atlantic was making its blue request through the gaps in the privet hedges. I did not let myself answer it.

1:48 p.m. Centre Court tunnel.

The tunnel at Newport was a hallway with carpet. Not a tunnel. The Hall of Fame is a country club with a horseshoe court at the back, and a hallway with carpet is what they call a tunnel because every other tournament has a tunnel and the broadcaster's graphics package needs the word.

I held the racquet bag in my right hand and a cold bottle of water in my left. The intern from the morning practice had walked us down and was now standing at the curtain with a clipboard and a headset. The headset was too big for her ear. I had asked the broadcaster's producer in 2019 at Cincinnati why interns got the bad headsets and he had said *the good ones go to the talent.* The intern who had asked me the question in Cincinnati had become, by 2024, the producer who had hired me for two B-roll segments at Indian Wells. I did not bring this up at Newport. The interns at Newport did not need to know they were going to be producers.

Tess was three feet to my left with her hand on her right quad. She had her warm-up jacket zipped to the sternum and a Velcro hair-tie on her wrist. The Velcro hair-tie was navy. The wristband was white. The shoes were Asics, the model she had worn since 2009, the model Asics had stopped making in 2018 and which she had told me at lunch yesterday she had bought sixteen pairs of off a closeout site in 2018 and which she would, by her own arithmetic, run out of in 2029.

"You good," I said.

"Yes."

"Two and a half hours of grass. Hip."

"Hip is fine."

"Right."

"Stop coaching me."

"I was asking."

"With your eyebrows up like that, it's coaching."

"I was asking, Tess."

"You were coaching, Jude."

The curtain opened. The intern said something into the headset. We walked out.

The crowd was not large. Two thousand at a Wednesday Round of 16 at a seniors-tour exhibition in Newport in late August was a crowd of two thousand, which is to say two-thirds of the horseshoe was filled and the rest was empty and the empty part was where the broadcaster's cameras did not point. The French were already on court. The man was tall, taller than me, taller than he had looked on tape. The woman was small and dark and stood at the baseline with her hand on her hip in the way you stand at the baseline when you have stood at the baseline at the Bois de Vincennes in front of fifteen thousand people and a Wednesday at Newport is not where your nerves live anymore.

Coin toss. I called rough. The umpire flipped. Rough was up. I chose to serve. The French chose ends.

First game, my serve. I put the first one into the body at 109 and the French woman blocked it cross to Tess and Tess hit a winner down the line that the French man got a racquet on and which did not come back. The crowd made the noise crowds make at the first winner of a match, the *oh* that is half-laugh, half-appraisal. Tess walked back to the baseline with her right hand on her left wrist for the half-second it takes to acknowledge a clean ball.

Hold to fifteen. We changed ends.

Second game. The French man served wide on the deuce. Tess returned middle. The point lived at the net for three exchanges. He poached. The ball came back at Tess's feet and she dug it up off the doubles alley with the slice she had been hitting since 2005 and the ball had so much underspin that the French man's volley went into the net cord and dropped on his side. Tess did not celebrate it. She walked back to the deuce side and tugged the back of her shorts down the half-inch they ride up after a slide.

Their hold. We changed ends.

The set went on serve to 4 all. At 4 all I went up 30 to love on my serve and then double-faulted twice on the second serves, which is not a thing I do, which is a thing the body does when the body is forty-four and has been on grass for two days and the second toss is half an inch in front of where the body wants it. Break. The French held to fifteen. They closed the set at 6 to 4 with a return winner up the line that I should have had a racquet on.

Tess walked off behind me to the changeover bench. She did not say anything. I did not say anything. I drank from the bottle. I retaped my left index finger where the tape had loosened. The intern with the small ear and the big headset put a fresh towel on the bench.

Second set we got going.

I broke their serve at 1 all with a return that bit at the French woman's shoelaces. Tess held to thirty. They held to fifteen. I served at 3 to 1 and the body was back in it the way the body comes back to you when the body decides to. The serve found the line three times in the game. We went up 4 to 1.

They broke me back at 5 to 3 on a let cord that took the ball over Tess's racquet by an inch. Tess closed the set at 6 to 4 on her own serve, an ace down the T at 91 mph that the French woman did not move on because the French woman had decided it was going to be wide. I clapped my left hand on the side of my racquet at the umpire chair. Tess did not look at me. She walked to the bench.

One set all.

3:14 p.m. The changeover at the break between Set 2 and Set 3.

The umpire chair was on the far side. Both of our bags were on our changeover bench. Mine was open; hers was zipped. The intern had set out two fresh bottles of water and one banana, which neither of us was going to eat because we do not eat bananas between sets at three in the afternoon in Newport. The banana was decorative. The intern was new.

I stood up from the bench with the racquet bag in my left hand because the umpire was holding up two fingers at the broadcaster, which meant the third set started in two minutes and the changeover wanted us moving. Tess stood up at the same beat. Her hair-tie was looser at the back of her neck where the sweat had gotten under it and the elastic had given the half-inch elastic gives at sweat. I did not look at the back of her neck. I had taken eleven steps from the bench toward the baseline when I put my right hand at the small of her back to move her around the umpire chair's footing, the foot of the chair, the place where the metal foot sits in the grass and where a foot in a tennis shoe at three-fifteen in the afternoon catches if a person is not paying attention.

Three seconds.

My palm flat at the small of her back where the practice shirt was wet through.

The heat of her coming through the wet of the shirt and into my palm.

The crowd noise dropped out.

I do not mean the crowd was quiet. I mean the crowd was as loud as it had been on the changeover bench thirty seconds ago and the loud had moved into the back of my hearing the way a refrigerator moves into the back of a kitchen and the front of my hearing had nothing in it but the wet of the practice shirt and the heat of her under it and the count, which was *one, two, three* and which I had been counting in my body without knowing I was counting it.

She did not slow. She did not speed up. Her shoulder blade moved under my palm by the half-inch a shoulder blade moves when a person walks. She rounded the chair's footing with my hand at her back and stepped clean past the metal foot and onto the grass beyond it. My hand came off her back at the third beat without my deciding it.

I did not look at her. She did not look at me.

She walked to the deuce side of the new end. I walked to the ad side of the new end. The umpire said *time*. The third set started.

I served first. I put the first serve in at 113. The French woman barely got a racquet on it. The ball came up over the net at Tess's hip and Tess hit a forehand drop volley that died inside the service line and the French man's lunge ended two feet short.

"Good ball," I said, low, on the way to the next point.

"Good serve," she said, low, on the way back.

She did not look at my hand. I did not look at her shoulder blade.

We held. We broke them at 3 to 2 on a return I had no business making, a backhand chip that died on the French woman's racquet at the net and which she put into her own foot. The French man said *putain* under his breath at the net, plain and short and with no addressee, and the umpire did not hear it because the umpire chair's directional mic was angled at our end of the court. Tess heard it. She did not smile.

We held to 4 to 2. They held to 4 to 3. I served at 5 to 3. The first serve found the line at 111. The second-serve return came at Tess's body and she got a racquet on it with her elbow tucked the way she had been tucking her elbow since I knew her and the ball went back at the French man's feet and the French man's volley went into the tape. The third went 6 to 4.

The French shook hands at the net with the small respectful nods professionals give other professionals at Newport on a Wednesday, and the French woman gave Tess the small flat handshake that meant *good match,* and the French man gave me a longer handshake that meant *next time,* and the umpire said *game, set, match* into a microphone whose signal was not going to be on the broadcaster's live feed because the broadcaster had cut to commercial.

4:34 p.m. The changeover bench at the empty end of Centre Court.

I had come back to the bench because I had left my Velcro hair-tie on it during the third-set changeover at 3 all and the hair-tie was the navy hair-tie I had bought at a CVS in Pacific Heights in 2022 and which I had a small, completely irrational loyalty to.

The court was quiet. The groundsmen had not started yet. The umpire chair's shadow on the grass at four-thirty in the afternoon was a long blue triangle that reached the changeover bench's left leg. Two towels were on the bench, one his, one mine, both folded by the intern who had come on at three. The banana was gone. Somebody had taken the banana.

I picked up the hair-tie. I did not sit.

The handshake-line replay was already on the small TV monitor mounted under the broadcaster's tripod at the side of the court, looping the post-match thirty seconds for whoever was editing in the truck. I could hear the broadcaster's call coming faintly out of the on-court speakers as the audio engineer levelled it. *…and Auster's backhand return looked, I'm going to say it, an apology of a ball, but the apology won the point…*

Earlier, on the handshake line, I had said it to Jude with the half-smile that meant *I am about to say something* and he had said *I was apologising* with the half-smile that meant *I am about to receive it* and the broadcaster had picked up exactly enough of the exchange to play it back on the post-match cut, and the broadcaster's colour analyst, who had been in the booth all afternoon, had laid the line *they have great chemistry, don't they,* over the changeover footage from between Sets 2 and 3, and the changeover footage had shown a man putting his hand at the small of a woman's back for three seconds to walk her around the foot of an umpire chair, and three thousand people watching the live broadcast had heard the line about chemistry while watching the hand at the back.

I stood at the bench and watched the loop go around once. I watched the hand at the back of the shirt. I watched the shoulder blade under the hand. I watched the colour analyst's audio land on top of it. I clocked the count of three, and that my own body had been keeping it at the time, without my say-so.

I picked up the strings of my match racquet between my thumb and forefinger and pulled at the centre cross the way I do at the end of a match before I put the racquet away. The gut was at fifty-six and the gut would be at fifty-six tomorrow, and there was the racquet on the other side of the bench where the intern had laid it out for the post-match photo and not collected it, the black overgrip frayed at the butt-cap, and the polyester at fifty-two was, for the record, where it had been at noon, and the difference between my own stiffness and his sat with me for half a second before I put it down. Doubles is a relationship. You can read it off the strings if you know how.

The loop went around a second time and the colour analyst's voiceover came back in the part of my hearing the refrigerator had moved into. The line was on the broadcaster's audio stem; it was going to be there for the highlights package; it would be there for the rest of the week. I had been on the receiving end of broadcaster lines about chemistry for two years on a Champions Tour with my first husband in 2009 and I knew the half-life of a broadcaster line. Three days, max, before the line moved to the next pair the broadcaster needed a line for. I had three days.

The hand had not done anything contractually disallowed. The hand had moved a doubles partner around a piece of metal that protrudes from the grass at the foot of an umpire chair. The hand had been at the small of my back the way a hand at the small of a back is, between two people who play doubles and have to walk past furniture. The hand had been there three seconds. Doubles courtesy. Contractually fine.

*Contractually fine.* The phrase arrived in my chest the way phrases arrive when they have been waiting in a back room since lunch. It was the file I would put this in, for now, in the way a person who reads boards for a living puts things in files. *Three seconds, between sets, on camera, didn't mention, did not look at the shoulder blade, did not look at my own hand at my own back.*

The file would hold the file until the file decided to ask me a question.

I put the hair-tie in my shorts pocket. I left the bench.

The intern was at the curtain with the clipboard and the too-big headset. She did not look up. The grass under the changeover bench was flat and short. Two towels on the bench. The umpire chair's shadow on the grass at 4:34 p.m. The day was, for the public record, a win.
