11:45 a.m. Bellevue Avenue. I carried her racquet bag on my left shoulder and mine on my right. She had let me take hers without protest at the door of the Cape, which was a thing that had not happened on Tuesday and was happening today, Saturday, because of whatever sequence of small admissions had occurred between Wednesday and now.

I did not name the sequence. The walk was three blocks of cracked sidewalk and a porch with a wind chime that had been hung wrong on a hook intended for a hanging basket. The wind chime did not chime. The hook was too low. I noticed myself noticing, which was a thing she had taught me to notice that I was doing.

"Italian woman has not lost a quarterfinal in seven years," she said.

"She also has not won a semifinal in seven years."

"Mm."

"Statistically she is overdue."

"Statistically she is owed a semifinal by the universe."

"The universe is not in our half of the draw."

"The universe is not in anybody's half of the draw."

She walked half a step behind me, which let me carry the bag on her side without our elbows arguing. The bag was heavy in the way her bag was always heavy. I had not asked about the small zip pouch on Tuesday at Logan and I was not asking now, and the not-asking was, today, doing the work of asking.

12:42 p.m. Players' lounge, stringer's booth. Marco Calabrese, fifty-one, Pisa, on the seniors-tour stringer circuit since 2009. I had been bringing him racquets since the year his daughter started college. The booth smelled the way Marco's booths always smelled: polyurethane, leather grip wrap, a faint chalk from the resin he used when his palms got slippery.

He had his hands on a Babolat bag that was not mine. A frame sat in the clamps, half done.

"Tess. Two racquets, the gut at fifty-six?"

"Yes."

"And the third?"

"The unrestrung one. Hold it."

"I hold it."

He took the bag. He laid the three frames on the bench in the order he was going to do them, left to right, the way he had told me he did them in 2012.

"The boyfriend's match racquet you saw me restring at fifty-four yesterday, did he tell you, by the way I knew his coach in the eighties."

The booth did not change. The Babolat bag on the bench did not change. I held my hand still on the strap of my own bag and watched my hand stay still.

"He did not."

"Ah."

"At fifty-four."

"Fifty-four. One frame. He came in at eleven-thirty. He paid cash. He asked me not to make a thing of it." Marco shrugged. The shrug was a Marco shrug. "I make nothing of it. I am making nothing of it now."

"Tess."

He looked up at me then. His glasses were on the workbench. Without them his eyes were softer. I had not seen him without his glasses in seven years.

"I will do the gut first."

"Yes."

I sat in the orange plastic chair beside the booth. The seat was cold. *He restrung one match racquet at fifty-four on Thursday and did not tell me.* The line sat in my head the way a line sits in my head when a juror has just done a thing on the stand that the lawyer paying me eight hundred an hour has not yet noticed. The data went into the column the body kept. The reading of the data was for later.

I did not read it as betrayal. Doubles courtesy did not require him to tell me what tension he played at. He had made a small private accommodation; he had not announced it because announcing was its own kind of asking-for-credit, and he was not a man who asked for credit. The reading did not stop me from clocking that he had not said.

Marco worked. The clamps clicked. The clock on the wall above the booth was a Wilson clock with a tennis-ball second hand and a small chip in the felt where the seven was.

1:14 p.m. Locker, row forty-seven. I laced my grass-court shoes with my elbows on my knees and did the thing I had done before every match since 1999, which was to stop being a person and start being the doubles specialist who had been hired to play this match.

The grip on the match racquet I had restrung Thursday felt different in my palm. Not the tension; the tension was Thursday's accommodation, fifty-four, the two pounds I had given my shoulder in exchange for the next three matches. My palm did not register tension at the handle. My palm registered the wrap, and the wrap had not changed since June. The difference was that I knew. I had told Marco not to make a thing of it. Marco had made a thing of it. He had told her at twelve-forty-two. I knew because at twelve-forty-eight I had walked past the lounge door and seen the angle of her shoulder change.

I did not feel betrayed. I felt the small embarrassment of a man who had asked for a privacy that was not a privacy that anyone owed him.

I put the racquet back in the bag.

1:54 p.m. Tunnel. The Italian pair was ahead of us, Federica Bianchi and Tomas De Luca, married, sixteen years on tour between them, the two of them speaking quietly in Italian with their heads tilted toward each other. Their shoulders were not touching. The not-touching was the marriage. Theirs had been in public so long it had stopped being a practice.

The umpire's intern came up the tunnel and said two minutes. We walked.

2:01 p.m. The grass under my shoes held my weight the way grass at Newport in late August holds weight. There is no other thing to say about it.

Three thousand in the stands, mostly local, ITHF members in linen at the boxes, broadcast cameras at the four corners. The Italians won the toss and elected to receive. Bianchi rolled her right shoulder twice at the bench, in the same direction both times. I noted it. The noting was the work I did when there was nothing to do.

First set went six to four ours. We broke at four-three on a De Luca second serve I returned to Bianchi's feet and Jude closed at the net behind her. The set was forty-two minutes. The walk to the bench was three steps. Jude gave me the racquet-tap on the way. The racquet-tap was professional. The racquet-tap was the same racquet-tap from Tuesday's practice. The racquet-tap landed.

Second set on serve to three all. Jude held at three-four with a forehand winner up the line that came off so clean the crowd's reaction was the half-second-late reaction crowds give shots they have not yet processed.

I served at four-all.

This is the part I will be asked about.

I have served at four-all in the second set of a singles match maybe two hundred times. The two hundred is the muscle memory. The two hundred is what is left after nine years. The two hundred is, today, in a wrist that has not been at four-all in the second set of a tournament match since New Haven in 2017.

Fifteen-love. Body serve at Bianchi, she chipped it back high, Jude put it away. Fifteen-thirty on a Bianchi return Jude left an inch out. Thirty-thirty on a De Luca return up the middle that took Jude's forehand off the racquet. Thirty-forty.

Break point.

The crowd quieted by half a notch. The umpire's chair creaked once. The wind off the Atlantic had moved during the second set; the flag on the umpire's chair was no longer hanging straight, which meant the toss would have to come over the right shoulder by an inch.

I served.

I will tell you the things I knew. I knew Bianchi was reading my body half a beat ahead of my racquet. I knew De Luca had been camping the deuce alley for two games. I knew a kicker would not do the work today. I knew the only serve that beat Bianchi at the line was the flat down the T at a hundred miles an hour because she was a half-step slow on the move-and-bend. I knew the wrist had not been asked for a flat-T at break point in nine years.

I asked the wrist.

The wrist gave.

The serve came off the racquet at one hundred and two, which the speed-gun behind the chair registered and the broadcaster repeated four seconds later in a voice I did not hear, because I was watching Bianchi's racquet not move. The ball skidded low at the T and was past her before the racquet had decided what kind of swing it was going to be. The line judge did not call. The umpire did not need to.

The crowd's noise came in two parts. The first part was the inhalation. The second part was the noise.

I walked to the deuce side. I did not look at Jude.

The thing that happened in my body at that moment is the thing I am going to describe because she has asked me, in the months since, and because Mae asked me at Christmas in a way that meant I had to find words for it and not just nod.

The serve came off the racquet at one hundred and two. I was at the net in the I-formation, crouched, the racquet in my right hand at thigh height, the left hand on the throat. My job at the net on a first-serve break point that my partner is taking down the T is to be in Bianchi's eye-line and to be ready for the return up the middle if the serve is a percentage and not an ace. My job is not to flinch.

The serve was not a percentage. The serve was the one she had hit in Brisbane in 2015 against Vinci on a break point in the third. It was the one she had hit in 2011 at the US Open in qualifying against the Hungarian when I had been on the other side of the net and twenty-six and had thought, that day, *she serves like that and she said yes to dinner, the universe is for me.*

The crowd inhaled. The ball was past Bianchi. Bianchi's racquet had not moved.

My right thumb was on the handle of the racquet at the point where the leather wrap meets the overgrip, which is the point my right thumb has been on between every point of every match I have played since 1999. My thumb does a small thing between points. A quarter-turn to the left, then a quarter-turn back. The roll is not conscious. The roll is the thing my thumb does.

The thumb stopped.

The thumb stopped before I knew the thumb had stopped. The next breath I took came in two parts. The first part was the inhalation that started in the diaphragm and stopped at the bottom of the sternum where something I did not have a name for caught it. The second part was the rest of the breath, which came in after I had told it to. The two parts were a quarter-second apart. The quarter-second was the size of the gap between knowing a thing and admitting it.

The towel was in my right hand. I had picked it up off the bench at the end of the last point because Jude Auster at break-point at four-all wipes the right palm on the towel during the walk to the I-formation, and I had wiped it, and the towel had been in my right hand at the net during the serve because I had not had time to put it back. The towel was a Wilson towel, white with a yellow stripe. The towel was light. The towel weighed almost nothing.

The towel slipped out of my right hand.

The towel landed at my right foot on the grass at the T of the deuce service box.

I did not pick it up.

The not-picking-it-up was the thing. I have spent twenty-seven years on tennis courts picking up towels and balls and the cap of a water bottle that has rolled under the bench. I have never not-picked-up a towel I had dropped. The body I am does not leave its towel on the grass.

The towel stayed.

The umpire's voice came over the chair speaker. *Game, Auerbach. Five-four, second set.* The crowd's second-part noise was tapering. We were on the changeover.

I walked to the bench. I left the towel.

She was already at the bench. She had taken her seat on the right because the umpire's chair was on the right and the changeover bench's right cushion was the cushion she had been taking since Tuesday. I sat. I did not look at her. I bent forward and put my elbows on my knees and looked at the grass between my feet.

Her hand came into my eye-line, holding the bottle. She did not say anything. I took it. I drank. I gave it back.

She looked at the grass at the T of the deuce service box. She saw the towel.

She did not say anything.

The umpire called *time*.

She stood. I stood. The towel stayed on the grass. The ball-kid in the corner had seen the towel; the ball-kid was a fourteen-year-old in a white polo who had picked up two of my towels already this week without being asked; the ball-kid did not move. The ball-kid was looking at the umpire's chair. Maybe the umpire had told her. Maybe she had read the air. The towel did not move and the next point started with my towel six feet behind me on the grass at the T.

We held at five-four. They held at five-five. I held at six-five. They held at six-six. Tiebreak. They took it seven points to four because at four points to three down I missed a forehand volley that I have made a hundred thousand times.

The towel was still on the grass at the T of the deuce service box.

3:47 p.m. Set break. Two minutes, not ninety seconds. I sat on the bench beside him. I did not say anything. He did not say anything. The wind had died.

His thumb was still not moving on the racquet handle. The not-moving was not a tightness; it was an absence. The thumb was resting on the leather where it always rested, and the small quarter-turn-and-back I had clocked seventy times in two days was not happening.

The umpire called time.

The third set is a haze, in the way third sets sometimes go a haze: a set you remember not as a sequence of points but as a sequence of small mechanical corrections you made to keep the body doing what the body was for. The thumb came back at one-all when I served and aced De Luca down the T with a serve at one hundred and fifteen, and the body came back with the thumb. The body did not come all the way back. The body came back enough.

We broke at three-two. They broke back at four-three. Five all. They held. Six all. Tiebreak to ten. We took it ten-seven on a forehand volley I did make.

*Match. Auster and Auerbach. Seven-five, four-six, seven-six.*

The crowd stood. I do not remember the standing as a sequence; I remember it as a wall of sound that arrived at four-thirty-seven and stayed for the length of time it takes to walk from the net to the bench. We shook hands with Bianchi and De Luca. Bianchi held my hand for a second longer than the handshake required and said something quiet in Italian I did not catch.

The ball-kid had picked up the towel, finally, somewhere in the third set, and had put it on the back of the bench. I did not look at it. The body that had not picked up the towel was forty minutes ago. I was now a body that won a semifinal in Newport. The two bodies were not the same body. I do not know what to say about this.

The broadcaster's interviewer was at the side of the court with the microphone. Ben Parrish, fifty-six, three Slams in the booth. The camera operator had a beard.

Tess walked to the interview spot. I walked behind her. Her hair at the back of her neck was wet. There was a small line of grass-dust at the back of her left calf where the skin had been damp and the chalk from the court had stuck. She did not know about the grass-dust.

Before Ben Parrish raised the microphone I put my hand up to her temple and wiped the sweat from the side of her face with the side of my thumb. The side of my thumb was the side of the thumb that had not moved on the racquet handle at four-all break-point in the second set. The thumb was moving now. I wiped from the hairline at her temple to the corner of her jaw in one motion. The microphone did not pick it up. The camera operator with the beard saw it. Ben Parrish did not.

Tess did not look at me. She looked at Ben Parrish.

She said yes when he asked her if the ace at break point had been the serve of the match. She said it was the serve of the week. She said she had not been sure she had it in her wrist. She said the wrist had told her. She said it dryly, in front of a man with a microphone whose job was not to listen for dryness.

Ben Parrish asked me about the second set. I said the Italians had played a fine second set. I said De Luca had been reading our second serve well. I said we had made the corrections we needed in the third. I said the things a doubles specialist says into a microphone in late August in Newport after a semifinal. The microphone went off. The camera operator with the beard nodded at me and looked at the grass at the T of the deuce service box where the towel had been and then looked back at me. I do not know what he saw. The broadcast will, on the changeover replay-roll on Sunday morning at the players' lounge breakfast spread, show the wide shot of the changeover at four-all in the second set, which is the shot that includes the towel on the grass and includes my hand on the racquet handle not moving. The broadcast will not say what the shot is of. The broadcast will leave it.
