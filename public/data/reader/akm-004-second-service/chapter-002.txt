3:58 p.m. Practice Court 6 at the International Tennis Hall of Fame.

The grass was greener than I remembered grass being. It was August grass, late-August grass, two-weeks-of-mowing-at-eight-millimetres grass, and the colour of it under the four o'clock light was the colour of the inside of a lime. The horseshoe stands were empty. A groundsman in a green polo was raking the back of court four with a rake the width of a doormat. The tournament intern on our umpire chair was twenty-two and reading a paperback he had folded backwards on its spine. He looked up when I came through the gate, then looked back down.

"Tess Auerbach," I said.

"Right," he said.

I set the bag on the changeover bench. I unzipped it. I took out the match racquet with the white-with-blue wrap. The cover came off the head. I checked the strings at the centre and at the top cross and at the throat and they were where they had been at 9:30 a.m. in California. Gut at fifty-six. The cobalt stripe ran around the grip. The cobalt matched the sky over the back of the stand at the four-thirty hour, which was an accident.

I had asked Jude for the court at four and for myself at three-fifty-eight. I needed twenty minutes alone with grass. I had been on grass twice since 2017: once for a Nike shoot in 2019 on a court in Wimbledon Village that I do not think was a tournament court, once for a charity hit-and-giggle in Atherton in 2022. Neither counted. Grass is a surface the body has to remember being on. The remembering takes twenty minutes or it does not happen.

I bounced a ball on the baseline.

The bounce was low. I had forgotten how low. The ball came up about as high as my mid-shin. I caught it and bounced it again. Low, same. Two-eighty-five hours of indoor hardcourt in California over the last four months and the body had built its assumptions on hardcourt, and the body's assumptions were now being corrected by August grass at one-thirty p.m. Pacific.

I served at half pace into the deuce box. The ball bit at the service line and squirted forward at a height my forehand was not ready for. I let it go past. I served again. Same. I served again. Same. The fourth one I caught at the bounce. The ball was three inches lower than where my hardcourt eye had been putting it.

I served twenty more. By number eight I was hitting them. By number twelve I was hitting them at the speed I could trust. By number twenty I had stopped narrating the bounces in my head.

I jogged to the net for the balls. Twenty balls scattered in the deuce box, a few stragglers in the alley. The jog cost me more than the jog should have cost me. I came back to the baseline with the basket and I told my legs that the legs were going to have to stop telling on themselves at twenty paces. The legs did not answer.

A sparrow landed on the back fence at 4:07 p.m. and considered me. The sparrow's head tilted. I tilted my head back at the sparrow. The intern did not look up from Didion. The sparrow concluded I was uninteresting and flew off toward the parking lot. I served four more half-pace into the ad box and then a full one. The full one went down the T and the bounce skidded at the back service line and would have been an ace in 2011 and was, in 2026, a serve. No celebration. The body added it to its column.

5:02 p.m.

Three-shot points are honest. Three-shot points tell you what a partnership is going to be when the legs are tired and the ball is coming back faster than you wanted it to. You serve, you get a return, you have to do something with it. The shot you do with it is the shot that says whether you and your partner can play doubles.

Her return was where I wanted it. She put it at my feet on the deuce side and I had to half-volley. I half-volleyed wide. She put it at my hip on the ad side and I had to half-volley. I half-volleyed into the net cord. The third one she put right at my left foot and I got under it and lobbed back over her and she ran it down and hit a forehand pass into my deuce alley.

"Tess."

"Yes."

"I am thirty-three years old."

"You're forty-four."

"I am thirty-three years old on this court."

"Right."

I tried the cross. She crossed me back. We played three-shot points for fifteen minutes. The alleys moved. They moved the way alleys move when two people have agreed to let the alleys move, which is the only way alleys move. Her ball into the deuce alley, my poach across the centre, her low backhand at my forehand half-volley. I had been drawing these shapes on the back of envelopes since 2009. They have not changed because the geometry has not changed. The geometry is twenty-seven feet by seventy-eight feet and four lines.
