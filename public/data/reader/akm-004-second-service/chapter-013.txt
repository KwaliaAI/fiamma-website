4:11 p.m. Pacific. The deck.

I had the phone propped against a brick at the edge of the boards and the tape measure in my right hand and my left knee on a cedar deck-board the contractor had laid Tuesday and not yet stained. The board under my knee was the lighter wood, the wood that has not been outside long enough to have an opinion about the sun. I had been on it for ninety seconds. The grain registered through the canvas of my pants in a way that my forty-one-year-old knee was going to complain about at 9 p.m. and not before.

"Three-and-a-quarter," I said.

"You said three-and-an-eighth on the last one."

"The last one was an eighth."

On the phone, Jude was barefoot on academy concrete in San Diego, between his three-thirty and his five o'clock. The audio carried the sea-air in the way audio carries sea-air, which is to say it carried it as a small additional silence at the back of his voice. His thumbnail in the corner of the screen showed half of his face and a chain-link fence and a yellow ball-hopper on its side.

"Bare feet," I said.

"It's a hundred and three on the court. I came off the court."

"On concrete."

"On concrete."

"In October."

"Tess."

"Just clocking."

He laughed once. The laugh came through with the sea-air on it. I clocked the bare foot through the thumbnail and registered myself clocking it, and then I made myself stop, because the noticing was a habit, and the habit was older than the deck.

The contractor had said three-and-an-eighth on the spacing. I had measured eleven boards. Eleven boards were at three-and-an-eighth. The twelfth was at three-and-a-quarter, which was, in the world of cedar decking, fine, and which was, in the world of a person who had charged eight hundred dollars an hour to watch a CEO's left hand for six months, not fine.

I sat back on my heel. The lighter board held under my weight. The grain showed where the stain had not reached.

"Mae's in Indian Wells the eighteenth," he said.

"I know."

"You said San Diego that week."

"Tuesday board prep, the Wednesday and Thursday with the legal team, Friday open."

"Mae plays Thursday."

"Mm."

"I'm not asking."

"I know."

"I am, in fact, not asking."

"Jude."

"I am noting calendar facts."

"Noted."

He drank water on his end. I heard the cup hit the bench. Paper cup, green bench, court four. I had been on that court for four hours in 2017 doing a clinic he had not been at, and I knew the bench, and I knew the cup, and I knew the fence behind the cup, and I had not been there in nine years and I knew it.

"Strings," I said.

"Strings."

"I restrung at fifty-four this week. Charity ex in November."

A small pause.

"I restrung at fifty-three."

"When."

"Tuesday."

"You didn't tell me."

"I am telling you."

"When I asked."

"That's still telling you."

I let the laugh out short. I did not let him see the laugh. I kept my eyes on the deck-board.

"Fifty-three," I said.

"Fifty-three."

"You're meeting me halfway."

"Yes."

The wind on his end moved one chain-link of the fence; the sound of one link against the next came through cleaner than his voice had a second earlier. On my end the breeze off the ocean two blocks west moved the wind chime my neighbour had hung in her lemon tree at the property line.

I shifted my knee off the lighter board onto the board next to it, the board the contractor had stained on Wednesday. The stained board was warmer. The lighter board sat where I had left it, the grain still showing, the stain still not reached.

"Five o'clock," he said.

"Go."

"Tess."

"Go."

"Tape measure."

"In my right hand. Yes. I see it. I will put it down. Eventually."

"Eventually."

"Bye."

"Bye."

He hung up. The thumbnail went out. The phone screen showed my own face for a half-beat and then went to the lock screen. I left it propped against the brick.

I did not stain the lighter board today.
