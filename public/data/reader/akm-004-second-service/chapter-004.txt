6:34 p.m. The lawn at the Casino held its August light the way Newport lawns do, giving back about a third of what it took. The tent was the small white one with the scalloped edge that the tournament put up for the cocktail and the trophy ceremony and nothing else, and the string quartet was set on the side closest to the box hedges, two violins, a viola, a cello, the cello played by a woman who I had watched tune her A string for forty seconds on the way in. Forty seconds is a long time on an A string.

The linen count was about fifty. *Newport-summer-business* the way the email had put it. The men were in linen jackets they had bought in 2018; the women were in dresses the colour of pistachios. Sponsor banners on three tables. A bartender at each end of the tent. A photographer at the centre with a Leica around his neck.

I had a glass of sparkling water with a lime in it. I did not want to be at the cocktail. I had said so in the car at 6:22 p.m. to Tess, in the flat tone a forty-four-year-old man uses when he is delivering a fact and not asking for a response, and she had said *yes, Jude, that is the contract* in the tone of a person who has been to nine hundred sponsor cocktails and has stopped arguing with them. She was across the tent now in pale green, talking to the sponsor's senior VP at the table closest to the box hedges. I watched her work.

She was not flirting. I know flirting. Flirting is a forward register. She was doing the opposite. She was making herself slightly smaller than him in space, an inch lower at the shoulder, a half-step back at the foot, her wineglass at the level of her own sternum and not raised. The VP was a man of about fifty-five with a face that registered being listened to. He leaned in three times in the ninety seconds I watched, and each time he leaned in, Tess repositioned an inch back and gave him the small empty space to step into again. The third time she did it I understood what she was charging her boards eight hundred dollars an hour for. She was letting him ask the question.

She reads the exec. I do not have her vocabulary for it. I have the vocabulary of net play. At net you do not chase the ball; you make the ball come to you by closing the angle the right amount and waiting. She was closing the angle on the VP without seducing him and without flattering him, by being a person who had decided what she wanted and then standing in such a way that he had to come to it.

I noticed myself noticing. I had been noticing for two days. The volume of the noticing was new. None of the fifteen years of coaching teenagers through pre-tournament meals had prepared me for being on a lawn in linen at 6:36 p.m. and watching her work.

"Auster."

The voice was a voice I had not heard in eight years. Tom Ruddick, fifty-eight, board member of the academy, formerly of the men's doubles tour, currently in a linen jacket the colour of weak tea. He had a glass of red and a wife on his elbow whose name I had blanked on in 2017 at a tournament dinner and then never recovered.

"Tom."

"You eaten today."

"I'm about to."

"What did you eat this morning."

He asked it the way a man asks a question to which he already knows the answer, because he has asked it in 2016 and 2018 and 2021 and once at a doubles clinic in Indian Wells when I was fasting for blood work and had answered honestly. I gave him the answer flat.

"Sausage egg and American cheese. Hot sauce in a packet."

"Where."

"There's a Cumberland Farms on Memorial."

"Jude."

"I walked there at six-fifteen."

His wife laughed, not at me, at him; it was a laugh that had ten years of the same conversation in it. "Tom has been trying to get him onto an egg-white scramble since 2016," she said to nobody in particular, to the tent, to the cello player, to history. "I have personally cooked an egg-white scramble for this man on three different occasions."

"It was a good scramble," I said.

"He ate it and then went and got the sandwich."

"That is the story, yes."

"On the way back from your house."

"It was on the way."

Tom set his red wine down on the table behind him, which had a sponsor's banner on it and a small dish of marcona almonds, and crossed his arms over his linen jacket. He had the body of a former tour player who had aged into a board seat: barrel through the chest, thinner at the leg, the slow careful posture of a man with one knee replacement and one in the queue.

"Tell me," he said. "Honestly. Why."

"Tom."

"Why."

"Because I like it."

"That is not a reason."

"It is a reason. It is not a *good* reason."

His wife laughed again. I drank my sparkling water. Across the tent the VP was nodding at something Tess had said, and Tess had her wineglass half a degree higher than she had been holding it ninety seconds ago, which I understood as the small reward she was giving the man for having asked the question she had been waiting on. I looked back at Tom. I had not engaged with the breakfast-sandwich question in eleven years. I was not going to engage with it tonight. The not-engaging was the answer. The not-engaging had been the answer in 2016 and 2018 and 2021 and was, on the Casino lawn in August 2026, in linen, with a board member who paid part of my salary, still the answer.

"All right," Tom said. "All right. Tell me about the doubles. The kid is good."

"Tess."

"Yes, Tess. The kid."

We talked about Tess. We talked about doubles. We talked about the French pair we had beaten on Wednesday afternoon, the Italian pair in the semifinal draw, the German pair who had crossed the bracket on Tuesday and were now the favourites to reach the final on the bottom half. Tom asked the technical questions of a man who had been on tour in the 1990s and I answered them in the shorthand. His wife drifted to the other side of the tent to look at the marcona almonds at the next table. The string quartet had moved from a Vivaldi thing to something that sounded like it had been written for a film.

7:14 p.m. I had not eaten anything.

7:21 p.m. I went toward the buffet.

The buffet was on the long table at the back of the tent, two chafing dishes of crab cake, a bowl of cold tomato soup with basil oil floated on top, a wooden board of cheeses with the rinds turned out, and a man behind the table in a white jacket carving a side of beef. There were three people in front of me. The third was a former men's doubles player whose name I did not have to retrieve because his name had been retrieved for him by the bartender three minutes earlier when he had ordered his fourth bourbon at eleven minutes past seven and the bartender had said *here you go, Pete.* Pete Tarrant. 2003 Wimbledon doubles semifinalist. Currently of Tampa.

"Auster."

"Pete."

"You and the kid."

"Tess. She has a name now, Pete."

"Right, right. Tess. How'd that happen."

"Tournament committee paired us. Liked the reunion-of-the-2011-qualifying-cohort story for the camera."

"The qualifying. Christ, two thousand eleven. I had hair."

"Yes."

"They tell me you two have great chemistry."

He said it the way Pete Tarrant says everything when he has had four bourbons, at the volume of a man hearing himself in slightly wider stereo than he is producing. He said it standing between me and a chafing dish of crab cake. I gave him the smile I give the parents of teenagers at the academy when they have asked me whether their son will play Division I tennis. The smile was on. The eyes were not.

"That's what they tell you."

"Hand on her back at the changeover. Saw it on the replay. They've been playing it in the lounge."

"It was a changeover."

"I know what it was. I'm saying what they tell me."

"They tell you a lot, Pete."

"They do."

He moved one step closer to the crab cake. I let him. I took a plate from the stack and the small tongs from the chafing dish and waited. The fourth bourbon was entirely visible in his face, in his timing, in the way he held his plate at the wrong angle to the buffet. He would lose a crab cake off the plate on the walk back to the table. I did not warn him.

The intern came up at 7:26 p.m. with a tablet.

She was the intern from the umpire chair at the Tuesday practice, twenty-two, ITHF lanyard, polo shirt. She had walked across the tent toward me with the tablet held at her sternum the way Tess had been holding her wineglass at hers, which made me wonder whether they had taught her that in the broadcaster's onboarding or whether she had figured it out on her own.

"Mr Auster."

"Hi."

"I'm not supposed to be doing this but I love the segment."

"Which segment."

"The Round of 16."

She turned the tablet toward me. The frame was the changeover frame. Tess walking ahead of me toward the umpire chair after the second set, her practice shirt dark with sweat between the shoulder blades, my right hand at the small of her back, three seconds in, the crowd a half-second from the kind of noise that means they have seen what they have seen and are deciding what to do with it. The frame was a still. The intern had paused the playback at the second of three.

"Cute," I said.

"It's the most-watched clip on the tournament feed."

"Mm."

"I'm not supposed to be showing players the clips."

"Then probably you should not."

"Right."

She put the tablet down at her side. I set the crab cake on my plate and stepped back and let the intern have her moment of having shown me the thing. I walked toward the table where the marcona almonds were and where Tess was standing with the senior VP, the VP a half-step closer than he had been ten minutes earlier, Tess at the same distance she had been holding all along.

I did not eat the crab cake on the walk. I ate it standing next to Tess at the table after the VP had been pulled away by his chief of staff, with a paper napkin folded once, the crab cake cool in the middle which I let pass without comment because no good comes of saying anything about a sponsor's crab cake on a sponsor's lawn.

"He lied," Tess said, low, to my shoulder.

"About what."

"The timeline."

"Mm."

"Walk back."

"Yes."

8:51 p.m. We left the tent.

9:31 p.m. We walked back along Bellevue in the dark. The magnolias had given up their day's heat and were giving back the perfume of it in long slow exhalations into the August night. The streetlights were the old Newport lanterns with the small bracket at the bottom for hanging a flag from on the Fourth of July. There was no traffic.

"Left hand on the glass," I said.

"When."

"When he gave you the *Q3 by Cincinnati* line about the new racquet rollout."

"I saw the hand."

"You saw the hand. You did not see what it was doing."

"It was on the glass."

"It was on the *base* of the glass. Stem grip, base of the bowl, three fingers wrapped, the index pulled back. That is the hand of a man who has decided he is going to have to clean up what he is saying with what he says next."

"Tess."

"And the right was free. Right hand off the glass entirely, near the back of his hip, the kind of free that means he was reserving the right hand for the recovery. His body had clocked the lie before he had."

"Mm."

"Then the chair-back lean. Did you see the lean."

"I had a crab cake to manage. I saw a man recline."

"Two inches back, weight off the front foot, breath in. That is the lean of a man taking the chair into evidence. He is not leaning *away* from you. He is leaning *toward* the room. He wants the room to remember he was relaxed when he said what he said."

Jude laughed. The laugh was short and from the chest, the same laugh he had given me at the Pell Bridge tollbooth on Tuesday morning about the string-tension argument, the laugh he produced when he had been pleased with something and would prefer not to say so. He kept walking. I kept walking. Our hands did not touch. I had a wineglass-worth of pinot grigio and a salt-air dehydration in me and the gladness of having been good at the thing I had been brought to be good at. There are very few gladnesses you can carry through your forties without anyone trying to take them away from you.

"And the sandwich," I said.

"What about the sandwich."

"You did not engage with Tom Ruddick about the sandwich."

"No."

"The not-engaging was the answer."

"It was."

"The jaw."

"What jaw."

"Your jaw. The small dig-in on the left side. When his wife said *egg-white scramble.* You did not engage in your face either, but your jaw engaged for you."

"Tess."

"I am telling you what I saw."

"I know what you saw."

"You are not apologising for the sandwich."

"I am not."

"Right."

He did not apologise. He walked on. Bellevue was empty of cars. I thought *the gas-station breakfast sandwich is the one negotiation in his life he has won by refusing to enter it*, and I kept the thought where it was. Somewhere in my early thirties I had stopped saying the things I noticed about the people I loved out loud, because the people I loved had started flinching when I said them. The noticing had not stopped. The offering had. Tonight on Bellevue I had given him the lean and the hand on the glass and the jaw, and he had taken them, and he had not flinched.

The Cape was four blocks. We did not say anything for the last two.

9:46 p.m. Kitchen.

I put the kettle on the burner. The kettle was the previous tenant's, the one that had been there on Tuesday at noon, blue enamel chipped at the spout. I had not touched it since. He came in from the front hall in his sock feet and took off the linen jacket and laid it on the back of the kitchen chair the way a person lays a thing down when they have been wearing the thing for three hours and have remembered, in the last forty seconds, that they did not want to be wearing it.

"French pair," he said.

"Their second serve."

"His, specifically. Hers is fine."

"He drops the wrist."

"On the deuce side. Not on the ad."

"On both. Smaller on the ad."

"All right. Smaller on the ad."

"The wife knows it. She poaches the deuce-side second serve at about a seventy-percent rate when she is on the right side of the court and they are receiving."

"And on the ad."

"Forty-five percent. She does not trust the smaller drop. She poaches anyway because the husband expects her to. They are arguing about it on the changeover. You can see it. He moves the towel from his left hand to his right hand when she has poached and missed. She does not look at him."

"They argue like a married couple."

"They are one. Twenty years."

"I know. Their second-serve quarrels read the way other couples' grocery-list quarrels read."

Jude considered the kettle. The kettle was beginning its small pre-whistle hum at the back of the burner, the hum that comes about thirty seconds before the actual whistle, the hum that on an old enamel kettle is two notes off the whistle's note and the difference between them is the entire warning.

"Yes," he said. "And the woman strings tighter than the man."

"Of course she does."

"Italians."

"Top half of the draw. The seedings are off because the committee did the draw before the French pair's Cincinnati result."

"They will hold the top half."

"They will hold it to the semifinal."

"Spanish."

"The Spanish woman has not played a senior tournament since Charleston. The husband has played four. He is rusty at the alleys."

"Good."

He had his phone out, looking at the academy schedule for next week's kids' clinics, the way he had been looking at the academy schedule on Tuesday night at ten. His right thumb scrolled the calendar three weeks forward and back. He had held a coaching life together while I had held three career pivots together; the difference between the two was a difference of weight, not of competence. He had stayed. I had moved. The lives we had built had built around the spaces.

He set the phone face-down on the yellow tile counter.

"I'm going up," he said.

"Mm."

"Tess."

"Yes."

"You were good tonight."

"I know."

"All right."

He went. His sock foot on the third step of the stairs hit the creak the Cape's stairs have given for forty summers. The fifth step creaked lower. The hallway light upstairs adjusted. The bathroom door upstairs opened and shut. The bathroom door opening and shutting is a sound that means *the other person is alive in their separate room*; I had heard it forty-eight hours ago and was hearing it again now and the meaning of it had not changed.

The kettle whistled at 10:09 p.m. I took it off the burner. I poured the water into a mug with a bag of the chamomile the previous tenant had left in the cupboard. The brand was not the brand I bought at home. I did not particularly want chamomile. The cupboard was what we had.

I drank the chamomile standing at the counter. The magnolia smell from Bellevue came through the kitchen window screen, which someone had cranked open a single turn at some point earlier in the week and not closed since.

10:11 p.m. The kettle on the counter, still warm, the lid askew where I had set it back down at an angle.
