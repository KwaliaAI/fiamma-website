10:13 p.m. Upstairs hallway.

The two bedroom doors were seven feet apart. I measured it standing barefoot on the runner because some part of me had decided in the last six hours that distances were now data. Seven feet. The runner was a kilim a landlord buys at a discount store off Route 1 and lays down to cover the floorboards where the previous summer had spilled something dark. Under my left heel was the part of the runner that smelled, faintly, like ten Augusts of bare feet. Under my right heel the wood.

I had on shorts and his T-shirt.

I had not planned to have on his T-shirt. There was no laundry at the Cape and I had brought four shirts for six days because that is the math a person who flies in for a tournament does. The fifth shirt was the shirt I had stripped off in the half-bath at 4:30 p.m. The sixth shirt was on Jude's bedroom chair. He had set it there at 6 p.m. without saying anything, the way a person sets down a glass for a guest who has not asked for one, and I had taken it without asking, and the cotton was the cotton of an academy promo run nobody had ordered enough of in 2019.

His door was closed.

His door had been closed since 9:48 p.m. I had heard it close from inside my own room. I had been sitting on the edge of the bed reading the back of an Advil bottle I had taken from the bathroom shelf, a bottle some previous renter had left, expiration 2024. I had read the back of the bottle the way a person reads a menu they are not going to order from. I had heard his door close at 9:48 and the bathroom door at 9:49 and water running in the upstairs sink for fifty seconds, the kind of fifty seconds that is a man washing his face and not brushing his teeth, and then his door again at 9:51.

I had not moved for twenty-two minutes.

Now I was in the hallway.

The hallway light was the kind Cape landlords buy at a hardware store on Memorial Boulevard, a brass-and-frosted-glass fixture screwed into the ceiling with two of the three screws still doing the work. The bulb was a soft white, sixty watts, and it threw a yellow circle on the kilim and a smaller, weaker circle on the wall above his door, where some previous renter had hung a picture and taken it down.

I looked at his door.

*Knock.*

*Do not knock.*

*The body knows what it wants and the hand knows where it is, and neither of them is authorized to act on what they know at 10:13 p.m. on a Friday in a rented Cape in Newport, Rhode Island.*

I made myself name the actual question, because the actual question had been doing laps in my head for an hour and had not yet been allowed to come up to the desk. The actual question was not *do I want to go in.* The actual question was *if I go in tonight, do we have a doubles partner tomorrow at noon.*

I stood on the runner and let the question sit there.

The answer was no.

The answer was no because the half-bath at 4:30 had been one kind of crossing and the upstairs bedroom at 10:13 would be a different kind, and Jude was forty-four and a doubles specialist who had built fifteen years of court-discipline on the principle that work was sequenced; tomorrow at noon was a semifinal against an Italian pair, and tonight's task was sleep, and right now's task was not me at his door in his T-shirt.

I knew the answer. I had known it on the kilim before I came out of my room.

I had come out of my room anyway.

This is what the body-language consultant charges eight hundred dollars an hour to explain to a CEO: the body knows the answer and walks into the room with the question anyway, because the question is the part it wants to have.

I went back into my room and shut the door without letting the latch catch loud.

The bed was the bigger of the two beds in the Cape, queen-size, with rental sheets the colour of bone and a duvet cover that smelled of the same washing-powder all rental houses smell of. I sat on the edge, on the side closest to the door. I did not lie down. The window was open three inches and the air coming through was cool by August standards, low seventies, the magnolia outside quieted for the night.

I put my hand flat on the duvet beside my left thigh.

This hand had played Wimbledon qualifying in 2009 and signed a marriage licence in 2014 and signed a divorce decree in 2022 and held a phone steady through a Fortune-500 board prep last March. This hand at 10:31 p.m. was on a rental duvet on a Friday night and was not shaking and was not still. It was warm in the way a person is warm after spending an afternoon doing the work she had not been authorized to do.

*The calf will be fine by 2 p.m.*

I had been telling myself the line for an hour. The right calf had carried me through three sets at 1 p.m. on Friday and would carry me through three more sets at 2 p.m. on Saturday. The work the calf would do on Saturday was the work the calf had been built to do. The work done at 4:30 p.m. on Friday was not in any contract.

I lay down on top of the duvet, on my back, in his T-shirt.

The ceiling of the bigger bedroom had a water-stain in the corner above the window the shape of New Hampshire. I had clocked the stain on Tuesday and had not looked at it since. I looked at it now.

11:14 p.m. I did not sleep.

12:08 a.m. Saturday. Dark kitchen.

I came down in socks. The stairs gave their five-step pattern; the second creaked, the fourth did not, the fifth lower than the second. I had memorised it by Thursday. I came down without turning on the hallway light. The kitchen was dark except for the porch light I had not turned off, which lay across the floor in a long rectangle and stopped at the leg of the table.

The half-bath door was six feet from the kitchen counter where I put my left elbow.

I did not look at it.

I stood at the sink with a glass in my right hand and did not turn the tap on. The cold-side tap was stiff and required the push I had figured out at 6 p.m. on Tuesday, and the push tonight was not going to happen because turning the tap on would mean drinking the water would mean putting the glass down would mean turning around would mean looking at the half-bath door.

I stood for two minutes. The kitchen ticked. The refrigerator held.

I set the glass on the counter empty.

The phone was face-down beside it. I had left it there at 10:02 p.m. on purpose. Mae stayed up to eleven on Fridays and Mae would have texted at 9:30 Pacific to ask whether the Italian pair had a weakness on second-serve return, and I had not wanted to coach my daughter through a semifinal preview from inside a Friday night in which the coaching brain was offline. The phone face-down was a small abdication. Mae could wait. Mae would understand at fourteen what she would not have understood at ten, which is that her father was, every once in a great while, a man who could not coach.

I looked at my own right hand on the counter. The thumb was not moving. The thumb was the thumb that had stopped moving exactly once at a changeover at Indian Wells in 2008 and once at a changeover at Halle in 2014, and which I had taught itself not to stop moving since. The thumb was not the issue.

The issue was the racquet bag at the kitchen chair.

I went and crouched in front of it.

The three match racquets in the side pocket were what they had been on Thursday afternoon at the players' lounge when I had walked them down and the stringer had asked me what tension and I had said *fifty-four* and the stringer had looked up at me once with the expression a stringer who has known you for twelve years has the right to and had said *fifty-four* back at me without inflection and had restrung them.

She had been on the porch when I had walked out with the bag Thursday morning. She had been arguing strings with me at 7 a.m. for an hour. I had walked the bag down to the stringer at 11:30 a.m. without saying anything. The bag had been in the chair by the kitchen door for two days.

She had not opened the bag.

She would, at the changeover on Saturday at 2:40 p.m. or thereabouts, register the difference in the way she registered everything, with her hand before her eye. The polyester she had been arguing against was at fifty-four now, two pounds towards her gut, the gap between a body that decides for itself and a body that has been listened to. The compromise was small. The compromise was a man who had been listening since Tuesday and had not said so.

I closed the bag.

I did not stand up for a moment. I was crouched in front of a racquet bag in a dark kitchen at 12:14 a.m. with my forearms on my thighs and my hands hanging between my knees, and the position was the one a doubles specialist takes at the changeover when the changeover is going badly, what the coaching manuals call *base.*

I stood up. The half-bath door was where it had been at 12:08 and where it would be at 7 a.m. I did not look at it. I went back upstairs. Her door was closed. Mine was closed. The hallway light upstairs was still on. I went into the smaller bedroom and shut the door behind me.

5:54 a.m. Saturday. Porch.

I had come down in shorts and the same T-shirt and bare feet and the coffee from the drip machine in the kitchen.

The porch table held my mug and the slack gut racquet from Thursday morning that nobody had moved since. The slack gut racquet was a racquet I now knew was Jude's, a backup, and the strings on Saturday morning smelled like the strings on Thursday morning, which is to say like the locker rooms of my twenties.

I sat in the rocking chair the previous renter had put there. The chair gave its one beat of complaint and rested. Bellevue at six was the version of Bellevue that did not have cars on it. A man with a Boston terrier passed at 5:58 and did not look at the porch.

The screen door ticked once at 6:02 a.m. and Jude came out with his own mug and the local paper.

He had on shorts and a clean blue academy T-shirt and grass-court socks and no shoes. The blue T-shirt was the second-tier shirt because the one he had given me at 6 p.m. on Friday had been the better shirt. I noticed myself noticing.

"Coffee," he said.

"Yes."

"You been up long."

"Since five-forty."

"Right."

He sat in the second porch chair, the one nobody had been in, and set the paper on the table between us. The paper was the *Newport Daily News.* The headline was about a zoning meeting in Middletown. The fold below the fold was the tournament, with a chyron-style preview of the semifinal matchups and a wire-service photo from Friday's quarterfinal in which Jude was at the net and I was a blur on the baseline. He turned to the tournament page without comment and read it the way a man reads what he has already read on his phone an hour ago.

We sat for four minutes. The bay through the gap between two houses on the other side of Bellevue was the colour a bay is at six a.m. in August.

At 6:18 he tapped the column-three lead with the back of his thumb.

"The Spanish veteran. The man."

"Yes."

"His second-serve wrist."

"What about it."

"It's wrong on the wrist." He kept his eyes on the page. "Watch the take-back. The wrist comes through behind the elbow on the second serve. He's been doing it for fifteen years and he's been getting away with it because the women on tour have not bothered to read the second serve at speed, and the men who have bothered have been polite about saying so on broadcast. The wrist is wrong."

I drank the coffee.

"Jude."

"What."

"The Spanish man has won three doubles slams."

"He has."

"On that wrist."

"On a wrist that's been wrong since 2011, yes."

"In what world is a wrist that wins three slams a wrong wrist."

"In the world where the right wrist wins five." He set the mug down. He set it down with the small grit-of-jaw I had clocked at the cocktail on Wednesday, the academy-board-member breakfast-sandwich grit-of-jaw. The grit was a grit I had now seen twice in four days. "The wrist costs him fifteen percent of his second-serve placement. He gets away with it because everything else about him is filthy. The wrist is wrong."

I let the laugh out short, the way I had let it out in the Subaru on Tuesday. He did not look up from the paper. He kept reading.

"Jude."

"Mm."

"You are wrong about the wrist in exactly the way you are wrong about the breakfast sandwich."

"I am wrong about neither."

"You are wrong about both, and you'll defend the sandwich next."

"Tess."

"Jude."

He did not concede. He had not conceded on the porch at 7 a.m. on Thursday about the strings either, and he was not conceding on the porch at 6:21 a.m. on Saturday about the wrist of a man whose wrist had been earning him a living since 2008, and the not-conceding was a small fact about a forty-four-year-old doubles specialist who had made up his mind on this one Spanish veteran's wrist mechanics in 2011 and had not since revisited the file. He would die on the wrist. I knew this by the hand on the mug, which had not moved.

"Italian pair," I said.

"Yes."

"Net game."

"Strong. The woman comes in on second serve return more than she did in 2024. The man covers the lob better than he reads it. We serve to her backhand from deuce, to his forehand from ad."

"My calf."

"What about it."

"The calf will be fine by 2 p.m."

"Good."

"I will ice at the venue at twelve-thirty."

"Good."

He turned the page. He read the fold below the tournament without comment. I drank the coffee. The screen ticked. A cardinal landed on the magnolia hedge at 6:27 a.m. and left at 6:28. The light over Bellevue went, in the way the light does in late August, from the unnamed colour at six to the colour a tournament day starts in.

"Tess."

"Mm."

"The shirt."

I had forgotten the shirt.

I had forgotten the shirt in the way a person forgets a shirt at 6:29 a.m. on the porch of a rented Cape after eight hours of one question in a hallway, which is by not forgetting it at all and by deciding the forgetting was the cover one needed.

He did not look at me.

"Yes."

"You can keep it."

"All right."

"I have others."

"I know."

He did not say anything for a beat. The mug was at his right hand. The thumb was not moving.

"The Italian woman's backhand from deuce," he said.

"Yes."

"We can win this match in two if we hold the first."

"Yes."

He folded the paper into a rectangle and pushed it to the centre of the porch table. He stood and took both mugs and went into the kitchen. The screen door ticked once on its closer and rested.

6:31 a.m. The porch table held the folded paper and the two mug-rings, one of them ringed by the chip in the rim I had been drinking around since Tuesday.
