The thermostat read fifty-eight when Robin stood in front of it in her sock feet at five forty-five and put a finger on the lever and did not move it. The propane truck was still not Thursday. The driver out of Bozeman had called Tuesday afternoon to say the schedule was sliding because of the cold and they would be on the route Friday, they would call when they were close, and Robin had said *thank you* in the voice she used for people who had not personally arranged the weather and had then sat at the kitchen table for the count it took to want to break something and let it pass.

She got the coffee going and stood at the kitchen window. The radio was saying minus twenty-two for the morning low and a high of seven and an advisory through the weekend, and the willow at the end of her doublewide drive was making the dry creak willows make at minus twenty when there was no wind. The window iced inside in the corner, a fresh half-inch of frost since yesterday. She put her hand on it and watched the print fade.

Hen was face-down in the toddler bed with Horse jammed under her armpit and her left fist closed around a corner of the blanket. The split in the corner of her upper lip had healed clean, two-year-old skin doing what two-year-old skin did, which was disappear an injury overnight, but the chip on the front tooth was the same chip, a sliver missing off the corner that the dentist in Bozeman had said would be fine and would chip-and-grow-out and was on a baby tooth anyway. Robin sat on the edge of the bed and put a hand on Hen's back.

"Bug."

Hen made the long flat refusal hum.

"I know."

She got Hen out the door and into the Camry through the back porch this time because she could not look at the front step yet. The door of the Camry was frozen to the frame and she used her hip on it again and it gave on the second try, and Hen watched her from her arms with the bored face of a person who had seen this trick before.

"Don't tell anybody."

"Mama," Hen said.

"Yeah."

Mrs. Thornberg took Hen at the door and handed Robin a cup of coffee like she did every morning.

"How's the tooth."

"Healed where it could heal. The chip is the chip."

"Drive easy."

The post office was warm in its accidental way. Pete had pulled her out of the Friday Grumman and put her in a different green Long Life Vehicle with the same right-hand drive and the same heater that worked when it felt like it. He handed her the bin without looking up.

"Long johns."

"Two pairs."

"Don't get cute."

She loaded the bin. She let the engine come up for nine minutes by the dash clock and watched her breath turn white in the cab and thought about nothing in particular. Then she put the truck in gear.

She had seen a black diesel on the county road Saturday afternoon coming the other way past the doublewide, and she had registered the wide-brim hat at the wheel and the cattle-guard mailbox forty miles north and made the equation in less time than it took to brake at the four-way. He had not been on errands generally; he had been on the specific errand of looking. She had decided over a long Sunday of laundry that she would not do anything with it. She would do her route. She would put his mail in his box. She would not stand at his cattle guard and accept thermos coffee and have the conversation.

That was Wednesday morning's plan.

The cab radio crackled at the four-way south of Pray. Pete, dry through the speaker.

"Heater."

"On."

"Stays on."

She clicked off and drove. She did the town walk after county and the early-afternoon county boxes after that, and by the time she came down the county road toward Galloway her hands had stopped feeling much of anything inside the gloves.

Galloway Road was a paler grey than it had been Monday. The gravel was buried under packed snow that had not given up any of itself in three days, and the bend at the willows where she had gone off was sanded now in a fresh stripe she could see from the top of the rise, a pickup load of grit thrown across the curve. Somebody had done that. The post office sand truck did not run Galloway. She did not let herself think about who.

She came around the bend at twenty-five, slow, and the truck took it like a truck. She let her shoulders down a quarter inch.

His drive was at the cattle guard fifty yards past the bend. A black diesel was parked broadside at the gate, same as Friday. Behind it, at an angle, was the same diesel with a plow blade pinned to the front frame, the blade angled down clean and the chains coiled and clipped and the truck running. He was not at the gate. He was up the drive, walking toward the barn with his hat on and his hands in the pockets of the sheepskin coat and a green canvas grocery sack hanging from one wrist.

Robin pulled up at the box. She rolled the right-side window down two inches because the truck was hot inside and the cab fogged if she did not, and she cut the engine and got out with his bundle in her gloved hand.

A parcel from the feed-store catalogue. An envelope from a tack supply. One thick handwritten letter from a Bozeman address she remembered from Friday's bundle. She slid them into the box and shut the flag. She turned to walk back to the truck.

He had seen her. She could tell from the angle of his shoulders up the drive, the quiet adjustment of his line that brought him on a course back toward the gate without his speeding up about it. He had a piece of bailing twine in one hand now that he did not have a minute ago, and he was using it to fasten the canvas sack closed as he walked.

She got in the truck. She closed the door clicking and not slamming. She put her hands on the wheel and made herself breathe out.

He came down the drive at the speed of a man who had decided the day was long enough for both of them. He came up to the driver-side window and he stopped the polite three feet back, Friday's same three feet, and he tipped his chin.

"Morning."

"Morning."

His face did the private accounting again, the angle of her jaw, her hands on the wheel and not in her lap, the cab heater that was not running. He did not ask anything yet. He set the canvas sack on the snow at his boot and pulled a glove off with his teeth and reached into the inside of the sheepskin coat and brought out a steel thermos.

"I made too much."

"I'm working."

"The kid all right."

She did not answer for a count. He had no way of knowing about Hen. He had no way of knowing there was a kid. The receipt from Mrs. Thornberg's had been in the bag he handed her Friday and she had not noticed if any of it had fallen out, and she did not know what he had seen of the inside of the bag while she had her hands on the steering wheel and her eyes on the willows. She decided, in the half second she had, that she was not going to ask how he knew.

"She's fine."

"All right."

"Why."

"You looked like a person who had been up some."

She let that go, because she had been up some, and a man who had pulled her out of a ditch four days ago was allowed to notice that, and the cost of saying *I'm fine, why are you looking* was higher than the cost of not.

He put the thermos cap on the hood of her truck. He poured. The steam came up out of the cup like Friday and the smell of it came across the cold air to her in the cab, coffee and a little chicory and milk that had been in the thermos long enough to be the same temperature as everything else.

"Take it for the road. Five minutes."

"I'm working."

"Take it for the road. You can hand it back at the gate Monday."

She looked at him. He had the wide-brim black felt hat on against the cold, pulled lower than Friday, and the sheepskin coat collar was up under his jaw, and his bare ungloved hand was steady on the cap of the thermos in a way she did not want to be charmed by.

She took the cup.

"Thank you."

"Yeah."

She drank. It was hot and it was good and it was better than Mrs. Thornberg's, which she would not say to either of them.

He did not stand and watch her drink it. He bent and picked up the canvas sack and set it on the running board of his diesel and untied the bailing twine, working with his back half to her, giving her the cup in privacy. She watched him in the side mirror and then looked away because watching him was not on Wednesday's plan either.

"Cold snap's got two more days in it."

"I heard."

"Was going to ask. I've got the plow on. It's a fifteen-minute job. You point me at it, I'll do your drive while you're on the route, you come home at four thirty and the drive's done. No charge. I'm doing four other people's today anyway."

She drank.

"I'm fine."

"Know you're fine."

"I'm fine."

He did not push. He nodded once, small, the same nod he had given her Friday when she had not taken the thermos the first time, and he did not lift the thermos like a man tipping a hat this time because she was holding the cup of it. He rolled the canvas sack closed around what looked like a twenty-pound bag of black-oil sunflower seed and lifted it onto the bed of the diesel.

"Suit yourself."

She drank the cup down. She handed it back through the window. He took it, screwed the cap back on without making a thing of it, slid the thermos into the inside pocket of the sheepskin coat.

"Drive easy."

"I will."

She started the engine. He stepped back from the door. She put the truck in gear and pulled out around the front of his diesel and onto Galloway and headed back up the gravel toward the county road, and her hands were not shaking on the wheel and her face was warm from the cup of coffee and she was already telling herself it had been nothing, the coffee, the offer, the no.

She got two miles.

There was a sound from the passenger side. A small, considered sound, the kind of throat-clearing a person makes when they have walked into a room they intend to stay in.

She glanced.

A cat was on the passenger seat.

He was orange and white and big with the slack heft of old cats, with a black nose and a tail laid neatly across his front paws and a left ear that was not so much an ear any more as the suggestion of one, the upper half clipped off in some fight years ago and healed to a soft scallop. He had eyes the colour of a day-old yolk. He was looking at her with the unfussed attention of a man at a poker table considering his hand.

Robin took her foot off the gas. The truck rolled. She put it in park on the shoulder where the snow was packed enough to take the weight, and she set the brake and turned in the seat.

"No."

The cat blinked at her. Slowly.

"Out."

He did not move. He put his chin down on his front paws by an eighth of an inch, which she read as settling rather than threatening, and he did not break eye contact.

"How did you," she said.

The window. The window was down two inches on the passenger side. She had cracked it at the box and not closed it because she had been thinking about a man with a thermos and not about the cab, and the cat had decided, in the count it had taken her to put mail in a box and refuse a plow and drink a cup of coffee, that the cab of a Long Life Vehicle with the heater off was still warmer than wherever he had come from.

"You're Earl."

The cat closed his eyes.

She had heard the name once, from Pete Raymond on Friday. *I'll send Earl out from maintenance.* Earl was a man in the maintenance shed, a post-office mechanic, a person Pete called when a truck was in a ditch. She had not connected Earl to a cat because nobody had told her there was a cat, but the cat had a name on him, an animal-name that arrived with the animal, and she knew Earl as plainly as she knew the rancher north of Pray tipped at Christmas.

"Earl," she said. "I have a route."

He did not respond to *route*.

She thought about it. The diesel was two miles back. She could turn the truck around and hand him out the window to a man in a sheepskin coat and have a second conversation she had budgeted no minutes for. Or she could finish her route. The route had two stops left. She could drop Earl at the cattle guard heading back, since she was coming back anyway.

She put the truck in gear.

"You stay there."

He stayed there.

She drove the next two miles with the cat on the passenger seat. He did not stand up when she took the curves. He did not protest the heater coming on. He did not make any sound at all except, once, a soft chuff when she hit a washboard, the kind of half-cough an old animal made when his ribs got asked to do a thing they had not consented to, and she said *sorry* before she had time to think about saying it.

The last stop was a place called the Engebretson barn, where there was no Mr. or Mrs. Engebretson any more and the box stood by itself at the head of a drive that nobody had used since October. She put the catalogue and the flyer in. She turned the truck around. She came back toward Galloway with the cat asleep on the passenger seat.

Her phone rang on the dash.

She checked the screen. Mrs. Thornberg's House Daycare.

"Hello."

"Hen wants to talk."

"Okay."

A small breath on the other end. Then Hen, in the deliberate diction of a person who had decided to make a phone call and was performing the role.

"Mama."

"Hey, bug."

The thinking-pause Hen used for holding Horse up to a receiver, which Robin knew because she had done it.

"What's Horse doing."

A long considered breath, the kind that meant *bath* because Hen had been asking since Sunday.

"Horse needs a bath?"

A small affirming hum, Hen's long-flat yes.

"Okay. We'll do it tonight."

A breath. Then in a smaller, regular voice: "Mama."

"Yeah, baby."

A small plastic sound of the receiver moving away from her face.

The phone went back to Mrs. Thornberg, who was laughing the laugh she had for productions. "She has been telling me for an hour."

"Thank you for calling."

"She's good. We're good. Drive easy."

Robin set the phone on the dash. She looked at the cat, who had opened his yellow eyes once during the call and closed them again, and she said *Horse needs a bath* to the cat, who did not weigh in.

She came back to the cattle guard at twelve-oh-six.

The black diesel was gone. The drive looked freshly bladed at the entrance, a clean cut from the cattle guard up forty feet to the first turn, where the snow piled on either side in the soft sweeps a plow blade made when the operator knew what he was doing. He was off plowing somebody else's drive on Galloway. He had said four others.

She rolled the passenger window down. She turned in the seat.

"Off you get."

Earl opened his eyes. He looked at her, then at the open window. He stood up slowly, an old cat gathering his legs underneath him in a sequence, and he looked at her one more time as if to confirm the terms, and then he stepped off the passenger seat onto the dash and from the dash onto the windowsill and from the windowsill out the window and onto the snow in a long slow settling, all four paws set down before he committed his weight, and then he walked away across the cattle guard and up the drive toward the cabin in the middle of the road like a ranch hand going to lunch.

"Bye, Earl."

He did not look back.

She rolled the window up. She watched him walk until he was out of sight at the first turn of the drive, and then she put the truck in gear and drove.

The county road was clear black asphalt. The afternoon went without further incident. She did the last of the town walk by a quarter to three. She had the bin back at the cage by three forty. Pete Raymond looked at her over the top of a parcel.

"You good."

"I'm good."

"Galloway."

"Galloway."

"Truck."

"Fine."

"Cat."

She looked up. Pete had gone back to the parcel, tagging it. He did not look at her.

"You knew."

"Knew Earl had been coming down Galloway looking for a ride since Tom went. Didn't know he'd found one."

"He's old."

"He's old."

"He gets out at the cattle guard."

Pete tagged the parcel. "He'll be back tomorrow."

She did not have a reply for that. She got Hen at four forty. Hen presented Horse to her at the door of Mrs. Thornberg's, and Robin said *bath* and Hen pressed Horse against her own chest with the satisfaction of a person who had been waiting for an outcome and had received it.

Robin came around onto her own street and pulled into the doublewide drive.

The drive was iced. The drive had been iced since Friday. The drive was iced now because she had said no to the plow this morning, and yesterday morning, and Monday morning, and she could see in the slant of the porch light along the surface that the drive was worse today than it had been yesterday, a glassy sheen across the ten feet between the parked Camry and the porch step where the runoff had come down in a thin sheet during the brief Sunday warm-up and had refrozen into something her own boots would not trust.

She lifted Hen out of the seat. She held her on her hip. She walked the ten feet to the porch step at the speed of a person who had decided she was not going to fall, and she did not fall, and she went up the step and got the door open and got them both inside and shut the door behind her and stood in the dark front room with Hen on her hip and the heat at fifty-eight and the cold coming off the window glass at her face.

She set Hen down. Hen ran to the bedroom for Horse without being asked.

Robin stood in the front room and looked at her own hands.

She had said no four times. She had said it Monday, when he had not asked yet but when she had said it inside her head before he could. She had said it Tuesday, though Tuesday he had not been at the gate. She had said it this morning to his face. She had said it again across the cab when he asked the second time.

She had said no because she was not going to owe him the conversation, and because she was not going to be a project, and because she had been getting through this drive all winter and she would get through this drive all winter, and a man with a plow blade and a thermos was not going to be the reason she stopped getting through it.

She also had a two-year-old daughter who was going to walk on this drive every morning for the next eleven days.

She put the back of her wrist against her eyes for a count. She lowered it.

Hen came out of the bedroom dragging Horse by one leg and held him up to Robin's knee with both hands and waited. The brown front of the stuffed horse had a streak of something that looked like yogurt and a streak of something that looked like dirt.

"Yeah, bug. Bath."

She put Horse in the kitchen sink in a quarter inch of warm water and a drop of dish soap. She watched Hen agitate Horse with a serious two-year-old hand. She thought about the bag of cat litter under the sink that she could throw across the worst sheet of the drive, and she did the cat-litter thing because she had it and could do it before the dish soap was rinsed out of Horse, and when she came back inside her hands were aching from the cold and Hen was wringing Horse out one-handed with the careful mimic-grip Robin had shown her for washcloths, and Horse was darker and smaller and Hen looked up and held him out, dripping.

"Mama."

"Yeah, baby. Horse is wet. We'll dry him."

She wrapped Horse in a hand towel. She gave him back. Hen took him with both hands, with the gravity of a person reclaiming a thing that had been held in trust, and went down the hall with him.

Robin stood at the kitchen sink and looked at the dish-soap suds on the side of the basin and at the ice on the inside corner of the window and at her own face faintly in the dark glass.

She thought about the cat on the passenger seat.

The cat had decided.

She did not, for the rest of the evening, think about the man.
