She was not going to go.

She told herself this Wednesday morning at five forty-eight at the kitchen window, with the thermometer on the porch reading negative fifteen, and she had been telling herself since Friday afternoon when her body had decided what temperature it was going to run at and the temperature had not changed. The cold-fury temperature. Absolute zero, no theatrics. She had lost Friday morning to it after the route, sitting at the kitchen table not eating a sandwich, and she had lost Friday afternoon to it pretending to fold laundry, and the weekend had folded itself around the temperature.

Hen had decided otherwise.

Mrs. Thornberg had brought out a snowsuit at pickup time Tuesday from the back closet, navy with eight pale horses cantering across the chest, donation-bag from the Hollenback girl, Hen's size for a week, and Hen had touched a horse on the front of it with one finger and looked at Robin with the long unblinking seriousness of a two-year-old who has registered an opportunity. Hen had carried it home in her arms in the back seat. Hen had slept in it.

This morning Hen was in it again, with Horse pressed into the collar where the wool came up under her chin, and Hen was at the door with her boots on and was waiting.

Robin put her own boots on.

"All right, bug. We're going."

She had been telling herself she was not going for four days, and she was going.

She got Hen into the Camry at six fifty-six. The drive to Mrs. Thornberg's was dark and the road was sand and salt rim on the curbs. Hen pressed her forehead to Robin's collarbone on the porch with the small humming sound she made when she was being warm and wanted to be warmer, and Robin held the back of Hen's head with one gloved hand for a count and carried her down the steps.

The route was a route. The first fourteen mailboxes went in the rhythm she did them in. Pete had her in the wagon again. The Subaru held the gravel. The willow bend was sanded fresh.

Wes's diesel was at the cattle guard.

He had been there every morning since Thursday last week, including the Monday she had said *no* and Tuesday when she had walked past him on Mrs. Thornberg's salted walk without looking. He was at the back of the truck with the blue thermos on the tailgate. He was in the navy Carhartt because the sheepskin was on the arm of her couch where it had been for nine days running.

He saw her come up. He did not lift his hand.

She rolled the Subaru up to the box. He came over with the thermos. Lid loose. Steam took the wind sideways inside three feet.

"Morning."

"Morning."

He held the cup out.

She had not drunk from it Monday. She had not drunk from it Tuesday. Both mornings she had taken her own thermos from the passenger seat and said *I'm good, thank you* in a voice that was nothing, and he had nodded and capped his thermos and stepped back.

She looked at the cup he was holding out.

She took it.

She did it without looking at him and without thanking him, and she took two swallows, and she handed it back. He capped it. He set it on the back of the Subaru. He did not say anything. He waited.

She got the bundle out and put it on the lid of the box. A propane bill. A Wyoming Christmas card from Marlowe again. A USDA notice for the boarding stable.

"All right," she said.

"All right."

She got back in. He stepped back from the door. She pulled away with the cattle guard rattling under the tires and the heater on low, and she did not look in the side mirror, and she registered halfway down the gravel that she had taken the coffee, and she did not name what the taking had been.

Earl was on the passenger seat.

He had let himself in while the door was open, and he had taken the seat, and he was sitting on his haunches with his half-ear toward her and his whole one toward the dash vent, the way a senior cat sits when a senior cat has decided about an arrangement.

"Out."

He did not look at her.

She pulled over at the bend before the Heaton place and put the wagon in park and reached across him and got the door open. The cold came in. Earl did not move. She put her gloved hand under his belly, and he allowed the hand the way a king allows a thing he could refuse, and she set him down in the snow at the side of the gravel.

He sat down in the snow. He looked at the open door.

"No."

She drove the rest of the route with the heater on low and the seat warm where Earl had been, and she registered that warmth too, and she did not name that either.

Mrs. Thornberg was at the door at three thirty when Robin came to pick Hen up.

"You going tonight."

"Hen wants to."

"I'm going at six with Loretta. Bringing a pan of corn pudding I been on since seven. You bringing anything."

"Rolls from the IGA."

"That'll do."

She helped Hen into the snowsuit with the horses on it, and Hen walked herself out to the Camry on the salted walk, careful of her boots, slow, the Hen-careful of a person whose tooth had reminded her about ice the week before.

The Hollenback place was twenty-two miles south on the county road, into the valley proper, where the Yellowstone curved into the cottonwoods and the ranch fence lines ran clean across the bench. The barn was the same barn that had hosted the Fourth of July dance two summers running, which Robin had not been to either summer, and the barn had been decorated for Christmas since November, with the same string lights along the eave and red and green bunting on the roll-up door. The propane jet engine of a heater was already going when she pulled up. She could hear it from the parking pasture before she had her key out of the ignition.

She got Hen out. Hen went on foot in the snowsuit with the horses on it, holding Robin's left hand with her right mitten and Horse against her chest with her left arm. They went up the path to the barn through the hay-bale lanes the Hollenback boys had set out, and the door was open with steam coming out of it, and the noise inside was the regular potluck noise of ninety people in a heated barn at six fifteen on a December night.

The Hollenback grandmother was at the door with a clipboard like a woman who had been doing the door since 1976.

"Robin McKee."

"Yes ma'am."

"And this is."

"This is Hen."

"How do, Hen. We've got a real horse named Sissy out behind the barn for the kids. Coats on the rack. Chile's at the far end."

Robin got Hen's snowsuit off down to the long-johns and the wool socks and the boots that Hen would not part with because the boots were the principle of the thing. She hung the snowsuit on the rack. She kept her own Carhartt on for a count of two before she clocked that she was the only adult still in her coat in the barn, and she took it off and hung it next to the snowsuit. The orange knit hat went in her coat pocket.

Inside, she registered the room without looking like she was registering it.

Wes was at the far end by the chile pots talking to one of the Hollenback boys about hay prices. He had on a clean collared shirt under a wool vest she had not seen before, and his hat was off and on a peg behind him. He looked at her once across the barn. He lifted his chin a quarter of an inch. He went back to the conversation.

She did not lift hers back. She turned and walked Hen down the long table.

The chile was in two cast-iron pots on a propane burner, green on the left and red on the right, and the Hollenback granddaughter was ladling. Robin got a bowl of green for herself and a piece of cornbread for Hen.

Hen pointed at the green pot.

"More."

"That's chile, bug. Spicy."

"More."

The granddaughter laughed. She got down a small ramekin and put a half-spoon of green in it from the top where the broth was thinnest, blew on it, set it on the table edge. Hen dipped a corner of the cornbread in it and put the cornbread in her mouth on the right side, because the right side was the chewing side now and had been since Thursday last. Hen chewed. Hen looked up at Robin without changing her face, and Robin gave her another piece with a bigger dip.

Robin stood up with her own bowl in one hand and Hen's small mittened hand in the other, and she turned, and Cash and Deb Hollander were three feet from her at the corner of the table.

Deb looked at her. Deb had on a green flannel shirt over a long-sleeved henley and a barn-coat draped over the back of the chair behind her. She had her hand around a beer. Robin had seen her once at the post office in October and once in the IGA in November and had nodded both times and not stopped to talk, because Robin did not stop to talk in the IGA, and Deb had nodded back the same way, which Robin had filed under *fine*.

"You're the carrier."

"I'm the carrier."

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"Deb. This is Cash."

"Cash."

"Hi, Robin."

Cash had a kind face and a wide smile and the kind of solid frame a man got working horses for thirty years, and he had a beer in one hand and a plate with three different kinds of meat on it in the other, and he was the kind of man who acknowledged a kid before he acknowledged the kid's mother.

"And who's this," he said.

"This is Hen."

"How do, Hen."

Hen looked at Cash. She looked at his beer. She did not say anything, which was Hen's way of working out whether a stranger was a Hen-stranger or a Mama-stranger, and the working out took the count it took. Cash waited the count. He did not crouch down at her, which Robin clocked. He let Hen do her own assessing.

Deb tipped her bottle at Robin's head.

"You know your hat is the only orange object in this barn."

Robin had her orange knit hat in her coat pocket, and the coat was on the rack behind her. Deb had clocked the coat and the hat and registered it at thirty feet.

"Yeah."

"I mean it as a compliment."

"All right."

"It's a good hat."

"Made it myself."

"That's what I figured."

Deb took a swallow. She did not say anything else about the hat, because Deb was the kind of woman who said one observation and let it be one observation, and Robin had registered her at thirty feet too. Cash said *we'll see you around* and put his free hand briefly on Deb's lower back, and they moved on to the cornbread.

Robin breathed once.

Hen tugged her hand.

"More."

She took Hen back to the chile pot. The granddaughter laughed and gave Hen another half-spoon. Robin ate her own bowl of green standing two feet away with Hen at her hip eating cornbread crumbs out of her fist, and the chile was good, and she ate it, and she did not look down the barn.

Mrs. Thornberg came in at six forty with a Pyrex of corn pudding wrapped in three dish towels. She set it on the warming end of the table, came down to Robin, crouched on her bad knee with the small grunt she always made.

"Peanut. Look at you. Horses."

Hen pressed Horse against her own chin.

"More."

"More horses, that's right."

Mrs. Thornberg straightened up the slow way and looked at Robin. The look was not a question. It was the look she had given Robin at the door the morning of the chipped tooth.

"You came."

"Hen wanted to."

"That's a good reason." She paused. Her hand was on the edge of the table. "I had a good Monday at five-oh-six."

"Norma."

"That's all I'm saying."

"Okay."

"Just that."

Robin did not say anything. Her chest did the shift it had done at the front door of the doublewide on Thursday a week ago, and she let it shift, and she did not name it.

Mrs. Thornberg patted her elbow once and went to find Loretta.

The barn warmed up over the next hour. People came in with pans and pies and crock-pots and the kind of casserole dishes that had been doing this since the Reagan administration. Two fiddles started up on the bench by the door at seven, easy stuff, no pressure. The Hollenback boys had set up a low daybed in the back room of the barn under the eave with three quilts on it and a stuffed bear and a basket of board books, because the Hollenback grandmother had been doing the potluck since 1976 and had clocked early on that two-year-olds peaked at eight thirty regardless of what their mothers had planned.

Hen peaked at eight twenty-two.

She had eaten chile twice and a piece of pumpkin pie and most of a roll, and she had walked herself out to see the real horse named Sissy in the lean-to behind the barn with three other kids and had touched Sissy's nose with a serious mittened hand, and she had been carried back inside on Robin's hip with Horse pressed into the wool of Robin's sweater, and she had said *more* at the chile pot once more without enthusiasm because she was running out, and her head had gone down on Robin's shoulder.

Robin walked her to the back room.

The Hollenback granddaughter was in there already, settling another two-year-old on the daybed, a Hollander cousin's kid by the look of him. She lifted a quilt and patted the spot next to him, and Robin laid Hen down with Horse in the crook of her arm, and Hen turned her face to the quilt and was gone in under a minute. The granddaughter winked.

"I'm in here every twenty checking. She's good."

"Thank you."

"Go get a piece of pie."

Robin came out of the back room and stood at the edge of the main barn and looked at the floor for a count.

She did not have a piece of pie.

She had, instead, a problem she had been carrying since Thursday a week ago and had not put down. The problem was a man at the end of the long table in a clean collared shirt who had been at the cattle guard every morning since, who had not once said her name, who had held the thermos out every morning with the lid loose, and Mrs. Thornberg had said *I had a good Monday at five-oh-six* with her hand on the edge of the table, and the temperature in Robin's chest had been moving for three days in a way she had not let herself register.

The cold-fury had been a real thing on Monday. She had been right to be cold-furious on Monday. He had called her daycare with a story that was not true, and Robin had been three years past having a man tell her stories, and the temperature had been the right one for Monday.

It was not the right one for Wednesday.

She walked the long way around the barn so it would not look like she was walking to him. She stopped at the pie table. She picked up a piece of pumpkin and a fork and held it for a count and put it back down. She walked past the pie table to the corner under the eave by the tack room door, where the propane heater's noise was less and the fiddles were less, and where the room was darker because the string lights did not reach.

He saw her go. He finished what he was saying to the Hollenback boy. He did not hurry. He came down the barn at the unhurried pace of a man who had been on his feet outside since before light, and he stopped at the edge of the dark corner with his hands at his sides, and he waited.

She did not look up.

"I was going to wait till you were ready," he said.

"I know."

"I figured I'd be at the gate every morning and you'd let me know."

"Yeah."

"I'm not going to apologise for the daycare thing here."

"Norma cleared that up."

"All right."

"She cleared it up Monday at three-forty."

"She told me."

"Yeah."

He stood with his hands at his sides. He had taken his vest off. He had on the collared shirt with the cuffs rolled twice and his forearms brown from the year he had spent helping his uncle, and his hair was flat on one side from the hat. He looked at her face.

"I'm going to apologise for the cattle guard."

"All right."

"I asked you on Monday inside seven days of meeting you."

"Yeah."

"I asked you the morning after you watched your kid walk past me on Norma's walk without looking at me, which was the first morning you'd had off the cold-fury since Thursday before, and I didn't give you the day."

"No."

"I'd been thinking about asking you since Thursday before. I had a plan for the timing. The plan was Monday, regardless of whether Monday was the right Monday. Tate told me to wait the weekend and ask you Monday at the earliest. I took *Monday at the earliest* and ran with it without looking at you when I did."

"Tate."

"My cousin. I called him Friday."

"Okay."

"He told me to ask once and accept the answer."

"You did."

"I asked. I accepted."

"Yeah."

She did not say anything else. She had one hand inside the front of her sweater holding her own forearm, and her socks were warm on the barn floor where the heat had pooled, and the propane heater was making the steady throb it had been making all night.

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"I'm sorry I asked the morning I asked. I was running a clock that wasn't yours."

"Okay."

"Yeah."

The fiddles changed key on the bench by the door. Somebody laughed near the chile pots. Somebody else was singing a low harmony two registers under the fiddle and not in any hurry about it.

Her chest did the shift again, and this time she let it have the room it wanted.

She looked up at his face.

His face was not waiting for anything. It was the face he had worn at the cattle guard every morning since Thursday before. Steady. There. Not pleading, not anxious, not hopeful in a way she would have to manage. He had been wearing the face for nine days and she had been clocking it for nine days, and it was the face of a man who had been inside one decision for nine days and was not going to be talked out of it by her bad week.

She closed the four inches between them.

She did it without saying anything because she did not have a sentence ready. She put her left hand flat on the front of his collared shirt where the buttons were, and she felt his breath stop under her palm for the count of a beat, and she went up on her toes and put her mouth on his.

His hands stayed at his sides for the first half-second. Then his right hand came up and his palm was at her jaw with the heat of a man who had been outside in his shirtsleeves and was warm anyway, and his thumb touched the corner of her mouth, and his mouth opened against hers a quarter inch, and she tasted woodsmoke and the last of the green chile and a cleanness of mint under it, and she tasted nine days of a man not asking, and she tasted the temperature of her own chest finally moving.

She held it for a count.

She stepped back.

He let her. His right hand came down. His face did the thing his face did, which was not move much, and the not-moving was where the seeing happened.

"Yes ma'am," he said.

She breathed in for four. She breathed out for six.

She looked at him for another count.

She was waiting for the next thing he was going to say. She was waiting for the *do you want to* or the *can I* or the *what is this to you*, and she had her own answer half-ready, half-not, the *I don't know yet* waiting on her tongue like a hard candy, and she would have given it to him plain.

He did not say a next thing.

He stood there with his hands back at his sides and his face doing the steady not-moving he had been doing for nine days, and the *yes ma'am* sat between them in the dark corner of the barn like a hat set down on a tailgate, and he looked at her, and he waited.

She waited too.

Neither of them said anything.

The fiddles played something that resolved on a long held note. Somebody's kid laughed somewhere in the main room. Mrs. Thornberg's voice called *Loretta* from the chile end.

"I have to get Hen home," Robin said.

"All right."

"She's been down forty minutes."

"All right."

"I should go."

"Yeah."

He did not move to come with her. He did not say *I'll walk you out*. He stood in the dark corner with his hands at his sides, and his face did not do anything she could pin down, and she turned and walked the long way around the barn back to the rack.

She got her Carhartt on. She got the orange hat out of the pocket and put it on. She walked back to the back room and crouched down and lifted Hen up off the daybed with Horse tucked into the crook of Hen's arm, and Hen made the small sound of a sleeping toddler being lifted and pressed her forehead to Robin's collarbone the way she did, and Robin walked Hen up the long table to the door.

The Hollenback grandmother was at the door with her clipboard.

"Heading out."

"Yeah."

"Drive easy."

"I will."

She went out into the cold. The wind had come down off the bench and the sky had cleared and the stars were the kind of stars you got at twenty below with no moon, and she walked the path through the hay-bale lanes with Hen against her chest and her own breath making white in front of her face.

She got to the Camry and got the back door open and the car seat ready and was bending Hen toward it when she heard the diesel start up behind her at the far end of the parking pasture.

She did not look.

She got Hen in. She buckled. She kissed Hen's forehead under the hood of the snowsuit. She shut the back door clicking and not slamming. She walked around the back of the Camry to the driver's side.

He was at her door.

He had come the long way around the parking pasture in his shirtsleeves and the wool vest and his hat on, and he had the sheepskin coat folded over his left forearm, and he was not blocking the door, just at it, and his face was the same face it had been in the dark corner of the barn.

"You'll freeze your hands on the wheel before the cab warms up."

"Wes."

"Take the coat home."

She looked at him. She looked at the coat. She had left his coat on the arm of her couch for nine days.

"I'll get it back to you."

"Sure."

He stepped to her left and put the sheepskin across her shoulders, the same two-handed shrug he had done at the cattle guard the first time and the second time, and the collar came up under her ear, and the inside of the coat was warm against the back of her neck because he had been wearing it on the walk across the parking pasture, and the smell was the same. Hay. A wood fire. The faint diesel that lived on the cuffs.

"Drive easy."

"I will."

He stepped back. He did not say the next thing this time either. He stood in the parking pasture with his hands in his vest pockets and his hat on, and he watched her get in, and he did not lift a hand when she pulled out, and she did not lift hers, and she drove home in the coat with Hen asleep in the back and the heater working a slow cycle and her own face doing something she would not have been able to name if anybody had asked.

She got home at ten oh-six.

She got Hen in. She got the snowsuit off. She got the boots off. She kept Horse tucked in. She got Hen under the quilt and sat on the edge of the bed for a count of four with her hand on Hen's back, and the small machinery breathed along, and Hen was already gone.

She turned off the lamp. She pulled the door to two inches.

She came out into the front room.

The arm of the couch was empty for the first time in nine days, because the coat was on her shoulders. She stood in the front room in her socks and held her own elbows inside the coat, and she walked the coat across to the front door.

There was a wooden peg by the front door that the previous tenant had put up for a baseball cap and a leash, and Robin had used it for her own Carhartt every winter for three years, and the Carhartt was on the back of the kitchen chair tonight because she had not put it on the peg when she came in.

She took the coat off her shoulders.

She hung the sheepskin on the peg.

She did not fold it on the couch. She did not set it on the chair. She put it where her own coat went.

She stood at the peg for a count of a breath with one hand flat against the front of the coat where the wool was, and then she went into the kitchen and washed her own bowl from breakfast, and she locked the door, and she turned off the porch light, and she stood at the sink in the dark with the thermometer outside reading negative seventeen and the cold holding flat against the window.

She put two fingers against the corner of her own mouth, and the corner of her mouth was warm.

She left her hand there.

In the front room behind her, on the wooden peg by the door, the sheepskin coat hung in the dark with the collar still holding the shape of her shoulders.
