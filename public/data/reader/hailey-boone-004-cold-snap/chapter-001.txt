The thermostat in the doublewide read sixty-two and Robin let it stay there. The propane bill from November was paper-clipped to the fridge as a reminder, and it was not the kind of reminder you argued with at five forty-five on a Monday morning.

She got the coffee going and stood in front of the kitchen window in her socks, watching the porch light catch on what the radio had said was minus eighteen and what her face would tell her was something different in about twenty minutes. The window was iced on the inside corners. The propane truck would be by Thursday and she would do the math then. For now there was Hen to bundle, the route to drive, the cold to walk through.

Hen was face-down in the toddler bed with one arm flung out and Horse upside down under her chin. The stuffed horse had been loved into a kind of laundered greyness that no longer responded to bleach. Robin sat on the edge of the bed and put a hand on Hen's back and felt her warm small machinery breathing along.

"Hey, bug."

Hen made the sound she made when she was being asked to do anything, which was a long flat hum of refusal that lasted as long as her two-year-old lungs could carry it.

"I know. I know. Up."

The snowsuit was the consignment one, the one a half size too big that they had paid eighteen dollars for at the kid place on Main in October, and Hen had decided in the last week that she preferred it to the smaller one because she could move her arms in big arcs inside the sleeves and it made her laugh. Robin zipped her in. Hen held Horse up to her own face and pressed nose to nose, which was a thing she did, and Robin stopped what she was doing for half a second to watch it.

Hen kicked one stocking foot out and pointed at the row of small boots by the door, and made the small impatient hum that meant Robin was being slow.

"Yeah, yeah."

She got Hen's boots on. She got her own boots on. She carried Hen and Horse and the diaper bag out to the Camry, which was a 2003 hatchback that had been on the road for about ten thousand miles longer than it should have been, and the door was frozen, and Robin set Hen down on the porch in the snow next to the boot scrape and used her hip to break the seal on the door.

Hen watched.

"Don't tell anybody I did that," Robin said, and Hen said *Mama* with the bored authority of a person who has heard her name called too many times that morning, and Robin lifted her into the car seat and clicked the buckle and shut the door.

Mrs. Thornberg's was a yellow house on the south side of Livingston with a garage that had been a garage and was now a warm-painted rec room with foam tiles on the floor, and the front porch was already lit at six-thirty. Mrs. Thornberg met them at the door in a sweater that had seen forty winters and a pair of slippers Robin coveted privately. She took Hen's mitten and led her in.

"Cold one."

"Yeah."

"You got the route."

"I got the route."

"Drive easy."

Robin nodded. Hen had already turned away into the rec room and was advancing on a basket of board books. Mrs. Thornberg gave Robin the small look that meant she would take her time letting the door close, and Robin walked back to the Camry and got in and breathed once through her teeth.

The post office on Park Street was warm by accident, with hot spots along the radiators and cold draughts under the front desk. Pete Raymond was at the sort cage in his cap and his fingerless gloves, and he handed her the route bin without looking up.

"Long johns?"

"Two pairs."

"Don't get cute."

She took the bin out to the lot where the Long Life Vehicle was parked with its right-hand drive and its government green and its specific flavour of dread, which Robin had been managing for six years by force of habit. The truck was a 1989 Grumman with the heater that worked when it felt like it and the door that froze if you slammed it instead of clicking it. She did not slam it. She got in. She put on her driving gloves, which were thin enough to feel a stamp through and warm enough to keep blood in her fingers for thirty minutes at a stretch, and she started the engine, and she let it warm for the four minutes the manual said and the eight she gave it on cold days, and then she put the truck into gear and drove.

The route was a hundred and seventy-one boxes across forty-three miles of paved and gravel road. She knew every dog along it, knew the three porches where the women came out on Wednesdays for the social security check, knew the rancher north of Pray who tipped at Christmas with an actual tin of caramels, knew the dirt drive on the Trail Creek loop where the coyote came out in the late afternoons with a lazy interest. She knew the gravel washboards. She knew where the ice pooled. She knew which mailbox flag was bent and which was just slow.

Galloway Road was last. Three miles of gravel that ran south off the county road through a draw, four mailboxes along it, the last one at the cattle guard at Tom Galloway's place. Tom Galloway had died in October. Robin had taken the obituary card down from the wall at the post office and put it in the file as they did, and she had thought about it once at his mailbox and not since.

The first three boxes on Galloway were quick. The Vassar place, two parcels and a stack of catalogues. The Kemmer place, a single flyer that Mrs. Kemmer would burn in the woodstove without opening. The Heaton place, nothing today, just a friendly empty box. The truck handled the gravel fine through the first mile and a half, and she had her hand loose on the wheel, and she was thinking about quesadillas for dinner and whether she could make the cheese stretch to Wednesday or whether she would have to stop at Albertson's on the way home, when the road dropped through the bend by the willows where the ice always pooled in the shade.

She knew the bend. She had taken it slow in worse weather than this.

The back end went first.

She steered into it. She did the thing she had been doing on this route for six years, which was nothing, which was let the truck find its own line, which was keep her hands light and her foot off the brake. The truck went anyway. The right rear caught the soft snow on the shoulder and the bank dropped ten feet into the seasonal ditch that ran along the willows, and the truck went over the edge nose-down, slow enough that she had time to think *no* twice, and settled into the snow at a forty-degree angle with the front bumper pressed into the ditch bottom and the rear axle high on the bank.

Engine still running. Mail still belted. Her own pulse loud in her ears.

She killed the engine. She sat there for a count of ten. She put both gloved hands flat on the wheel and watched her breath turn white in the cab and waited for her body to catch up with her brain.

Her brain said: *fine.* Her body said: *not yet.*

She had a small white scar at the left corner of her mouth from a horse fall when she was sixteen, and her tongue went to it, the way it did when she was thinking about something she did not want to think about. She held still. She let her body have its minute.

Then she said one word out loud that she would not have said in front of Hen, and she kicked the dashboard, which was a thing she had not done since she was nineteen, and which felt about as good as it had then.

Mail bin upright and the door not jammed. She tried the radio. The radio worked. She tried the post office on the line and Pete picked up on the third ring.

"Galloway Road," she said.

"Where on it."

"End. The bend by the willows."

"You hurt."

"No."

"Truck."

"In the ditch."

There was a silence on his end which she could read because she had worked with Pete Raymond for six years. It was the silence of a man who had been telling the postmaster they needed to send the Galloway run out in one of the all-wheel-drive rigs in this kind of weather and had been ignored.

"I'll send Earl out from maintenance."

"Earl's in Bozeman till tomorrow."

"Right." Another silence. "Stay with the truck. I'll figure it."

"Pete," she said. "I've got daycare at five."

"I know."

She hung up. She sat. The cab was already losing the heat the engine had been making. She put her hands inside her jacket. She counted her breath in for four and out for six and watched the willows on the bank above her and thought about how the snow was the kind of snow that fell when the air was just on the edge of being too cold for snow, fine and dry and not really falling so much as drifting sideways, and she was not crying, but her eyes were stinging from the cold and probably from something else.

Then somebody knocked on the passenger-side glass.

She turned her head.

A man in a black wide-brim felt hat was bent down at the window, sheepskin coat collar up under his jaw, breath fogging in front of his face. Behind him on the road, a diesel truck was idling with a chain on the hitch, and an older man was nowhere in sight because the older man was just him.

He made a small circling gesture with one gloved finger. *Roll it down.*

She rolled it down two inches.

"Morning," he said.

She did not say morning back.

He looked at the angle of the truck, the bank, the snow piled against the front bumper, and his face did the thing some men's faces did, which was a small private accounting that did not require her input. Then he looked at her.

"You okay."

"I'm fine."

"Anybody else in the truck."

"No."

"All right." He straightened up and looked at the bank above and the road behind her with the flat unsurprised attention of a man who had seen this kind of problem before, and then he bent back to the window. "I've got a chain. I can pull you out. Take twelve minutes."

"I'm waiting on the post office."

"You can wait on the post office sitting in the cab while I do this, or you can wait on the post office in my truck, which has heat. Either way, I'm pulling you out."

She looked at him. He had a long-healed broken nose that sat slightly crooked at the bridge, brown eyes, the kind of weather on his face that came from being outside for a job, not from a hobby. Maybe thirty-eight. He was not asking her permission. He was telling her what was about to happen, and underneath it he was waiting to see if she had any objection that mattered, which was a kind of asking, just not the kind that wanted to be talked back into anything.

"Who are you," she said.

"Wes Galloway. I live up the road."

She took half a second with that. *Galloway* was the dead man at the end of the road. Tom Galloway's nephew, then, the one from the rodeo, the one the Heatons had mentioned twice in November.

"Tom's nephew."

"Yeah."

"I'm sorry about your uncle."

"Thank you."

She let a beat go by. The cold was doing things to her thinking that she did not have time for. She looked past him at his diesel idling on the road, the chain coiled on the bumper, his weight slung onto one leg and his hands loose at his sides. He was not in a hurry. He was not waiting for her to be charming about it.

"All right," she said.

"All right."

He stepped back from the door and went up the bank in the long unhurried strides of a man who had been on his feet outside since before daylight, and Robin watched him go and put her gloves back on and sat in the cab and did not let herself think about anything that was not the immediate next thing.

He was back in three minutes with the chain. He went down on one knee in the snow at the front of the truck and looked underneath it for a long count, and then he hooked the chain to the frame at a spot she would not have picked and gave it two yanks to test it, and then he stood and brushed the snow off his thigh and walked back up to his diesel.

He leaned in the open window.

"Put it in neutral. Don't steer. I'll pull you straight back. Truck's gonna bounce when it hits the road. Let it."

"Okay."

"You okay."

"Yes."

He nodded once and walked away.

The diesel pulled. The chain came tight with the small singing sound that chains made under load. The Grumman moved an inch, then two, then a foot, and the rear axle cleared the bank with a heavy lurch and the front bumper pulled out of the snow with a slow scrape and the whole truck slid backward up the bank in a long unhurried arc, and then she was on the road, and the truck rocked once on its springs as he had said it would, and stopped.

He came back and unhooked the chain. She got out. Her legs were stiffer than she had expected. She stood in the road and watched him coil the chain with the same unfussed economy he had used to hook it, and she put her hands in her jacket pockets, and she looked at her truck and then back at the road, and her brain did the math she had been holding off on. The truck was upright. The truck had been driving fine before the bend. The truck would drive fine again. She had two more boxes left and forty minutes of paved road back to town.

"Truck's all right," he said.

"Yeah."

"Run her ten minutes before you take her on anything. Make sure nothing's complaining."

"I will."

He coiled the chain into the bed of his diesel and shut the tailgate and turned around with the thermos in his hand. It was a steel thermos with the paint chipped off the bottom from being set on tailgates, and the cap was a cup, and steam came up out of it when he twisted the lid.

"I made too much."

She looked at the thermos. She looked at his face. She looked down the road where her route was still waiting and at her watch which said it was eleven forty-three and at the cattle guard fifty yards away that meant she still had to put his mail in his box.

"I appreciate it," she said. "I'm working."

"Take it for the truck. Five minutes."

"I appreciate it. I'm going to keep going."

He looked at her for a count of two. Whatever he saw in her face made him lift the thermos a fraction, like a man tipping a hat, and then he screwed the lid back on without making a thing of it.

"All right."

"His mail," she said, and she was already walking back to the truck because she could feel her face doing something that was probably not anything but felt like everything, and she needed to not be standing still. She got the bundle out of the bin: a feed-store catalogue, a property tax notice, a Christmas card from a Bozeman address. She walked the bundle to the cattle guard and put it in the box and shut the flag down.

He had walked back to her open driver's-side door. He was holding the mail bag.

She had not even noticed him pick it up.

"This was on the floor."

"Right."

"Rest of it's in the bin."

"Yeah." She took the bag from him. "Thank you."

"Drive easy."

"I will."

She got in. She shut the door clicking and not slamming. She started the engine. She let it run a count that was less than ten minutes and more than thirty seconds, and she watched him in her side mirror walk back to the diesel without looking back, and then she put the truck into gear and pulled away.

He had said *drive easy*. He had not said *be careful* or *be safe*. He had said *drive easy*, which was what a horseman said to another horseman about a green colt, and what her father had said when he handed her the keys to the truck at sixteen, and it was the kind of *drive easy* that men who worked with their hands gave each other when they meant *I see what you're doing and I'm not going to insult you by pretending I don't*, and her hands were shaking on the wheel by the time she was a mile down the gravel.

Adrenaline, not the cold.

She stopped at the county road and put her forehead on the wheel for half a count and lifted it again. Her phone was on the dash. Aunt Ruth's number was the second one in the favorites under Mrs. Thornberg, and Aunt Ruth lived eleven minutes from where Robin was sitting and had a four-wheel-drive and would have come without saying anything, and Robin looked at the phone and thought about the conversation that would follow, the *honey, you have to let me help*, the *I worry about you out there alone with that baby*, the long slow Tuesday afternoon coffee at Aunt Ruth's that would lead to the part about Todd, which would lead to the part about Robin getting back out there, which would lead to Robin smiling at her aunt across a coffee mug and lying and saying she was thinking about it.

She did not pick up the phone.

She put the truck into gear and drove.

The county road was clear black asphalt with sand on the shoulders, and she took it at fifty-five and breathed and watched the sun move down through the cottonwoods and tried not to think about the man at the end of Galloway Road, which she did not entirely succeed at, but she got close enough to count it.

She came into Livingston at two forty. She finished the afternoon boxes on the town walk in an hour and twenty. She had the bin back in the cage at the post office at four-oh-five. Pete Raymond looked at her over the cage.

"You good."

"I'm good."

"You sure."

"Pete."

He held up both hands. "All right."

She drove to Mrs. Thornberg's with her hands at ten and two and her shoulders down from her ears for the first time in eight hours. Hen was at the play kitchen with a wooden spatula in her fist and a serious face, and Mrs. Thornberg was in the recliner reading the *Livingston Enterprise* like a woman in a movie about a grandmother. Robin signed Hen out. Hen pointed her wooden spatula at the door.

"Go."

"Yeah, bug. Go."

In the Camry on the way home, Hen fell asleep before the second stoplight. Robin drove the long way home down Geyser, past the Christmas lights on Madison strung from the eaves of the bungalows, past the elementary school where she would be applying for Hen's pre-K spot in two years if she stayed, past the corner where the sidewalk was always the worst sheet of ice in town. She carried Hen in. She did not wake her up for the last hundred yards of the day. She put her in the bed in the snowsuit because the house was sixty-two degrees and the snowsuit was warm and she was already asleep, and Hen made one small sound that meant nothing and went deeper.

Robin made quesadillas for one and ate them standing at the counter. She washed the pan. She turned off the porch light. She sat on the edge of her own bed in the dark with her hands on her knees for a count of four, and then she lay down in her clothes, which she had not done since Hen was a newborn, and she closed her eyes.

Twelve miles north, the lights at the end of Galloway Road stayed on late.
