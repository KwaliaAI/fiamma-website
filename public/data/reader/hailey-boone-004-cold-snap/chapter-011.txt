The thermometer on the side of the barn read thirty-six at two on Christmas Eve, and the air did the thing the radio had been promising for two days, which was the slow surrender of a snap.

Robin stood on the porch in her socks with the door half open and listened.

The eaves were going, not the slow drip of the morning but the steady run of a roof that had stopped pretending. A crow went over the spruce calling about it. Down the hill at the boarding paddock, two of the geldings had come out of the run-in shed and were standing with their noses up in the wind, and a third had walked to the south fence and was nudging at the snow there with a hoof, the nudge of a horse remembering there was grass.

She had spent two weeks listening to the cold. The cold was leaving. What came up through it was the wet smell of pine she had not smelled since October, and the boards under her socks giving back a heat they had not been giving back at sun-up, and the warm air at her back from the open kitchen door where Wes had a kettle on for the second time since lunch.

She closed her eyes and breathed in.

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"You'll get your socks soaked."

"They're already soaked."

"Come in."

She came in.

He was at the stove with the kettle pulled half off the burner, in the bibs and the henley and his socks. The kitchen smelled like coffee and like the loaf his cousin had brought up the gravel an hour ago wrapped in a tea towel, and now also like the wet pine coming through the window over the sink.

Hen was on the floor by the woodstove with Horse on her lap, sitting on the rag rug in her socks with one of Wes's wool socks pulled over her left forearm to her elbow because she had decided at breakfast that Horse needed a coat. Earl was on the woodstove side of the room watching Horse with the patient indignance of a cat who suspected Horse of plans.

Aunt Ruth had brought Hen up at noon. Ruth had set the duffle on the porch and said she was not coming in, and she had given Robin one long look across the cab and a nod that was not a wink and meant *I see you*, and she had backed down the drive.

Hen had walked into the cabin in her snowsuit and looked at the woodstove and looked at the kitchen and looked at Wes in the kitchen and said *Mama* and let Robin take the snowsuit off her, and she had been on the rug since.

Wes poured the water into the press.

"McClarens at four," he said.

"Yeah."

"Wyatt said come early."

"How early."

"Three thirty. Cash and Deb are coming over from Bozeman. Cash brought a bottle from a place in Idaho he wants his brother to taste."

"Mm."

"You all right with it."

"With Cash and Deb."

"With any of it."

She looked at him. He had asked the question without making it the kind of question that needed an answer to be made. He was looking at her face and at Hen on the rug and back at her, and he was waiting because he had decided to wait.

"Yeah," she said.

"All right."

She took the cup he had poured and sat at the round table. Hen looked up from Horse.

"Mama."

"Yeah, bug."

Hen pointed at the open kitchen door, at the porch, at the boards beyond.

"The snow's going. You want to go look."

Hen considered the question with the serious thoroughness she gave to all questions, and put Horse on the rug, and stood up.

***

They went to the McClaren ranch at three twenty in the diesel. Robin had Hen on her lap in the snowsuit because the diesel did not have a car seat, and Wes drove the eight miles slow, and the windshield was streaked with the wet that had been melting onto it off the spruce since noon.

Wyatt and June lived a mile off the county road past Pray. Cash and Deb were already there when the diesel came up the drive, the borrowed Subaru parked along the fence, and June was at the door in an apron with flour on it.

"Robin. Honey. Come in. Hen, sweetie."

Hen looked past June at the inside of the house and put a mittened hand against June's thigh in a way that meant she had a position about going in.

"All right, sugar. Come see."

Hen went in.

Dinner came on the way it came on at the McClaren table. A long pine board with mismatched chairs, the bird in a pan and not a turkey because Wyatt had been done with turkeys since 2019, and Wyatt said grace in the short flat run he had used at this table since his father had stopped being able to.

Hen ate two bites of mashed potato and drank water from a cup too big for her hands, and Deb sat next to her and ran a small narration about the rolls and the cranberry that had been Deb's grandmother's recipe, and Hen listened with her face flat and her eyes moving.

Robin sat between Wes and June. She did not say much. She did not need to. Cash had a long story about a colic that had turned out to be a piece of baling twine, telling it with his hands as much as his mouth, and the table laughed at the right places, and once Wes laughed beside her and she felt the laugh in her ribs because his arm was against hers under the table.

She was not going to perform the dinner. She ate the bird. She listened to her sister-in-law's laugh that was not a sister-in-law's laugh anymore, and at one point during the cranberry course June reached across and squeezed Robin's wrist once, no agenda, and Robin did not pull her wrist back.

They left at seven.

June walked them to the diesel. She had Hen in her arms because Hen had decided in the last hour that June was acceptable, and she set Hen in Robin's lap in the cab with the small unhurried care of a woman who had spent the dinner watching how Robin held her child.

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"Come Sunday too."

"I'll see how the day goes."

"Sunday too."

"All right."

June stepped back. Wes shut the door for her. They drove the eight miles back up the gravel with the heater on low and the diesel quiet in the cab, and Hen fell asleep on Robin's chest before they passed the bridge, and Robin held the back of Hen's head with the same hand she had used on Friday night before she had carried her out to Aunt Ruth's Tahoe, and the held head was the same head and the night was a different night.

She did not say anything about it. Wes did not either.

***

At Wes's they did not go straight in. He pulled the diesel up to the porch and shut the engine and looked at the barn down the hill in the wet grey of evening, and the lights were on in the barn because the timer was on a six-to-six and the lights had come up at six o'clock, and somewhere in the barn one of the geldings blew a long breath out into the lit space.

"You want," he said.

"I want."

"Hen."

"She'll come."

She woke Hen the small way she knew to wake her. She set Hen on her hip in the snowsuit, and they walked down the gravel to the barn, and Wes had her gloved hand at the elbow because the gravel was slush and he had been at her elbow on slush six times since Wednesday last week, and Earl came up the path behind them at his own pace because Earl had decided when Earl had decided.

The barn was warm in its accidental way. Fourteen horses' worth of warm, hay smell, sweet feed in the tack room from the bag Wes had opened that morning, the small tick of the water heater on the south wall. The geldings were at their buckets along the alley. Two of the boarders' mares were head-to-tail in the second stall. The retireds were out together in their paddock at the back, where the half-door was open to the run-in.

Robin set Hen down on the concrete in her boots.

"Bug. Stay on the rubber."

Hen stayed on the runner. She walked the length of it slow with her hands out at her sides for balance, an aisle-walk she had done in every aisle she had ever been put down in, and she stopped at the second stall where the bay mare had her head over the chain and was watching.

She pointed at the bay.

"Mama."

"Yeah, bug. That's a horse. The one in your bag is a horse too."

Hen looked back over her shoulder at Robin. Her face did the small considering thing it did when she was filing a piece of information, and then she looked at the bay again.

The bay blew out a breath that ruffled Hen's hair under the hood of the snowsuit, and Hen laughed once and put her mittened hand up flat to the chain.

Wes came up beside her with a flake of timothy in his arm.

"You want to give her some hay."

Hen put both mittens on the flake without saying anything.

He went down on his heels next to Hen with the flake in both hands, and he showed her how to hold a flake of hay flat under the chain so the mare could reach without leaning her weight on a small girl, and Hen watched him do it once and then took a piece of hay in her own mitten and held it up. The mare took it.

Earl jumped onto the chain.

He did it without fanfare, the half-eared barn cat going from the floor to the rail of the chain in one slow leap, and then from the chain to Hen's left shoulder in a second, the climb of a fourteen-year-old cat who knew what fourteen-year-old cats could climb. He stood on Hen's shoulder. Hen did not move. She did not say anything. She held very still, both mittens at her sides, head up, the cat's whole crooked body settled across her shoulders like a stole, and Robin had not seen Hen still for ten seconds in two years and she watched Hen be still for ten of them.

She got her phone out. She took the picture without making a thing of it.

Wes had the flake held against the chain, watching Hen, and he did not say anything either. The bay mare had her nose down working through what Hen had given her. The cat sat. Hen's mouth was doing the small open shape it did when she was paying attention. The barn was warm. The eaves outside were dripping. Robin put the phone away.

After a count Hen lifted one mitten very slow.

She put it on Earl's back.

Earl did not move. Earl had been through forty winters in the West and a small mittened hand was nothing he had not been through. Hen patted him once. Hen patted him twice. She did not say anything. She did the slow blinking thing she did when she was deciding to like a thing, and she patted him a third time, and she leaned her cheek down against the cat's flank for half a count and then sat back up.

Hen turned her head a fraction to look up at Wes. Wes had not moved off his heels. He had the flake still in his hand and his face was doing the thing his face did when he was about to do something he had been thinking about doing.

He bent his head down to Hen's left ear.

He chanted.

It came out low and rolling, the rhythm she had heard once at the Bozeman sale barn when she was eight and her father had taken her, the sing-song run of numbers and fillers and *gimme-gimme-gimme*, an up-and-down that was not English and was not nonsense and lived in the cadence between, and he chanted into Hen's ear a flat run that climbed and dropped and climbed again, *one-fifty-one-fifty-one-fifty-now-two-now-two, gimme-two, two-and-a-quarter, two-and-a-quarter, who'll-gimme-two-fifty, two-fifty-now, two-fifty-now, sold-American to the lady-with-the-cat*.

Hen laughed.

She laughed like a two-year-old who had found the funniest thing that had ever happened, the laugh coming up from the diaphragm and going on past the place a polite laugh stopped, and she put both mittens flat on Earl's back and laughed with her face up at Wes, and Earl took it for ten seconds and then jumped down off her shoulders onto the runner without making a thing of having jumped, and Hen kept laughing.

She laughed a long count.

She laughed past the count Robin had ever heard her laugh.

She laughed with her shoulders going, head back, both mittens up at her own face, the snowsuit hood falling back off her head, and Wes on his heels in front of her with his face open in a thing she had seen him do once two mornings ago in his bed and not since, and Robin stood at the open barn door in her socks under her boots with her hand flat on the jamb and watched her daughter laugh at the chant of an auctioneer in a boarding barn at seven oh four on Christmas Day evening.

The thing in her chest that had been deciding stopped deciding.

It was a quiet stop. It was not a thing that asked her to do anything with her face. She felt the stop go through her ribs and into her hands on the jamb, and she breathed in the wet pine that was coming up the hill from the river, and she looked at the man on his heels in front of her child, and she did not put a word on it because there was no word she needed for it.

Hen ran out of laugh.

She came down out of it slow. The laugh tapered into the small huffing sound that toddlers made at the back of a long laugh, and Wes lifted her up off the runner and carried her three steps to the bench by the tack-room door and set her down on it, and he sat next to her, and Hen leaned the side of her head against his arm.

She looked down at his feet.

His boots were on the bench under it where he had pulled them off when they came in from the diesel, lined up straight beside the boots Robin had pulled off her own feet a minute ago. Hen looked at his boots. She looked at her own boots on her feet. She looked back at his.

"Boots," Hen said.

She said it clear. She said it the way she said *Mama*, with her chin up and her face flat, the announcement of a two-year-old who had decided a thing and was telling the room.

Hen reached down and put her mitten flat on the toe of his right boot. She left it there. She did not move her hand. Wes turned his head a quarter to look down at her, and his face did the small still thing it did when she did not need his input, and he did not say anything. The barn made the quiet sound it made when fourteen horses were chewing hay at once, and the eaves dripped, and Robin took her hand off the jamb because the jamb was not what she needed to be holding.

She walked the runner to the bench.

She crouched in front of Hen. She put her hand on the small of Hen's back over the snowsuit. Hen did not look at her. Hen kept her mitten on Wes's boot. Robin looked at the mitten and at the boot and at the small fingers inside the mitten and at her own daughter's flat decided face, and she registered that she was going to be in this barn at this hour on this Christmas Day for the rest of the days of her life she did not yet know she was going to have, and that her child had told her so.

"Yeah, bug," Robin said. "Yes."

***

They put Hen down on the daybed in the mudroom at nine. She had eaten a piece of toast with peanut butter and drunk most of a cup of warm milk, and she had carried Horse and the wool sock of Wes's that she was not going to give back, and Wes had pulled the brown wool blanket over her up to her chin, and Robin had stood in the doorway watching while Hen fell asleep with one fist around Horse's middle and the other wrapped in the cuff of the wool sock.

Robin shut the door to two inches.

She went into the bedroom.

Wes was sitting on the edge of the bed in the henley and his bibs and his socks, and he had pulled the curtain over the south window because the porch light was on for Hen in the mudroom and he did not want the light to come through, and the woodstove in the corner was loaded for the night and was throwing the warm into the room slow.

She came in and shut the door behind her, careful with the latch.

She stood at the foot of the bed.

"Wes."

"Yeah."

"Come here."

He came. He stood in front of her, no hurry, in the same not-pushing he had brought to the porch rail Friday night when she had been on the gravel in the sheepskin coat and he had been waiting to see what she was going to do with her body. His hands came up to her face. They cupped her face the same as the morning two days ago when she had come back from the Camry to the door. He did not kiss her yet. He looked at her.

"You all right."

"I'm here."

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"Tell me."

"I'm here."

"All right."

He kissed her.

He kissed her with his mouth open against hers from the start, no question in it and no asking, the kiss of a man who had been told once today by a two-year-old that the boots stayed on the bench and was going to act on the telling. His thumb moved across her cheekbone. His other hand was at the back of her head with his fingers at the place where her hair had come down out of the band on the drive home from McClarens. He took the band out the rest of the way. Her hair came down to her shoulders.

She put her hands on his chest. The henley was warm with him under it, the heat of a man who had been five feet from a woodstove for three hours, and her palms went down to the buttons at the collar and undid them one by one with the slow she had learned in this room two mornings ago. She pulled the henley up over his head. She set it on the chair where he had folded her flannel Friday night.

She undid the buckles of his bibs.

She did it standing in front of him with her face level with his sternum, and she did not look up while she did it. Her hands were on the leather and on the metal and on the thick canvas, and she felt him watching her work, and she felt his hand come up and rest at the back of her neck, no pressure, the heat of his palm against the place where her hair was. His other hand stayed at his side.

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"Look at me."

She looked up.

His face was the face she had watched at the porch rail and the cattle guard and the kitchen and the barn doorway tonight, the face of a man who was not going to be the one to break, and the face was doing a thing now she had not seen it do, which was the small open thing the laugh had done in the barn an hour ago, only without the laugh under it.

"Hi," he said.

"Hi."

"You with me."

"I'm with you."

"All night."

"All night."

He reached down and pulled her flannel up over her head with both hands, slow, and the long-johns came after, and she had not put a bra on after the bath, and she came out of the long-johns into his palms and his palms were warm.

He took her down on the bed.

He took her down slow. He came down on his elbow over her with one hand at the side of her face and the other flat on her stomach, and he kissed her once on the mouth and once at the corner of it and once at the hinge of her jaw and then he was at her throat, and he stayed at her throat. He put his mouth at the hollow at the base of it and stayed for a count, breathing her, and his hand went down her side from her ribs to her hip and stayed there, not moving, his palm flat against the skin under the waistband of her jeans.

She felt him breathe in, hold it, and let it out.

"Wes."

"Yeah."

"Take your time."

"I will."

He took his time. He undid her jeans slow, and he pulled them off, and he pulled the long-johns off after, and at every step his mouth was on the place his hands had just left, and he was not in a hurry, and the not-being-in-a-hurry was the whole register of the night. His mouth went down her sternum and stopped at her breast and stayed for a count there, and the count was long, and his hand was at her hip and was not going anywhere in particular, and she put her hand into his hair and let him stay.

He came back up.

"Wes."

"Yeah."

"Yours."

He stood off the bed and took the bibs the rest of the way off and took the long-johns off and came back to her. He was hard against her thigh when he came down, and he was the body she had learned in the dark Friday night and in the blue light Saturday morning, and what was different now was that the body was a body she knew, and the knowing was the whole thing. She put her hand around him, low at the base, and he made the small low sound in his chest he had made twice in three nights, and she stayed with her hand there. She was not going to first-name what she was holding. She had named it three nights ago. The hand was on him because the hand had been told.

He took the foil off the nightstand.

He rolled it on with his eyes on her face, the same as Friday, and he came down to her, and he braced his hand by her shoulder and slid one thigh between hers, and he moved into her slow with his face an inch from her face.

She let her breath out.

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"Look at me."

"I am."

"Stay there."

"I'm here."

He moved.

He moved slow with his mouth open against hers, and the room was warm around them and the woodstove was ticking and the curtain was over the south window and the eaves outside were tapping in the slow run that had been running since two o'clock. He said her name. He said it once and then again, low, into the side of her throat, and he kept saying it, and his voice in her ear did not make a sentence and did not try to, and her hand was at the back of his head with her fingers in the rough cropped hair at his nape.

He went slow because slow was what the night was. Friday had been finally. Saturday had been the small giving back of a woman who had stayed. Tonight was a different thing entirely. Tonight was a man inside a woman in a cabin where her child was asleep one room over and where her child had told the room she was going to stay, and there was no rush in his hands and there was no rush in her hands and there was no rush in either of their hips, and her mouth was at the side of his throat where the salt of him was, and she breathed him in and let him take his slow.

She did not say much.

What she said, she said into the meat of his shoulder where her teeth had been Friday night, and what she said was the unprintable thing twice and a third time and the fourth was something that was not a word, and the saying was hers and was not for him to hold.

She came once, slow, with her hand at the back of his head and her mouth at his neck, and he came right after with his face against her temple, and he said her name against her skin one last time, low, and held there.

He breathed.

She breathed.

The woodstove ticked. The eaves dripped. Somewhere down the hill at the boarding barn a horse blew out a long breath. He kept his weight on his elbows and his face at her temple, and his hand was in her hair, and she did not move her hand off the back of his head.

After a while he turned his face and kissed the corner of her mouth.

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"All right."

"Yeah."

"All right."

He moved off her then, slow, and took care of the foil at the wastebasket, and came back and pulled the brown quilt up over both of them, and she came in against his shoulder with her face in the place she had bitten Friday night, where the mark was already going down to a fade.

She put her hand flat on his chest. His heart was going under her palm, not fast, and she closed her eyes against his shoulder and slept.

On the rug at the foot of the bed, Hen's stuffed Horse lay on its side where it had fallen out of the snowsuit pocket coming in from the barn, one cloth ear bent under its cheek.
