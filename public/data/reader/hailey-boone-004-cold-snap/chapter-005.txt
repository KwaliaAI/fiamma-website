The kitchen window read minus nineteen at five forty-eight on Friday morning, and the coat on the arm of the couch had been there for thirty-six hours. Robin had walked past it nineteen times. She was counting now. She knew she was counting because she was telling herself she was not.

Hen was already up. The chipped tooth had decided overnight that it was going to be a tooth, and Hen had decided to be on the floor with Horse and the basket of board books, and the cracker she was holding in her right hand was being rationed against the morning by some logic that was hers.

"Soft side, bug."

"Mama."

"Yeah. The right side. Chew on the right."

Hen put the cracker in on the right. She watched Robin watching her. She made a small show of chewing on the right and then, with the dignity of a person who had clocked that the rules were dumb, switched to the left.

"Hen."

"Mama."

"Right side. The way we said."

Hen switched back. She did it without looking at Robin, which was Hen's way of communicating that the law had been registered and she would obey it under protest.

Robin made the coffee. She made the oatmeal with applesauce in it because the dentist in Bozeman had said *soft food two days* and the woman at the counter had said *Merry Christmas* and Robin had said *you too* and walked Hen out to the Camry holding a tooth that was not her own. She could still feel the chip under her thumb, which was a thing the brain did, and she did not have the time today to argue with her brain.

On her way to the door she picked the sheepskin coat up off the arm of the couch, folded it over her arm, and carried it out to the Camry, and she logged that as a kind of decision.

In the Camry on the way to Mrs. Thornberg's, Hen was quiet in the back. Hen had decided sometime in the last week that the morning drive was a thinking time, and she did her thinking in the small profile she got from staring at the back of the passenger seat. Robin watched her in the mirror at the stoplight on Park, the chip catching the dashboard light when Hen yawned. Cosmetic, the dentist had said. The world said cosmetic about a lot of things that left a mark.

Mrs. Thornberg crouched and looked at the chip without touching it.

"Healing fine. Cosmetic, the world says."

"Right."

"You good."

"I'm good."

"You sure."

"Norma."

Mrs. Thornberg straightened up slow on her bad knee and did the thing she did, which was let the question be asked once and not pressed.

"Drive easy."

"I will."

Pete had the bin ready and the keys to the Subaru on the hook again because Pete had decided, evidently, that Robin was in the wagon for the cold-snap stretch and was not going to be argued out of it. He did not look up when she came in.

"Wagon."

"Wagon."

"Long johns."

"Two pairs."

"Don't get cute."

She loaded the bin, put on the driving gloves, set the sheepskin coat folded on the passenger seat of the Subaru, and backed out of the lot at seven thirty-one.

The county road was clear black again. Sand on the shoulders from the grader run on Tuesday. Her shoulders came down out of her ears around the Vassar place where they always came down. The county had put fresh sand at the willow bend after she went in, and the corner was still rough with it. She took the bend at eighteen and her stomach did not drop this time, which she registered as a kind of progress.

Wes's mailbox was at the cattle guard.

His diesel was at the gate.

She slowed the Subaru fifty yards out and did the small accounting her brain did now that she had been doing this for six years and one week. Ten forty-three on Tuesday. Ten forty-eight on Wednesday. Ten fifty on Thursday, which had been the bad morning. He had been at the gate every one of those mornings inside of a five-minute window of when she pulled up to the box. She filed it under *it's his property*. She had filed it under that on Tuesday and she had been filing it under that ever since, and the file was getting full.

He had the tailgate down. The blue thermos was on it. He had the sheepskin coat on and the wide-brim black felt hat and a pair of leather gloves that had work on them. He saw her and lifted a hand in the not-quite-wave.

She rolled up to the box.

"Morning."

"Morning."

He came over with the thermos. The cap came off with the small turn of his wrist. Steam went up and the wind took it sideways before it had cleared his face.

"You took the wagon."

"Pete put me in it."

"Smart of him."

He held the thermos out. Same hand-off as the bend, same as yesterday, the steel cup hot through her gloves. She took it. Two swallows. It tasted like it had been ground that morning and put in the thermos at six.

"Thank you."

"Yeah."

She handed it back. He capped it.

"There's a signature on the bundle today," she said. "Property tax. I have to walk it up."

"Yeah?"

"Yeah."

"All right."

She got the bundle out. The notice was on top, the green slip clipped to it, the kind of slip that needed a pen and a name and a date in the carrier's hand and the recipient's hand. She had the pen in her jacket pocket already. She got out of the Subaru.

The wind came around the draw and went through the seam at the collar of her Carhartt and she did the thing she did, which was tuck her chin and hunch.

He took the sheepskin coat off.

He did it without a word, the same two-handed shrug he had done yesterday, and he stepped to her left and put the coat across her shoulders. The collar came up under her ear. The inside of the coat was warm against the back of her neck, warm from him, and under the sheepskin there was the smell she had logged yesterday and could now identify, hay and a wood fire and the faint diesel that lived on the cuffs.

"I brought the other one back."

"I know. I saw it in the truck."

She did not move out from under the coat. The coat sat on her shoulders the way a coat sits on you when somebody has put it there with the idea that you will keep it on, and she walked the bundle up the drive in it without taking it off, which she registered halfway up and chose not to fix.

He had not asked if he could put it on her. He had not asked yesterday either. There was a thing about being asked, which Robin had not had time for in three years, and there was a thing about not being asked, which men got wrong about half the time. He had not got it wrong. He had put the coat on her like a man putting a coat on a fence post in a wind, because that was where it ought to go.

The cabin was log, low, set into the south-facing draw, with a porch that had been swept that morning. She knocked.

He came to the door. He had taken his hat off inside, which she clocked because cowboy or not, indoors was indoors. The cabin behind him was warm in a way her doublewide was never warm, the warm of a woodstove with a quilt that had been on the back of a chair in front of it for a long time. She did not look past him.

"Sign the slip," she said.

She held out the notice and the pen. He took it. He read it through, which she also clocked, because most people on her route signed the slip without reading. He signed. He handed it back.

"Thanks."

"Yeah."

She hesitated for half a count. Not for a reason she could name out loud. Then she turned and went back down the drive in his coat, and she was at the cattle guard before she had thought a thing through, and he had got back into his diesel, and the diesel was idling, and he lifted a hand from the cab as she pulled in to the Subaru.

She drove off in the coat.

Three miles down the gravel she registered it.

She did not turn around.

She told herself she would drop it Monday. She would drop it Monday at the box, on top of the bundle, with a *thanks again* on a sticky note and her pen at it.

She picked Hen up at one-twenty.

Hen had the tractor book in her left hand and a piece of graham cracker in her right. Mrs. Thornberg was in the recliner with the *Livingston Enterprise* open to the obituary page, reading it like a woman who read obituaries with the same regular interest other women gave horoscopes.

"How was she."

"She was a peach. Ate her applesauce. Slept hard. Tooth's fine."

"Good."

Mrs. Thornberg folded the paper.

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"You eating."

"I'm eating."

"You sure."

"Norma."

"All right."

Robin signed Hen out. She got Hen's boots on. She buckled Hen into the car seat and shut the door clicking and not slamming. She did not look at the couch when she got home, and she logged that too.

She made soup with the ground beef and the tomato and the noodles. Hen ate it slow, on the right, then on the left, then on the right again. Hen put a piece of noodle on the tray of the highchair and looked at it for a count, and Robin let her look. Whatever Hen was working out about a noodle, Hen was going to work out, and Robin had nothing to add.

"Mama."

"Yeah, bug."

"More."

"More what, bug."

"More."

"All right. More."

She gave Hen another half-cup of the soup. Robin ate her own bowl standing at the counter, watching her kid eat. She got Hen into the long johns and the wool socks and sat on the edge of Hen's bed for a count of four with her hand on Hen's small back, and the small machinery breathed along, and Hen was asleep before the count was out.

She turned off the lamp. She pulled the door to two inches.

She went down the hall.

The coat was on the arm of the couch where she had set it when she came in. She had not moved it. She did not move it now. She stood in the front room in her socks and looked at it, and she put both hands inside the front of her hoodie and held her own forearms.

She thought about the mailbox on Galloway Road.

She had been to it five mornings this week. Five mornings, five thermoses, five times the same diesel idling at the side of the gate, five times a man not blocking the road and not pulled off in a *hey* way and not standing in the middle of the gravel like a fool, just there, and steam coming off the cup, and the coat coming off his back the second time, and her wearing it home.

It would be easy to read that wrong.

She was not going to read it at all.

She tightened her arms across her ribs inside the hoodie and shifted her weight off her left foot.

She had Hen and a route and a cracked Camry and a furnace that was working a job it did not love and a propane bill that had been clipped to the fridge for three weeks. She was not in the market for reading mailboxes. She was in the market for Monday morning.

She sat down on the floor with her back against the couch and her socked feet on the rug and her hands flat on her knees, and she said one word out loud that she would not say in front of Hen, and she breathed in for four and out for six.

She did not pick the coat up.

She did not move it.

The heat came on in the doublewide with the clunk and the hiss it always made, and the propane did its job, and the house was, for the first time that week, actually warm.

\* \* \*

Wes called Tate from the porch at three on a steel grey afternoon.

Tate picked up on two rings. *Galloway Auctions and Livestock Brokerage, Tate speaking.* The voice he used for the line was the auctioneer voice on idle, low and flat. When he heard who it was he dropped half a register.

"Cousin."

"Tate."

"You alive in that draw."

"Alive."

"Heard the cold snap broke trees up by the pass."

"It will. It hasn't yet."

"All right." A pause. "Why are you calling on a Friday afternoon. You don't call on Fridays."

"I'm going to ask her out by the weekend."

"Ask who."

"The mail carrier."

A long count. Tate had the kind of long count a man developed when he chanted livestock for forty minutes at a stretch and was used to letting silences do their own work. Wes could hear him put the phone down on something hard, a desk maybe, and pick it back up.

"Wes."

"Yeah."

"She say anything to make you think she's ready."

"Not in so many words."

"So. Not yet."

"I know what I'm doing."

"You always say that."

"Tate."

"Asking is fine, in general. Asking inside seven days of meeting her is a different thing, and asking inside seven days of meeting her after she watched her kid chip a tooth is a different thing again. You're putting weight on a board that ain't ready to hold it."

"Coat's on her shoulders."

"What."

"The coat. She's got it. I put it on her at the cattle guard yesterday and again this morning. She wore it home. Both times."

Another count.

"That's a different thing to me than it is to her, cousin."

"I know."

"Do you."

"Yeah."

"All right." Tate breathed out the long way. "I'm not going to talk you out of it. I'm telling you. Wait the weekend. Ask her Monday at the earliest. And ask her once, and accept the answer she gives you."

"All right."

"You hear me."

"I hear you."

"Tell me you hear me."

"I hear you, Tate."

"All right."

Wes set the phone on the porch rail and watched a single hawk circle low over the pasture. The hawk had been doing this for an hour. It would go home empty. He stood for another count and went back inside.

\* \* \*

Robin got up off the floor at nine.

She did not pick up the coat.

She washed the soup pot, locked the door, and turned off the porch light. She went down the hall and stood at Hen's door and listened for the small breath, and the small breath was there, and she pulled the door to two inches and went to her own room.

She lay on her back on the bedspread in her hoodie and her jeans and her socks, and she put her hand flat on her chest because the heater had clunked off and the room was colder than the front room and she wanted to feel her own ribs for a count, and she felt them, and her hand under her own collar smelled of hay and a wood fire, faintly, where the coat had been against her shoulders for an hour that morning.

She closed her eyes.

Outside the kitchen window the cold held flat at twenty below, and the sheepskin coat sat folded on the arm of the couch where she had put it on Friday, where it would still be on Saturday morning when she woke up, and she had not yet decided what she was going to do with it.

The coat sat on the arm of the couch and waited.
