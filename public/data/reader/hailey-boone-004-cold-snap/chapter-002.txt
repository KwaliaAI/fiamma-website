The fence post at the end of the doublewide drive had been at the end of the doublewide drive since before Robin moved in, and she had never once thought about it as a problem that could find its way into her life. It was a four-by-four pressure-treated post sunk in concrete with a reflector on it that had faded from red to pink. She had been backing past it twice a day for two and a half years.

She backed into it at six twenty-eight on Tuesday morning.

The Camry was warmed to the point that the dashboard was no longer fogged, and she had Hen buckled in the back, and Hen was telling Horse a story that had no consonants in it, and Robin was easing the car backward through a crust of refrozen slush in the drive, watching the rearview, when the rear quarter panel met the reflector with a small gritty scrape that traveled forward through the body of the car and stopped just under her left ear.

She sat there with her foot on the brake.

"Mama."

"I know."

"Mama. No."

"I know, bug. I stopped."

She put the car in park. She got out into the cold and walked around to the driver's-side rear and looked. The scrape ran from above the wheel well to the back of the bumper in a thin pale curl, paint lifted off the gray in a stripe about as wide as her thumb. She could see the primer underneath in two places where the post had bitten harder.

She put her glove against it. She did not press.

In the back seat, Hen had stopped narrating to Horse and was watching her through the window with a face that was already learning what an adult-bracing-for-something-bad looked like. Robin saw the look and made her own face do the thing she had been making it do since Hen was about ten months old, which was put on a *we're fine* and mean it from the eyes down.

She held up one finger to the window. Hen held up one finger back.

Robin counted. *One. Two. Three. Four.* She had read in a parenting magazine in Mrs. Thornberg's waiting room that you could count to four in front of a child instead of yelling, and she had taken it as license, because she was going to count to four anyway and she might as well have it called good parenting. By four she had the kind of household-arithmetic clarity she could work with. Touch-up paint at the auto store on Main was twelve dollars for a bottle. A body-shop estimate on a quarter-panel scrape was three hundred minimum and they would not even look at it until April when the temperatures were paint-grade. Three hundred was the propane bill or it was Hen's snowsuit zipper, repaired by the seamstress two doors down from Albertson's, plus the difference left over for groceries through the second week of January. Three hundred was not something she had.

She got back in the car. Hen had gone quiet, which was what Hen did when she was filing information.

"It's okay," Robin said. "I scratched the car."

Hen pressed her palm flat against the window in answer, a thing she did when she had heard a piece of news and was holding it. Robin pressed her own glove on the other side of the glass.

Robin put the Camry in drive. She pulled forward two feet onto the gravel of the doublewide street and put it in park again and just sat for a second with her hand on the gearshift, because what she wanted to do was put her forehead on the wheel and what she could not afford to do was let Hen see that, so she settled for a long even breath out through her teeth and pulled away.

The fence post stayed where it was. It had won by being older than she was.

At Mrs. Thornberg's, Hen marched in pointing at the Camry over her shoulder and Mrs. Thornberg did not miss a beat.

"Something happen to the car?"

"Mama," Hen said, and pointed harder.

Mrs. Thornberg's eyes flicked up to Robin's. Robin shrugged with the corner of her mouth, the gesture for *not now*, and Mrs. Thornberg, who was as gentle as a person could be about other people's mornings, gave a small nod and steered Hen into the rec room with a hand on her hat.

"Drive easy, hon."

"I will."

It was the second time in two days a person had told her *drive easy*, and the first time had been a man in a sheepskin coat at the bottom of a ditch on Galloway Road, and Robin did not let her thinking go any further down that road. She got back in the Camry. She drove to the post office.

Pete Raymond was at the sort cage with the ear flaps of his cap pulled down, even indoors, which meant he had been out at the dock loading the routes and had not bothered to pull them up. He looked at her over the cage with the kind of attention he had worked out was the maximum she would tolerate.

"You good."

"I'm good."

"Backup truck."

"I see it."

The backup truck was a Dodge Grand Caravan with a magnetic post-office decal on each side and a partition behind the driver's seat. It did not have right-hand drive, and the heat worked, and Robin had driven it twice before.

"Ross said to tell you he's putting the Grumman on the lift Thursday."

"Fine."

"Maintenance on Galloway Road. He's going down to look at the bend."

"Fine."

"Cold's holding."

"Pete."

He held up both hands, a quarter inch off the cage rail. "Just talking."

She took the bin out to the lot and loaded it into the Caravan. Driving on the left side of the cab meant a left-hand-out-the-window technique she had developed on previous backup days, and she went slow enough to make it work. The cold sat on the lot in a flat heavy way that did not stir, the kind of cold that was its own weather.

Her phone buzzed in her pocket on the way out the door of the post office. Mrs. Thornberg, the text said. *Forgot to mention. Hen's nose was running this morning. Watching it. No fever. Will let you know.* Robin typed back *thank you* with one thumb and got the phone back into her jacket and went out to the lot.

She drove.

The first three quarters of the route ran on familiar tracks, ranches and gravel turn-offs and the four-mile stretch on the county road where she took the speed up to fifty-two and let her shoulders come down for a minute before the next gravel section. She did not think about Galloway Road.

At the Vassar place, Mrs. Vassar was at her box in a parka over a nightgown, waiting for the social-security check, which had not come yet because it was Tuesday and the check did not come till Wednesday. Robin handed her a sheaf of catalogs and a flyer.

"Tomorrow, Hannah."

"I knew that."

"I know you knew it."

"I came out anyway."

"I see that."

Mrs. Vassar looked at the Caravan and at the post-office decal on the side of it. "You in the wagon today."

"Grumman's at the shop."

"That bend at the end of Galloway."

"Yep."

"I told them three winters back."

"I know you did."

Mrs. Vassar nodded once, satisfied with the morning's confirmations, and turned and walked back up her shoveled path in slippers and the parka. Robin watched her get to the porch and waited until the storm door closed before she put the Caravan back in gear.

The Kemmer place came and went, and the Heaton place after it, both of them quick today, a single envelope at one and an empty box at the other.

Galloway Road was last.

She turned off the county road and took it slow on the gravel and felt her hands tighten on the wheel without her permission. The bend was where the bend was. She came through it at a crawl with her foot ready on the brake and she did not slide and she did not look down into the ditch as she passed it. A scrape in the snow on the bank was still visible where the Grumman had gone over. Sand had been thrown across the road from a county truck overnight, brown grit on the white. She kept her eyes on the road.

Wes Galloway's mailbox was at the cattle guard. There was no truck in the drive that she could see from the road. There was a small column of woodsmoke straight up over the spruce. She slowed the Caravan to a roll and rolled the window down and took the bundle from the bin: a feed-store flyer, a property tax notice in the same envelope as yesterday's that had been delivered to Tom Galloway for thirty years, a Christmas card with a Bozeman return address. She put them in the box. She closed the flag. She put the Caravan in gear and drove back out the gravel.

She did not look down the drive.

The afternoon came on. She finished the town walk in the cold and signed the bin back in at four-oh-two. Pete made no remark this time. She drove to Mrs. Thornberg's.

Hen was at the play kitchen with a wooden spoon. Mrs. Thornberg had put a tea towel over Hen's head as part of a game whose rules Hen alone understood. Robin signed her out. Hen came over and stirred the air twice in front of Robin's face with the spoon, then pointed it at Robin's coat sleeve and out the window in the direction of the Camry. The same fierce finger from the morning.

"Yes, bug. We're going to fix it."

Hen waved the spoon at the door, which was Hen's way of saying she had decided the day was over.

Robin carried her out. Mrs. Thornberg held the storm door from inside.

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"Pete called over. Said you were quiet."

"I'm always quiet."

"I'm just checking."

Robin paused on the porch with Hen on her hip. She did not have spare change for this conversation either. She did have, in a kind of bone-tired way, the recognition that Mrs. Thornberg was not asking out of curiosity.

"I'm fine," she said. "Yesterday was bad. Today wasn't."

"All right."

"Thanks."

"Drive easy."

She got Hen in the car. She buckled the harness and double-checked the chest clip and Hen said *Mama* and showed her Horse, and Robin pressed her own nose against Horse's threadbare nose and Hen laughed once, a short fat laugh, and then they were on Park Street headed home.

She stopped at the Town Pump because her gauge had been at the bottom of the E-and-empty zone since Saturday and Tuesday was the day the direct deposit cleared. She put twenty in the tank. The pump clicked off at $20.04 and Robin watched the four cents like it owed her something and then put the nozzle back and got the receipt and got back in.

Hen pressed Horse's nose against the window and watched the lights go past, and she did not say anything else, and she was asleep before Sixth.

She got Hen out of the car seat without waking her. The body of a sleeping two-year-old was a heavier thing than a person who did not have one would guess, and Robin had developed a hip carry that put Hen's weight on the bone and let Robin's arms be free for the door and the inside latch and the fumble with the porch light. She put Hen in the toddler bed in her snowsuit because the house was sixty-two and Robin had decided she was not fighting that war this week. Hen turned her head and put one cheek on Horse and went deeper.

In the kitchen she put a tortilla in the dry pan and got the cheddar out of the drawer and grated what was left of the block with the side of a knife because the box grater was in the dishwasher. Her phone buzzed once on the counter. Mrs. Thornberg. *No fever. See you in the morning.* Robin typed *thank you* and turned the phone face-down. She made one quesadilla, ate it standing at the counter, washed the pan, and turned out the kitchen light.

She sat on the edge of her own bed in the dark with her hands on her knees and she did not think about the man on Galloway Road.

She did not think about him for the slow space of about eleven breaths, and then she did, for about three, and then she put both hands flat on the bedspread and stood up and went to brush her teeth.

She lay down in her clothes, again, second night running. She closed her eyes.

---

The post office on Park Street ran a thin shift on Tuesdays between twelve and one, when the window clerk was at lunch and the carriers were out on their routes. Wes pushed through the front door at twelve-ten and the bell jangled and Pete Raymond looked up from his stool at the sort cage and gave him the kind of nod a man gave another man he had met twice at a livestock sale.

Wes touched the brim of his hat.

"Ma'am want me to wait."

"She's at lunch. Come on back."

He came around the counter. The corkboard on the back wall behind the sort cage had the route maps and the holiday schedule and the carrier directory laminated and pinned at four corners. He found the rural-route page. He read down the list. *McKee, Robin. Rural Route 4, Paradise Valley. Six years.* There was a small photo at the corner, a bad one, taken on her first day, orange knit hat already in evidence and the smile the United States Postal Service required of anyone applying for a route. He looked at it for a count of four. He read the address that was already in his head from yesterday's envelope. He put the laminate flat against the cork and touched it once with his glove and walked back out front and bought a roll of stamps he did not need.

---

The cold on the doublewide street had a pale moon over it that was bright enough to read by. Wes drove past at six-fifteen on his way home from the hardware store with two new chain hooks and a quart of bar oil on the passenger seat. He had not planned to come this way. The Murphy Avenue route would have got him to the county road faster. He had told himself in the Ace parking lot that he needed milk and the IGA was on this side, and he had known when he said it that he was not going to stop at the IGA.

The doublewide street was the one with the orange-painted mailbox. He found it. There were three trailers between the corner and hers, and her drive was the one with the fresh tire tracks pulled in nose-first, and her front window was lit and there was a small lamp on inside. He could see a fence post at the end of the drive with a faded reflector on it. He could see the rear quarter panel of the Camry by the lamp light, a curl of pale where the gray paint had been.

He let the diesel roll past at twenty.

He did not stop.

He told himself she had got home fine, which she had, and that he had needed to know, which he had, and that he had not done a thing he had to apologize for, which was a separate question, and he kept driving until the doublewide street ran into Madison and Madison ran into the four-lane and the four-lane ran out to the county road, and somewhere along that stretch he stopped telling himself things and just drove.

---

In the toddler bed, Hen had turned in her sleep and put Horse upside down under her arm again, the way Horse always ended up by morning. The snowsuit had bunched at the knees. Robin came in once before she went to bed herself to look at her. She did not adjust the snowsuit. She did not adjust Hen. She pulled the door to and left it open the inch Hen needed it open.

Outside the doublewide, the moon went on doing what moons did, which was not much.
