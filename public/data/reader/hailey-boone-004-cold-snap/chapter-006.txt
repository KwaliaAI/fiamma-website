She left the sheepskin coat on the couch.

She did it on purpose, which she did not admit to herself until much later, after the day had finished doing what it was going to do. She came out of the bedroom at five forty-three a.m. with her hair pulled back tight and her own Carhartt buttoned to the collar, and the sheepskin was folded over the arm of the couch where it had been since Thursday night, and she walked past it on her way to the kitchen and did not look at it.

Hen was still asleep. The thermostat read fifty-nine. The propane company had not been by yet, and the propane company was not going to be by until Wednesday, and Robin was going to manage. She put coffee on. She looked at the dark window above the sink.

Monday. Day eight of the cold snap. The radio in the kitchen had said negative twelve at four a.m. and the wind was coming down the valley out of the north at fourteen, which made it negative thirty on her face whenever she got out of the truck. She had laid out three pairs of socks and the bigger gloves and the wool balaclava she had not worn since the route to Pray went sideways in 2024. She had laid them out the night before because she had known Sunday evening that Monday was going to ask things of her she did not feel like giving.

She got Hen up at six twenty. She got Hen into the snowsuit. Hen pressed her forehead to Robin's collarbone on the way to the car and made the small humming sound she made when she was warm and wanted to be warmer, and Robin held the back of Hen's head with one gloved hand for a count of two and then carried her down the steps.

The drive was iced. Not a sheet now, just patches, because the wind had moved most of the snow off it over the weekend and what was left was the harder kind. Robin had gone over it Saturday morning with the eighteen-dollar plastic shovel and a coffee can of stove ash, and the ash had done what the ash did, which was buy her another four days of not asking anybody for help.

The Camry started on the third try. She let it warm. She watched her own breath fog the inside of the windshield and did not turn the defrost up because the defrost would not work until the engine was warm.

At Mrs. Thornberg's, Mrs. Thornberg held the storm door open with her hip. She took Hen's mitten in her own and smiled at Robin the careful smile of a woman who had decided over the weekend to not bring up Thursday's phone call again unless Robin did.

"Cold one."

"Yeah."

"Drive easy."

Robin nodded. She turned and walked back to the Camry.

She did not think about the coat. That was the sentence she said in her head, and the saying of it was the first time in twelve hours that she had thought about anything else.

Pete had the bin ready and the Subaru keys on the hook because Pete had decided three days ago that Robin was in the wagon for the duration of the snap.

"Wagon's warm."

"Thank you."

He looked at her face and decided not to say what he had been going to say.

"Drive easy."

The county road was clear black. The first fourteen mailboxes went in the steady rhythm she knew with her hands more than her head, and by the time she swung onto the gravel into Galloway country at ten thirty-two she had her shoulders down where they were supposed to be for the first time that morning. The bend by the willows was sanded again, fresh sand, and she took it at sixteen and the Subaru held the line.

Wes's diesel was at the cattle guard.

She had known it would be. He had been there every weekday since Thursday, and she had let herself accept that as a fact of the route, like the ice that pooled by the willows or the Kemmer flag that stuck on damp days. She had a thermos of her own coffee on the passenger seat that she had made at the doublewide that morning in deliberate refusal of his, and she had not worn the sheepskin because she had wanted, she thought, to give the coat back, although if she had wanted to give the coat back she would have brought it.

She rolled the Subaru up to the box.

He was at the back of his truck with a parcel in his hands, taping it shut for the outgoing. He set it down on the tailgate when he saw her. He came over.

"Morning."

"Morning."

He had on the wide-brim black felt and the Carhartt bibs and a heavy Carhartt jacket she had not seen on him before, navy and clean. The sheepskin was not in his truck. She knew this because she looked. He had on the navy because he did not have the sheepskin, which was on her couch, which she had left there.

"You doing all right."

"Yeah."

"Hen."

"She's at Mrs. Thornberg's."

"Good."

He did the thing he did, which was wait. She put the bundle for him on the lid of the box and started to write up the parcel notice, and he watched her do it without commenting on the fact that he was standing five feet away and could just take it from her hand.

"Robin."

She looked up.

His face was set the same as men's faces went when they had decided ahead of time what they were going to do and had stopped looking for the easy way to do it. It was not a hard face. It was a settled one. She registered it and her stomach did the small drop it had done at the bend on Galloway four days ago, the drop that meant something was coming.

"Hollenback potluck's Wednesday night."

"I know."

"I was going to ask if you wanted to go with me."

She did not answer right away. She held the parcel notice in her left hand and the pen in her right, and she looked at him with her face doing nothing because her face had decided before her brain had what it was going to do.

"As a date," he said. "Just so we're clear."

"No."

She said it flat. It came out flat because that was the temperature she had been at since Thursday at five p.m. when Mrs. Thornberg had said the words *Wes Galloway* and *emergency contact* in the same sentence on the phone, and the temperature had not changed over the weekend, and it was not going to change because a man in a clean navy Carhartt asked her a clear question at a cattle guard.

He took it. He took it without his face doing anything she could pin down. He nodded once, small.

"All right."

"I have to finish the route."

"All right."

She handed him the parcel notice. He took it. She got back in the Subaru and shut the door clicking, not slamming, and she put the truck in gear and pulled away.

In her side mirror, he was standing where she had left him with the parcel notice in his hand. He was not watching her go. He was looking down at the notice like a man rereading a receipt to make sure it was right.

She drove the gravel back to the county road at eighteen miles an hour. Her hands were not shaking. Her chest was tight in a way that had nothing to do with the cold. She did not give the tightness a name. She did the route.

She finished at one-oh-five. She swapped the Subaru for the Camry at the post office and went home for lunch and ate a piece of toast standing at the counter and drank water and did not eat anything else. The sheepskin was on the couch. She did not look at it. She washed her face and brushed her teeth. She put a different hat on, the orange one with the pom that Hen liked. She drove back to the post office and did the afternoon town walk in an hour and ten.

At four-forty she clocked out.

The Camry was a sheet of frost on the windshield. She scraped it. She got in. She turned the key. She let the engine warm a count of two minutes which was less than she should have and more than she had time for, and she pulled out of the lot with her gloves still on.

Mrs. Thornberg's house was the third on the right. The porch light was on. The driveway was cleared, sand on the walk. There was a black diesel pickup parked at the curb behind Mrs. Thornberg's silver Civic.

It was Wes's.

She knew it from forty feet away. She knew it from the brand of the chrome on the back, from the angle the wide-brim sat in the rear window where he had set it down, from the pattern of mud frozen on the rear quarter panel that she had been looking at over the cattle guard for eight days running. He was sitting in the cab with the engine off and his hands flat on the steering wheel and his head down.

The kitchen of her brain went cold. Not loud cold. The cold that came at her from inside, the same one that had come at her at the sink Thursday night.

She pulled in behind him in the Camry. She put it in park. She turned the engine off. She did not look at the diesel. She did not look at the cab. She got out and shut her door clicking and walked up Mrs. Thornberg's salted walk with her gloves still on and her face arranged.

Mrs. Thornberg met her at the door.

"Hen's just finishing her snack."

"Okay."

"Robin."

"Mhm."

Mrs. Thornberg paused with her hand on the door. She had on the same sweater. She had the same look she had given Robin Thursday morning, which was the look of a woman who had been doing this for thirty-seven years and had read Robin's face through the storm door before Robin had a chance to fix it.

"He's been in the truck since four-fifteen."

"I see that."

"He came in at three-forty. He said he had been thinking about it all weekend and wanted to come and apologise to me directly. He said he was the one who told the office he was on Hen's emergency contact list, and he was wrong to say it. He said he was sorry I had been put in that position. He said he was going to wait outside until you came to pick Hen up and apologise to you too if you would let him."

Robin did not say anything.

"I told him he could wait in the kitchen. He said he wanted to be outside. He has been in the truck almost an hour."

"Okay."

"I thought you would want to know before you came up the walk."

"Thank you."

"Come in, then."

She went in.

Hen was at the small table by the window with a graham cracker in two hands. Robin sat down on the small chair across from her. Hen looked up and the look did the thing Hen's looks did, which was register Mama and decide whether Mama was Mama or Mama-in-a-mood. Hen decided. Hen pushed the second graham cracker across the table at Robin.

"More."

"Yeah, bug. More."

Robin took the cracker. She held it. She did not eat it. She watched Hen finish her own and brush the crumbs off her front in two careful sweeps and stand up and go for her boots without being told, because Hen had figured out boots-meant-Mama-was-here a year and a half ago and had not let go of the connection.

"Mama."

"Yeah."

"Mama."

"I know."

Robin got the snowsuit on Hen. She got the boots on Hen. She got the mittens. She picked up Horse from where Hen had left him on the basket of board books, and she put Horse under Hen's arm. She picked up Hen's bag with the spare clothes and the pull-ups and the small sippy.

She turned to Mrs. Thornberg at the door.

"I was late."

"You were six minutes past five. That is not late."

"I told myself I was running late."

"All right."

"The route ran. I got bogged on the gravel. The wagon stuck on the Heaton drive."

Mrs. Thornberg looked at her with the long steady look of a woman who knew which winters Robin had finished her route by three and which winters she had finished by four, and which winters she had walked in here at five-oh-six with her shoulders up around her ears, and the look said, *I can tell you the day, honey, and the wagon was not stuck.*

"All right," Mrs. Thornberg said.

"I'll see you in the morning."

"You will."

Robin opened the door. The cold came in around her hip. She got Hen out onto the porch and walked Hen down the salted walk to the Camry without looking at the diesel parked behind it. She got Hen in the car seat. She bent into the Camry to thread the harness clip.

The diesel's driver door opened. Two inches. Three inches. Stopped.

She caught the movement at the edge of her vision while her hands were on the buckle. She did not turn her head. In the chrome of the Camry's open back door she saw a gloved hand on the inside frame of the diesel and the other hand flat on his thigh, white at the knuckle. The hand on the door pulled it closed again. Quiet click.

She kissed Hen's forehead under the hood of the snowsuit. She shut the back door clicking.

She walked around the back of the Camry.

Wes was getting out of the diesel.

He did it slow. He had his hand on the door and he was waiting for her to reach a place on the sidewalk where she would have to walk past him, and she could see his face, and his face was steady, not anxious, not pleading, just settled.

She did not look at him.

She walked past him at the rear of his truck. She walked the eight feet to her driver's-side door. She put her hand on the handle. She opened the door. She got in. She shut it clicking.

He had not said anything. He had not stepped toward her. He had not put a hand out. He had stood at his open door with the dome light on him in the dusk and watched her walk past him without one syllable.

She started the Camry. She put it in reverse. She backed out around the diesel at four miles an hour with both hands on the wheel and her eyes on the rearview, where Hen was looking at her from the car seat with the small unblinking seriousness of a two-year-old reading a room.

"Mama."

"Yeah, bug."

"Mama."

"I'm okay."

She put the Camry in drive and pulled away.

In the rearview, Wes was still standing at his open door. He had not moved. Mrs. Thornberg was at the storm door of the yellow house with her hand at her chin. The sky was the kind of blue dusk got in the cold, the blue that read as black in twenty minutes.

Robin drove the long way home. Hen watched her in the rearview. Hen did not say anything for the eight minutes of the long way. Hen had read the silence in the car and was working with it.

At the doublewide, Robin got Hen in. She got the snowsuit off. She got the boots off. She set Horse on the couch where Horse usually went, which was next to the sheepskin coat, although Robin did this without thinking about where the coat was because she had decided not to think about it.

She made oatmeal. She made it with applesauce stirred in because the tooth was still a tooth. Hen ate slow, on the right side. Robin sat across from her with her own bowl and did not eat it.

"Mama."

"Yeah."

"More."

"More applesauce."

"More."

Robin got up and got the applesauce out of the fridge and stirred another spoonful into Hen's bowl.

The phone rang at six-eighteen.

She did not answer it and let it go to voicemail. She did not look at the screen. She knew it was going to be him, and she knew if she looked she would have to make a decision about it, and she did not have one in her yet.

She gave Hen a bath. She got Hen into the long johns and the wool socks. She got Hen under the quilt with Horse. She sat on the edge of the bed for a count of four with her hand on Hen's back, and Hen reached up and put her own small hand flat against Robin's cheek for the count, and then turned over and shut her eyes.

Robin turned off the lamp.

She came out of the hall into the front room.

The phone had not rung again.

The sheepskin coat was folded over the arm of the couch where Hen had set Horse next to it. The two of them, the coat and the horse, in a stack. The coat was big around the horse. Horse had his nose pressed into the wool the way he pressed his nose into Hen's chin, which was the position he held by default, which was one of the things about Horse.

She stood in the front room in her socks for the count of a breath.

She did not move them apart.

She washed the oatmeal pot and locked the door. She turned off the porch light.

In the kitchen with the lamp off, the window above the sink was a black square with the thermometer tacked outside it reading negative seventeen, and the coat on the couch held the small grey horse against itself in the dark of the front room.
