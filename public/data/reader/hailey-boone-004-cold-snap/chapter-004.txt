The phone clock said five fifty-one when Robin pulled up the dentist list on the laptop, and the front room read fifty-eight on the thermostat because the night had gone to twenty-three below and the propane was working a job it did not love. She sat at the kitchen table in two pairs of socks with the laptop open and the bag of frozen peas back in the freezer where it would be ready again at seven, and she scrolled.

Hen was still asleep down the hall. The frozen peas had taken her down at nine the night before, and she had cried herself out in most of an hour and her body had finally decided to take the deal. Robin had checked her at midnight and at three. She had laid her hand on the small ribs and counted breath against her own watch, the way she had done at seven months when Hen ran a hundred and four for two days and Robin had been twenty-five and alone and certain she was going to break.

She was not certain of anything this morning. She was tired in a way that lived in her teeth.

Bozeman Pediatric Dental was not open yet. She left a voicemail and went back to the list. Livingston Family Dental did not see kids under three. The number for Park County Children's Dental rang to a recording that said winter break started the following day and the office was closed for emergencies through January 5. There was a walk-in clinic in Bozeman that took kids, and the woman who picked up at six-fifty was already up and answering the phone, which Robin clocked as a kindness she had not earned and would take.

"How old."

"Two."

"What happened."

"She slipped on the ice in our drive last night. Fell forward. Caught the bottom porch step with her face. Chipped a baby tooth in the front. Upper. Left side. Just the corner."

"She bleeding."

"Last night. Stopped after we got the peas on it."

"Eating."

"Won't try yet. Hasn't tried."

"Pain."

"I think so. She's two. She tells me with her face."

The woman on the line, whose name was Carla and who had the voice of a woman who had been doing this since before Robin was born, made a small humming sound that meant *come in*.

"We've got a one-thirty walk-in slot. Any earlier and she'd have to sit and that's not going to be a thing. One-thirty. Tell the front you talked to Carla. Bring her insurance card if she has it."

"Yeah," Robin said. She was already opening the calendar. "Yes. I'll be there."

"Drive easy."

"I will."

She hung up and sat with both hands flat on the table and breathed in for four and out for six and looked at the dark window above the sink. The window had iced on the inside corner all week, and this morning the rim of frost had thickened and crept another half inch toward the centre, and outside there was nothing. No snow falling, no light yet, just the dark with the kind of cold in it she could feel through the wall.

She called Pete Raymond next. Pete picked up on two rings because Pete was always at the post office by six twenty in this kind of weather, sorting on his feet with a coffee that had gone cold an hour ago.

"You got the route."

"I got the route till noon. I need the afternoon."

A pause. She could hear the sort cage, the slap of paper on metal.

"Hen."

"Yeah."

"Bad."

"Chipped tooth. They want her in Bozeman at one-thirty."

"I'll cover the school runs."

"Pete."

"I said I'll cover them. Get her in. Don't drive cute on the gravel."

"I won't."

"And take the four-wheel-drive. Earl came in yesterday. He's cleared it."

She had already been planning to take the Camry to Bozeman. The four-wheel was a twelve-year-old Subaru that the post office kept for the carriers who needed it on bad days, and she had not asked for it once in six years. She heard herself say *all right* before she had decided to. She heard Pete grunt in approval and end the call.

She put the phone on the table. She closed the laptop. She looked at the door of Hen's room down the dim hall, and then she stood and went down to it because the kid would be up in nine minutes and she wanted to be the face she saw.

Hen was lying on her back with Horse on her chest. Her mouth was open the small distance toddlers leave it open, and the chip on the tooth showed in the porch light bleeding under the curtain. It was a corner. Maybe three-sixteenths of an inch. Robin's father had chipped a front tooth on a barn ladder when he was eight and had carried that chip the rest of his life and she had loved that chip for forty-one years before he died, and it would be cosmetic by the dentist's standards and it would be a thing Hen carried for two more years until the baby tooth went, and this is what Robin's brain was doing at six fifty-eight on Thursday morning while her body was making up the snowsuit.

"Hey, bug."

Hen's eyes opened.

"Good morning."

"Mama."

"Yeah. We're going to go see Mrs. Thornberg, then I'm going to do my route, and then we're going to go to a doctor in Bozeman who's going to look at your tooth. Okay."

Hen put a hand up to her mouth.

"I know. I know. Real careful with it."

"Mama."

"Yeah."

She got Hen into the snowsuit. She got Hen's boots on. She put a small zipped pouch of Cheerios in the bag because the kid had to eat something before she could be told not to chew on the left side, and she put a sippy of water in beside it, and she carried Hen out to the Camry because the Camry was what they were taking to Mrs. Thornberg's and the four-wheel would not be on her until the post office.

The doublewide drive was a sheet. Concrete, then iced air, then concrete in a rim of frozen footprints from the night before. She had not put down salt because she did not have salt, and she had not had Wes plow it because she had said no, and the consequence was on her hip in the snowsuit watching her negotiate a step at a time.

She set Hen on the porch boards before she went down. She held the rail with her right hand and put her left foot on the step where Hen had gone down and tested it with her weight, and the boot slid two inches before it caught on a grain of grit, and her stomach dropped the same drop it had taken at the bend on Galloway Road four days ago. She put her hand on the post.

Then she went back up and got Hen and carried her down the iced steps with both hands and her own boots toed-out for grip, and her right hand was around Hen's small back where the snowsuit's seam was, and her left hand was around Hen's small thigh, and her hand was shaking. Not shaking from the cold. A different shake. The shake that had been waiting since the moment last night when she had heard the sound Hen had made on the way down and had known before she got to the porch what it was.

She got her into the car seat. She buckled. She kissed Hen's forehead under the hood of the snowsuit, and Hen said *Mama* at her, flat and patient, the forty-seventh kiss since the fall and counting.

"Yeah, bug. I know."

She backed the Camry down the drive at three miles an hour and did not clip anything this time because the post she had clipped on Tuesday had taught her where it was.

Mrs. Thornberg was at the door in her sweater. She took one look at Robin and one look at Hen and one look back at Robin, and she did not say anything that was a question, because Mrs. Thornberg had been doing daycare in Livingston since 1989 and had read Robin's face through the storm door before Robin had a chance to fix it.

"Come in."

"I called you last night."

"You called me at nine forty. I heard."

"She slipped on the porch. The tooth."

Mrs. Thornberg crouched down and looked at Hen's mouth without touching it, with the ease of a woman who had looked at a thousand toddler mouths.

"Okay, peanut. We're going to be gentle today. Soft food."

"I brought Cheerios."

"I've got applesauce. And yogurt. And the little cereal that goes soggy. We'll sort her."

"I have a one-thirty in Bozeman. Walk-in dental."

"I figured. Pete called. He said you'd be coming through to swap to the Subaru."

Robin looked at her.

"He called you," Robin said.

"He called me at six thirty-three. He told me you'd called him at six fifty-something. He wanted to know what I needed for the kid till you came back."

"Pete."

"Mmhm."

Robin put both hands inside her jacket pockets and held her own forearms.

"I'm fine," she said.

"You don't have to be. You can be here for a minute."

"I'm fine, Norma."

Mrs. Thornberg looked at her. It was not a long look. It was the look of a woman who had decided in 1991 that the best gift she could give a young mother in a hurry was to let her say *I'm fine* and not push.

"All right. She'll be here. You go."

Robin signed Hen in. She watched Hen go down on her knees by the basket of board books and pull out the one with the tractor on the front, which Hen could not yet open right-side-up but loved anyway. She watched for half a second. She turned and went out the door.

In the Camry, on the way to the post office, she did not cry.

She didn't.

She did stop at the corner of Yellowstone and Park and put her forehead against the wheel for the count of a breath, and then she lifted it, and she drove.

The post office was warm in its accidental way. Pete Raymond had the route bin ready and the keys to the Subaru on the hook by the door, and he had the look of a man who had decided not to make conversation, which Robin took as a kindness and registered as one. He handed her the keys.

"Wagon's warm. I started it at six forty-five."

"Thank you."

"School runs are mine. Get her seen."

"I'll have her back by four."

"Get her seen."

She took the keys.

The Subaru was a 2014 Outback with the kind of mileage that made it useful and ugly. Robin had driven it twice in six years, both times in March of a bad year. She loaded the bin. She put on the driving gloves. She backed it out of the lot at seven thirty-three, ten minutes off her usual, and she pushed the route fast.

The county road was clear black. The first fourteen mailboxes went in a steady rhythm of *box, flag, box, flag*, and by the time she swung onto the gravel into Galloway country at ten forty she had her shoulders down where they were supposed to be for the first time that morning. She did the Vassar place, the Kemmer place, the Heaton place. The bend by the willows had been graded by the county since Monday, sand on the curve, and she took it at a careful eighteen and the Subaru held the line.

Wes's mailbox was at the cattle guard. She had the bundle ready: a Sandy Hook Christmas card from somebody named Marlowe in Wyoming, a notice from the propane company, a parcel with a Stockmens Bank return address. She slowed.

His diesel was at the gate.

Not pulled up across the road, not blocking. Off to the side, idling, with him at the back of it. He had the tailgate down and was setting a blue thermos on it and tightening the cap. He had on the sheepskin coat and the wide-brim black felt hat. He saw her and lifted a hand, the lift that was less than a wave.

She rolled the Subaru up to the box.

"Morning."

"Morning."

He came over with the thermos, and the cap was the steel cup, and steam came up off it in a column the wind took sideways inside of three feet.

"You took the wagon."

"Pete put me in it."

"Smart of him."

"Yeah."

He held the thermos out, lid loose. Same hand-off as at the ditch.

She looked at it.

She looked at him, and his face did not move, and he did not say anything about her face which she was sure was doing a thing.

"For the road," he said.

She took it. Both hands. The cup was hot through the gloves, and the steam came up into her face and was the first warm thing on it since she had walked from the Camry to Mrs. Thornberg's porch.

"Thank you."

"Yeah."

She took two swallows. It was strong and a little too hot and it tasted like it had been made fresh that morning and put in the thermos at six. She handed it back. He capped it but did not screw it tight, and he set it on the back of the Subaru with one hand and waited.

His eyes went past her once, into the cab, and clocked the empty passenger seat. They came back to her face. He opened his mouth and closed it again, and what he asked instead was whether Pete had put her in the wagon for the day or just the morning.

"Just the morning," she said.

"All right."

The wind came up the draw and went through her Carhartt at the seam between the collar and her hat. She did the thing she did when the wind hit her, which was tuck her chin down and hunch.

He took off the sheepskin coat.

He did it without a word, just the two-handed shrug a man does in his own dooryard, and he stepped to her left and put it across her shoulders. The coat was warm on the inside from his back. It smelled like hay and a wood fire and the faint diesel that lived on the cuffs of every man who started his truck before light. The collar came up under her ear. She did not move.

"You don't need it," she said.

"I'm in the truck in two minutes."

"I'll get it back to you."

"Sure."

He stepped back. He picked the thermos up off the back of the Subaru and set it in her cab on the passenger seat through the open door, on top of the route map she would not be needing today, and he did it in the same neutral way he had set the coat across her shoulders. No flourish. No look held a beat too long.

"I'll bring it tomorrow," she said.

"Sure."

She got the bundle out and walked the parcel down to his porch. The walk was a hundred yards of frozen dirt. She felt the coat across her back the whole way, the weight of it doing what the coat had been built to do, which was be there. She set the parcel on the porch under the eave. She walked back. Wes was already up at his diesel with the hood of his Carhartt sweatshirt on, hands in his back pockets, watching the road.

She got in.

"Drive easy."

"I will."

She pulled away with the coat still on her shoulders.

She did not give it back.

Three miles down the gravel she registered it, and she did not turn around. She told herself she would drop it tomorrow at the box. She told herself this twice in a mile and stopped because she could hear her own voice doing the thing.

The Subaru rolled into Livingston at twelve-forty. She swapped to the Camry at the post office without going in and took the I-90 to Bozeman with Hen's car seat empty in the back. She stopped at Mrs. Thornberg's and lifted Hen out of the kitchen where Hen was holding a graham cracker in two hands like a rare book, and Hen came along.

The walk-in clinic was in a strip mall off Main on the Bozeman side. Carla turned out to be a woman about sixty with a long grey braid and a face that Hen liked on contact. She walked Hen through the chair. She looked. She tapped, gentle. She had Hen open and close. She nodded.

"Baby tooth. Chip's clean. No nerve exposure. She's not flinching when I tap, see that. She'll be fine. The tooth's going to come out around five anyway. Until then it'll feel like a chip. Soft food two days. Cold things if she'll take them. Tylenol if she gets fussy at bedtime."

"That's it."

"That's it."

"How much."

"One-eighty for the visit, no x-ray today. We can bill you."

"I'll pay it."

"Front desk."

Robin sat in the chair with Hen on her lap while Carla went to write up the chart, and she put her face down into the top of Hen's hair and breathed once with her eyes closed and counted to four and lifted her face back up.

Hen pointed at the ceiling. There were paper fish on it, hanging on threads, blue and orange and green.

"More."

"More fish."

"More."

"Yeah, bug. So many fish."

She paid the one-eighty at the front desk with the debit card and the small clutch of dread that came any time she ran a card she hadn't budgeted, and the woman at the desk said *Merry Christmas, hon* and Robin said *you too* and got Hen and the diaper bag and the Cheerios and left.

In the parking lot, the Camry's door was frozen. She used her hip. Hen watched.

"Don't tell anybody I did that," Robin said.

Hen said *Mama* with the bored authority of a person who had registered hip-to-door as a normal Mama maneuver.

She drove home.

The interstate was clear. The Absaroka was a wall of white to her left, the Bridgers a longer wall to her right, and the sun came through the windshield warm enough that she had to ease off the heater. Hen fell asleep before Bozeman Pass. Robin drove with the heater on low and one hand on the wheel and the other on her own thigh, and somewhere between the pass and the gas station at Trail Creek she registered the sheepskin coat in the back seat under Hen's bag. She had carried it out of the Subaru and into the Camry without thinking about it.

She did not think about it the rest of the way.

She got home at four ten. Hen woke up in the drive and walked herself in, careful of the steps because Mama had said to be careful of the steps and Hen had a position on it now. Robin made oatmeal with applesauce stirred in. Hen ate slow and on the right side. Robin watched her do it and did not say anything that was sentimental because Hen would have rejected sentiment with the hum of refusal.

Mrs. Thornberg called at five.

"How was the dentist."

"She's fine. Chip on the baby tooth. Cosmetic."

"Good."

"Yeah."

"Robin."

"Yeah."

"I had a call this morning from the Galloway boy."

The kitchen tilted half a degree. Robin held the phone away from her ear for a count and brought it back.

"What."

"Wes Galloway. From the end of your route. He called the office at nine-something. Said he was a neighbor, said he was on Hen's emergency-contact list. Asked if she was all right. I told him she was."

"Norma."

"I was going to mention it tomorrow morning at drop-off but I figured you'd rather hear it now."

"Wes Galloway is not on her emergency-contact list."

"Uh."

"He is not."

A pause. Robin could hear Mrs. Thornberg pulling the form, the rustle of her opening the file.

"Honey, I have him on the form. With his number."

"Since when."

"Since...." A long silence. "I don't know. I don't have a date. I update from what comes in. Sometimes Pete gives me a number, sometimes you do. I've got him written in pencil and the pencil is recent."

"Norma."

"I don't remember adding him."

"He is not on her list."

"I hear you."

"I never put him on it."

"I hear you."

"Okay."

"Honey."

"Norma. I have to go. I'll see you in the morning."

She hung up.

She stood at the sink with the phone in her hand and looked at the dark window above it, and the cold came at her from the inside this time. She put the phone on the counter. She put both hands flat on the counter. She breathed in for four and out for six.

Hen was at the kitchen table with her oatmeal half eaten, watching her.

"Mama."

"I'm okay, bug."

"Mama."

"I'm okay."

She made herself a bowl of oatmeal she did not eat. She ran the bath. She got Hen in and out of it without asking the tooth to do anything brave. She put Hen in the long johns and the wool socks and got her under the quilt with Horse, and she sat on the edge of the bed for a count of four with her hand on Hen's back and felt the small warm machinery breathing along.

She did not know yet what she was going to do.

She knew she was going to do something.

She turned off the lamp. She sat in the dark for the count of another four. She pulled the door to two inches and went down the hall to the front room, and she stood in the front room in her socks and looked at the sheepskin coat folded over the arm of the couch where she had set it when she came in.

She did not move it.

She washed the oatmeal pot. She locked the door. She turned off the porch light.

Outside the kitchen window the cold held flat at twenty below, and somewhere up the valley a diesel started and a man's headlights went on.
