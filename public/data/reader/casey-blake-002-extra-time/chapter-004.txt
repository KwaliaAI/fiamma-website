Four flights to the apartment plus four more to the roof means I have earned this coffee twice, which is the math I am doing instead of the math I came up here to do.

The machine pulled the 4:02 shot on the first try, which I still cannot accept is my life now. I iced it within an inch of its identity, because it is eighty-eight degrees on this rooftop and climbing, and then I carried it up here like an offering. The Stanley sweats against my shin where I've parked it. My shoulders do the tight sunburn pull when I haul the laptop out of the bag, the pull that says you did this to yourself on day one, Brennan, and you will be feeling it through the group stage. Down in the jacaranda the cicadas are tuning up like an opening act that knows the headliner isn't coming. It is 4:18 in the afternoon and they have zero chill. NONE.

The sidebar is open before I'm all the way into the chair. Liga MX to El Tri, the pipeline piece, eleven hundred words on how this roster actually got built, due tonight. It's fine. It's honestly good. It has a nut graf my editor will pretend not to love, and I have read it eleven times today, which is how I know the sidebar is not what I came up here to do.

What I came up here to do is the thing I have been doing for two days, which is stare at one line of Sunday's roster release like it owes me money.

Here's the thread, the way I'd lay it out for Maeve if I were laying it out for Maeve, which I am not. Sunday afternoon the federation publishes the tournament roster, the real one, injuries settled, twenty-six names. Sunday night, eleven-ish, Mateo takes a call on the balcony, in Spanish, forty minutes, his voice down in the low private register it never uses on television. I was at the kitchen table pretending to transcribe a coach's presser and absorbing exactly none of it. Twice, clear as anything from where I sat, he said primo. Primo means cousin. I know maybe nine words of Spanish and that is somehow one of the nine.

And on Sunday's roster, third-choice goalkeeper, there is a Reyes.

That's it. That's the entire thread, a surname and a phone call, which adds up to a coincidence plus a language I don't speak, and a normal person closes the tab. I have not closed the tab. The tab has been open for two days, and I keep it open the way you keep a tooth your tongue won't leave alone, and every time I tell myself it's roster diligence I get a little spike at the back of my neck that has nothing to do with diligence and everything to do with the fact that I am currently living four floors above the most connected man in Mexican football media, and my press pass is downstairs on his kitchen table right now, where it lives between matches, having a better week than I am.

Two days of being suspicious of my own curiosity. That's a first for me. My curiosity and I are usually on excellent terms. My curiosity pays the rent.

The rooftop is technically a shared terrace and functionally my office. There's a plastic table, two chairs that don't match, and a view that runs over rooftops and water tanks until the haze takes it. I have filed every piece of this tournament from this table, sweating onto my own keyboard like it's part of the process, and the wifi up here is terrible in a way I have decided to find charming, the way you'd find a coworker charming if the coworker kept you honest.

The plan, such as it is: finish the sidebar edit, file by six, earn the sunset. Great plan. Functional-adult plan. The kind of plan made by a woman who hauled a laptop and an emotional support beverage up eight flights specifically to avoid a man who is, at this exact moment, four floors down doing something unforgivable like rolling his sleeves up to wash a pan.

I take a sip. Iced oat-milk, my standing order, so much oat milk it stops being coffee, and that's the point. I scroll up from the sidebar to the roster tab the way I have fifty times already. For diligence. Goalkeepers, three names: the starter everyone knows, the backup everyone knows, and the kid.

I'm mid-sip when it happens.

*Iván Reyes, 22, GK, U-23 promotion 2025, from Pachuca.*

I have read this line thirty times in two days. I've read it until it stopped being a sentence and turned into wallpaper. And on read thirty-one, with oat milk on my tongue and one cicada going off like a car alarm, my brain finally does the thing it allegedly went to journalism school to do.

Mexican naming convention. Father's surname first, then the mother's. The roster prints a single surname because that's the shirt version, and the kid's shirt version is Reyes, and Mateo's shirt version is Salas, and those are different names, and that is exactly why this stayed a thread instead of a fact for two whole days. But the mother's surname is right there on the long paperwork, on every federation page, if you bother to look past the shirt. Iván Reyes is the short version of somebody. Iván Reyes could be the short version of Iván Salas Reyes.

And the rest of Sunday surfaces with it, the part I half-heard from the kitchen table and filed nowhere: the name that went by riding next to the one word I did catch. Primo Iván. Sunday night, the balcony, forty minutes in the low register, the same night the roster dropped.

I set the Stanley down on the railing.

The oat milk has separated in the heat, a band of milky white sitting on top of the coffee like it also needs a minute. I watch it for a count of three.

Cool. Cool cool cool.

The cousin was the cousin.

"Shit," I tell the jacaranda. A cicada answers.

I don't move for a count of forty. I count it, one Mississippi at a time like a kid waiting out a thunderstorm, because moving feels like making it official and standing still feels, weirdly, like due diligence. The cicadas hit full volume at 4:30 on the dot, very professional of them. Off to the southeast the Estadio Akron sits in the haze, minding its business. My sunburn pulls across both shoulders, and somewhere around Mississippi thirty-eight I notice I have the railing in a grip I should probably apologize for.

Forty.

I pick the Stanley back up. The separation band has not resolved itself. Same, honestly. My mouth still tastes like oat milk, and the coffee has gone watery and too strong at the same time, the way everything on this roof is suddenly two things at once.

It's a maybe. A strong maybe, the kind with a first name and a date attached, but strong maybes are why God invented the fact-check, and my fact-check is downstairs, standing next to the one man on this continent I cannot ask.

I go down the four flights like a person and not like a reporter. The difference is all in the knees.

By the time I hit the kitchen doorway the whole floor smells like green salsa going quietly nuclear in a pan. Mateo's at the stove, sleeves rolled to mid-forearm, stirring with one hand while the other goes up for a bowl on the open shelf, and the shoulder that comes with the reaching is, professionally speaking, a goddamn problem. There's sweat at the base of his neck from the stove heat, one dark line of it sliding down into his collar. I would like the record to show that I came down here for facts.

"Chilaquiles," he says without looking up. "Verdes. The Sunday salsa earned a second life."

"It's almost five."

"It's lunch. Here the clock works for the food, not the other way around."

I park the Stanley on the counter, where it lands with its usual forty-ounce authority.

"You carry that thing like it's a medical device."

"It is. It is keeping me alive."

"It is keeping the oat milk industry alive."

"Someone has to. They're farmers, Mateo. They farm oats."

He glances back at me, and that's when I clock the sneakers. White, box-fresh, a white that should be legally impossible in the second week of June.

"Day four of a World Cup and your shoes look like that."

"Like what."

"Like they have never seen a pitch."

"They're broadcast shoes, Brennan. They see lighting rigs."

"They're whiter than my byline photo."

"Everything is whiter than your byline photo. You burned by the first Tuesday."

He's not wrong, and it's annoying, so I open the fridge to give my face a job. There's an avocado in there from a moment of optimism at the OXXO, and I would like it entered into the record as a contribution.

"I have avocado. You want it on a toast situation?"

"Avocado on toast is a federal crime," he says, without turning around.

"In which country."

"All of them."

"You said xG on national television like it was a person's name, and toast is where the line is?"

"A man needs lines." He tips the pan and the salsa goes over the totopos with a sound like soft applause. "Without lines it's anarchy, and then it's brunch."

I put the avocado back. The avocado has been through enough.

"What are you filing tonight?" he asks the stove.

"Sidebar. Tournament history, pipeline stuff. Extremely sexy material."

"Send me the history paragraphs first. Last time you had Chivas winning a league they did not win."

"That was a typo and it was one time."

"It was a golazo of a typo. I framed it."

"You did not frame it."

"It's in the studio in Mexico City. Ask the crew."

He might actually have framed it. That's the thing about this man: the bits are load-bearing, and there is a nonzero chance my worst typo is hanging on a wall somewhere next to an Emmy.

And here is where I am supposed to be a normal houseguest, eating a late lunch, losing an argument about a typo. Instead I'm watching his hands move through the plating, wrist economy, no waste, the same hands from the espresso lever, and the whole time one question is sitting behind my teeth with its seatbelt off. Is the kid on the roster your primo. Eight words. I could say them in the time it takes him to crack the egg, and he would answer me, because it's an easy question if you ask it as family and a loaded one only if you ask it as press, and I am, fun fact, both things at this table.

The egg hits the pan. I say nothing. Saying nothing, it turns out, tastes like green salsa.

"Sit. Eat."

"I have a deadline."

"The deadline keeps. The chilaquiles have a window."

I sit. The deadline does, in fact, keep.

He slides the plate in front of me, says, "Provecho," and turns back for his own.

"Which means?"

"Eat while it's hot. More or less."

"That is not what it means."

"It's the spirit of what it means. The dictionary version is boring, and the abuela version takes an hour and comes with a second lunch."

The chilaquiles are stupid good, is the problem. Totopos still half-crisp under the salsa, crema, a fried egg doing quiet heroic work on top. I make a noise I would not want broadcast. He doesn't gloat, and somehow the not-gloating is its own kind of gloating.

We eat. He talks about Saturday, the second match, the drive out to the Akron, what time the production crew calls for him, and I do the thing where I am fully present at this table and also running a second program in the back of my head, the one with the surname in it, and the second program is loud. He eats the way he talks on air, complete sentences, pauses in the right places, and somewhere in the middle of it he refills my water without asking, which does not help the second program at all. Then he reaches across for the salt and the forearm comes with it. Day four. I am not going to survive the group stage.

"Your family watching the matches?"

I ask it like lunch conversation, because it is lunch conversation, because every reporter on this beat asks everybody this exact question every June of a World Cup year, and because apparently I am the kind of person who needs to poke the bruise to check that it's a bruise.

"All of them. The things my aunt says about the back line would get her a red card."

"Sounds like my kind of source," I say, into my plate.

"She's nobody's source."

He says it light, the way he says everything, already up and moving back to the stove, and it lands in the middle of the table anyway, four words with their own specific gravity. He doesn't notice. That's the part I will be carrying back up the stairs with the Stanley: he handed me the entire shape of his wall, easy as passing the salt, and went back to his egg.

I finish everything on the plate. I would lick it if I lived alone.

"Thank you for this. Seriously."

"You say it like I pulled you out of a fire." He shrugs with the spatula. "It's leftover salsa."

He runs water over the pan, his back to me, and I get my phone out under the table edge like a person checking the weather, the universal posture of a person who is not checking the weather.

Liga MX's site, the official one. Player pages load the slow way, sponsor banner first, then the kit photo, then the line I came down here for.

*Iván Salas Reyes, 22, GK, son of Mariana Reyes y Luis Salas.*

Both names. Both of the names, sitting in one bio line on an official page that anyone on earth can read for free.

Instagram next, thumb on autopilot, because the cross-check lives there and I know exactly where it lives. Mateo's bio is seven items long and has been seven items long for as long as I have been creeping it, which is, fine, a while. And there, item four, where it has been sitting in public this entire time: *hijo de Mariana.*

Confirmed.

A source would call this corroboration. Two official pages, independently published, mutually reinforcing, the kind of paper trail I'd brag about if it had taken any work at all. In my chest it files under something else entirely, and I do not open that folder at his table.

No siren goes off. The water keeps running, and the kid on the roster is his primo, his actual blood, the family back line, and the whole investigation took ninety seconds at his kitchen table with his food still working on me. That's the part that lands wrong, the ease.

(Since Sunday. The word primo has been sitting in my notes app since Sunday, time-stamped, filed under roster misc, while the man who said it cooked me two meals and called my Stanley a medical device. I transcribe people for a living. I fact-check pronunciation guides recreationally. Sunday.)

He shuts the water off, and the phone is face-down on my thigh before I have consciously decided to put it there. My heart is going like I took the stairs already. I stand, I rinse my plate next to him at the sink, I top the Stanley off with water because eight flights in this heat is a blood sport, and the whole time he is drying the pan and humming something I don't know and suspecting nothing, which is somehow the loudest thing in the kitchen.

I head for the stairs.

"You live on that roof now?"

"The roof respects my process."

"Tell the roof you have a deadline."

"The roof knows."

Four flights. My heart rate is a number I am choosing to attribute to the climb.

The laptop's where I left it, lid up, sidebar waiting, the afternoon starting to loosen toward evening over the rooftops. Work first. Be a professional first and a disaster on my own time.

I read the sidebar once more, and there it is, the thing I already knew would be there: paragraph four, the pipeline-kids paragraph, the U-23 promotions, the names nobody profiles. There is a slot in that paragraph shaped exactly like him. Twenty-two, Pachuca, third keeper at a home World Cup, family already inside football media. One sentence of work. One sentence, and the sidebar goes from good to the thing other writers screenshot and send each other with the knife emoji.

I don't write the sentence.

The actual journalism of it, with my feelings parked on the railing next to the coffee: what I have is a bio line and a balcony call I was never supposed to hear. What I don't have is the kid, a single quote, or any reason this matters beyond being delicious. A surname plus proximity makes a coincidence with great hair, and the difference between a coincidence with great hair and a story is the entire job. The cousin is a different story, maybe a real one, maybe a good one, and I don't have it yet, and you don't break a story by smuggling its ankle into a sidebar.

So the sidebar goes in clean. Filed at 5:52, eleven hundred words, zero cousins. ESPN dot com will have it up by morning, and for one full minute I sit there with my hands off the keyboard feeling like a professional.

The minute ends.

I open my thread with Maeve, because that is what the thread is for. Maeve has heard about this man's forearms in forensic detail. Maeve has a standing position on the shoes. Maeve answered a photo of the espresso machine with a wall of crying-laughing faces and the words sis, run. My thumbs go:

*okay so. the roster. you are not going to believe*

Delete.

*you know what, nothing. weather's great.*

I look at that one for a long time. It's a good text, normal, sisterly. It is also a lie of omission to the one person I tell everything, which is a brand-new product I am apparently shipping today. And Maeve can smell a withheld update from two thousand miles out; she once diagnosed an entire breakup off the word fine.

I don't send that one either.

Because telling Maeve makes it real, and telling Maeve makes it a story with an audience, even an audience of one in Boston who would take it to her grave. Right now it is neither of those things. Right now it's a coin I am holding that nobody knows I am holding, and the second I show it to anyone, anyone at all, I have to decide what it's worth and who pays.

The phone goes face-down on the table. The cicadas have downshifted into their evening setting, same as the day setting with the smugness turned down.

The sun goes fast at the end here, like it remembered another appointment. Somewhere below me a door closes, water moves in the pipes, the ordinary sounds of a man who has no idea, doing his dishes in broadcast shoes. The dregs in the Stanley have gone tepid and the separation band never did fix itself, and I drink them anyway, because I paid for this coffee in flights of stairs and I am taking everything I have earned today.

Four floors down, my press pass is on his kitchen table where it lives between matches, flat and quiet, and tonight is the first night since I landed that I can feel it through the floor.

I pull up the Liga MX page one more time, because apparently I need to see the line again, and the rooftop wifi, two bars of pure theater, does what it always does at this hour.

His bio sits on my phone, stuck at half a loading bar, and I let it stay stuck.
