My alarm goes off at 4:15 a.m. and I am up before the second ring, because it is World Cup opening day and I am, professionally, a child on Christmas morning.

I packed last night: pencils, two notebooks, the ESPN laptop. I packed them the way other people lay out a wedding outfit, everything squared up on the desk, charger coiled, and then I lay in the dark for forty minutes listening to my own heartbeat doing little drum fills. The press pass goes over my head before I am even out of the bedroom, which means that for a solid ninety seconds I am wearing a media credential with my pajama shorts, and I regret nothing. My sunburn objects to the shirt. The shirt wins. By 4:38 I am dressed and approximately human and following the smell of coffee down the hall.

Mateo is already in the kitchen. Univisión polo, dark jeans, dress sneakers, because he is on television today and shoes matter on television, a fact he explained to me on day two with the gravity of a man explaining the offside rule to a toddler. It is 4:40 in the morning and he looks broadcast-ready. He looks like the part of the morning show where they cut to the handsome one. I hate it here.

"There's a cup by the machine. Specific angle. You have four minutes."

"I need six."

"You have four."

The cup is waiting by the machine, empty, and I pull the shot myself at the angle he showed me, and the machine gives it up clean, no whale noise, no ceremony, like we're past ceremony now. I drink it standing at the counter while he checks his kit bag, and it is, damn it, a genuinely good cup of coffee, and I tell him nothing, because we are colleagues and roommates by clerical error and his face already knows.

In the elevator I open the Uber app and he glances at my screen.

"We are not paying twice for the same airport at the same time."

"I'll Venmo you half."

"Órale." It is the órale with a period built into it, the tone that means not happening.

"That's not an answer."

"It's not, no."

So we share the car. Guadalajara at five a.m. is empty and orange under the streetlights, the OXXO on the corner glowing like the only lit window in a model train set, and neither of us talks much, which suits me, because I'm running my pre-match checklist and he's reading something on his phone with his game face already half loaded. I can see it happening in the reflection off his window, the way the broadcast version of him assembles itself piece by piece on the drive, the jaw first, then the posture. By the time we hit the airport drop-off he is fully the man from the gantry promos, and the man from the kitchen, the one who leaves a cup out by the machine at 4:40 a.m., has been put away somewhere for the day.

At security he goes through ahead of me, and when he turns to collect his bag off the belt, the shirt does a thing across his shoulders that I am not going to describe, on the record or anywhere else, because describing it would require me to have noticed it, which I did not, for more than four full seconds.

At the gate I put one earbud in and play the voice memo Dani sent at 11:40 last night, because Dani Park communicates exclusively in voice memos recorded at hours that suggest she has defeated sleep in single combat.

"Tess. Scene-setter by five local, give me the postcard, jacarandas, all of it. Don't spend the whole postcard on day one."

Noted. Postcard rationing, day one of a possible twenty-nine. I text Maeve, because it's morning in Boston and my sister gets up at six specifically to spite that city.

*press box at the azteca today. the actual azteca*

*scream*

*if i cry at kickoff that stays in this chat*

Maeve has watched maybe forty cumulative minutes of soccer in her life and would also take a bullet for my byline, in that order, and the order is correct.

Mateo drops into the seat next to mine at the gate, kit bag between his feet, the wired earpiece coiled neat around the mic foam on top. He looks at the credential on my chest. I look at the credential clipped to his bag. Same airport, same match, different uniforms.

"Nice lanyard."

"Nice lanyard."

"Mine gets me into the press box."

"Mine gets me a chair with my name on it."

"It does not have your name on it."

"It's taped on. It counts."

He delivers this with the straightest face in Jalisco, and I have to study the departures board so I don't give him the laugh. He knows I'm not giving him the laugh. That is the entire game, and we are both very good at it, which is a problem I plan to deal with at no point ever.

We board the 5:50 Aeroméxico with the rest of the bleary media economy. He puts his bag in the overhead bin and the forearm situation is. Fine. It's fine. Men have forearms, that's where they keep their watches. I take my window seat and open the laptop, and somewhere over the clouds he leans into the aisle.

"Your sidebar said El Tri hasn't won an opener at the Azteca since 2010."

"Because they haven't."

"2011."

"I'll check it."

I check it. Twenty minutes later, with the seatbelt sign on and my dignity stowed in the overhead next to his forearms, I find it: 2011, a comfortable win against a side everyone had forgotten by August. He's right. He's right and he knew he was right when he said it, which means he read my sidebar closely enough to catch a one-year error in a subordinate clause, which I am choosing not to think about at altitude. I fix the line, push the correction to Dani with a note that says "my catch," may God forgive me, and tell him nothing, because the first rule of professional sparring is that you do not announce the score while you're losing.

He doesn't bring it up again either. He just goes back to his phone with the small specific stillness of a man banking a point for later. Forty minutes, wheels down, Mexico City.

We split at arrivals without a goodbye, because Univisión has a car for talent and ESPN has a shuttle for the likes of me, and that's the day in one sentence. The press shuttle smells like deadline, sixteen of us with lanyards out and laptops already open on knees while Mexico City does its enormous gray-green sprawl past the windows. And then the Azteca comes up out of the south of the city, concrete and flags and seventy years of ghosts, and the shuttle goes quiet in a different way, the way a room goes quiet when somebody famous walks in.

A volunteer in a pink vest looks at my lanyard and walks me forty meters out of her own way to point me at the media lift, which is the kind of thing that will make me cry today if I'm not careful. Up in the working press section on the second tier, the bowl opens up below me, green and absurd and huge with the haze sitting behind the far rim, and I stop walking, holy shit, and a man from a Dutch paper walks straight into my back. He apologizes. I apologize. Neither of us means it.

I have never seen a stadium look more like a postcard. I am going to throw myself off the press box.

I set up the way I always set up, laptop centered, two notebooks stacked, pencils where my right hand can find them blind, while the press section fills around me in a dozen languages, the whole second tier humming like one enormous newsroom. The scene-setter is due by five local, four hundred words, and the postcard writes itself, because the jacarandas in the press parking lot are dropping purple onto every windshield in the row and the concourses are already three generations deep in green shirts. I write the jacarandas. I write the grandmother two sections over with a flag tied like a cape, and the vendor outside the media entrance who upsold me a bag of mango with chile on the grounds that I, quote, looked like I needed it, and he was right, and none of that last part goes in the piece.

What I keep looking at instead, between paragraphs, is the Univisión gantry up on the stadium rim, where a silhouette I could pick out of a police lineup at four hundred meters is getting fitted with an earpiece. The little red mic flag catches the light even from here. Somebody adjusts his collar. He stands the way he stands in our kitchen, weight on the left leg, hands going while he talks, and the hands keep talking even while a tech threads the wire down the back of his polo. I watch him work for thirty seconds. Timed. On my phone. (A grown woman running a stopwatch on her own staring, like it's a parking meter. Growth.)

Then I go back to my laptop and write the last hundred words clean, and they're good, and I file at 4:51 local.

Dani replies with a thumbs up, and then, separately: "good jacarandas." From Dani, that is a Pulitzer.

Kickoff at six. Here is what nobody tells you about the Azteca: the noise has mass. When the teams walk out, the sound comes up through the concrete and into the press-box table, and the dead coffee in my Stanley shivers in its cup holder. The whistle goes and the place DETONATES, and I have a job to do, so I do it, with my heart rate at a number I will not be sharing with my physician.

El Tri leans forward from the first minute, the crowd leans with them, and being inside that is less like watching a match and more like working in weather. I'm writing the entire time, running copy in one notebook and color in the other, my pencil hand cramping in a way I have decided to find athletic.

Goal in the 38th. The bowl goes off all at once, eighty-seven thousand people becoming one animal, and the word golazo travels the concourse below us like it's being passed hand to hand. Nobody in the press section cheers, because press boxes don't cheer, anywhere, ever; we just type harder, two hundred keyboards going at once. I get the goal down, minute and feel and the shape of the noise, before the replays finish on the monitor above my head, and my hands are steady and my eyes are stinging, and the second thing stays off the page, per the chat with Maeve, per the rules.

At halftime I file two hundred running words and eat a granola bar of unknown vintage from the bottom of my bag. The second goal comes in the 67th, the noise finds a level above the first one, which I had not budgeted for, and I lose two paragraphs of running copy to my own hands shaking the laptop tray.

It ends 2-0. The final whistle releases the stadium like a held breath, and then the long emptying starts, and I do what I do best, which is stay, writing for ninety minutes while the press section drains around me and the bowl goes from full to a few thousand stragglers singing in the south end. This is the part of the job I would do for free, and my employer should never, ever learn that.

A kid in an attendant's vest works down our row collecting cups, slow, and slower the closer he gets to me, until he stops next to my Stanley, which is forty ounces of matte sage-green engineering and admittedly looks like lab equipment. He taps it.

"¿Qué es?"

"Emotional support water."

Blink.

"Agua."

Thumbs up.

He moves on down the row with his bag of cups, and I put my head down and finish the match story, and it is, if I say so myself, pretty good. Anyway. Filed at 9:40 local. I pack the pencils.

The mixed zone under the Azteca is a corridor with crowd-control barriers and the acoustics of a parking garage, which it partly is. I get a defender for ninety seconds on the clean sheet and a winger for thirty on nothing at all, and I take both, because thirty seconds of nothing from the right mouth is still a quote with a name on it.

Coach Aguilar comes through last, freshly showered into a suit, and gives his answers in Spanish while the FMF translator hands them back in English with the edges smoothed off, and the gap between those two versions is where half my best paragraphs live. Somebody from radio row shouts a question about the third substitution, the 81st-minute one nobody understood, the holding mid off for a second striker with the game already won. Aguilar gives it four words. The translator gives it six. Neither set of words means anything, and all of us write them down, because that's the job.

There's no Mateo down here, and I knew there wouldn't be. Color commentators don't do the mixed zone; they come off the gantry when the broadcast wraps and go straight back to the production truck, and the production truck does not have a crowd-control barrier for the likes of me. On my way out, through the glass by the loading tunnel, I get a few unticketed seconds of him: crossing the lot in the back of a little shuttle cart, still in the polo, sweat dark at his hairline from four hours under broadcast lights, laughing at something the producer next to him is saying, the full laugh, head back, the one I've only gotten the edge of across a kitchen counter. Then the cart turns behind the trucks and he's gone, and I'm in the shuttle queue with my laptop bag trying to separate my shoulder from my body, hungry enough to consider the granola bar's brother.

The Uber crosses Roma Norte a little before eleven, windows down, Phoebe Bridgers in one ear and the city in the other, because Mexico City after a win smells like fried tortilla and somebody's party, and the horns have not stopped since the 38th minute. A man on a traffic island is conducting the honking like an orchestra. I collect my bag from the hotel the network is paying for, where my total stay clocks in at eleven minutes, and aim for the airport and the midnight flight home.

In the cab I do the thing. He gave me his WhatsApp number this morning at the gate, for logistics, and this is logistics adjacent.

*good match. one sentence on Aguilar's third sub? context, not attribution* sent at 11:58 p.m.

It's a real ask and a small one. He sat in that production meeting; a color commentator knows why a coach makes a sub like that the way I know where the bodies are buried in a transcript. One sentence of context. Nothing he'd ever be quoted on, and I said so in the text, in writing, like a person with ethics and everything. I send it and put the phone face down on my knee, and Phoebe sings something devastating directly into my one occupied ear, and the driver and I sit in win traffic together watching a city be happy.

The phone stays quiet through the rest of Roma Norte. It stays quiet through the hotel lobby and my eleven glamorous minutes and the ride out to the airport, and I check it exactly as often as you think I do.

I'm at the gate when the reply lands. 12:41 a.m.

*thanks*

That's it. That's the whole message. I read it three times to be sure there is nothing underneath it, no second bubble coming, no typing dots, no follow-up sliding in late. Thanks, lowercase, no punctuation. The man bullied me about a 2011 scoreline at thirty thousand feet this morning, and he taped his own name to a chair. He told me I had four minutes with the confidence of a man who owns time itself. And the one moment I ask him a question that sits anywhere near his actual sport, his actual team, I get a word you'd give a hotel doorman.

Interesting.

And here is the thing: it's a fine reply. It is a perfectly polite reply from a colleague at the end of a long working day, and if I showed it to anyone they would tell me it's nothing, and they'd be right. The thing they would miss is the cup. The man set a cup out for me at 4:40 this morning, before a national broadcast, with a countdown attached, and somewhere between that cup and the taped-on chair I had quietly promoted us to a two-person bit. The lanyard thing. The four-minutes thing. A bit needs both people in it, and *thanks* is him doing his half from behind a closed door. He'll fact-check my sidebar mid-flight for sport. His own team, his own locker room, his own people: a one-word wall, with good manners on it.

I don't text back. I clock it the way I'd clock a midfielder who keeps refusing the line-breaking pass: noted, dated, filed. A pattern needs two points minimum, and right now I have exactly one.

Boarding starts. I put the phone away and get in line with the equipment cases.

The midnight Aeroméxico home is half empty and fully asleep, broadcast people with crew tags on their bags and their mouths open, and I am the only human on board voluntarily conscious, doing my expenses in a notes app like the glamorous international sports journalist I am. Somewhere over the dark middle of the country I reread my own match story on ESPN dot com, live on the homepage with my byline under the headline, Tessa Brennan, the full formal version of me, and I let myself feel it for one descent's worth of minutes. Day 0 in the books. Twenty-eight more if the bracket is kind. We land at 1:30 into a Guadalajara that has also been celebrating and has also, mostly, gone to bed.

The four flights up to the Airbnb at 2:14 a.m. are, fuck me, a personal Everest. My legs are jelly by the second landing, and on the third I stand there a minute, breathing in a way I would describe as private, holding the strap of my laptop bag like it owes me money. Then I climb the last flight, because the alternative is living on the third-floor landing, and I get the door open quietly out of some instinct I don't examine until I'm inside.

The instinct was right. Mateo is asleep on the couch.

He's on his back, one arm over his face, Univisión polo half-unbuttoned, dress sneakers side by side on the floor where he stepped out of them. His kit bag is by the door. His earpiece is on the coffee table, still wired to its little belt pack. He got the earlier flight, obviously, the one that leaves while print journalists are still transcribing a translator, and he came home and he landed on the couch that has been my bed since I got here, and he is profoundly, completely out. Four hours of live national television will apparently do that, even to a man who irons his casual clothes.

I stand in the dark and consider my options, which is a generous word for them. I'm too tired to litigate real estate at 2:14 in the morning, and you cannot bill a sleeping man for squatter's rights. The man pays half of everything and got home first. Fine.

I do one stupid thing before bed, which is pull a short cup at the machine, specific angle, the second clean pull of the day, and drink half of it standing in the dark, because my body is so far past tired that caffeine is just a flavor now and the ritual is the point. The machine sits quiet on the counter afterward. We have an understanding lately, the machine and I.

Then I dig my sleeping mat out of the closet and roll it out on the floor, eight feet from the counter, a polite diplomatic distance from the couch, and I lie down in my clothes minus the lanyard, which worked its first match today and has earned its night off on top of my bag. The ceiling fan ticks. Mateo breathes slow and even, the breathing of a man with no unread messages. I think about the *thanks* one more time, turn it over, file it where it goes.

I am fine. Source: me. The source declined to elaborate.

His earpiece blinks its little green light on the coffee table, and I watch it from the floor until I don't.
