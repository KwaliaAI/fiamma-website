It is 7:42 a.m. and I am losing to a kitchen appliance for the second morning in a row.

The machine does the sick-whale thing again, that long wounded note from somewhere deep in the chrome, and gives me a hiss, a shudder, and an empty cup. I locked the portafilter in and tamped the way the tutorial said and pulled the lever with the confidence of a woman who has ordered espresso on three continents, and the machine took all of that under consideration and said, in whale, no.

It is also already hot. The kitchen window has the sun in it, my hair has responded to Guadalajara humidity by developing a personality, and somewhere out there the city is waking up in that easy rolling way it has, mopeds and birdsong and a bus exhaling down on Avenida Chapultepec. Two days to the opener. I have a group-stage preview to build, a producer who calls me Tess in voice memos while my contract says Tessa, and a standing order of iced oat-milk coffee that does not exist within four blocks of this apartment. What I have instead is this machine, and the machine has decided we're rivals.

My back has opinions about the couch, the couch has opinions about my back, and nobody in this arrangement has had caffeine. We settled the sleeping situation last night in two sentences flat, his reservation, his bedroom, the couch that does not pull out, and now the next four weeks have terms. Fine. Great. The plan this morning was modest: one espresso, plus one of the airline packs of oat milk I personally escorted through Mexican customs like contraband, and I become a functioning member of the international press corps. The machine has now voted against this plan two mornings running, and I am taking it personally, because it is personal.

For the record, getting to this kitchen required walking past Mateo Salas at seven-forty in the morning, on the living-room floor, mid-hamstring stretch, in shorts. There was a foam roller involved. There were hamstrings involved, plural, both of them operational. Here is the thing about retired footballers that nobody warns you about: the equipment does not retire. The man does mobility work like it's a sacrament, every morning so far, and the quads alone are a broadcast-rights situation. I clocked it the way you clock a speed limit sign, noted, legal, kept driving, and took the long way around the coffee table anyway because I am a professional and professionals manage risk.

Second attempt. I refill the basket, tamp it tighter, lock it in like I mean it this time, and pull. The machine starts its long complaint again, same pitch, more conviction. Somewhere in the building a pipe answers it, which feels like mockery from a colleague. My cup stays empty, and my reflection in the chrome looks back at me, unemployed.

I say a word my byline would never print. I say it quietly and with feeling.

Yesterday I lost with grace. Yesterday I told myself the machine and I had simply gotten off on the wrong foot, that we'd find our rhythm, that this would become one of those travel stories I tell at weddings. Today I understand the machine for what it is, which is undefeated at home.

Mateo comes in off the living-room floor with sweat at his hairline and a Univisión tee with the sleeves cut off, which I assume is a contractual matter between him and his shoulders.

"Okay. Twice on your own. You've earned the workaround."

"It beat me. I didn't lose."

"That's what losing is, Brennan."

"I had it on the ropes."

"You had it making the whale sound. The whale sound is what it does when it's winning."

He says my last name with the press-table emphasis, like he's calling on me from a podium, and the worst part is that it lands. I step half a pace sideways to surrender the machine, and he doesn't take it.

"You do it," he says. "I'll show you."

So this is how, at 7:46 on a Wednesday morning, I end up with Mateo Salas standing behind me at an espresso machine, his hand over my hand on the lever. He doesn't grab the machine and do it for me, which I notice, because men mostly grab the machine. His hand is big and dry and a little rough at the palm, and the weight of it registers somewhere well south of professional. (I am a grown woman with a press credential and a 401(k), and a man put his hand on my hand on a coffee lever and a part of my brain made the slot-machine sound. I will be reporting myself to HR, which is also me, so the investigation should go smoothly.)

"Ready?"

"It's espresso, Salas, not a penalty."

"Everything is a penalty if you respect it enough," he says, and I can feel the grin without seeing it, which is a skill he should have to register somewhere.

"Half a turn past where it fights you. Stop. Tilt the cup, like that. Now press."

Under both our hands the lever travels down to the place where it has spent two mornings stonewalling me, and then it goes half a turn further, and something inside the machine unclenches. The give is so smooth it feels rigged. The espresso pulls. It actually pulls, dark and then gold at the top, crema like the photo on whatever box this thing arrived in a decade ago, and the machine says nothing at all, which from this machine is a standing ovation. The kitchen smells like actual coffee for the first time in two days, and the shot just sits there, golden, looking like it was never the problem.

We both look at it. Then we look at each other, then back at it, because it feels rude to look away from a miracle for too long.

"Eso es," he says. Quiet, to the machine, like the two of them have just concluded a negotiation.

"What did you tell it?"

"That's it. That's the one." He shrugs. "It likes being told."

"That's not a workaround, that's a séance."

"It worked, didn't it."

"You could have shown me the day I landed."

"You hadn't lost yet." He says it like a man quoting scripture. "It wouldn't have meant anything."

"That's deranged."

"That's coaching."

He steps back, and the kitchen returns to its usual square footage. I notice exactly where he goes, the way you stay aware of a ball that's out of play but not out of bounds, and I make a note to stop noticing, and the note goes where my deleted paragraphs go. He reaches past me for the cup, a vein down his forearm running its own programming, and hands it over like a trophy I have in no way earned.

"Salud."

"Thanks."

"You said that in English on purpose."

"Mm."

I drink it. It is the best espresso I have had in any country. It is possibly the best espresso anyone has had in any country. I am going to need a wellness check. My face, meanwhile, does absolutely nothing, because my face and I have trained for exactly this moment our whole career.

"Say it."

"I'm not saying anything."

"Your face already said it."

"My face is a known liar."

"Your face should write for ESPN dot com." He makes himself a shot while he says it, half a turn, tilt, press, infuriatingly casual, like the two of them have never had a single bad morning together. "'A quality espresso, per sources.'"

"Better than your shop. Univisión would call it the greatest espresso in the history of this proud footballing nation and then cut to commercial before anyone could check."

"We would also be right."

"I've seen your clips, by the way. You say Manchester like it has four syllables."

"It earns four. A historic club deserves time." He sips. "And your last piece said 'the Mexican national team' three times in one column, like El Tri wasn't sitting right there."

"House style."

"House style," he repeats, in an American accent that should be illegal somewhere, and then he leans against the counter, ankles crossed, completely at home, and looks at me over the rim of his cup. "Brennan."

"Salas."

"See, when you do it, it sounds like an accusation."

"When I do it, it is."

He grins at that, the full prime-time one, teeth and everything, and I look into my crema like there might be news in it.

"Your byline says Tessa."

"Everyone calls me Tess."

"Tess." Once. The way you say a name you are putting on a shelf for later.

And that's it. Nothing happens. The radio of my body puts out one urgent bulletin and I decline to read it on air. He's already turned back to the machine to make a second shot, completely unbothered, a man with no idea what he just did or, worse, a complete idea. I take my espresso to the table like a normal person who has just heard a completely normal thing, and I open my laptop, and the day starts behaving like a workday. He showers and comes back with his hair wet and a legal pad. I put in one earbud and leave the other out, which I tell myself is situational awareness. By ten the apartment has divided itself into two bureaus with one espresso machine between them, neutral territory, recently pacified. Okay so the day has a shape now: he preps, I prep, and the machine sits over there being fixed and unbearable about it.

By noon the kitchen counter is a Univisión facility. His broadcast kit is open next to the sink, cables coiled in the tight loops that tell you a person has done this nine hundred times, a laminated team sheet, and the mic itself lying on the counter with its red flag up like it's already on air. The foam windscreen is propped against his coffee mug, casual, the way things lean on other things when they live together. There's gaffer tape in the kit with his initials on it in silver Sharpie, which is the single most broadcast thing I have ever seen.

He's at the table with a legal pad and his phone, doing whatever commentary prep is, which turns out to be murmuring surnames at half volume and leaning on different syllables until one of them sounds like television. Every few minutes he starts a sentence with the rising note that I already know becomes the goal call, and stops himself, saving it, the way you don't let a good wine breathe too early. It's annoyingly likable, watching someone be excellent at a job you don't fully respect. I'm working on the second part. I'm across from him doing what writing prep is, which is typing a paragraph about Group A and then deleting a third of it for being too pleased with itself. We have been like this for over an hour and it has been, horrifyingly, fine. We work the way two people work who have both done this a long time, in silence with occasional outbursts. He mutters a date, checks it, knocks the table once when he's right. I read a sentence of mine out loud under my breath to see if it survives air. Neither of us narrates what we're doing, and both of us are obviously listening.

I get up to rinse my cup. The mic is right there.

I pick it up.

I don't know what I expected. It's heavier than my press lanyard by a factor I'd have to call editorial. The red flag on its head is warm from the bag, because the sun has been coming through that window all morning, and the body of it is dense in the hand, balanced, a real instrument. My lanyard is a piece of plastic on a ribbon that says I'm allowed to ask questions from row two. This thing is a scepter. Row two asks; this answers, to however many million people Univisión pulls on a group-stage Saturday, a number I am absolutely not going to look up later tonight on the couch. I hold it for exactly two seconds and then put it back where it was, flag at the same angle, like a woman re-staging a crime scene.

He has not looked up from his legal pad once. He has also, and I cannot tell you how I know this, seen the entire thing.

"It's heavier than it looks."

"I didn't say anything."

"You weighed it like fruit at the market."

"I was being a journalist."

"You were being nosy."

"Same job."

That gets the low laugh out of him, the one that doesn't go on television, and he goes back to his surnames, and I go back to my paragraph, which I delete. It had it coming. The paragraphs that survive me are the plain ones, which my editor calls discipline and I call attrition.

By four o'clock my shoulders have filed a formal grievance. The day-one sunburn has settled in like it pays rent, the strap of my bag finds the exact line of it every time I move, and the sad travel-sized tube I flew in with is not going to survive four more weeks of this sun. So: sunscreen run. The OXXO is two blocks down, fluorescent and cold and stocked with everything a human being could want at any hour of her life, and the guy at the register is watching a match replay with the sound all the way up, and nobody in here is going to ask me a single question about xG. I could live in an OXXO. I would need a cot and a better attitude, and I could live in an OXXO.

I buy the highest-number sunscreen they carry and a Coca-Cola Light out of the cold case, because Coca-Cola Light tastes better in Mexico. Everyone says this and everyone is right, and I'm not going to investigate why, because some sources you protect. I drink half of it on the sidewalk in the shade of a jacaranda, cold enough that I briefly consider filing eight hundred words about it instead of football, and take the long way around the block for no reason I am prepared to discuss. A dog the size of a small bear sleeps in front of the pharmacy like he owns the street, which, looking at him, he does.

Then: four flights, because the elevator's fuera de servicio sign has three layers of tape on it and is clearly load-bearing by now. By the third flight I'm sweating through the back of my shirt, and by the fourth floor I am a different person, glistening, humbled, closer to God.

The balcony door is open when I let myself in, and Mateo is out there on the phone, back to me, one foot up on the rail's crossbar, speaking Spanish at game speed. He's laughing as he talks, conducting with his free hand, the whole conversation moving at a tempo I couldn't merge into with a running start.

My Spanish is press-conference grade. I can do lineups, injuries, and one clean question about a back three, delivered slowly enough that everyone in the room has time to feel sorry for me. What's happening on that balcony is another sport entirely, faster and looser, the sound of a man whose guard went home for the day, and it keeps a laugh folded into it the whole time. Whatever his producer is saying, he's enjoying it, and whatever he's saying back is landing, because he keeps having to wait out the laughing on the other end. It's a good sound, his off-duty voice. The kind of thing a person notices once and then has to manage for four weeks.

I'm not listening. I'm putting sunscreen on the counter and the surviving half of a Coca-Cola Light in the fridge and absolutely not listening, which is how I catch one word, primo, somewhere in the middle of all of it.

Primo. I file it for later, between the OXXO receipt and nothing. In the bag. Whatever.

He comes in off the balcony a few minutes later, phone tucked into his waistband, and finds me applying sunscreen to my own shoulder blades with the contortions of a woman who refuses to ask for help in any language. He clocks it. He looks at my shoulders once, opens his mouth, looks at the sunscreen, and visibly decides to survive the evening instead, which I appreciate so much I could throw the bottle at him. Smart man. Television smart. Then he sees the OXXO bag on the counter.

"Do y'all have OXXOs in Boston?"

"Never say y'all again."

"Órale."

"Or that."

"You bought the good sunscreen," he says, looking in the bag like it's a press release, "and a Coca-Cola Light. You're assimilating."

"I'm hydrating."

"It has caffeine and gas in it."

"I'm assimilating."

Evening comes down slow over the rooftops, and the apartment does the thing where it gets gold for twenty minutes and everyone's life looks better than it is. We eat standing up at the counter over takeout because the table is covered in both our jobs, his legal pad, my laptop, two networks' worth of homework. He eats like an athlete who no longer has a nutritionist and is celebrating. He asks what I'm covering Saturday beyond the match itself, and I say color, atmosphere, the city, and he nods like a man deciding not to offer his services as a source, a decision that lasts four seconds.

"I know places," he says. "For your color. You'll thank me in print."

"I will never thank you in print."

"A footnote."

"Not even a footnote."

He hums something between bites, some song I don't know, and it's catchy, and I hate that it's catchy, and by the third pass I am also humming it, which he notices and has the basic human decency not to mention. This is the problem with the man, if I'm filing honestly. The flagrant ones are easy. It's the ones who hum while they coil cables that you have to watch.

After, he re-packs the broadcast kit. Mic into the foam bed, windscreen on top, cables in their loops, zip, and the duffel settles on the floor by his feet while he answers messages on the couch, one bare foot resting on top of it like he's keeping it from leaving without him. The red flag disappears last. I watch it go and do not weigh anything, like fruit or otherwise, and consider myself a model of growth.

I text Maeve from the other end of the couch, lights mostly off, the day finally quiet. *he fixed the espresso machine with his hand on top of my hand.*

Maeve, eleven seconds later, because Boston is wide awake: *babe that's a rom com.*

Me: *it's a WORKAROUND.*

Maeve: *sure babe. is he hot.*

I don't answer that, which is an answer, and Maeve knows it's an answer, which is why she sends nothing else. My sister has been playing the long game since 2003 and has never once lost.

I lie there with the phone on my chest for a while. Four floors down the city is still going, somebody's music, somebody's dog, a bus. I put one earbud in, Phoebe Bridgers, low, and take it out again two songs later because the day was too good for that and Phoebe deserves my full commitment or nothing.

Mateo gets up at some point, knocks twice on the kitchen counter on his way past, and says buenas noches to me and possibly also to the machine, in roughly equal measure. "Goodnight," I say, in English, on purpose. He laughs once, down the hall, and his door clicks shut. My press lanyard is lying on the kitchen table next to the machine, the two of them side by side, both off the clock until Saturday, and one of us in this apartment should probably think harder about that arrangement than I am currently willing to.

I put my phone face down on the floor and pull the blanket up over the parts of my shoulders that can take it.

My sunburn pulls when I turn over, and the last thing I see is the machine on the counter, quiet, smug, fixed.
