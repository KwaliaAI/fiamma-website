I file my eight hundred words on a goalless Group B snoozer at 11:43 p.m. and check the Monterrey score before the send confirmation even loads.

1-0. El Tri. Stoppage time, a set piece in the ninety-third minute, which means that while I sat here watching two mid-table teams pass the ball sideways for ninety minutes as if scoring were a rumor neither of them believed, a man I live with was four hundred miles away losing his entire mind into a microphone in front of eleven million people.

My editor replies in under a minute, *good, sleep*, which is editor for *I read the first paragraph and trusted you on the rest*, and below me the Akron starts shutting off its lights section by section, top tier first, the way a stadium goes to bed. My match had one shot on target, and the keeper caught it the way a man receives mail. I packed eight hundred words around that shot like bubble wrap and I am not proud of all of them. I skip the mixed zone, because nobody in either dressing room said anything tonight that they didn't also say last week, and I find a cab.

The cab back into Guadalajara has the Monterrey post-match show on the radio, two hosts talking over each other at a volume I can only describe as devotional. The radio call of the goal already happened and it is not the call I want to hear, which is a sentence I am choosing not to examine at midnight in a moving vehicle.

The Airbnb is four flights up with no elevator, and on Day 12 of this tournament my quads have begun drafting a formal complaint to management. I make the climb sweating through the back of my shirt, let myself in at 12:20 a.m., and lift the lanyard over my head. The press pass lands on the kitchen counter next to the espresso machine, dark and smug, and I do not attempt it, because midnight me has been humbled by that appliance before. I wash my one sad bowl with the lemon dish soap instead, because apparently that is who I am now, a woman doing dishes at half past midnight so her hands have something to do that isn't opening Twitter.

The TV takes three remotes and I have it solved by 12:24, which is the most competent I have felt all day. Univisión is re-running the match in full, start to finish, because Mexico won and Mexican television understands the assignment: the country would like to win it again, slower this time, with feeling.

I tell myself I am watching for work, because Group D shapes the bracket and the bracket is my beat. I open a notes file on my laptop to make it official, consider putting on Phoebe Bridgers instead like a person with self-respect, and do neither, because the second half is starting and Mateo's voice is coming out of the television into the living room where his running shoes are sitting by the door in real life.

His broadcast voice is his regular voice in a suit. Same register, same patience, and then little flares of the other thing underneath, the thing that makes producers say he's going to be a star in two languages. Twenty scoreless minutes, and he carries them like a privilege. He gets a yellow-card decision right before the referee does and doesn't gloat about it, which somehow makes it worse for me personally. He pronounces every Polish surname correctly, and I check two of them against the official team sheet because I am petty and small, and I lose both times, and he would dine out for a week on knowing that.

At halftime I refill the Stanley with tap water and no ceremony and stand in the kitchen for a minute regarding the dark espresso machine like we're coworkers. The apartment is loud with him and he isn't even in it: his mug in the drying rack, his banda playlist asleep in the speaker on the windowsill, a silence with a shape. I go back to the couch and tell the notes file we are starting fresh in the second half, and the notes file believes me, because it's new here.

The substitutions come. The legs go. You can hear a match tilt before you can see it, and he hears it a beat before the crowd does, his sentences shortening, the Spanish leaning forward.

The goal, when it arrives again at 90+3, is a set piece so clean it looks rehearsed, the near-post flick, then the header, and the stadium audio rises like weather. And then there's Mateo.

I have heard tens of thousands of goal calls in nine years of doing this job, and I am telling you, as a professional, that this one rearranged my internal organs. He goes up through the gears and holds the o. He holds it past the replay, past the pile-on at the corner flag, past what I would have sworn were the physical limits of the human diaphragm, and then he lands the de México soft, almost gentle, the way you set something down after carrying it a long way. Somewhere in the middle his voice cracks, and the crack is the whole broadcast, the sound of a man who loves this ridiculous sport so much he will bleed for it on air at full volume and call it Tuesday.

I have never been more attracted to a sound in my life. Anyway.

At 12:48 a.m. I find the clip on Twitter, where it is already doing numbers, and I watch it twice. For accuracy. I am fact-checking the call, is what I am doing, the way one does, at one in the morning, alone, in the dark, with the same match still playing on the television four feet away. (I have a national byline. I have covered three majors and a Champions League final. I am watching a man yell about a corner kick, twice, on purpose, and the second time through I watch his hands on the desk instead of the replay behind him. The technical term for what I am doing has not been invented yet, but it is not fact-checking.)

I am a liar, for the record. I wanted to hear his voice. Now I have heard it eleven times if you count the television, and I feel like I drank the espresso the machine refuses to make.

I close the laptop. Then I reopen it, because the notes file deserves better than four words, and the four words it has are *set piece, near post*, which any intern could have typed with their thumbs. The TV murmurs on through the post-match panel, four men in suits being euphoric responsibly, then hands off to a reporter outside the stadium in Monterrey, where it is apparently raining sideways and her umbrella is losing, and I have stood where she is standing, so I send her professional solidarity through the television. On the coffee table my Stanley has gone full meltwater and sloshes a quiet little judgment at me when I shift my weight. I pull the throw blanket up and negotiate: ten more minutes of euphoric suits, then I will brush my teeth and be a functioning adult with a deadline structure. The laptop settles onto my stomach, somebody on the panel says his name, and the last clock I see says 1:30.

The deadbolt wakes me at 3:01 a.m.

I know it's 3:01 because my phone is on my chest under the laptop and I grab it the way you grab a railing. The door swings open and my body knows it's Mateo before my brain finishes the thought, which is information about my body I am electing to deal with later.

He is supposed to be in Monterrey, asleep, with a civilized flight tomorrow morning, the kind with daylight and a window seat and a small juice. Instead he is standing in our doorway at 3:01 a.m. with his duffel over one shoulder, and he is drunk, not the messy kind, the kind a man gets when he has been celebrating in the booth with the production crew, then on a plane, then in a cab, the loose-shouldered kind, where everything in him has unclenched at once. His Univisión polo looks like it lost a fight with a suitcase. His hair is doing what hair does after three hours under broadcast headphones, which is making independent decisions. His shoes squeak on the tile when he steps inside.

"You're up," he says.

"You're dripping."

"Monterrey had weather." He looks down at his shoes like they belong to someone else. "Thunderstorm at the airport. It followed the plane, I think. I'm hecho una sopa."

"You're what?"

"Sopa. Soup." He says it slow and generous, drunk-teacher patient. "Made into a soup, Brennan. It is a thing we say. The rain wins, you are soup."

"You took a one a.m. flight in a thunderstorm so you could be soup in Guadalajara specifically."

"The soup is incidental."

"How drunk are you, on a scale of one to postgame?"

"I'm." He considers the question with his whole face. "Celebratory. The crew had a tequila agenda. I held the line for an hour."

"And then?"

"And then we won a World Cup match in the ninety-third minute, Brennan. The line fell." He reports this like weather, no defense offered, and drips on the doormat a little more.

He stands there a second longer in the doorway, big and rumpled and pleased with the entire concept of tonight, and I watch him decide against whatever he was going to say next. He swings the duffel through his bedroom door without looking, a no-look pass he will absolutely deny is showing off, and heads for the kitchen, because some autopilot in every drunk man on earth knows the way to water.

I sit up. The laptop slides to the cushion. The smart move at 3 a.m. is to say goodnight, congratulations on the win, and lie back down on this couch like a person with judgment. We have been carefully, professionally fine since the fight, the kind of fine that requires maintenance, and the maintenance schedule does not include kitchens at 3 a.m.

I follow him into the kitchen.

He has the tap running and a glass filling and the only light is the small one over the stove that we never turn off. The espresso machine sits dark behind him on the counter, silent for once, off duty. My press pass lies where I dropped it at 12:20, flat on the counter between us, lanyard coiled, and the kitchen smells like Tuesday's mezcal and the lemon dish soap from midnight, which together smell like a bar that cleans up after itself.

"That gol lasted longer than my cab ride. Did your producer survive?"

"He cut my mic halfway. I finished it for the room." He drinks the whole glass of water in one go, throat working, and refills it, and there's the grin, the prime-time one, slightly melted at the edges tonight. "The room appreciated me."

"Eleven million people appreciated you. I checked the numbers. It's my job to check numbers."

"Mm." The grin shifts into something more awake. He looks past me at the living room, where the TV is still going on mute, still re-running his own match, frozen on a lower-third graphic of the goal with his network's logo in the corner.

"You watched the call," he says.

"I was fact-checking the pronunciation."

"Of gooooool?"

"You'd be shocked how often it's wrong."

He laughs, quiet, into the second glass of water, a laugh that's mostly breath, and the laugh does something to the air in here, because when it fades it doesn't get replaced. He sets the glass in the sink with more care than the glass needs. The tap drips once. Four floors of sleeping building hold still around us, and the celebration drains out of his shoulders by degrees, settling rather than leaving, the way crowd noise settles after the final whistle when everyone remembers all at once that they have homes.

He looks at the counter. At the press pass on the counter. The look lasts longer than it should and he doesn't dress it up as anything else, doesn't pretend to be hunting for a snack, doesn't reach past it for the fruit bowl that isn't there. He just looks at it.

The drip from his shoes has slowed to a tick. Outside, Guadalajara is as quiet as a city of five million ever gets, which is a hum with edges, and inside this kitchen the quiet is total and somehow extremely loud. I should say something funny. I have built a whole career on having something to say, and nothing arrives.

I am standing across the kitchen from him in sleep shorts and the t-shirt I filed in, barefoot on tile that his shoes are quietly raining on, and I become aware of my own heartbeat the way you become aware of a refrigerator, a sound that has been running this whole time and chooses now to get loud. He's still in the wrinkled polo. There's a crease across his chest where a seatbelt sat for an hour. He smells faintly of tequila and airports and rain, and under the stove light I can see the damp at his collar and the line of his shoulders through the cotton, and I think, with the clarity of 3 a.m., that I have spent twelve days being extremely normal about those shoulders and the streak is in danger.

There are maybe six feet of tile between us, which in my professional life is a press-conference distance, a mixed-zone distance, me on my side of the rope line. Neither of us moves to make it five.

I could go to bed. I am aware of the option the way you're aware of the gym. It exists, it's right there, people speak highly of it.

"I should not be in this kitchen," he says.

His face, when he says it, does something I have never seen it do on television. A fast honesty. There and gone in under a second, like a frame spliced into the broadcast, the prime-time everything dropping away, and what's underneath looks younger and not at all drunk and slightly stunned at its own arrival.

"No," I say.

"I came home anyway."

"I know."

The pipes tick somewhere in the wall. My heart is doing something arrhythmic and unjournalistic. For twelve days I have watched this man fix appliances and stretch hamstrings on my living-room floor and pronounce Polish surnames like a show-off, and I have filed every single day of it under colleague, and the folder has been bulging since the Akron, and at 3:04 in the morning, in a kitchen that smells like mezcal and lemon, the folder gives out.

He puts his hand flat on the counter, next to the press pass, not touching it. His forearm in the stove light is all standing tendon and one raised vein, a hand that has been talking for him on camera all night and has now run out of things to say. He looks at the pass for a long second. One Mississippi. Two. I watch him look at it, and I do not say anything useful, because everything useful is on the other side of a line that one of us is about to draw or cross, and the espresso machine sits dark behind him like furniture at a hearing, and somewhere outside a single car goes by four floors down and takes its sweet time about it.

Then he looks at me.

"Tess."

"Mateo."

He crosses the kitchen.

He comes around the counter slow enough that I could stop it with a syllable, slow enough that the whole journey is a question, and I do not say the syllable, and his hand comes up to the side of my neck like he has done it in his head before and is checking his work. Someone is going to have to notify my next of kin. That is the entire thought. My brain produces it, files it under pending, and goes dark.

He kisses me.

It starts slow and careful, almost formal, a first touch of mouths so polite it belongs at a podium, and I can feel him holding the rest of it back, the restraint running down the line of his arm into the hand at my neck. He tastes like Casamigos and airplane coffee, and the rain off his shoes is spreading on the tile under us, and my bare toes are an inch from the puddle and I could not possibly care less.

Then I make a sound against his mouth, small, involuntary, a sound I will deny until I am DEAD, and the slow part ends.

His thumb settles under my jaw and tips my head and the kiss goes deep and certain, twelve days of carefully professional deciding it is done being either, and my hands find the front of that wrinkled polo and pull, and he comes in close, all of him, the heat of him arriving a half second after the rest. He is big and rain-damp and absurdly solid, and he kisses with the same disgraceful patience he gives a scoreless first half, and one of my hands gives up the polo and finds the back of his neck, where his hair is still ruined from the headphones, and ruins it further. The counter edge presses into my back. My press pass is six inches from my hip, flat on the counter, and neither of us touches it. His other hand stays planted on the counter the whole time, braced, like that hand is the designated adult, while the rest of him kisses me like he caught a 1 a.m. flight for exactly this, which he did, which we are both pretending requires no further comment, and I kiss him back with every single one of the twelve days I spent pretending I wasn't going to.

We break for air. His forehead rests on mine. I can feel him breathing, can feel the heat coming off him through the damp cotton, his thumb still under my jaw like it lives there now, and my entire nervous system is on its feet doing the wave.

Cool. Cool cool cool.

He kisses me once more, softer, shorter, a signature at the bottom of something, and then he stops.

He breaks first, half a foot back. His thumb leaves my jaw last, like it disagrees with the ruling. We stand there breathing at each other in the stove light, his shoes wet on the tile, the machine dark, the building asleep, and I watch him come back to himself the way a man surfaces, in stages, eyes first.

"The press pass," he says.

"I know."

"I'm going to bed."

"Yeah."

He goes. He walks down the little hallway with the careful straightness of a drunk man who has decided to be dignified about it, and his bedroom door doesn't close all the way. It stops half shut, a strip of darker dark, and I hear the bed take his weight and then nothing.

The TV is still on mute in the living room, still looping his best night on delay. I find the remote by feel and put the broadcast to bed too.

I boost myself up onto the kitchen counter, on the far side, away from the pass, and I sit there in the dark for two minutes with my heels against the cabinet and my mouth still tasting like his.

My phone is on the couch, eight feet away. It is after 5 a.m. in Boston, which means Maeve is awake, because Maeve gets up at five to row a machine to nowhere on purpose. She would answer in nine seconds. She would have a flowchart by ten. I look at the dark shape of the couch with the phone on it and I stay where I am. Some things you have to hold in your own two hands for one night before the group chat gets them.

I slide off the counter, and the tile is cold, and there is rainwater on it that came here all the way from Monterrey.

The couch takes me back without comment. I pull the blanket up and lie there with my eyes open, listening to the building hum and the half-closed door not closing, and the ceiling does absolutely nothing for two hours while I supervise. The last time I check my phone it says 5:20 a.m. Eight feet away in the dark, my press pass is still on the counter, right where neither of us touched it.
