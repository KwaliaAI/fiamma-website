Wyatt was up at four-forty-five because he was always up at four-forty-five and a woman in the main house had not changed that, even if she was the whole reason his head had started at three-fifty-eight and worked on the problem of her for forty-seven minutes before giving up and standing up.

He put the kettle on. The kettle was a real kettle on a propane burner because the electric coffee machine had broken in December and he had not replaced it, on the grounds that replacing things was for later, and later had quietly become April.

The WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE cookie jar was on the counter next to the sink, where it had been for six years. Deb had given Hal the same jar the year before, because Deb's way of loving two men at once was to give them matching cursed ceramics. Wyatt took a packet of instant out, tore it with his teeth, shook it into the mug Deb had given him with the mug and the jar as a matched pair of jokes about his life. He poured water. He drank the coffee standing up, because sitting down at four-fifty meant he would stay there and think about a woman again for another forty-seven minutes, and the cows had not agreed to that.

He thought about her for eight seconds and decided the thinking was not productive. He put the mug in the sink and went out to the barn.

The mud in the yard was the same mud from yesterday, which was the same mud from the day before, because it was April and that was what April did. The sky to the east was the colour of something that had been left out in water. Somewhere in the main house a light was on in a kitchen that had not had a light on at five a.m. in eleven years.

He did not look at it for long.

By five-twenty he was in the calving barn going through the heifers. Number 142 was close, not today, probably Friday. Number 88 had dropped hers two nights ago, a slick black bull calf who was already finding the teat with the bored competence of a child who had been born into the family business. Wyatt stood in the dim close dark of the barn with his hand on 88's hip and thought about how much easier this particular kind of morning was than the one that was coming up in the yard.

He was not going to be the one to make her coffee.

He was going to bring her coffee because she did not have a machine and the instant was in his jar, and that was a neutral act of competence, not the kind of thing a man did when he had woken up at three-fifty-eight.

He filled a thermos.

At five-fifty-five he stood on the main house porch and knocked. He heard her say shit from the kitchen, in the way a woman says it when she has just bumped into a piece of furniture she did not remember existing, and then her socked feet crossed the floor toward the door.

She opened it.

She had on jeans that were clearly bought at a Seattle REI last Tuesday. The fleece had a tag sticking up out of the neck at the back. Her hair was in a ponytail pulled too hard at the temples, the way a woman pulls it when she has decided in advance that today she is not going to care what her hair is doing. She was wearing a pair of Hal's old work boots with the laces done up tight at the second hook, which meant she had stuffed socks in the toes. They were two sizes too big. He could see the extra room at the heel.

She looked at the thermos.

"Is that for me."

"Yes."

"What is it."

"Coffee."

"What kind."

"The bad kind."

She took the thermos, unscrewed the cap, poured an inch into the cap and drank it standing in the doorway in her boots-too-big, watching him over the rim.

She said, "This is terrible."

He said, "I know."

She drank the rest of the capful.

"I'll come out in ten."

He walked back to the barn.

The thing that had happened to his chest when she stood in the doorway with her hair pulled hard and said is that for me was a thing he was not going to think about in a barn where the cows could see him.


She came out at six-oh-three, which was close enough. The boots flapped. She had the thermos in one hand and a steno pad in the other, as if she had decided on the walk across the yard that she was going to take notes, and decided halfway across that she was going to pretend she had not decided that. She was holding the pad against her thigh, facing in.

He opened the passenger door of the ranch truck.

She got in. She took one look at the bench seat, which had a split in the vinyl and a coil wrapped in electrical tape coming out of the split, and sat on the coil side without comment.

He closed the door. He went around.

The radio came on when he turned the key. It was already on the station, because it had been on the station since 2012, and the station was playing From This Moment On, Shania Twain, because the station was a Shania Twain station that occasionally played weather. He watched her face in his peripheral vision as she worked out that this was not a joke, and then worked out that there was not another station, and then set her jaw.

She said, "Is this — "

"Yes."

"The whole drive?"

"Yes."

"All three hours?"

"Yes."

She looked straight out the windshield. She did not ask him to change it.

He drove her out on the loop he did every morning. Fence-line on the west boundary, up to the BLM permit ground where the cows would go on the first of June, back along the creek where the calves had been dropping, and east to the county road where the neighbour's bull had come through in '22 and was still the reason Cal and the neighbour did not speak at the feed store. He did not simplify. He said heifer and breech and cow-calf and carrying capacity and did not explain any of them, because she had a jaw that was not going to ask, and he was curious how long the jaw would hold.

The jaw held.

Somewhere around the second hour they crested the ridge above the north pasture, and he pulled the truck off the two-track and stopped. The post at the corner was leaning.

She looked at it.

She said, "That post is crooked."

"That lean's from wet ground. Post's fine."

"Are you sure."

"Yeah."

She made a note on the steno pad. He did not look at the note. He thought, without deciding to, that he liked the way she wrote — fast and hard and slightly to the left — and left the thought where it was.

They went back down the ridge, the truck bouncing through the ruts. She put her hand flat against the door to brace, and the other hand on the seat between them, and when they hit the cattle guard she made a small noise in her throat that was not a complaint.

At nine-forty he asked her, because he had been curious about it since yesterday, "You schedule the spring brand inspection yet."

She said, without hesitation, "Yeah. I handled it on the drive down."

He looked at her.

She looked straight ahead.

He said, "Good."

He let it sit.

He had been on this ranch eleven years and the brand inspector was Cal's cousin Ray and Ray did not schedule anything with anybody who was not Cal. He had been about to ask her how it was done, because he was trying to get her to ask him things, not the other way around. She had cut him off at the pass by the simple expedient of saying she had already done it, and she had said it with the face of a woman who had said it before she had thought about saying it, and she was now watching the fence-line through the windshield as if the fence-line had personally betrayed her.

He kept driving.

What stuck with him was the speed of it, the way the lie had arrived before the question was finished, which meant she did it a lot, which meant she had a list somewhere in her head of things she had said yes to without knowing what they were, and he was now on the list.

He started a list of his own.

He put brand inspection on it.


Back at the barn at eleven-thirty. He pulled into the yard and parked the truck in the same rut he always parked the truck in. She got out on her side before he could come around, because the boots flapped and she was proud of the boots flapping.

He said, "Come here a minute."

He walked her down the barn aisle to the far stall. A bay mare was standing in it with one hip cocked, head down, ears forward, watching them come without moving anything else.

He said, "That's Cinder. She's yours if you want to ride. She's been Hal's since she was four. She's sixteen now."

"Okay."

"She does not load in a trailer. Don't try."

"Why not."

"She has strong feelings."

"You people anthropomorphize your animals."

"Try loading her."

She looked at him.

He looked back.

He said, "The trailer's around back. Rope's on the hook. She'll walk to the ramp. That part she does fine. Ramp is where you'll find out."

She said, "Fine."

She walked off down the aisle with the thermos still in her hand.

He watched her until she was out the barn door, and then he went to the tack bench and sat down and started mending the halter that had been waiting on him since Tuesday, which had nothing to do with Cinder or Juniper McClaren or the thing in his chest, and everything to do with the halter, which was broken.

The thing in his chest was a thing he had not felt in a long time, and he was not going to call it by a name in a barn at eleven-thirty on a Tuesday, because naming it would make it the kind of thing that changed the way he moved, and the cows needed him to move the way he moved.

He threaded the leather through the buckle.


June walked Cinder out the back of the barn on a slack lead and thought, hard and hot, about the word anthropomorphize, which she had just said out loud in a barn to a man who had used the words BLM permit ground and carrying capacity at her without blinking for two hours, and she thought she might have been a little condescending.

Cinder followed her like a tall polite person going to a party she had no intention of enjoying.

The trailer was exactly where he had said. The ramp was down. The interior of the trailer was the colour of old straw and smelled like every horse that had ever been in it, which to June, who was three days into smelling new things, was now a smell she recognised.

She said, to Cinder, "Okay. In you go."

Cinder walked to the base of the ramp, looked at the ramp, looked at June, and did not go up the ramp.

June clicked her tongue. She had seen people click their tongues at horses on television and she had assumed this was a thing horses did things for.

Cinder blinked.

June said, "Walk on." She tugged the lead. Cinder's head came forward an inch. Cinder's feet did not.

"Cinder."

Cinder's left ear rotated slightly to track a fly. Everything else about Cinder stayed exactly where it had been.

June had been a litigator for seven years. In that time she had cross-examined a man who ran a shell company out of a Tacoma storage unit and made him cry on a Thursday afternoon, negotiated the MediaCo closing over a dinner where opposing counsel ordered the thirty-four-dollar salad and then asked for a box, and, once, made a senior partner at Perkins apologise to her in writing. She was now being outlasted by a horse.

She said, "Please."

Cinder sniffed her hand.

Cinder did not go up the ramp.

Cal came out of the side door of the barn with a feed bucket and watched for two full minutes without moving. Cal was fifty-eight, lean, had the kind of face that did not show anything on it, and was wearing a ball cap with Hutterville Feed across the front that was the colour of a cap that had been on a head since 2006.

Cal said, "She's got you."

June said, "I can see that."

Cal said, "She got Hal too. For about six years."

He walked off.

June stayed for ten more minutes because she was not ready to walk away from something she had failed at. She tried the halter pressure thing she had seen in the article she had read at eleven last night, tried walking Cinder in a small circle and coming back at the ramp, then tried standing next to the ramp and pretending the ramp was not there. Cinder stood the whole time with an expression of mild academic interest in what the human was going to try next.

At twelve-thirty-five June gave up and walked Cinder back to the stall, where the horse went in without comment and began eating hay immediately, because the hay was in the stall and the stall was not a trailer.

June said, "Fine."

Cinder ate hay.

She walked back into the barn aisle. Wyatt was still on the tack bench with the halter. He did not look up. She was relieved he did not look up, because the heat in her face was not something she wanted him to see, and she was also annoyed he did not look up, because she had wanted him to see her coming back with her jaw a little lower than it had been, as proof that she was capable of being wrong, which was a thing she was trying to practice.

She said, "Lunch?"

He looked up.

He said, "Cinder?"

She said, "Cinder is a bitch."

He said, "Yeah."

She could see him not laughing.


The main house kitchen at one-twenty was colder than his barn by about eight degrees, because Hal had gone cheap on the insulation in 1987 and nobody had gone back and fixed it. She made sandwiches at the counter while he sat at the table with his boots off and his socks on the rung of the chair across from him. She had offered him a sandwich. He had said yes. She had said what kind and he had said the kind you're making, and she was not sure if that was dry or just economical.

She was making ham and cheese.

The ham she had bought at the gas station yesterday. The cheese was a block of cheddar she had found in the main house fridge that she was ninety percent sure had been there when Hal was alive. She had sniffed it twice and decided it was fine, because she was a woman who had flown to Montana in eleven-month-old underwear and she was not going to be precious about cheddar.

Her phone buzzed on the counter — Ruthie, one word, surviving? — and she turned it face-down without answering.

"Mustard."

She said, "What."

Wyatt said, "On mine. Mustard's in the fridge."

"Your fridge?"

"Yeah. I brought it over."

She opened the fridge.

The main house fridge had, on the top shelf, four yellow mustard squeeze bottles of slightly different sizes, arranged like a family photo.

She said, "I meant which mustard."

He said, "Any of them."

"There are four."

"I know."

She closed the fridge, opened it again, and the mustards were still there.

She said, "Wyatt."

"Yes."

"Did you bring four mustards over here, or do you have eight at your house."

He thought about it.

He said, "At my house I have five."

She closed the fridge.

She went to his truck in her socks and came back with the thermos. She put it on the table, opened the fridge a third time, chose a mustard at random, and brought it to the counter with the knife.

She said, "This is the fridge of a serial killer."

He said, "Thanks."

She made the sandwich without looking at him, because if she looked at him right now she was going to laugh, and she was not ready to let him have the laugh twice in two days. She pressed the top slice of bread down onto the sandwich with more force than the sandwich needed.

Behind her at the table there was a small sound.

It was the sound of a man laughing without opening his mouth. It was a sound that came from somewhere under the sternum, and it lasted about a second, and then he coughed to cover it.

She said, to the counter, "Was that a laugh."

He said, "No."

She put the sandwich on a plate.

She turned around. She set the plate down in front of him. He looked up at her from the chair with his face entirely still, and she watched the corner of his mouth do a thing that was not a smile but was a thing.

She sat down across from him. They ate their sandwiches without talking about the mustards.

At one-fifty-two, Cal came to the kitchen door and said to Wyatt, "142's acting up." Wyatt said, "Okay," and put his boots back on and left. Cal, from the porch, said to June, "By the way. Ray called me yesterday about the brand inspection. Said he'd swing by Thursday." June said, "Ray." Cal said, "My cousin. Brand inspector. He'd have called me, not you, he doesn't have your number." Cal tipped his cap and walked off across the yard after Wyatt.

June stood in the kitchen with a half-eaten sandwich on a plate and the fridge door she had closed three times and the mustards she now knew had been brought over as a joke or a test or a bit, and she realised she had lied to Wyatt about a brand inspection two and a half hours ago, and that Wyatt had let her lie, which meant he had known, which meant he had not corrected her, and she did not know yet whether that was kindness or something else.

She sat back down at the table and ate the rest of the sandwich.

She did not correct it.


At two-ten Wyatt was back in the calving barn. 142 was not acting up. She was fine, had just stood up and laid down twice because she sometimes did that, and Cal was leaning against the post outside the stall with his arms folded because Cal had also known 142 was fine.

Wyatt said, "You pulled me out for nothing."

Cal said, "I pulled you out because you were sitting in her kitchen."

Wyatt said, "I was eating a sandwich."

Cal said, "Yep."

Cal was quiet for a minute. Then Cal said, "She's going to be trouble."

Wyatt said, "Yeah."

Cal said, "You ready for that."

Wyatt did not answer.

Cal looked at 142, who was chewing, and then at the aisle. He took his cap off, ran his hand through his hair, put the cap back on.

Cal said, "That's what I thought."

Cal got about six feet down the aisle and stopped.

Cal said, without turning around, "You working through tonight."

Wyatt said, "Yeah. I'll get the feed done, redo the loose boards on the north side of the corral, bring the tractor around."

Cal turned and said, "You working through because the calves need it, or because she's going to be out here in the morning with a steno pad."

Wyatt did not answer.

Cal looked at him a beat longer, tipped his cap again, and went out the back door.

Wyatt stood at 142's stall with his hand on the top rail. The yard would look like a yard that had been worked. That was the actual reason. He did not name the reason out loud.

He pushed off the rail, and his back started up somewhere under the left shoulder blade, the way it did when he had slept four hours and was planning on sleeping three more, which he was.

He went to get the feed.
