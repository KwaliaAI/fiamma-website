At six in the morning the east pasture looked like somebody had set up a small county in it overnight.

There were four pickups by the gate and three more coming down the ranch road with their headlights still on against the flat grey light, and a stock trailer on the near side of the pens with a Miles City plate. Two long folding tables were set up under a tarp against the loading chute. A coffee urn the size of a small child was on the near table next to a stack of paper cups and a tin of sugar.

June walked across the grass in the boots she had bought at Cooper's on Thursday. They fit. Her left heel did not move inside the leather. She had not pretended today, not even to herself, that this was the same thing she had done in Hal's work boots a week ago, and the difference was in her ankle.

Wyatt was at the pens with Cal. He had his sleeves already pushed up and his hat at the angle he wore when he was not going to take it off for the next six hours. He saw her come across the grass. He looked. He went back to the gate.

Ruthie was with him at the bottom of the rail and did not break off to come find her.

June stood ten feet from the coffee urn with her hands in her jacket pockets and did the thing she always did when she had not been to a thing before, which was look for a woman older than she was and ask her a question. The woman nearest the urn was in her fifties in a barn jacket and a Carhartt beanie and looked like she had been up since three.

June said, "Is there a place I should put myself."

The woman looked at her. She said, "You're the granddaughter."

June said, "Yes."

The woman said, "Pour the coffee for a while. Somebody'll tell you when."

She did not say her own name. She walked off toward the chute. June poured coffee into paper cups for twenty minutes and did not count how many people came to the table.


A man with a grey moustache took a cup without breaking stride and said, "Hal would've loved this." He was past her before she had answered, and she watched his back go toward the pens with the cup still in his hand. He had a boy with him of about sixteen who came up to the table five minutes later on his own and held out a cup with both hands.

The boy said, "Ma'am."

She said, "Cream?"

He went red to the ears and shook his head and took the cup and walked off fast. His father turned round and lifted the cup at her in thanks and kept walking.

A woman named Shelley arrived in a maroon coat and said hello and asked, before she had finished the hello, whether June had taken any meetings yet with Ballard Land Company out of Bozeman. June said she had not. Shelley said, "Well. Their offer last year was generous," and took her cup and walked off toward a man who turned out to be her husband, and June watched her go with the urn spigot still open over the cup.

She met Mary from the diner again. Mary said, "Over easy, wasn't it." June said, "Yeah." Mary said, "Good for you." June did not ask what the good for you was for.

A man named Pete came up with a thermos the size of a rifle scope and poured the urn coffee into it on top of whatever he had in there already, and said, "Saves me going back to the truck," and walked off.

She got Cal's wave from across the pen at seven-fifteen. He was at the branding fire with an iron in his hand, not waving her over, just acknowledging her. She waved back.

She made coffee for another ten minutes and then left the urn to a high schooler named Kayla who had turned up with her mother, and she went to the pens and asked what to do.


Cal put her on a calf for about thirty seconds at nine.

He had been watching her for two hours at that point without her knowing it, she would figure out later, and he had gauged the moment. The calf was already stretched out and a man named Shane had the head end and another man was on the brand and Cal said, "You. Back legs, hold the top one."

She got down on her knees and put both hands on the calf's upper hind leg above the hock and held where he showed her. The calf was hot under her hands and stronger than she had any reason to expect, and she braced her weight against it the way Cal had shown her with a flick of two fingers. The iron came down. The smell went up. She did not let go until Cal said okay, and the calf stood up and ran fifteen feet and looked around with an expression of mild offense, and Cal said, "Good," without looking at her, and walked to the next calf.

She sat back on her heels for about four seconds. Her hands were shaking a little. She did not know whether the shaking was from the bracing or from something else, and she was not going to know today, and she was not going to look at it today either.

Across the pen Wyatt was at the far gate with his shoulder to the rail, watching her through the work. She looked up and caught him. He did not look away. He went back to the gate a beat after she did.

She did that three times in the next forty minutes. Every time she looked up he was already looking.

Neither of them said anything about it. She did not think he would.


At eleven-thirty Cal walked up to her with a woman June had not met.

Cal said, "June. Deb. Deb, June."

Cal walked off.

Deb was Wyatt's sister in the way a fact is a fact. The jaw was his jaw. The mouth was not. Deb was about five-seven and broad through the shoulder, in jeans and a barn jacket and boots that had been on her feet for a long time, and she had a coffee in her hand that had come out of the urn at some point and gone cold. Her hair was dark and cut short and the grey had started at the temples. She had her brother's eyes and a mouth that was wider and did more things.

Deb looked at June for about two seconds. She looked across the pen at Wyatt for about two more. Then she turned back.

She said, "So you two are sleeping together."

June said, "Excuse me."

Deb said, "I've seen my brother look at a woman twice in forty-two years, including his late wife. This is twice."

June's throat closed around a breath for half a second and then let it go. She kept her face quiet, because her face was a thing she had trained, and she said, "That's a very direct opener."

Deb said, "I drove two hours, I don't have time for subtle."

She smiled. The smile said this is how I am, you can take it or you can spend the day avoiding me, and June knew the smile because she had given the smile to junior associates, and she also knew that the woman giving it to her now was not somebody she was going to avoid.

June said, "Okay."

Deb said, "Okay. Get me a new cup, mine's cold."

June got her a new cup.


They worked the gate together from noon.

Deb had done this for twenty-five years and did not explain herself; she let June watch and copy. Deb opened the gate at the right second, swung it closed on the hip of the next calf, and kept her weight on her back foot in a way that would not put her down if an animal hit the gate from the wrong side. June did the same thing about eight calves in and Deb said, "Good," in the same tone Cal had said it, and June knew that tone was how this family said things.

Deb talked while they worked.

She said, "He broke his thumb the Christmas I was thirteen. We were up at our uncle's in the Bitterroot and he went over a railing chasing our cousin and landed on his hand. He drove home one-handed because Dad was drunk. He was eight."

June said, "Eight and he was the one driving?"

Deb said, "Yeah. He was eight and Dad was drunk. We didn't tell our mom till the next day. Our mom took him to the doctor and the doctor asked who had been driving and my brother said he had, and the doctor looked at my mom and my mom looked at my brother and she said yes he had, and that's the family."

She swung the gate. Another calf went through.

She said, "He learned to ride the summer he was seven. Hal taught him on a grey mare named Peggy that had no business being taught on and he fell off twice before lunch and cried twice and got back up twice. He taught me to drive stick at fourteen. In the truck that is currently out in the yard with my plates on it. He was sixteen. He was patient. He was not patient with himself at that age. He was patient with me."

June said, "Huh."

Deb said, "What."

June said, "I've known him eleven days and I have learned more about him in forty minutes with you than in eleven days with him."

Deb said, "Yeah, that tracks. Swing the gate."

June swung the gate.


By two-thirty the work was most of the way done, and a folding chair had appeared under the tarp, and somebody had put a bottle of red wine on the end of the second table with a stack of paper cups next to it. Deb poured two.

They sat in the shade with their boots out in front of them. Across the pen Wyatt was tagging a calf with Cal and was not looking in their direction, which meant he had been looking in their direction a minute ago and had decided to stop.

Deb said, "Tell me about the last one."

June said, "The last what."

Deb said, "The last guy. Don't pretend there wasn't one."

June took a sip of the wine. It was the kind of red somebody's uncle had put in the back of a pickup and it tasted like it, and she drank a second sip anyway because she was tired and because Deb was easy and because her brain had gone into the mode it went into at three in the morning in a hotel bar with a friend she would not see for a year, and the mode was not careful.

She said, "Partner at the firm. Forty-three. He said he was getting divorced. He was not getting divorced. He was getting a summer."

Deb said, "Oh, honey."

June said, "Yeah." She watched the tarp ripple where the wind caught the corner. She said, "He broke it off in September by text. I was in Chicago on a deal. I read it in a cab at eleven at night and I said okay out loud and I paid the cab and I went up to my hotel room and I did not cry in the cab."

Deb said, "When did you cry."

The question was easy and it was not pushing and it was exactly the kind of question a woman asks another woman when she has two hours and a cup of bad wine, and June answered it before she had decided to.

She said, "In November. In the shower. On a Thursday. I was shampooing my hair and I started and I could not stop for about seven minutes, and I washed my hair twice because I did not want to get out until I had stopped, and then I got out and dried my hair and went to a deposition and deposed a man about the setback line on a parking lot."

She knew, about eight words in, that she should stop talking. She kept going. She heard the whole paragraph come out of her mouth and she heard it at the same time as Deb heard it, and a part of her brain said stop, and her mouth did not stop, and she finished the sentence because the sentence had a shape and the shape wanted to finish.

She drank the rest of the wine.

Deb was quiet for a second. Deb said, "That's a lot to tell a woman you met at lunchtime."

June said, "I know."

Deb said, "I'm not going anywhere with it."

June said, "I know that too."

Deb said, "Okay."

She poured them each another half cup.


A little after three somebody whistled at the far end of the pen, and Cal lifted his hat at Deb, and Deb said, "That's me, I promised him I'd help load." She stood up. She put her hand on June's shoulder on the way past and did not say anything, and June sat in the folding chair alone for about four minutes watching a woman she had just met walk across a pen she did not yet know the whole shape of.

The wine was in her sternum. The paragraph was still in the air somewhere above the tarp, and she could see it sitting there like a balloon she should not have let go of, and she was going to spend the evening with the balloon, and tomorrow, and possibly Monday. She had not been mortified in this specific way in a long time.

She stood up. She went back to the pens. She worked the gate for another forty minutes until Deb came back and waved her off, and then she went up to the main house to start on the kitchen.


The kitchen at the main house had not cooked for more than two people since Hal, and it had not cooked for seven since before June had been born. Deb had come up at five with a casserole dish and a bag of rolls from a gas station and a bottle of red that was better than the one from the pen, and she had put all three things on the counter and said, "You make the salad, I make the rest."

June made the salad.

Most of the neighbors had gone. Two trucks were still down by the pens with their tailgates open. Wyatt was still out there finishing. He had come up to the porch once at four and put his head in the kitchen door and said, "Half hour," and gone back out.

Deb poured two glasses.

She said, "Three weeks."

June said, "What."

Deb said, "He said you're here three weeks."

June said, "Yes."

Deb said, "Then what."

June said, "Then I go back to Seattle."

Deb said, "Okay." She drank. She set the glass down. She said, "My brother is very bad at asking for things he wants. He will not ask you to stay. I say this as a public service announcement, not an instruction."

June said, "Noted."

Deb said, "Also I like you and I would like more of you in my life. That's also a PSA."

June said, "Noted twice."

Deb laughed. She drank the rest of the glass.


At six-fifteen Wyatt came up to the porch and stood in the mudroom taking his boots off, and June heard the boots come off one at a time and then the door open to the kitchen. He had on the shirt he had been wearing at six in the morning and it had the day in it. He looked at his sister. He looked at June at the counter with the salad in front of her. He said, "Hey."

Deb said, "Hey yourself."

He said, "I'm washing up," and went down the hall to the bathroom.

Deb watched the hallway until the door closed.

She turned back to June. She was two glasses in. Her face was loose with the wine in the way June's had been loose with wine under the tarp, and June saw it and did not have the energy to be careful with it.

Deb said, "You know he's going to ask you to stay, right?"

June set the knife down on the cutting board.

She said, "You said three hours ago he would not."

Deb opened her mouth and closed it and opened it again.

Deb said, "Yeah. I did." She looked down into her glass. She said, "I don't know which of those is true. I said both."

June said, "Okay."

Deb said, "I shouldn't have said either one."

June said, "It's fine."

Deb said, "It's not, but thanks."

She did not pour another glass. She went and sat at the kitchen table with her hands around the empty one, and when Wyatt came back from the bathroom in a clean shirt she said hey at him in a register one lower than she had used all day, and he looked at her for a beat before he sat down.

June finished the salad.

She heard everything and she was not going to know what to do with any of it tonight.


Deb left at twenty past eight.

She hugged June at the door, long enough that June understood it was both a hug and an apology, and then she hugged her brother in a way that had more than one sentence in it. She got in her truck and took the ranch road slow because the light was going, and from the porch step June watched the brake lights flash once at the cattle guard and then the truck was up the grade and out of sight.

The sky was still blue over the ridge. It was not dark yet and it would not be for another twenty minutes. Deb would be home by ten-thirty, which was a respectable hour for a two-hour drive after a day like this one.

Wyatt came up behind June on the porch and put his hand on the back of her neck.

He did not squeeze. He did not stroke. He put his hand there, palm flat, and stood.

She leaned back into it about a quarter-inch and he took the quarter-inch and did not ask for more.


A little while later Ruthie made her choice.

Wyatt had walked down off the porch and across the yard toward his house to swap his shirt again for a flannel, and Ruthie had been on the boards at June's feet where she had been on and off for six days, and Ruthie came up off the boards and trotted after him. June said, "Ruthie." Ruthie did not turn. She said it again. The dog went round the corner of the porch with Wyatt five steps ahead of her, and both of them disappeared behind the line of cottonwoods, and Ruthie did not come back.

June stood with the leash she had never once put on Ruthie looped around her fingers and watched the place the dog had been.

She understood she had been traded. She stood with that for about half a minute. She did not feel bad about it; she felt correctly placed in a hierarchy that had always had another name at the top. She hung the leash on the hook by the door.

When Wyatt came back from his house in a flannel he had not had on earlier, Ruthie came back with him, and went and sat on the mat by the door, and put her chin on her paws.


At nine Wyatt was in the chair on the porch and June was on the porch floor with her back against his leg, and Ruthie was on the mat between his boot and her hip. His hand was on the back of her neck again. It was not asking anything. She let her head rest against the inside of his knee.

Neither of them was talking.

She was thinking about what Deb had said. She did not bring it up.

He said, "Long day."

She said, "Yeah."

He said, "Bed?"

She said, "Bed."

They walked across the south pasture together in the last of the light. The grass was wet over her boots at the ankle. His hand brushed her hand once and then did not, and she was glad about both, because she did not want him holding her hand for the quarter-mile and she wanted him to have thought about it.

In his bed they did not have sex. He took her shirt off and pulled a t-shirt of his over her head and put his arm around her and lay down, and she lay against him with her face in the hollow between his shoulder and his chest, and Ruthie was on the foot of the bed.

She closed her eyes.
