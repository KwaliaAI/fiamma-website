At four-oh-seven that morning she had googled brand inspector Montana in the dark under her comforter. At three-fifty-two she had remembered she had lied to a man about scheduling a thing she did not know the definition of. A brand inspector in Montana, it turned out, inspected brands on cattle being moved across county lines, and was almost always a guy named something like Ray. She had put the phone back down at four-twenty-eight and not slept.

She did not tell him any of this when he knocked at five-fifty-seven.

She opened the door in yesterday's jeans and Hal's second-best Carhartt off a hook in the mudroom. The boots were the same too-big boots with more socks in the toes. He held out the thermos. She took it. She said, "Thank you." He said, "Fence-line today." She said, "Okay." Neither of them said brand or inspector or Ray. That not-saying sat in the doorway for the four seconds it took her to close it behind her and follow him off the porch.

It kept sitting between them in the truck.

He drove north. The radio was already on. It was already Shania. She looked out the window because looking at his hand on the wheel was a fool's errand for six in the morning. Ruthie was on the bench seat between them with her head on June's thigh.

At six-forty they stopped at a gate and he got out and unhooked it and drove through and got out and hooked it again, and she watched how he did it so she could do it next time without him saying anything, because she was trying not to make the Ray mistake twice.

He said, "Post driver's in the bed. Grab it when we stop."

She said, "Okay."

She did not ask what a post driver looked like. She was going to find out.


They stopped at a section of fence along a draw where the wire had gone slack and two posts were basically lying down. He pulled up parallel to the line, cut the engine, and got out. Ruthie got out too and walked twelve feet away and sat down in the dead grass with the expression of an overseer.

The post driver was a length of heavy pipe with handles welded on, closed at one end, and it weighed more than any piece of sports equipment June had touched in her life. She hauled it out of the bed with both hands. Her lower back was already tired.

He said, "You slide it over the top. Lift. Drop. Let the weight do the work. Don't swing it."

She said, "Okay."

She slid it over the top of a new post. She lifted. She dropped.

The post moved approximately a quarter of an inch.

She lifted. She dropped. Quarter inch. She was going to be at this post for the rest of her life.

Wyatt was already two posts down the line, working his own driver with the boring competence of a man who had driven several thousand posts. His went in. She watched his shoulders for half a second and then made herself stop, because watching his shoulders was not driving her post.

She went back to her post. She lifted. She dropped.

He came back at her third post, when he saw it was going in at a lean that was going to make the wire do something unhelpful in two years. He said, "Your grip. Here," and put one finger, briefly, on the knuckle of her right hand and turned it about fifteen degrees. "Lead with the thumb. Don't choke it."

His finger was on her knuckle for about a second.

She said, "Thanks."

He went back to his line.

She lifted. She dropped. The post went in straighter. She felt professionally useful for about forty-five seconds, and then she went on.

The fifth post was the one.

She slid the driver over it, lifted, and the head of the driver wobbled at the top of its arc. Her right wrist compensated. She dropped, and the angle was off, and the post took the hit and went in crooked.

He was next to her before she had fully straightened up.

He said, "Here."

She stopped.

He put his whole hand over hers on the handle, his palm over the back of her hand, his fingers over her fingers, his thumb against her thumb. He said, quietly, "Like this. Let it come down square. You're muscling it. Don't."

His hand was big. It was entirely over hers.

Three seconds.

He took his hand back.

She said, "Okay."

He stepped back a half-step and watched. She lifted the driver. She let it come down square. The post went in straight.

He said, "Good."

He went back to his line.


She stood at her post for three seconds after he had walked away, listened to her own breathing, and then drove the next post. It went in fine. She drove the next one. It went in fine. She was not thinking about the fence-line.

She was thinking about his palm.

A gust came down the draw and flattened the dead grass and moved on. She tightened her hand on the driver.

She was thinking about the fact that his palm had been over hers, and he had taken it off, and the taking-off was the interesting part, because he had taken it off the way he did everything, unhurried and not apologising for it.

She drove another post. It went in a little crooked. She drove the one after it. That one went in crooked too. She did not look over at him.

She drove five more along that stretch. Two were fine. Three were not. She kept going.

At the bottom of the draw she slipped, just a boot on wet grass on a slope, and sat down hard on her own heel with the driver clanging against the post. She said, "God damn it," mostly to the grass. She got up and went on.

By nine-thirty her shoulders were a foreign country. Her palms were hot inside her gloves by ten, and by ten-fifteen she had unbuttoned the Carhartt down to the third button because she was sweating under it. She kept going.

He corrected her a third time around eleven.

She was driving a post at a ridiculous angle because her brain was somewhere else and her hands were doing an approximation of what they had learned. He walked up behind her. He was holding his own driver. He said, "Hold on. Let me — "

She pulled her hand back.

She said, "I've got it."

He stopped.

He looked at her for the length of a breath. His face did not change. He said, "Okay."

He walked back to his own line of posts, fifty feet away.


She drove posts for the next two hours without him coming near her again. She knew she was getting the angle wrong on about half, because each one went in and then leaned a degree too far, and then the next would go wrong in the other direction. She drove them anyway.

He did not look over. She knew he was not looking because she was looking enough for both of them. He was twenty yards away now, driving a post every ninety seconds, straight every single time, with the horrible predictability of a man who had done the work a thousand times and was not going to offer again.

At noon he walked back to the truck, got out two sandwiches and two bottles of water, and set them on the tailgate. He did not call her over. He started eating one leaning against it.

She dropped the driver in the grass and walked over.

She said, "What is it."

He said, "Ham."

She said, "Yours or mine."

He said, "Cal made them this morning."

She said, "Cal makes sandwiches."

He said, "Cal makes sandwiches for me on fence days. It's been a ten-year thing."

She ate it. She was hungrier than she had any business being. Cal had put a single leaf of iceberg lettuce on it, as if the lettuce was the part Cal had put thought into.

She said, "Cal has feelings."

Wyatt said, "He has about four of them. The sandwich is one."

She did not say anything for a minute.

She said, looking out at the line of posts, "I'm getting some of them wrong."

He chewed. He swallowed. He said, "Yeah."

She said, "I know."

He said, "Okay."

He did not say anything else, and she did not either. She drank half her water in one go.

A four-wheeler sound came off the ridge to the east. Cal, in the same Hutterville Feed cap, waved at them without slowing and went on west along the two-track. The sound faded out behind the cottonwoods.

She said, "He didn't stop."

Wyatt said, "Nope."

She said, "Why."

Wyatt said, "He doesn't want to see me eat a sandwich with you. He's embarrassed on my behalf."

She laughed, against her will, into the crust.

Ruthie came up from wherever she had been, sat down on Wyatt's boot, and waited. Wyatt tore off a corner of his sandwich and gave it to her. She took it with a professional lack of comment, moved from his boot to June's, and sat on top of her toes and waited. June tore off a corner and gave it to her. The dog ate it, stood up, and walked twelve feet away to lie down in the grass again.

June said, "She charges."

Wyatt said, "She's been charging me for seven years."


They worked until four. She got some posts right and some wrong. The wind picked up around three and went through the Carhartt. She buttoned it back up. She did not ask for any more corrections and he did not offer any, and the weird specific thing between them was that she knew he had stopped watching her posts an hour ago, and she knew he knew she knew, and she still drove the bad ones.

Around half past three she slipped a second time, on the lip of a rabbit hole, and went down on her hip and had to put her hand in the grass to get back up. Something brushed her face as she stood and she swatted it off without looking. She dusted herself off and went back to the post.

By four the line was done, or done enough. He drove the truck up the draw. He said, "We're done today." She said, "Okay." She walked the driver over, dropped it in the bed with both hands, and climbed into the passenger side.

Ruthie jumped up over her lap into the truck bed, because Ruthie had decided at some point in the last two hours that she was a truck-bed dog today.

They drove.


The radio was on. They were on the county road going south at twenty-five miles an hour behind a hay trailer they were not going to pass, and Shania Twain was singing You're Still the One with the mournful confidence of a woman who had been proven right about a man once in her life.

June watched the fence posts go past on her side. She was trying very hard not to sing along.

She knew the song. She had heard it in a dentist's office in 2002 and at her sister's wedding in 2018, and now here, in a truck, in Montana, with a man whose jacket smelled like diesel and dogs. The song had gotten into her the way songs do without asking, and her jaw kept wanting to move, and she kept not letting it.

He was not looking at her. He was, she was fairly sure, also trying very hard not to notice she was trying very hard not to sing along.

She looked at her own hands in her lap. They were black with dirt up past the wrist. There was a grass stain on the left sleeve of the Carhartt she had been pretending was not there since lunchtime. She could feel a line of dried mud on her forehead, and she had decided to ignore it on the theory that if she did not acknowledge it, it was not happening.

The song ended. The next song came on, also Shania Twain. It was Honey, I'm Home.

She said, "Is this real."

He said, "What."

She said, "This station."

He said, "Yeah. Guy named Dean owns it. He was married to a Shania impersonator in the nineties. Ended badly. The station's his response."

She looked at him.

He did not look back. He was watching the hay trailer.

She said, "That cannot be true."

He said, "It's what Cal told me."

She said, "Cal tells you things that are not true."

He said, "Cal tells me things that are better than true."

She looked out the window. The hay trailer turned off onto a side road. They drove the rest of the way in Shania-shaped silence.


At the main house yard he pulled up to her porch. She did not get out. Her hands were too dirty to open the cabinet the soap was in. She said, "Can I use your sink." He said, "Yeah." He drove on across the south pasture to his house.


His kitchen at just past five held more heat than outside by a long way, because he had left the wood stove low that morning and the cast iron was holding it. She went in ahead of him because he held the door, and because his boots were worse than hers and he needed to get them off. She walked to the sink with her hands held out in front of her like a surgeon, turned the tap on with her wrist, and started washing.

She heard him come in, take off his boots, and cross the floor in socks. She did not turn around, because the sink was full of black water now and she still had a ring of dirt at the base of her thumbnail she was trying to get out, and she was aware that he was behind her, and she was aware that she was aware.

She heard him walk up behind her. She heard him stop two feet back.

He said, "Hold still."

She held still.

She felt his hand come up, slow, and go into her ponytail.

He was not rushing. He went into the hair near her crown, found something, and drew it out the length of her ponytail and into the air beside her head. She watched it come into her peripheral vision. A piece of grass. Six inches long, pale at one end and darker at the other. He held it up next to her ear for a second and dropped it into the sink.

It landed on top of the black water.

She stared at it.

She knew, then, what he had been doing when he started. She had not stopped him.

She turned around.

He was right there. His face was a foot from hers. His hand was still half-up from where he had dropped the grass.

She kissed him.

She did not think about it first. She put one wet dirty hand on the front of his shirt, went up on the balls of her feet in her socks, and kissed him, and he was there, instantly, his hands coming up to the sides of her jaw, both of them, holding her face as if he had been waiting to do exactly that for three days. Her hands went to his chest, flat, leaving two black prints on the front of his shirt in the shape of her palms. She did not care. He did not stop her.

She pulled back an inch.

He was looking at her. She was looking at him. She started to smile because there was water running behind her and her elbow was two inches from a faucet she was going to knock if she moved wrong, and she did not care about any of that.

He said, "Took you long enough."

She said, "I didn't know it was a race."

He said, "It wasn't. I was hoping."

She laughed, once, short, into the space between them. Then she kissed him again.

This one was longer.

His hands went from her jaw down her back, slow, over the canvas of the Carhartt, and stopped at her waist. He pulled her a half-step in. Her hip met his. He was bigger up close than he had been from across a yard, wider in the shoulder, heavier in the way he stood, and she could feel his belt buckle through her jacket, and his other hand had come up into her hair at the base of her skull and was holding her there, not hard, just there, like she was something he already knew how to hold.

Her elbow hit the faucet.

Neither of them stopped.

The water was still running. His mouth tasted faintly of the coffee he had finished in the truck. His hand at her waist went up under the Carhartt, under her flannel, onto skin at her lower back. She breathed in against his mouth. He made a small sound in the back of his throat that was not a word.

She lost track of how long they stood there.

It was about four minutes. Somewhere in the middle she got a second wet handprint on the back of his shirt when she wrapped her arm around him, so that by the time his phone rang, his shirt was going to have to go in cold water.

His phone rang.

He did not let go of her for the first two rings. On the third he pulled his head back an inch and looked at her the way a man looks at something he is not going to be able to put down easily, and pulled the phone out of his back pocket with the hand that had been in her hair.

He looked at the screen.

He said, "It's Cal."

She said, "Go."

He said, into the phone, "Yeah." He listened. He said, "How long's she been down." He listened. He said, "South pasture? Ten minutes."

He hung up.

He said, "Cow's down."

She said, "Go."

He said, "We're going to continue this."

She said, "Obviously."

He kissed her once more, quick, on the mouth, and then on the forehead where the line of dried mud was. She felt his thumb run along her jaw before it left her face. He stepped back and crossed to the mudroom for his boots.

She said, "Wyatt."

He turned.

She said, "Good luck."

He said, "Thank you."

He was out the door.


She stood at his sink for another full minute after he was gone.

She turned the tap off. She looked down at the sink. The piece of grass was sitting on the lip of the drain, caught. She picked it up with one wet finger and put it on the counter. Then she reached up into her own ponytail, because she had a sudden doubt, and her fingers found a second piece up near the elastic. She pulled it out and dropped it on top of the first one.

Two pieces of grass.

She looked at them for three seconds, then washed the rest of the way, dried her hands on a dish towel with a hand-painted rooster on it, and let herself out the front door.

The walk across the south pasture took her eleven minutes in the slanted five-thirty light. The grass was wet at her boots. She walked with her hands in the pockets of Hal's Carhartt, and she did not stop smiling until the main house porch, and she did not particularly stop there either.

Inside, she took off the boots and walked to the kitchen in socks.


At a little past eight she was making a ham sandwich standing up, because her fridge was the ham-and-cheddar-in-defiance-of-spoilage fridge from yesterday and she was too tired to cook. Her phone was face-down on the counter. It had been face-down since she came in, and she had been pretending it was face-down because her hands had been dirty, and her hands had not been dirty for an hour.

She pressed the bread down on the sandwich.

The phone buzzed.
