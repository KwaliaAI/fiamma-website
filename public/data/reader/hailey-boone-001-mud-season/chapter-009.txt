Day seventeen. Two in the afternoon. Wyatt was in Billings at a cattleman's meeting and would be back at seven, and Cal had the ranch, and the main house was in the part of its day where it remembered it had once been lived in by one man who was dead.

June was in the mudroom looking for laundry soap.

There was a pile of wet clothes in a bucket at her feet — jeans, a sweatshirt, two towels, a sock she did not remember losing. Wyatt's washer had been running with his own load when she had gone over at noon, so she had walked the bucket back across the south pasture to the main house and opened the cabinet above the washer for the soap she was certain Hal had kept there, because Hal had kept everything somewhere.

The cabinet had four shelves. The soap was not on the first three.

On the fourth shelf, behind a stack of old Field & Stream magazines and a box of fabric softener that had expired in 2019, there was a manila envelope that had been opened.

She took it down. She put it on top of the dryer.

There was no soap on the fourth shelf either.

She looked at the envelope because it was there.


It was from First Interstate Bank. The postmark was October. The address on the front was HAL McCLAREN at the P.O. box in Bridger Creek. It had been opened neatly with a knife down the top edge.

She took the paper out.

It was a line-of-credit statement. She read the heading. She read the account number, which was nine digits she did not recognize, and the security heading, which was one line of capitals: SECURED BY REAL PROPERTY, PARADISE COUNTY, MT, MCCLAREN RANCH.

Her eye moved down the page to the two numbers that mattered, because she was a lawyer who had done real estate and her eye knew what to find on a loan statement without being asked.

Credit limit: $340,000.00.

Balance: $211,048.62.

There were tabs on the side of the statement and two small yellow-highlighter marks, one on the interest rate and one on the maturity date. The handwriting of the tabs was not Hal's.

She stood at the dryer and read the numbers a second time.

She did the arithmetic. It took about eight seconds. Eighteen percent of 1.2 million was two hundred and sixteen. The balance was two hundred and eleven. The numbers were close in a way that was not an accident.

She sat down on the mudroom floor with the statement on her knees.


She dialed Mariana. The phone rang three times.

Mariana said, "June."

June said, "Hi."

Mariana said, "Are you all right."

June said, "I'm at the ranch. I found an October statement from First Interstate in a cabinet in the mudroom. Line of credit against the property. Three-forty ceiling. Balance two-eleven."

Mariana was quiet for a beat. It was the beat of a lawyer doing her own eight seconds of arithmetic, and June heard her do it.

Mariana said, "I assumed he told you."

June said, "He didn't."

Mariana said, "Okay." Then she said, "I need to tell you what that line is for, and it's not mine to tell. But I'll tell you what it buys."

June said, "Go."

Mariana said, "Hal drew it in 2018 to buy a quarter-section off his neighbor to the north. Hunter Creek. He had been leasing the ground for cattle for eleven years, and when the owner's widow went to sell, Hal bought it rather than lose it. Without that quarter the ranch's carrying capacity drops by about forty head. About eighteen percent of annual revenue."

June did not write anything down. She was looking at the baseboard opposite her, which had a crack in it someone had meant to caulk.

Mariana said, "The balance is being paid down on schedule. It's not in default. The ranch is not in distress because of it. But if you sell, the loan comes out of your proceeds, and the buyer's price is going to reflect a property carrying that debt. Which means your number is not your number. Your number is about eighteen percent less than what you've been calculating. I'm sorry."

June said, "Okay."

Mariana said, "I should have led with it in my first disclosure. I assumed it was in the family conversation. That was my mistake."

June said, "You're fine."

She said, "Goodbye."

She hung up.


She sat on the mudroom floor for about thirty seconds.

She did not cry. She was not near crying. She was in a room in her head she had not been in for three months and had forgotten the furniture of, which was the cold room, and in the cold room the decisions were clean.

She stood up.

She picked up the bucket of wet clothes. She picked up the statement. She carried the bucket to the kitchen and set it by the back door, because the clothes did not matter today, and she carried the statement across the living room and out onto the front porch and she walked across the south pasture in jeans and a fleece and the boots that fit now.

Ruthie came up off Wyatt's porch when she got to the cottonwoods and fell in at her heel for the last fifty yards. June did not pet her. Ruthie did not ask her to.

She let herself into Wyatt's house at two-forty.


His kitchen had his morning in it — a blue mug at the sink, the WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE mug upside-down on the drying rack, the newspaper folded to the page he had been on. She put the statement in the middle of the table.

She took the mug he had given her all week down from the cupboard. She poured herself coffee out of the carafe on the counter. It was the bad coffee, hours old, already thick.

She sat down across from where he would sit when he came in.

She waited.


At three she was still at the table. At four she got up and rinsed the mug and poured a new one. At four-forty she walked out to the porch and stood there for a minute looking at the ridge, and Ruthie sat on the boards beside her, and then she went back in. At five she thought about food and did not do anything about it. At five-thirty she watched the clock for a while because watching the clock was an honest thing to be doing.

She read the statement through a second time at six, line by line, the way she would have read it for opposing counsel. The interest rate was prime plus one and a quarter. The maturity was 2028. There was a footer in small type she had not noticed the first time, which said that the borrower had elected to pay down principal ahead of schedule in three of the last four quarters. That was Hal. That was also probably not Hal. She did not examine which of those it was. She put the paper back on the table exactly where it had been.

At six-oh-five she heard the truck come up the ranch road.

At six-forty she heard it pull past the main house and keep going along the south pasture track, and at six-forty-two she heard it stop at his house. The door of the truck, then his boots on the gravel, then the boards of his own porch taking his weight. Ruthie, from the mat, thumped her tail once against the wood.

The door opened.

He came into the kitchen with his jacket half off one shoulder.

He saw her. He saw the statement.

He said, "Shit."

She said, "Sit down."

He sat down.


He had been on I-90 for six hours. There was road dust on the back of his jacket and a gray ring at his hairline from a hat he had taken off in the truck and put back on, and his eyes were the eyes of a man who had sat through three panels on land-use policy and eaten a hotel salad for lunch. His weight went into the chair the way a sack of feed goes onto a shelf. His hands came down flat on the table on either side of the statement. He did not pick it up. He did not need to.

She said, "Tell me about this."

He looked at her and did not look at the paper, and he was not going to manage her. She could see him decide, in the two seconds before he answered, not to manage her, and she did not know whether the decision helped him or hurt him with her, and she was not going to know yet.

He said, "I didn't think you wanted to know on a timeline you couldn't change. You'd have read it in Mariana's disclosure next week."

She said, "Bullshit."

He said, "Yeah, that was bullshit. The truer answer is I was hoping you'd stop wanting to sell before you found out, so we wouldn't have to have this fight."

She said, "So instead we're having it tonight."

He said, "Yeah."


She pushed the mug away from her hand so she would not pick it up. The kitchen clock said six-forty-four. The statement was between them on the pine. His breathing was slow the way it was always slow. Hers was in her throat.

She said, "How long."

He said, "I've known the number since 2018. I've known you were going to find out for two weeks."

She said, "Two weeks."

He said, "Since Mariana told me what she was going to include in her disclosure pack."

She said, "She told you what was going in to me."

He said, "She told me because she thought I had told you. She told me once she realised I hadn't. That was eight days ago."

She said, "Eight days."

He said, "Yeah."

She was counting back. Eight days was Tuesday. Eight days was the feed store, the diner, the kitchen floor in waders, the cab of the truck at six. She set that down because she was not going to carry it into this conversation.

She said, "You were going to let me find out from the paper."

He said, "I was going to let you find out from Mariana. Who is a lawyer. Who would have given you the number and the context at the same time."

She said, "That is not the same thing as you telling me."

He said, "I know it isn't."


She stood up.

The stand was small. Her chair scraped an inch back and she put her palms on the table and leaned on them, and she was not yelling. Her voice had gone quieter than it was in the mornings. She could hear it go quieter and she let it.

She said, "I have been eating at this table. I have been sleeping in your bed. I have been helping you move a cow you were right about that I was wrong about, and I did not apologise for being wrong, and I am not going to apologise tonight for anything either, but I was wrong about the cow and you have been wrong about this since Tuesday."

He said, "Yeah."

She said, "Don't yeah me."

He said, "Okay."


She looked at him. He looked at her. His face did not do the thing she had half expected, which was soften. He did not try to make it easier. He sat in the chair across from her with his hands flat on the table and he took what she was saying and he did not hand it back.

She said, "You did not tell me because you were hoping I would choose to stay before I had the numbers."

He said, "Yes."

She said, "That means the staying wouldn't have been a free choice."

He said, "Yeah. That's the part I was going to have to live with."

She sat back down.

She did it slowly, because she was not done, and sitting was a way to say she was not done.

She said, "I want to know why you thought that was okay."

He said, "I didn't think it was okay. I thought it was the version of wrong I could stand better than the version where I handed you the statement at dinner on day nine and you were on a plane the next morning. I was picking between two bad things."

She said, "That is also bullshit, Wyatt."

He said, "Yeah."

She said, "You picked the one that was better for you."

He said, "I picked the one that kept you here longer, and I knew that the night I picked it. Call it better for me if you want. I'd call it a coward's version of the same thing."

She said, "That is a very tidy sentence, Wyatt."

He said, "I've had eight days to work on it."

She said, "I can tell."


The room was quiet for a beat.

Outside, the light across the south pasture was going, and the window over the sink had that band of orange on it that came once a day for about eleven minutes, and Ruthie was on the mat by the door with her chin on a paw.

She said, "I'm going back to Seattle tomorrow."

He said, "I know."

She said, "Don't come to the main house tonight."

He said, "I wasn't going to."


She stood up again.

She picked up the statement.

She folded it along the crease it already had. She put it in the inside pocket of her fleece and zipped the pocket. She pushed the chair in neatly because she was not going to leave his kitchen looking like somebody had stormed out of it, because that was not what had happened.

She walked to the door.

At the door she put her hand on the frame and stopped for about a second, and she did not look back, and he did not say anything into the second, and when the second was done she opened the door and walked out onto his porch and pulled the door shut behind her.


The sky over the ridge had gone to the color it went in the minute before it stopped being day. Her hand was steady on the porch rail. She did not touch her chest.

She went down the steps.

She heard the door of his kitchen open behind her. It did not close. She did not turn.

Ruthie came down the steps after her and fell in at her heel across the grass.


She walked the quarter-mile in the last of the light.

Her boots were wet at the toe by the time she got to the cottonwoods, and the grass had gone silver in the flat light, and the main house was ahead of her with no lamp on yet inside it because she had left at two and the light had been fine at two. Her breath was even. Her hand on the paper inside her jacket was still.

At the porch she stopped.

She stood there for a full minute. She did not count it; she just stood. She was holding the statement through the fleece with one hand on her own chest the way a person holds a thing that has a spine. When she was done standing she walked up the steps and put her other hand on the doorknob and went inside.

Ruthie came up onto the porch step behind her and sat down.

June turned in the door.

Ruthie was sitting on the third step with her ears forward and her eyes on the quarter-mile of grass that ran back to Wyatt's house. She did not look up.

June said, "Ruthie."

Ruthie's ears moved a quarter-inch toward the sound and went back to where they had been.

June stood in the door another second. Then she shut it.


In the kitchen she laid the statement on the table.

She did not look at it again. She went upstairs.

She packed.

She did it the way she packed for depositions. Suitcase on the bed, open, and the items went in in the order she had put them in the closet when she arrived, which was her own order and not the order the closet had wanted. The pantsuit rolled, the blouse she had not worn laid flat on top, the running shoes she had not run in tucked into the shoe pockets. Toiletries in the quart bag she had brought them in. The laptop slid into its sleeve and into the carry-on. The charger came off the nightstand, and when she remembered there was a second one she went downstairs for it.

The grey heels went in last.

The Carhartt was on the hook by the bedroom door. It had been his sister's first and then his and then, for a week, hers, and she left it on the hook. The pair of borrowed work gloves on the dresser stayed too.

In the nightstand drawer was the small notebook Mariana had given her on day one, which she had put there that first night and never opened. She took it out. She held it for a second longer than she had held anything else that evening, and she put it in the suitcase.

She opened the top drawer of the dresser in the main bedroom. The rodeo belt buckle was in the handkerchief where it had been in 1998 and every year since, and she closed the drawer without touching it.

She booked a flight on her phone sitting on the edge of the bed. The 6 a.m. out of Bozeman was $740 and had an aisle. She took it. She booked the Bozeman hotel she had been planning for the night before her original return. She set an alarm for four.

She went downstairs and she filled a glass of water at the sink.


Eleven. Her suitcase was by the door. The porch light was on. She had turned it on out of habit when she had come down at seven-forty with her hand on her own chest, and she had not turned it off since, because the turning-it-off was a small thing she had not had in her yet.

The kitchen was quiet.

Her phone buzzed on the counter.

She looked at it. Deb.

> he told me. he is an idiot. i love him anyway. he will not ask you to stay. you know this.

She read it through. She read it again, with the phone a little closer, and then a third time with her thumb on the edge of the screen.

She did not answer.


She walked to the front window and looked across the south pasture. A quarter-mile across the grass, his kitchen was dark, and the only light anywhere on the quarter-mile was hers.

She flicked the porch light off.
