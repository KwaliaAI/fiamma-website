The grey pantsuit was laid out on the bed because she was flying to Montana to not cry about a grandfather she had never known. The pink silk camisole underneath was because she had not packed with her head. She had packed with some other organ, the one that kept track of how long it had been since a man had put a hand on her ribs, and that organ had opinions about what a woman wears under a suit when she is going to be alone in a hotel in Livingston with a minibar and a Wi-Fi signal.

It had been eleven months. She was counting in a way she was not proud of.

She put the camisole on. She put the suit on over it and told herself this was a packing decision.

At five-forty the alarm went off. She drank a glass of water at the bathroom sink and told the mirror she was going to Montana to close an estate and come home. The mirror did not argue. The pink silk under the grey was not going to be a problem, because there was nobody in Livingston to show it to.

The last man she had slept with had a name she could no longer remember and a laugh she could. She had asked her sister once whether Seattle men were really that bad and her sister had taken a long drink of wine and said, "Jules, if you have to ask." That had been February. This was April. The math was what it was.

She caught the 7:12.

At the gate a woman in 14C showed her husband a photo of a horse, and the husband said, "Honey, it's a horse, I know what a horse looks like," and June thought, I do not know what a horse looks like up close, and also, I have not been properly touched since the Seahawks lost the playoffs, which was a thing she was going to have to stop tracking by sports seasons because it was becoming a genre.

She did not cry on the flight. That was the baseline. The lawyer in Livingston had called three weeks ago and said granddaughter and ranch and estate and June had said who? out loud to nobody, because Hal McClaren had been a tall man with a mean mouth who she had met once at a Denny's in Billings when she was thirteen and had given her a blue-ribbon rodeo belt buckle that lived in a shoebox in her closet between a pair of unopened Jimmy Choos and a backup vibrator. Three weeks. Closed file.

She picked up a rental SUV in Bozeman from a man named Dale who took eleven minutes to process a three-minute transaction and asked her twice if she wanted the extra insurance. She said yes the second time because he clearly needed a win. The SUV smelled like a pine air freshener working overtime on a Marlboro habit. She drove east.

Mariana Ortiz's office sat above a bakery on Main Street in Livingston. The whole stairwell smelled like cardamom. Mariana was in her fifties, had a good haircut, and said, "You must be Juniper," before June had fully come through the door.

"Yes."

"Hal said you would be tall."

"I don't think we ever stood next to each other."

Mariana looked at her for a long moment. "He said a lot of things. Sit."

The deed came in a manila folder. The ledger was thick and legal-size with a rubber band around it that was older than Mariana's second marriage. The keys were on a worn leather loop. There was also a thin black notebook with a broken spine.

Mariana pushed the notebook across the desk last.

"He asked me to give you this. He was not specific."

"About what."

"Any of it."

June took it. She put it in her bag without opening it. She had already decided she was not going to read a dead man's notebook at the airport bar on her way home.

Mariana said, "The foreman has been running the ranch alone since Hal's decline. You'll want to let him do that for a few more days. The books are in his head more than on paper."

"That's not ideal."

"It wasn't ideal. It was necessary."

"What's he like."

Mariana's mouth did the thing a woman's mouth does when she is choosing between professional discretion and personal honesty with another woman.

"He's direct. Hal liked him. My husband liked him, which is a harder test. He'll fill a doorway and underestimate the fact that he's doing it. You'll figure him out."

"Fills a doorway."

"Mm."

"That's a specific detail."

"It is."

Mariana said, "You should also know Hal co-signed the loan on Wyatt's house. Wyatt bought it from him in 2021. It has financial implications you haven't looked at yet."

June nodded. She had looked at cattle-land comps on the flight. That had turned out to be a different project from understanding what was underneath the balance sheet.

At the door she asked, "Did he ever ask if I would come."

Mariana looked at her steadily. "He asked me that twice a year for four years. Then he stopped asking. Then a month before he died he asked again."

"What did you tell him."

"I told him my job was to settle his estate, not to predict his granddaughter."

"That's a lawyer's answer."

"It is. I'll give you the other one if you want it."

"Not today."

"Okay."

June signed three forms, thanked her, and was in her SUV by three-forty. She drove south.

She waved at a man on a tractor who waved back with one finger without looking up, apparently she was already a known vehicle. At a gas station run by one teenager and three dogs the teenager said, "You going out to Hal's place?" She said yes. He said, "Wyatt know you're coming?" She said, "The lawyer told him." The teenager nodded gravely and said, "Tell him Jackson's back from Denver." She said, "I will," before it occurred to her to ask who Jackson was. She did not go back in. She wanted to know who Wyatt was, suddenly, and she did not feel like letting a gas-station teenager watch her find out.

The gate at the end of it all was white painted pipe, the word MCCLAREN worn to bare strokes. She opened it, drove through, closed it, because she had read on her phone the previous night that ranches had rules about gates, and then stood for a second with her hand on the latch telling herself the feeling in her stomach was from not eating the yogurt.

The gravel road ran five miles. She stopped once because a cow was standing in the road with her entire career ahead of her. The cow walked off to the left with a lot of feeling about being asked to move.

At five-thirty-eight she came around the last stand of cottonwoods and saw the main house, which had a tin roof and a wood porch with a blue heeler on it that did not lift her head when the SUV pulled in. A man was sitting on the porch steps with his hand on the dog's back.

She saw the hand first. Big, loose, fingers curled easy into the fur at the dog's shoulder, just there and connected. June had dated three Seattle lawyers, two consultants, and a urologist, and none of them had ever touched a dog like the dog was his personal business. She filed the hand somewhere she was not going to label.

Then he stood up.

He stood like standing up cost him nothing. He was taller than she had prepared for, in a way that read as weight rather than height, the kind of tall that makes a doorway narrower without actually touching anything. Mariana's fills a doorway. June had the brief, inconvenient thought that if you asked him to leave a room he would take most of the oxygen with him, and she could not tell if that was going to be a problem or the best thing that had happened to her in eleven months. Dark hair kept short, a jacket the colour of the work he had been doing that afternoon, and a scar through the right eyebrow visible from fifteen feet.

He did not wave.

He was watching her watch him, and not minding.

She realised she had been watching him for two full seconds longer than her job description required. She opened the SUV door.

Something brown came in fast on her left.

It was a brown chicken, and with real commitment it walked up to her front-left tire and bit it. Hard. June sat back down with one foot on the running board and watched the chicken bite her rental tire again, harder, then a third time without looking up, the way a person grinds out a cigarette they have feelings about.

She looked at the porch. The man was still watching her. He tipped his chin slightly toward the tire, as if to say yes, that is happening, and no, I am not going to explain it.

June said, to the chicken, "Are you going to let me out of the truck." The chicken, having made her point, walked three steps away and stood with her back to the SUV like a woman who had finished a phone call.

June stepped down.

Her right heel went into mud past the ankle. Black thawed April mud, six inches deep, cold enough that the water pushed in through the leather from both sides before she had finished standing. The sound it made was the sound of something being swallowed. The cold reached the bone in her ankle in roughly the time it took her to register that her grey heels were the wrong shoes for this weather in principle, and she was going to be learning a lot about her own decisions in the next twenty-one days.

She looked up.

He was still looking at her.

He said, "You want a hand?"

His voice was low and slow and the opposite of an offer. It was the voice of a man asking a question he already had the answer to. It went through her collarbone in a specific direction and came out the soles of her feet into the mud.

She said, "No."

She put her weight on the other foot and pulled her right foot up. The shoe stayed. It sat in the mud like it had been born there, and she stood on a dry left heel with her right sock going brown at the bottom. She pulled her left foot up next, slower, and the other shoe stayed too.

Two socks deep in the yard she had just inherited.

The running email to her managing partner that usually composed itself at roughly this time every day was gone. Her brain had stopped dictating. She walked.

The yard was maybe thirty yards across. She walked it in socks with her face set. The mud went up past her ankles in places. She kept her eyes on the porch because the porch was a fixed point, and she kept her spine straight because in certain rooms that was the whole job. Halfway across she noticed the dog had sat up and was watching her with the mild patient face of an animal that had seen many things happen in this yard and was reserving judgment on the current one.

The man had not moved. He stood with his weight on his back leg, one thumb hooked into his belt, the other hand still down where the dog's shoulder had been. He was a man who was not going to step forward to rescue her from a situation she had chosen, and June respected that and hated it in roughly equal measure.

At the porch she stopped, three steps below him, the dog between them and not getting involved.

She said, "I'm Juniper McClaren. You must be Wyatt."

He said, "I was."

She said, "You was what?"

He said, "Wyatt. You dried up the 'must be' halfway through the yard."

She laughed.

She had not planned to. It startled her, and by the look on his face it startled him too. He did not smile. He did not stop looking at her. He stood at the top of the steps for about two seconds, during which June became extremely aware that she had walked thirty yards through cold mud in a pantsuit to stand under a man with a scar through one eyebrow and a mouth that was doing almost nothing. The dog came off the porch and sat on her foot.

He said, "That's Ruthie. She's unreliable."

Ruthie closed her eyes on June's sock with the committed serenity of a dog who had made a real estate decision.

June said, "Hi, Ruthie."

She looked up. Wyatt was still watching her, and up close the scar through his eyebrow was a thin clean line that had healed white, and his eyes were grey-blue. June felt the pink silk camisole under her suit jacket as a physical fact, which was not how she had planned to feel it.

Wyatt said, "I'm not coming in. Cal's by at six tomorrow."

"Who's Cal."

"Senior hand. He'll show you around. You'll want to ask him the questions you were planning to ask me."

"I wasn't planning to ask you questions."

"You were going to. I'm saving you."

She decided not to respond to that. She had been here nine minutes and she had already lost control of the conversation twice and sent a chicken-related facial expression to a man in a dirt-coloured jacket. She said, "What about the house."

He said, "Front door works. Back door sticks. Don't use the back door. Hot water works. Let it run a minute. The heater's fine. Propane tank's out back. The pilot on the water heater goes out if you close the valve. Don't close the valve."

"Okay."

"Coffee maker's broken."

"I drink coffee."

"Yeah. The one on the counter's broken. French press is in the third drawer. Instant is in the WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE jar above the sink. Don't judge the jar."

"The jar."

"My sister. It was a gift."

She stopped herself from saying anything further about the jar. She said, "Thank you."

He said, "It's good you came."

He did not elaborate. He said, "Ruthie, c'mon," to the dog, who did not move. He met June's eyes for the third time, and his voice went down a half-step.

"She stays where she wants. She's been like that since Hal."

"Since Hal."

"Since Hal died. She's staying with you tonight, apparently."

June looked at the dog because she needed to look at something that was not him. The dog was looking at a point on the horizon where nothing was happening.

Wyatt said, "I'll walk across at six tomorrow if you have questions Cal can't answer."

"Across what."

"The pasture. My house is on the other side. You'll see the light from the kitchen window."

He walked off her porch, around her SUV, and across the yard toward the cottonwoods without looking back. She watched him the whole way. He moved the way a man moves when he has not been in a hurry in several years and it is working out for him. Somewhere between the SUV and the pasture her stomach did a thing that was not about the yogurt.

She went inside.

The kitchen smelled like old pipe tobacco and wood-cabinet oil, the kind of smell that had been living in the room longer than any human memory of the room. The smell was someone else's. She stood in it for a minute and then opened the cabinet above the sink.

There was a ceramic jar in there that said WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE in hand-painted letters across one side. Under the letters, in smaller hand-painted letters, someone had written (that's what she said).

June looked at the parenthetical for a long moment. Then she said, out loud, to the jar, "Okay. Sure."

She opened it. Instant coffee packets. A man who stands like that drinks instant coffee out of a cookie jar his sister gave him as a joke about his sexual availability. She put the lid back on very carefully.

Ruthie settled onto a rag rug by the mudroom door like she had settled onto that rug a thousand times already.

She walked through the rest of the house. Living room: wood stove, couch, armchair facing the picture window, rodeo belt buckle on the mantel (third place 1979) next to a framed photo of a horse. No photos of people. First bedroom: bed made up military-tight. Second: boxes of paperwork she was not going to open tonight. She closed the doors.

She remembered her shoes. The mudroom held a bench, a bootjack, three pairs of rubber boots in three different sizes, and a set of kitchen tongs hanging on a nail above the bench. She did not ask herself why there were kitchen tongs in a mudroom. She took them off the nail, went outside in socks, retrieved one heel with a small wet sound, set it at the edge of the gravel, and got the other one the same way. They had been six-hundred-dollar shoes four hours ago. They were now archaeological.

A quarter-mile off, through a stand of cottonwoods, a single kitchen window was lit. A shadow moved in it once and did not come back. She watched the window for longer than she wanted to.

She went back inside.

At ten-forty she was in bed in Hal's guest room in a cotton t-shirt. The pink silk camisole was folded on the chair because this was not, apparently, going to be a camisole night. She scrolled the photos she had taken between the airport and the kitchen. Mostly mud. One was blurred and showed a chicken hitting a tire with its face. She zoomed in. She could not tell whether the chicken had a facial expression.

Through the upper window that had been left ajar, she could hear a radio across the south pasture.

Any man of mine, Shania Twain informed her, better walk the line.

June laughed into the pillow. She was laughing because a man she had known for nineteen minutes had a scar through his eyebrow and a hand that knew where a dog's shoulder was, and she was in a dead man's guest room with mud on her socks and a chicken as a personal enemy, and it had been a long time since the day's events had been this specific.

She rolled over.

There was a scar on her left palm from an oyster-shucking incident in law school. It had caught on something when she pulled her second shoe up out of the mud, and it was a little sore. She turned her palm up on top of the blanket.

She thought, very briefly, about the shape of his hand on the dog.

She put her palm back down. She slept.
