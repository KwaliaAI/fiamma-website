Nine in the morning, the main house kitchen, day twenty-one. The tea in the WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE mug was her second and still too hot to drink. The mug had lived at the main house since last week, when Wyatt had carried it over one night with coffee already in it and left without it, and she had kept it because it was funny and also because she wanted it.

She was at the kitchen table in his blue t-shirt and her own jeans, with the laptop open and an email draft on the screen. The email had been on the screen since last night at ten. She had written it, read it, closed the laptop, opened the laptop, read it again, and gone to bed without sending it. She had slept at the main house on purpose, to give the email a night to turn out to be a bad idea.

It had not turned out to be a bad idea.

The email was to the Montana State Bar Admissions Office. The subject line said Motion for Admission by Reciprocity, Juniper A. McClaren, WSBA #50412. The attachments were the motion itself, her Washington State Bar card scanned front and back, her University of Washington School of Law transcripts with the registrar's seal on the first page, the receipt for the $500 fee she had paid last night from the card she used for groceries, and a short cover letter she had drafted and redrafted four times and finally left at three sentences. She read the email a third time. The cursor blinked at the end of the signature line.

She hit send at 9:14.

The sent-confirmation line slid up the screen. She watched it go. She closed the laptop. She got up and refilled the mug from the kettle and stood at the kitchen window looking across the south pasture at his house, which from this angle sat lower than it actually did because of the lay of the ground. She drank half the tea standing up. A magpie landed on the fence and did what magpies do. She watched it.

Her shoulders had come down. They had been up somewhere near her ears since she had gotten on the plane in Seattle on day one, and she had not known they were up there until they came down. She rolled them once. She rolled them again. She set the mug on the counter and put both hands flat on the cold tile and stood that way for a minute, looking at her knuckles. Her knuckles were the knuckles of a person who had handled a fence-post driver, a saddle, a ranch ledger, a rope, and, yesterday, the kitchen sink trap, which had come off in her hand. There was a fresh scrape across the second knuckle of her right hand from the trap. She had not bandaged it. She had not been bandaging small things this week. She had been doing the thing and letting the scrape be a scrape.

She picked the mug back up.

The day outside was the kind of day mud season turns into when it remembers how. The ground was still soft, and the cottonwoods by the creek had green on them now, a green that had not been there four days ago. The sky was washed the way Montana skies got washed in late April, and the light off the east flank of the Absarokas was the long kind that made everything look like the first hour of something.

She was not nervous.

She had been nervous every morning for two and a half weeks. She had expected the nervous this morning and she had stood at this window ready for it and it had not arrived. She stood another minute to make sure. It did not arrive.

She went upstairs and brushed her teeth. She put on a bra. She put her hair up and took it down and put it up again. She came down and put her fleece on over the blue t-shirt because the thermometer said forty-one and the wind was coming off the river.

At ten fifty she laced the boots Cooper had sold her and went out the back door.

She took the long way around the barn because she wanted to see the horses. The pens were in the sun. Cinder was in the far pen with two other mares, her head up, ears forward. Cinder watched her walk by. Cinder watched her the way a cat watches a neighbour's dog on a porch. June did not stop. She nodded once at Cinder, which she was aware was an idiotic thing to do at a horse, and kept walking. Cinder snorted, which June chose to believe was engagement. The two mares with her did not look up. They were working on hay. They had nothing to prove to the new person.

The south pasture was wet to the ankle. The boots held. She walked the line she had walked thirty times now, past the cottonwoods, across the small rise where you could see both houses at once, down the slope to the fence he had mended in March, through the gate, up to his porch.

On the rise she stopped for ten seconds. She looked back. The main house sat square against the foothills behind it, the tin roof catching the sun at the angle it caught it at between eleven and noon. Hal's chair was on the porch, and the other chair beside it. Two chairs on a porch, both empty, both at the same angle to the yard. She turned around and kept walking.

His kitchen door was open. It was always open in the morning if it was not raining.

She walked in.


He was at the table. He had a mug of coffee beside him and the Sunday Billings Gazette folded flat in front of him, because the mail had come Tuesday and the Gazette came late out this far. He was reading something in the sports section with the kind of unhurried attention he gave to boring things. He had shaved. His hair was a little wet at the back from the sink.

He looked up when she came in.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey," she said.

She sat down across from him. She did not take the fleece off. He watched her sit, and set the paper aside, and waited.

"I'm not selling," she said. "I'm not staying only either. I'm going to be here half the time."

He waited. He did not ask what that meant. He had done this with her for three weeks and he knew she had more.

"I applied for a Montana bar license this morning," she said.

"When," he said.

"Sixty-two minutes ago."

He laughed.

It was the real one — not the small Wyatt-laugh that lived in his chest and sometimes got as far as his shoulders, but the real one, the one she had pulled out of him once before, in the front yard of this ranch on the day she arrived, when she had stood in the mud in one sock and one shoe and said you must be Wyatt and he had corrected her. That laugh had startled both of them then. This one did not startle him. He leaned back in the chair with his whole body in it, and the laugh came up out of his chest and into his head and rocked his shoulders, and he put his hand flat on the table the way a man does when a thing is too good to sit through upright.

She watched him laugh. She had made a man laugh twice in three weeks, and she was going to count it against the running tally she kept of her life in Seattle.

He caught his breath. He looked at her across the table.

He said, "Juniper McClaren, you are the most annoying person I have ever been in love with."

She set her hands flat on the table, exactly where his were.

"Say the last part again," she said.

"Which part," he said.

"The part where you're in love with me."

"I'm in love with you."

"Okay."

"Okay?"

"Okay, good. Because if we're doing this I'm going to need you to build me a mudroom —"

"— Done —"

"— and replace the coffee machine in the main house —"

"— yeah —"

"— and tell Cinder I am not the enemy —"

"— Cinder has her own opinions, I cannot —"

She kissed him.

She was half across the table to do it, and his hand came up to her jaw in the way it always did, and she felt the small rough spot at the base of his thumb that she had been learning since Chapter three of her own life out here, and he kissed her back with his mouth closed first and then not.

They pulled apart. She sat back down. She was grinning. He was, too, with the smaller of the two laughs, which was a smile that did not quite leave his eyes.

"Also," she said, "I want to go to the feed store."

"Now," he said.

"Now."

They went.


The feed store took forty minutes because the feed store always took forty minutes. She bought chicken feed and a garden trowel and, at the last second, a bag of peppermints for Cinder, because if Cinder was going to have opinions then June was going to have a bribery campaign. The cashier, who had known who she was the first day and had been unsurprised about it ever since, said how's the place and she said good and he said yep and that was the whole transaction. In the truck on the way back Wyatt said, "Peppermints." She said, "Yeah." He said, "On me." She said, "You hate that horse." He said, "I do not hate that horse. She is not my horse. I have professional respect for her opinions." She looked at him and did not say anything for half a mile, because the thing she would have said was that he had just told her in more words than he usually used that he was going to be kind to the horse she had decided to keep. He watched the road. She watched him watching the road. Shania was on the radio, singing about whose bed her boots had been under, and neither of them touched the dial.

Back at the ranch they re-hung the gate on the east pasture that had been pulling itself off its hinges for a week. He held the gate. She held the bolts. She handed them to him one at a time and the one she handed him second was the wrong one, because she had pulled two sizes out of the bin by mistake, and he said, without looking up, "Bigger," and she said, "How do you know that without looking," and he said, "Eleven years," and she swapped it, and the gate was rehung by four, and they were back at the main house by four-fifteen because Ruthie had followed them across the pasture and Ruthie wanted in. The gate, when he set it, swung once and held, and he put his weight on the corner of it and pushed down and it did not drop, and he said, "Yeah," to the gate, which was how he talked to a job that was done. June watched him say it.

They made dinner at the main house. She did the onions. He cut the chicken. She put the radio on and it was the one station and the one station was doing a Shania block, and she did not say anything about it, and he did not say anything about it, and they cooked the dinner to two and a half Shania songs in a row. At the fourth song she sang along to the chorus, once, quietly, in a voice that had been pretending for three weeks that it did not know the words. He kept his eyes on the chicken. His ears were, she was sure, the reddest she had seen them.

They ate at the table in the kitchen she had inherited. Ruthie lay down under the table between their feet. The table was Hal's table. There was a burn mark at the near corner from a cigarette that had been left on the edge in 1999 and the wood had taken the shape of the burn and held it. June had run her thumb over it at every meal for three weeks. Wyatt, across the table, ate left-handed, because he was left-handed, and she had not noticed that about him until day six. At five forty-five he put his plate in the sink and she put hers in the sink and he said, "Porch?" and she said, "Porch."


Six o'clock, the porch of the main house.

Wyatt took Hal's chair without thinking about it, realised what he had done, and made to move. June waved him back.

"Stay," she said.

She took the other one, the one that had been hers for three weeks and was also the one he had used every time he had knocked at this porch in three weeks and been invited to sit, which was nearly every time. She turned it a quarter-turn toward him. The porch boards were dry. The sun was on the far side of the house now. The light on the south pasture was getting long.

Ruthie came out and lay down on the boards between them with her chin on her paws.

He sat in Hal's chair. He put his coffee on the porch rail. He watched the yard.

Hal's chair sat him a little further back than his own did. He could feel the difference in his lower back. The chair had been out here for, say, twelve years, which was how long he had known Hal, and the seat had given to the shape of Hal's weight, and now, awkwardly and politely, to his. He kept his feet flat on the boards.

In the yard, the rental truck was at the near side of the drive. June had never moved it back toward the gate. It had been mud to the door handles since day one and it had acquired fresh mud twice more since, and it was by any reasonable standard a truck that had stopped being a rental and become a fixture. The driver-side tire was the one that faced the porch.

Garbage the chicken came around the side of the house.

She walked across the drive with the particular self-important walk a chicken walks when she has a job. She stopped at the back tire. She looked at it. She pecked it, which did nothing. She walked two steps back. She walked forward. She bit the tire. Hard.

Wyatt watched it happen.

"She's doing it again," he said.

"I know," June said.

She put her hand on his on the armrest between them.

He turned his palm up and her fingers slid into his. He closed his hand.

They sat.

The sun was behind the ridge now, and the light on the pasture was the going-kind, not the coming-kind, and the temperature had dropped three degrees since they sat down. The horses were at the far fence, black against the light. A semi was downshifting somewhere out past the county road, the sound coming and going with the wind, and the pines behind the main house made their dry end-of-day sound. Cal's truck pulled into the ranch road a quarter-mile away, slowed, saw them on the porch, did not turn in. The truck kept going toward Cal's place, and Wyatt watched the taillights go, and knew Cal had seen them and had kept going on purpose, and was grateful, and did not say so. He would tell Cal tomorrow, in the barn, by handing him a coffee he had not asked him for. That was how he and Cal did it.

Garbage bit the tire again.

The chicken, Wyatt thought, had a program and the program was the tire, and the tire was not going to be defeated by the chicken, and the chicken was going to keep working on it for as long as the sun was up. He admired the chicken.

He said, "Juniper."

She said, "Yes."

"I was going to say something."

"What."

"I forgot."

She laughed. Her head went back against the chair and she laughed up at the porch ceiling, which Hal had painted blue in 1988 to keep wasps off and which had held, and when she brought her head back down she did not take her hand off his.

He had, he realised, actually forgotten.

It had been a real thought, about an hour earlier, when they were re-hanging the gate and he had thought, looking at her handing him bolts, of something small he wanted to tell her, and he had saved it for the porch, and now, at the porch, he had lost it. He was at peace about losing it. The thing was probably that her hair looked good in that light. The thing was probably that he liked her better than he had liked anyone in the part of his life he had been living before she got out of the rental truck. Whatever the thing was, he had said the larger thing this morning in his kitchen at eleven, and she had asked him to say it again, and he had, and the larger thing had made the smaller thing he had meant to say on the porch unnecessary.

He let it go.

They sat another hour without saying much. She told him, once, that her mother had called yesterday and June had told her about the summer, and her mother had been quiet for a long time and then said okay. He said that's fair. She said yeah.

The light changed twice. First the high long yellow off the Absarokas thinned to a paler yellow that took the edges off the pasture, and then, around seven-thirty, the pasture itself turned the slate-blue it turned in the half-hour before full dark. A nighthawk came out over the creek and did its cut-and-dive. Wyatt watched it. June watched him watch it. She did not ask what it was because she had decided ten days ago to stop asking him what every bird was; he would tell her when she did not ask, and he did, a minute later, quietly.

"Nighthawk," he said.

"I thought so," she said, which was a lie and which he knew was a lie and which he let stand.

At eight Ruthie got up, stretched, and came over to put her head on his knee. He put his hand on her head without looking. Ruthie went back down by June's boots.

At eight-fifteen she said, "Wyatt."

He said, "Yes."

She said, "Half the time means I'll go back to Seattle in October. Full teaching year at a clinic I'll pitch them on taking. Three days a month back. I'll fly Friday, fly Monday. I already ran the numbers."

He said, "Okay."

She said, "I wanted you to hear the shape of it once, sitting down, with the light off."

He said, "I hear it."

She said, "Okay."

They did not talk about it again.

At nine the temperature dropped again and June got a blanket from inside. She came back out and put half of it over her and half of it over him. She sat back down. She put her hand back on his.

At ten he said, "Cold."

She said, "Yeah."

They stood up together. The blanket came with her. He picked up both mugs. Ruthie stood up. Garbage was still at the tire. She was a morning-and-evening chicken, and she would be there until full dark, and then she would roost, and then she would start again tomorrow.

They walked across the south pasture to his house in the last of the light. Neither of them had turned off the porch light at the main house when they came out, and neither of them had turned off the kitchen light at his house that morning when he had walked over. Both houses were lit. The porch between them sat dark. Ruthie ran ahead.

He opened his kitchen door and held it for her.

The screen door closed behind them.
