She woke in his bed at six-forty and did not immediately move.

The room was grey. The window faced east and the curtains were the kind of curtains a man had hung himself, off-centre by half an inch in a way that would bother nobody except the person who had hung them. Wyatt was on his side of the bed with his back to her and the duvet pulled up around his shoulder in a shape that was his. She was wearing his shirt. It was the chambray one with the tear at the elbow that she had pulled off him on night two, and she had put it on last night after getting out of bed to pee and never gotten around to putting anything else on over it. She could feel him in her hip. She could feel him in her thighs. She could still feel last night, if she was being accurate about it, in a couple of places she was not going to list even to herself.

She got up without waking him. She found her socks under the chair. In the kitchen the WORLD'S OKAYEST UNCLE mug was already out on the counter, empty, because she had made herself tea at eleven-forty-five when she had come back in from the porch.

She put water in the kettle. She stood at the window in his shirt and watched the cottonwoods. A magpie was on the fence post at the edge of the yard, making its sideways inspection of nothing in particular.

She was on her second cup when he came into the kitchen in jeans and nothing above the waist and kissed her on the side of the neck without saying anything and went to the mudroom for his shirt. She did not turn around. She held the mug in both hands and looked at the magpie, which had not moved.

By seven-fifteen he was up and dressed and had the truck running in the yard, and she had pulled jeans on over his shirt and tucked it in and put her own boots on the right feet, and they were pulling out of the gate before the sun was fully clear of the ridge.


The feed store sat at the corner of Main and Fourth in Bridger Creek. It had a gravel lot, a hitching post that was decorative now and had been since 1987, and a sign that said COOPER'S FEED & SUPPLY in paint that had been laid on with a brush. Wyatt parked nose-in and got out.

June got out on her side and shut the door. A man in a green cap across the lot looked at her and then looked at Wyatt and then looked at his own phone.

Inside the store smelled like grain and rubber and the kind of dust that had been there for forty years. Wyatt went to the back for fence wire. June walked the front aisle the way a tourist walks a chapel.

The cashier was a woman in her sixties with grey hair cut short at the nape and a sweatshirt that said MONTANA GRIZZLIES on it. She looked up when June stopped at the counter with a tin of lip balm in her hand.

She said, "Hal's granddaughter."

June said, "Yeah."

The woman rang up the lip balm. Three forty-nine. She said, "He told me about you once."

June did not ask. She felt the question form at the top of her throat and she pushed it back down with the heel of her tongue and did not ask. She paid in cash because Wyatt was walking up behind her with the wire on his shoulder and she wanted to be already moving.

The cashier put the lip balm in a brown paper bag and slid it across the counter. She said, "The air here will kill your face if you let it."

June said, "I noticed."

She was out the door before Wyatt had paid for the wire. She stood in the gravel lot with the paper bag in her hand and did not examine why she had not asked. She watched the green-cap man get into his truck. She did not think about Hal and the cashier. She thought about the lip balm, and her chapped lower lip, and the dry gust coming off the road.

Wyatt came out with the wire and put it in the truck bed and tied it down with a piece of rope that had clearly been tied to many things before, and he did not say anything about how fast she had moved through the store. He did look at the paper bag in her hand for about a second, and then at her mouth, and then at the road. She pulled the lid off the tin in the truck and ran the balm across her lower lip with her thumb and did not offer him any, because she was not sure he would take it and because she did not want to find out.


The diner was on Main across from the one bank. TEAGAN'S was painted on the window in white block letters that had been painted over TEAGAN'S every three years since 1998. There were three booths along the window and five stools at the counter and a pie case with one coconut cream pie in it and a cooler of Coke bottles that had been in service since the Clinton administration.

When they came in, every head turned. A man at the counter turned on his stool all the way around. Two women in a back booth stopped talking. A boy of about ten looked up from a plate of hash browns and then looked at his mother, who did not stop looking at June.

Wyatt filled the doorway for a second before he stepped inside and let her in behind him. He did not notice he was doing it. June noticed. The whole diner noticed.

Teagan was behind the counter. She was in her sixties with a small mean face she wore for recreation and lipstick the colour of a stop sign and short grey hair that had been cut by the same person for three decades. She came around the end of the counter with two menus and a coffee pot in the same hand.

She sat them in the window booth. She said, without looking at June, "Nice to see you in daylight."

Wyatt did not answer.

June said, "He does exist in daylight. It turns out."

Teagan laughed, once, a short bark. She poured coffee into June's mug and into Wyatt's and then put three pats of butter on the saucer at June's elbow without asking. She said, "Eggs how."

June said, "Over easy."

Wyatt said, "Same."

Teagan said, "Hash browns. Bacon or sausage."

Wyatt said, "Bacon."

June looked at Teagan. She said, "Can I get a beer."

Teagan's mouth did a very small thing at the corner. She said, "At eleven in the morning you can get whatever you want." She looked at Wyatt. Wyatt did not look up from his coffee. Teagan wrote on her pad and walked away.

June said, "My head."

Wyatt said, "I know."

He said it without moving his face.

The beer came in a bottle with the cap already off. She drank a third of it before the eggs arrived. The eggs came. Teagan set the plate down and did not ask anything. The bacon was good bacon. The hash browns were the same hash browns Teagan had been making since 1998, which meant they were overcooked on one side and undercooked on the other, and nobody mentioned it because mentioning it was not what a person did here.

Halfway through the eggs she said, "Another, please."

Teagan, from behind the counter, said, "Mm." And brought it.

Wyatt still did not comment.

That was the thing that made her want to double down. He was not pretending he had not noticed, and he was not making a face at it, and he was not rolling his eyes in a visible way. He was drinking his coffee and eating his eggs and leaving her to her own choices the way a man leaves a horse he does not own in a field he does not own, which was a thing she had heard Cal say once about somebody else's bull. She drank the second beer. She paid for both beers with her own card before Wyatt could reach for the check. Wyatt paid the rest of the check in cash.

They stood up. A woman at the counter said, "Wyatt." Wyatt said, "Mary." Nobody introduced June.

They went out the door and down three concrete steps to the sidewalk.

She stepped wrong on the last step. Her foot came down slower than her weight, her knee did not lock, and her hip started going one way before the rest of her had agreed.

Wyatt's hand was on her elbow before the half-beat finished. It was brief. It was his whole hand, under her jacket sleeve, steady. He let go when her foot was on the sidewalk. He did not look at her. He did not slow down either.

They got in the truck.


She did not say anything for the first mile. He drove at forty-five with one hand on the wheel. Shania was on the radio — Any Man of Mine, which she had heard three times this week already. She watched the fence-line go past and thought about Teagan not asking her again, and about the second beer at eleven-twenty-seven, which had been fine, and about how her foot had landed, which had not been.

She said, "I don't usually drink at lunch."

He said, "I know."

She looked at him. He did not look back. He was watching the road.

She sat with that for twenty miles.

It was not a long twenty miles by Montana standards — thirty-five minutes at the speed he was driving — but it was the longest twenty miles she had had in the truck with him since she had arrived. He did not turn the radio up to cover it and he did not turn it down. He let it sit, and Shania sang about a boy who had been hers and was no longer, and the fence-line went past on the right. She thought about her own face and what it was doing and then about the heat coming out of the dash vent and then about a cashier in a sweatshirt who had said he told me about you once and she had not asked, and the not-asking was, she was beginning to suspect, the same muscle that had just ordered a second beer.

She put her forehead against the cold window for a second.

He did not say anything.

At the gate he got out and unhooked it and drove through and got out and hooked it again, and she watched him do it because watching him was easier than watching herself. He had a routine with the chain, which went through the loop twice, and he pulled once to check, and he got back in without looking at her.


At the ranch he parked by the calving barn. He said, "Waders in the mudroom. I've got to move a cow through the draw. She's been there since yesterday."

She said, "I'll come."

He looked at her.

She said, "I have waders at home."

He said, "You don't."

She said, "I might."

He said, "No." He was already walking.

In his mudroom there were three pairs of waders hanging on hooks and a fourth pair folded on the floor because a hook had broken. He took the green ones for himself and pointed at a brown pair that were going to be too big for her. She took them off the hook and carried them into his kitchen because the mudroom was cold and the kitchen was not.

She sat down on the floor with her back against the cabinet and started putting them on.

It took her two attempts to get them oriented. The second time, she got the shoulder straps into the right arrangement and pulled the body up and stood.

Both of her feet were in the same leg.

She stood there for about a second with one leg bent at a ridiculous angle and the other trapped beside it, looking down at herself, and then she laughed and fell sideways onto the kitchen floor.

She laughed for long enough that she started coughing and had to roll onto her back. The kitchen ceiling had a water stain in one corner and a lightbulb on a chain that had a little pull-cord with a plastic bead at the end of it, and she looked at both of those things while she caught her breath.

He came in from the mudroom. He stood over her with his own waders already on and his hands on his hips. He looked at her arrangement. He said, "New one."

She said, "For what."

He said, "The list."

She said, "What list."

He said, "The list of things you pretended to know. You're at seven."

She said, "You've been keeping a list."

He said, "Yep."

She said, "For how long."

He said, "Since Tuesday."

She lay on her back on his kitchen floor with both feet in one leg of his second-best pair of waders and thought about throwing the lip balm tin at him. She had it in her jacket pocket. She could reach. She did not.

She did not because she would miss him.

He put his hand down. She took it. He pulled her up in one smooth motion, held her upright while she disentangled her legs, and did not comment on any part of the process.


Before the cow she went over to the main house to grab her laptop charger, because her battery had been at eight percent since the diner. She plugged the laptop in. Nothing happened. She followed the cord, which ended at a chewed plug.

She went to the porch. Ruthie was on the front step with her chin on her paws and the charger cord in her mouth, one end visible between her teeth.

June said, "No."

Ruthie did not release.

June went inside to the fridge, took a strip of bacon she had been thinking about eating herself, and came back out.

She held the bacon out. Ruthie dropped the cord. June handed over the bacon. The dog ate it without ceremony and walked off the porch into the yard.

June picked up the cord. She texted Wyatt: your dog is negotiating with me for my charger.

Her phone buzzed a minute later.

She put the phone in her back pocket.


The cow in the draw took two hours.

The cow was a red heifer with one white sock and an opinion that the draw was a fine place to live and die, and the wet muddy bottom of the draw was not so bad now that she had been standing in it for a day and a half. Wyatt had a rope. June's waders leaked at the left knee within ten minutes, and she did not mention it because mentioning it would have been the sort of thing that would have put her back in the kitchen and she was not going back to the kitchen.

She stood in six inches of cold muddy water with the rope end in both hands and held pressure while Wyatt worked the cow's head. The cow moved one step. Then another. June was laughing most of the time because her left foot had gone numb at twenty minutes in and the laughing was easier than noticing. Wyatt called her Counsellor once, when she had braced the rope around her hip in a way that was not going to hold if the cow lunged, and the word made her ears go pink in a way he could not see under her hat.

He said, "Counsellor, step back."

She stepped back.

The cow went sideways at forty minutes in and put her shoulder against the bank and stopped. Wyatt said, "She's thinking." June said, "About what." He said, "Whether this is worse than where she was." June said, "Is it." He said, "No, but she hasn't figured that out yet. Hold."

She held.

The cow thought for about three minutes, which was a long three minutes standing in ice water. June's teeth started doing the thing her teeth did when she was cold, and she clamped her jaw to stop it and tasted iron where she had bitten the inside of her cheek at some point in the last hour. Wyatt was at the cow's head with one hand flat on the animal's jaw, murmuring something June could not hear, in a register that had nothing to do with her. The cow's ear flicked twice.

Then the cow moved again.

The cow came up onto dry ground at five-ten.

June was soaked to the middle of her thighs. Her hair had come loose on one side. She was standing in the grass at the edge of the draw panting and laughing and smelling like muddy water and cow, and Wyatt coiled the rope with his eyes on the cow and did not look at her.

He said, "Truck."

She said, "Truck."


He drove home with his hand on her thigh.

The drive from the draw to the ranch yard was eleven minutes. Shania was on. She was not trying not to sing along anymore. She was just listening. He put his hand on her thigh at minute two, on the outside of her wet jeans, and did not move it.

Her entire body was screaming for the eleven minutes.

His hand was heavy. The palm was broad enough that his thumb rested on the inside of her thigh and his little finger sat on the outside, which meant she could feel all of him through the denim. The jeans were cold at the skin and his hand was a live weight through the cold, and the pressure of his palm was there without promise or question, and she could feel the callus at the base of his thumb through two layers of wet fabric, and she knew where every ridge of it was.

She watched the road. She did not look at him. She did not look at his hand. She looked at the fence-line and she listened to Shania sing about a man who had been done wrong, and she thought, this is eleven minutes, I can do eleven minutes, and then she thought, oh no, I cannot.

He did not move the hand.

At the turn-off to the ranch he did not go left to the main house. He went right, to his house. He parked in front. He took his hand off her thigh to put the truck in park, and then he got out and came around to her side, and when he opened the door he did not step back.

She got down.

They went inside.

The door closed behind them.


At ten-thirty she was in his bed in another one of his shirts, because her own clothes were still on his bathroom floor wet from the draw and she had not gotten around to putting anything else on.

She said, "Wyatt."

He said, "Yes."

She said, "You kept a list for a week."

He said, "Yes."

She said, "You are a menace."

He said, "Takes one."

She turned out the lamp.
