Five-forty. The main house was cold because the furnace had run out at two and she had not gotten up to relight it, and she had slept in her clothes on top of the duvet the way a person sleeps who does not intend to be there in the morning. The alarm on her phone had gone at four. She had been awake before it.

She was in the grey pantsuit trousers and the fleece she had bought at the feed store on day four, and her hair was in the low ponytail she did for court, and she was not going to cry. She had decided that at three a.m. and she was going to keep it.

The Carhartt was on the hook by the bedroom door where she had left it last night. She did not take it. She did not look at it on the way past either, which was a small, unserious discipline she gave herself credit for anyway.

Her suitcase was by the door. The carry-on was over her shoulder. The notebook Mariana had given her on day one was in the carry-on because she had put it there at eleven last night and had then gotten back up twice to make sure it was still in there.

She did not leave a note. She thought about it and she did not. A note was a small fiction in which she was soft about him, and she was not soft about him. She was going to be the thing the note was not.

She locked the main house with the key Mariana had given her on day one. The porch boards took her weight in the cold without comment. Ruthie was somewhere that was not the porch, which was Ruthie's own small public opinion, and June did not argue with it.

She drove out.


The rental SUV had been on this ranch for seventeen days. It had mud halfway up the doors and a dent on the back bumper from the time she had backed into the post at the feed store on day five, and the pine air freshener that had come with it had given up on day nine. The dash said forty-two degrees and falling.

She made Livingston at six-thirty. The town was not yet awake except for the bakery, which had its light on, and a truck at the gas station with the pump running and nobody at it that she could see.

She took the I-90 entrance south toward the airport at six-thirty-five. She was a quarter-mile down the on-ramp when the SUV made a noise she could not name.

It made a worse one ten seconds later. Something in the engine dropped half a register and then a whole register and then started knocking like a door.

The dashboard went red.

She coasted to the shoulder on the momentum she had, got her hazard lights on, and turned the key. The engine turned once and did nothing. She tried it again because of course she did, and it did nothing the second time either.

She sat for a second with both hands on the wheel.

Then she picked up her phone and called the rental company.

The agent had a voice like a person who had been awake longer than she had. She told him the car had died. He wanted the vehicle ID and she read it off the key tag. He said, "Ma'am, we've got a dealership in Livingston, but that location opens Monday. Today is Thursday."

She said, "Monday."

He said, "Yes ma'am."

She said, "Is there another car available anywhere. Livingston, Bozeman, Butte."

He tapped. He tapped again. He said, "Ma'am, we're dry through Saturday. There's a cattlemen's conference in Bozeman and Big Sky is on spring closure, so every car in the region is out. Saturday morning, eleven, I can get you a compact out of Bozeman."

She said, "Thank you."

She hung up.

She sat for about ten minutes on the shoulder with her hazards clicking. A big rig went by her, and then another, and a Subaru with Idaho plates that slowed a little and then kept going.

At six-fifty-four a Montana Highway Patrol cruiser pulled up behind her with its lights on. The trooper who got out was about her age, and he was courteous, and he called her a tow for the SUV and a shuttle van for the airport. He stayed with her for twenty minutes until both showed up. He did not ask her where she was headed or why. She was grateful about that in a way she could not have named at any earlier hour of her life.

The shuttle van was a white Ford with a driver named Chuck. She got to the airport at nine-fifteen.


The ticket counter was one woman behind a kiosk. June put her driver's license down. The woman typed.

The woman said, "Ma'am, your three p.m. is oversold. I can put you on the nine-fifteen tonight with a confirmed seat, or you can standby for the three."

June said, "Confirmed."

The woman said, "That's twelve hours."

June said, "I know."

She took the boarding pass. She walked to the gate area and found a bank of seats that had a plug behind it and she sat down and put her carry-on on her lap and her phone face-down on the carry-on.

She did not look at email. She did not call anyone. She did not text Wyatt. She did not text Deb.

There was a magazine on the seat next to her, a March issue of Big Sky Journal with a photograph of a trout on the cover, and she picked it up because the trout was the only thing in her line of sight that was not the floor or a stranger. There was an article inside about a man restoring a fishing lodge in the Bitterroot. She read it twice. On the second pass she got through a whole page without taking in a word, which meant the first pass had not been a pass either. She put the magazine down.

At eleven-thirty she went to the diner in the B concourse and ordered a coffee. She did not order food. The waitress looked at her the way a woman looks at a woman who she has understood on sight, and poured the coffee and went away without pushing the menu.

The coffee was bad. She drank it anyway. She was a woman who had drunk Wyatt's coffee for three weeks. This one did not measure.

She put her phone face-down on the formica.

She thought about ordering toast. She did not. She thought about calling her sister, and then about how the conversation would go, and about the things her sister would say that would be accurate, and she put that phone call in the drawer in her head with the other things she was not doing today.

At one p.m. she moved to a different bank of seats because the first one had started to feel like a small apartment. The second bank had a view of the runway. There was nothing on the runway. She watched the nothing for a while.

At three-thirty she walked over to the bar.


The bar was at the end of the B concourse past the newsstand. The sign above the door said TARMAC. Inside, it was darker than the rest of the airport and smelled like old fryer oil and lemon cleaner, and there were four televisions above the bar tuned to a golf tournament nobody in the room was watching.

The bartender was a woman in her fifties with a grey ponytail and a dark red lipstick that had been applied carefully and about four hours ago. A name tag said CAROL.

June sat down on the third stool from the door.

Carol said, "What'll it be."

June said, "Tequila."

Carol said, "Any preference."

June said, "The house."

Carol poured. She set the shot glass down on a cocktail napkin. She said, "You want a lime."

June said, "No."

She drank it. It was present going down, which was what tequila did when it had been twelve hours.

She said, "Another."

Carol looked at her for a second. Then she poured another.

June drank that one, and then she said, "Can I have a margarita."

Carol said, "Sure." She made it with fresh lime and the rocks she had already, and set it down.

June sipped it.

Carol was ringing a tab behind the bar with one hand and watching June with the other eye.

June finished most of the margarita. She said, "Tequila."

Carol said, "After the margarita or with it."

June said, "After."

Carol poured. She put a tall glass of water down next to the tequila. She said, "That's the water."

June said, "I see it."

She drank the tequila. She did not drink the water. She put her phone face-up on the bar because face-down had started to feel like a stunt and she was tired of performing for herself.

It was four-fourteen.

She thumbed the messages icon. She scrolled to his thread. The last message in it was his, from day three, the still coming over when I'm done that she had reread eleven times and then stopped counting. She looked at it. It looked at her.

She typed.

I hate you.

She sent it.

She put the phone back on the bar face-down.

Carol, without any kind of face on at all, said, "Honey."

June said, "I'm fine."

Carol said, "Mm."


Wyatt's phone buzzed in his back pocket at four-sixteen.

He was in the calving barn. Cal was at the workbench with his reading glasses on tightening a bolt on the gate of the third pen, because the third pen's gate had been an intermittent problem for a week and Cal had decided today was the day it stopped being one. There were two heifers in the pens. A third, 142, was fourteen hours from delivering and moving slow.

Wyatt got the phone out.

He read the text.

He did not move.

Cal, without looking up from the bolt, said, "What."

Wyatt walked over to the bench. He held the phone out so Cal could read it without taking his hands off the wrench.

Cal read it.

Cal set the wrench down.

Cal said, "Go."

Wyatt said, "I can't leave. 142 is going tonight."

Cal said, "I've done this for twenty-two years. Go."

Wyatt put the phone in his pocket and walked out of the barn.

Cal picked the wrench back up and finished the bolt.


At the bar, the next drink came.

Carol set it down in front of June and watched her. June picked it up and took one sip.

Carol reached across the bar and took the glass out of her hand and set it back on the rail on her side.

Carol said, "Honey, you're done."

June said, "I'm fine."

Carol said, "No you're not."

Carol poured a full pint glass of water and slid it over. She said, "Drink that. All of it. Then I want you to walk down this concourse to arrivals and sit on a bench. I'll call you a cab to the arrivals side so if you need one later it comes to where you are. You can't stay here. The Port Authority doesn't want drunks at a gate, and I don't want you at my bar."

June drank the water. Most of it. She got through most of it.

She paid. She left a tip that was twice the tab. Carol looked at the tip and pushed half of it back across the bar.

Carol said, "Don't tip me. Just get home."

June did not explain that she could not get home. She picked up her carry-on. She put the water that Carol had just given her into the mesh side pocket of the carry-on, and Carol watched her do it and did not comment.

June walked out of the bar.

Arrivals was at the far end of a long tiled hallway. She walked it with her carry-on rolling behind her and her phone face-down in the outside pocket. The hallway had a slight lean to it, or she did, and she listed once and corrected, and a woman with a little boy on her hip moved out of her way politely and then kept a little more distance than was necessary after.

The baggage-claim hall at the end was low-ceilinged and lit too white. There were three benches along the far wall, empty, because the last flight in had been at one and the next one was not until five.

She meant to sit on one.

She got about ten feet short of the first bench and her legs did not feel like her legs anymore, and she sat down on the linoleum with her back against the wall and her carry-on on its side next to her like a small dog. The wall was cold. She did not mind it.

It was five-fifty-five.

She put her head on her own shoulder.

She did not mean to fall asleep.


Wyatt was in the ranch truck by four-thirty.

His personal truck had a bad front tire and he had been meaning to change it for a week, and he was not going to change it this afternoon, so he took the ranch truck because the ranch truck had studded tires on and because if he was going to drive into what was coming he was going to do it in the thing he trusted.

What was coming was a late-April blizzard, and it came in the way late-April blizzards come in, which is to say fast and wet and with visibility that started at two miles and was at a quarter-mile by the time he hit the county line. The snow was the kind that stuck to the windshield and turned to slush on the wipers and then froze on the second pass so the wipers dragged a layer of ice back and forth and did not clear the glass.

He kept it at forty-two on the highway. Anything faster was someone else's problem and was going to be his if he got close enough to it.

The radio was Shania. It was the only station that came in, and he did not change the station even when it went to the ads, because changing the station was a decision he did not have to make and he had been making decisions all afternoon.

Around the two-hour mark, near the Three Forks exit, a memory caught him and stayed for a moment longer than he would have let it any other day.

He had driven this stretch once before in weather like this. It had been the worst night of his life, and the woman asleep beside him was the reason he was out in a second storm.

The dashboard read thirty-one. The passenger-side wiper had iced up two inches out from the base and was making a wet slapping sound that was not going to fix itself. A semi came past him in the oncoming lane with its taillights running red and went by in a wash of slush that shook the truck a quarter-inch on its springs, and then was gone. The turn-off to the Paradise Valley ranch road was ninety minutes out.

He took a breath and kept driving.


He made Livingston at eight-fifteen.

The airport parking lot had eight cars in it and the light over the entrance was yellow through the snow. He parked. He walked in. His hat was off and in his hand because he had not put it on when he got out, and his hair was wet on the side the wind had taken.

The terminal was empty, the small-airport empty of a Thursday at eight. He went past the ticket counter, which was closed, and past the newsstand, which was closed, and down the hallway toward arrivals.

He saw her before he got to the benches.

She was not on a bench. She was on the floor about a row in front of the first bench with her back against the wall and her carry-on on its side next to her. Her head was on her own shoulder. Her mouth was slightly open. One of her heels had come off and was on the linoleum next to her foot. The other heel was still on.

He stood there for a full minute.

Then he took his canvas barn jacket off and he put it over her, tucking it in at her neck so it would not slide, and he sat down on the bench directly behind her so he could see her without her having to see him first.

He did not wake her up.


She opened her eyes at eight-forty-two.

The hall was still too white. Her mouth was dry in the way her mouth was dry when she had been sleeping with it open, and there was a jacket over her that was not hers and she was on a floor, and it took her about three seconds to put all of those together because her head was not working at the speed it usually did.

She turned her head.

He was on the bench behind her. His elbows were on his knees. He was in a flannel over a henley and his hat was in his hand and his hair was flat on one side from the hat and wet on the other from the snow.

She said, "Oh no."

He said, "Yep."

She said, "I hate you."

He said, "Yep."

She said, "I'm too drunk to be angry about this."

He said, "I know. That's why I came."

She closed her eyes again for about ten seconds. She opened them. He was still there.

He held out his hand without getting up. She took it. He pulled her up with one pull, carefully, and then stood when she was standing, and she was on one heel and one bare foot for a second and then he crouched down and put the other heel back on her foot because she was not going to manage the buckle at that angle.

He picked up the heel that had come off and put it in the side pocket of the carry-on. He put the carry-on on its wheels. He slung her bag over his own shoulder.

He said, "Truck's in the lot."

She said, "Okay."

She walked beside him. The jacket was still around her shoulders because he had not taken it back.


The cab of the truck had the heat on full from the drive in, and it hit her in the face like a room she had not earned. He helped her up into the passenger seat. She did not argue. He reached across her and did her seatbelt, and she felt it click and she did not say anything about it.

He shut her door. He walked around the front of the truck with his hands in his pockets and the snow on his shoulders. He got in on his side.

He pulled out of the lot at eight-fifty.

She put her head against the passenger window inside of twelve minutes. The glass was cold and it felt good against her temple, and then she was asleep.

He drove at thirty-two because the road was ice. He kept the heat on her side high and his side low.

The window on her side fogged from her breath. A small clear spot opened at mouth-level, about the size of a heart, and did not close again for an hour.

At mile two-thirty a deer stood at the edge of the highway in the snow with its head up and its ears forward, and it did not cross. He let off the accelerator for half a mile and then put his foot back on it when the deer dropped its head.

The water bottle Carol had put in her carry-on was in her bag on the floor of the cab. He had seen it when he picked up the bag. He did not wake her to make her drink it. He would put it on her nightstand later.

Her phone was on her lap, face-down. The jacket was over her to the chin.

They did not talk. There was nothing to say that was going to be said at thirty-two miles an hour in a blizzard, and she was asleep, and he was driving.

He got them to the main house at one in the morning.


He pulled into the yard. The main house was dark except for the porch light, which she had left on at some point in the last twenty hours because she had forgotten to turn it off on the way out.

He cut the engine.

She half-woke. She said, "Oh no," in a voice that was not all the way back yet.

He said, "You're home."

She said something that was not quite a word and put her face against the window again.

He got out. He came around to her side. He opened her door and unbuckled her belt. He lifted her out of the seat because she was not going to walk the twelve feet from the truck to the porch, and asleep she weighed less than she weighed awake, which was the thing bodies did. The jacket was over her shoulders and he shifted her in his arms once to keep it there.

He carried her inside. He used the key she had left in the porch light's lip because he had seen her do that on day six and had said nothing about it then and was grateful for it tonight.

He took her up the stairs. He put her down on the bed in her own room in the main house, on top of the duvet first, and then he pulled the duvet back and put her under it. He took off the one heel that was still on her foot. He set both heels by the door in the pair, aligned.

He did not get into the bed.

He pulled the duvet up to her chin.

He went downstairs. He filled a glass of water at the kitchen sink, the full glass, and he got two aspirin out of the bottle in the cabinet above the stove, and he carried both up to her room and set them on the nightstand.

He found a pen in the drawer of the desk by the window.

On a sheet of the ledger notebook he tore out, he wrote, in the block capitals he always wrote in:

> WATER ON THE NIGHTSTAND. ASPIRIN. CALL IN AN HOUR. W.

He set the note next to the glass.

He took his jacket off the bed where he had laid it when he set her down, put it on his shoulder, and walked out of the house. He locked the door behind him with the key from the porch light. He put the key back in the lip.

He walked across the south pasture to his own house in the snow, and he did not look back at the main house once on the way, because looking back was a small thing he was not going to do tonight.


She woke at nine the next morning.

Her mouth was sand. Her head hurt in a way she was going to deserve for the next six hours, and the light through her curtains was the sharp late-April light that came in after a storm.

The water was on the nightstand. She saw it before she saw anything else. The two aspirin sat on a torn half-sheet from the kitchen ledger pad, lined up straight, and the note was beside them.

She recognised the handwriting before she read the words. It was the same block-capitals hand as the three-word label on the tack-room key hook, TACK ROOM DO NOT LOSE, which she had read on day two when she had been looking for a bridle she could not have named if he had asked her to.

She read the note.

She read it again.

She drank the water in three swallows. She took the aspirin. She sat with the note for a minute.

She did not call in an hour.

She called in seven minutes.

He picked up on the second ring.
